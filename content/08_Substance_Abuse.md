# Part VIII: Substance Abuse and Drug Policy

Substance abuse constitutes one of the most severe and deeply entrenched social policy challenges confronting the Republic of Maldives. What began as isolated incidents of hashish use in the late 1970s has evolved over five decades into a nationwide crisis affecting every atoll, every age cohort, and every dimension of social welfare -- from public health and criminal justice to youth development, family cohesion, and national security. The Maldives' geographic position near the Golden Crescent and Golden Triangle trafficking routes, its extreme population density in Male, its youthful demographic profile, and the paradoxes of rapid modernisation alongside institutional fragility have together created conditions uniquely conducive to the spread of drug use and the entrenchment of a drug economy. This chapter provides a comprehensive account of the historical evolution, legal architecture, epidemiological evidence, treatment infrastructure, institutional responses, and unresolved policy tensions that define the Maldivian drug situation as it stands today.

## 8.1 Historical Evolution of Drug Use in the Maldives

### 8.1.1 Pre-1970s: A Society Without a Drug Problem

Drug abuse was not a recognised phenomenon in Maldivian society before the mid-1970s. Although anecdotal accounts reference limited opium use in the early twentieth century, these cases were confined to small numbers of individuals and did not constitute a public health concern.[^1] The Maldives remained geographically isolated, economically simple, and socially cohesive; the conditions that typically enable the emergence of drug markets -- urbanisation, cash economies, exposure to international trafficking networks -- were largely absent.

### 8.1.2 The 1970s: Tourism, Globalisation, and the First Cases

The appearance of drug abuse in its modern form coincided with the introduction of tourism in the early 1970s. Hundreds of tourists began arriving daily from Europe, Southeast Asia, and South Asia. This period also coincided with the global escalation of drug abuse and a dramatic increase in overseas travel by Maldivians.[^2] It would be hasty to draw a direct causal relationship between tourism and drug use, as the authors of the UNAFEI resource paper have noted, but the two phenomena were undeniably co-temporal.

Official recognition of the drug problem came in 1977 when a person was arrested with 350 grams of hashish. This event triggered the passage of the first principal legislative act dealing with narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances: **Law No. 17/77 -- The Law on Drugs**.[^3] This legislation represented the Maldives' initial attempt to create a legal framework for drug control, establishing basic prohibitions on possession, sale, and trafficking.

### 8.1.3 The 1980s and 1990s: Escalation and the Heroin Crisis

Throughout the 1980s, commonly abused substances were hashish oil and cannabis derivatives. The situation escalated dramatically in 1993 with the detection of the first case of heroin abuse.[^4] The introduction of heroin -- particularly the crude form known as "brown sugar" -- fundamentally altered the Maldivian drug landscape. Drug abuse among young males aged 16 to 25 escalated sharply. By 1996, a total of 241 cases of substance abuse were reported to the police. In 1998, over 450 arrests were made for drug abuse and related offences.[^5]

The first major seizure of cocaine occurred in September 1993 at Male International Airport, when eight kilograms of cocaine were found concealed in the false bottom of a suitcase carried by a foreign national.[^6] In 1997, three Maldivians were discovered attempting to smuggle 1,372 grams of hashish oil in seven professionally packed cans of corned beef while boarding a flight from Trivandrum Airport to Male.[^7] These incidents revealed the growing sophistication of trafficking networks targeting the country.

### 8.1.4 The Institutional Response: Narcotics Control Board (1997)

The government responded to the escalating crisis by establishing the **Narcotics Control Board (NCB)** in 1997, alongside the **Drug Rehabilitation Centre (DRC)** on the island of Himafushi.[^8] The NCB was tasked with coordinating supply reduction, demand reduction, and prevention activities. However, as subsequent evaluations would reveal, the institutional response was fundamentally under-resourced relative to the scale of the problem.

### 8.1.5 The 2000s: From Crisis to Epidemic

By the early 2000s, the Maldivian authorities reported that "the problem of drug-related offences [had] become the most frequent one faced by the Maldivian criminal courts, showing a 200% increase in recent years."[^9] The majority of drug abusers were young people aged 16 to 30. For a country where more than 50% of the population was below 16 years of age, this represented an alarming demographic trajectory.

The period saw several significant developments:

- **2003**: The first Rapid Situation Assessment (RSA) of drug abuse was conducted with UNODC support, providing the first empirical window into the scale of the problem.
- **2006**: The NGO Journey conducted its own rapid assessment, finding that 28% of respondents reported having injected drugs at some point.
- **2008**: The Biological and Behavioural Survey (BBS) estimated 375 injectors in Male and Addu alone. UNODC assisted in developing the first national **Drug Control Master Plan 2008-2012**.
- **2010**: UNODC signed the first country-specific technical cooperation project for the Maldives, titled "Strengthening the National Response to Combat Drug Abuse in Maldives."[^10]

By 2010, it was commonly reported that one in 100 adults in the Maldives struggled with substance abuse. Informal estimates suggested between 10,000 and 30,000 young people were using drugs -- representing between 8% and 23% of the youth population, figures plausible by comparison with European, North American, and Australian norms.[^11] It was also commonly reported that most Maldivian extended families had at least one active drug user, and in one group of users interviewed by the World Bank evaluation team, 32% of their siblings also used drugs.[^12]

## 8.2 Legal Framework: Drugs Act 2011 and Related Legislation

### 8.2.1 Law No. 17/77: The Original Drug Law

The original 1977 legislation was a relatively blunt instrument. Section 2 of the Law on Narcotics criminalised planting, production, import, export, selling, buying, giving, and possession with intent to sell of illegal drugs, with penalties extending to life imprisonment.[^13] Sections 2 and 3 also prohibited the manufacture of controlled substances and made the importation, supply, and sale of precursor chemicals punishable on the same basis as trafficking offences. The law drew no meaningful distinction between drug use, possession, and trafficking -- a conflation that would have profound consequences for the criminal justice system.

### 8.2.2 The Drugs Act 17/2011: A Paradigm Shift

In 2011, the legal framework was comprehensively overhauled with the passage of the **Drugs Act (Law No. 17/2011)**. This legislation represented a significant shift in the state's approach to drug policy, establishing several new institutions and mechanisms:[^14]

**National Drug Agency (NDA)**: The Act established the NDA as the primary government body responsible for drug prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation. The NDA replaced the earlier Narcotics Control Board and was given a broader mandate encompassing treatment standards, prevention programmes, laboratory services, and data collection.

**Drug Court**: A specialised Drug Court was established to handle substance use cases, with the explicit aim of diverting drug-dependent persons toward treatment and rehabilitation rather than incarceration. The Drug Court can order individuals convicted of drug use offences to undergo assessment and treatment programmes administered by the NDA.

**Treatment over Punishment**: The Act reformed measures to motivate drug-dependent persons to enrol in treatment and rehabilitation programmes, marking a formal legislative acknowledgement that addiction is a health condition requiring medical and psychosocial intervention rather than solely a criminal matter.

**Classification of Offences**: The law distinguished more clearly between categories of drug offences -- use, possession, and trafficking -- establishing differentiated penalties for each. Trafficking offences continued to attract severe penalties, including life imprisonment, while use offences could be channelled through the Drug Court system toward treatment.

### 8.2.3 The Gap Between Law and Implementation

Before the 2011 Drug Act, the Drug Treatment and Rehabilitation Centre (DTRC) on Himafushi was the sole drug treatment facility in the country. By 2021, ten facilities across the country had been established, of which seven were in operation, serving as treatment centres for drug-dependent persons.[^15] However, the 2010 World Bank evaluation found fundamental structural problems that persisted after the legislative reform:

- Penalties for drug offences remained high and inflexible in practice.
- Too many drug users were imprisoned to no obvious gain to the community -- neither in terms of deterrence nor rehabilitation.
- Drug users became trapped in a penal and rehabilitation system that had the unintended consequence of making rehabilitation harder.
- The links between the criminal justice system and treatment may have worked against recovery.[^16]

The 2019 Youth Vulnerability study noted that "there has not been a full assessment of the 2011 Drug Law and its effects," and recommended that the Disability Act and Mental Health Strategic Plan "should be re-invigorated urgently along with full implementation of the 2011 Drug Act."[^17]

### 8.2.4 Drug Scheduling and Precursor Controls

The Maldives is not a producer or manufacturer of illicit drugs. There has never been a reported case of illegal drug manufacturing or cultivation on Maldivian territory, and there is no chemical or pharmaceutical industry that could serve as a source of precursor chemicals.[^18] The anti-drug policy is therefore aimed primarily at preventing narcotic drugs, psychotropic substances, and precursor chemicals from entering the country. However, the geographical formation of the country -- 1,190 islands spread across 90,000 square kilometres of ocean -- makes comprehensive border interdiction extraordinarily difficult.

## 8.3 National Drug Surveys (2003, 2008/09, 2011/12)

### 8.3.1 The 2003 Rapid Situation Assessment (RSA)

The first systematic study of drug abuse in the Maldives was the **Rapid Situation Assessment** conducted in 2003 with UNODC financial support and UNDP Maldives project funding. The RSA employed a combination of quantitative and qualitative methods:[^19]

- **Primary interviews** with 204 drug users (above age 16, reporting drug use in the previous six months) drawn from 471 potential drug users contacted.
- **111 key informant interviews** and **49 focus group discussions** covering 443 persons.
- **13 in-depth interviews** with drug users and **19 family member interviews**.
- **Secondary data** from 2,304 persons arrested by the Drug Control Bureau and 547 clients referred to the NCB.
- In total, drug-related information was obtained from **3,909 individuals**.

The sites selected -- Male, Miladhunmadulu Dhekunuburi, Faadhipolhu in the north, and Addu Atoll in the south -- were chosen based on higher reported drug use. Key findings included:

- **Drugs of initiation**: Primarily opioids, especially brown sugar (43%), followed by cannabinoids (34%), alcohol (12%), "cola water" or eau de cologne (5%), and sedatives/hypnotics (4%).
- **Primary drugs in previous month**: Opioids (76%) and cannabinoids (12%).
- **Age profile**: Mean age of respondents was 21.4 years (range 15-42). Almost half were under 20; 32% were aged 20-24; 13% were aged 25-29.
- **Reasons for initiation**: Peer pressure (38%) and desire to experiment (26%).
- **Regional variations**: Key informants from the north reported frequent abuse of "cola water" (eau de cologne) and "Dunlop" (glue, an inhalant), alongside cannabis, pharmaceuticals, alcohol, ecstasy (MDMA), and the local *oshani* for intoxication.[^20]

### 8.3.2 The 2008 Biological and Behavioural Survey (BBS)

The 2008 BBS, supported by UNDP, recruited 147 people who inject drugs in Male and 128 in Addu. It found that less than half (46%) had injected in the last 30 days (Male 43%, Addu 50%). In total, 16% (44 of 275) were injecting every day, and these were mainly older drug users. In Male, 22% had shared a needle or syringe the last time they injected, and 25% had done so in Addu.[^21]

The BBS reported no cases of Hepatitis B among drug users (consistent with the high vaccination coverage of 90-98%) but found one case of Hepatitis C in Male and one in Addu. There was a cumulative total of 14 HIV cases among Maldivians, of whom three were still living, and none were injectors. Only 18% of injectors in Male and 16% in Male had ever had an HIV test.[^22]

### 8.3.3 The 2011/12 National Drug Use Survey

The first National Drug Use Survey was conducted in 2011-2012 with UNODC support. Its results revealed a high prevalence of substance use, accounting for more than **7,000 drug users** in the Maldives.[^23] This survey established the baseline against which all subsequent data would be measured. After its completion, a decade passed with no new surveys or studies conducted to understand the evolving drug situation.

### 8.3.4 The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis

After a gap of nearly ten years, the National Drug Agency partnered with the World Health Organization to conduct the **Situational Analysis of Drugs in the Maldives 2021**. This study comprised:[^24]

- A **quantitative survey** of 403 drug users representing all atolls.
- **Qualitative key informant interviews** with 24 stakeholders from eight institutions.
- **Longitudinal secondary data analysis** covering 2011-2020 from eight stakeholder institutions: NDA, Maldives Police Service, Drug Court, Maldives Correctional Services, Juvenile Justice Unit, Indhira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH), Maldives Customs Services, and Journey/Hands Together.

Key findings from secondary data (2016-2020) included:

- More than **50,000 laboratory tests** for drugs were carried out at NDA, with an average of 10,000 tests per year.
- Patients testing positive for **opiates increased more than two-fold** from 2016 to 2020.
- The preference of substance users had **shifted from naturally occurring substances to synthetic drugs**.
- The mean age of substance users arrested by police **increased from 27.7 years (2015) to 31.3 years (2020)**, suggesting an ageing cohort of entrenched users alongside continued new initiation.[^25]

## 8.4 Prevalence, Patterns, and Demographics of Drug Use

### 8.4.1 Scale of the Problem

Establishing precise prevalence figures for drug use in the Maldives has been hampered by the absence of regular population-based surveys and the fragmentation of data across multiple institutions. The 2011/12 survey identified over 7,000 drug users. The 2021 Situational Analysis noted that between 2016 and 2019, the Statistical Yearbook of the Maldives reported a 20% increase in the import of drugs, a 47% increase in the sale of drugs, a 4% decrease in possession of drugs, and a 22% decrease in drug use -- though these figures reflected reported offences rather than underlying prevalence.[^26]

### 8.4.2 Gender Dynamics

The drug industry in the Maldives is overwhelmingly male-dominated. Across all data sources -- police arrests, Drug Court cases, NDA treatment files, Customs seizures, and hospital presentations -- males constitute the vast majority of substance users, offenders, and trafficking operatives.[^27] The 2021 Situational Analysis confirmed this pattern across all secondary data categories:

- Drug offenders: predominantly male
- Illegal importers: predominantly male
- Drug Court cases: predominantly male
- Health-seeking substance users: predominantly male
- Juvenile substance abuse cases: predominantly male

However, female drug use is believed to be significantly underreported due to stronger social stigma, fewer pathways to detection (women are less likely to be stopped and searched), and the near-total absence of gender-sensitive treatment services. The 2010 World Bank evaluation noted that there was no access to detoxification services for females.[^28]

### 8.4.3 Age Profile and the Youth Dimension

Drug use in the Maldives is disproportionately concentrated among young people. The 2003 RSA found a mean age of drug users of 21.4 years, with almost half under 20. Prior to 1993, the majority of drug offenders were between 25 and 40 years of age; the introduction of heroin shifted the profile dramatically downward to males aged 16-25.[^29]

Arrests of juveniles for drug offences increased from 16 in 2001 to 79 in 2006 and to 164 (29% of total juvenile arrests) in 2007.[^30] The 2021 Situational Analysis tracked juvenile substance use from 2014-2020, revealing persistent high levels of youth involvement across all atolls.

The 2019 Global Youth Tobacco Survey (GYTS) found that among students aged 13-15:
- **45.7%** used tobacco products
- **46.1%** used smokeless tobacco[^31]

These figures, while specifically concerning tobacco, indicate the broader normalisation of substance use among Maldivian adolescents.

### 8.4.4 Substances Used

The primary drugs of abuse in the Maldives have evolved over time:

- **1970s-1980s**: Hashish and hashish oil dominated.
- **1993 onwards**: Heroin ("brown sugar") became the primary substance, alongside cannabis derivatives.
- **2000s**: Pharmaceutical preparations, MDMA/ecstasy, alcohol, inhalants ("cola water" and "Dunlop" glue), and sedatives/hypnotics joined the market.
- **2010s-2020s**: A diversification toward **synthetic drugs and new psychotropic substances**, with a shift from traditional plant-based substances.[^32]

Injecting drug use, while remaining relatively low by regional standards, has been a persistent concern. The 2003 RSA found 8% of drug users had injected. The 2006 Journey assessment found 28% had injected at some time. The 2008 BBS estimated 375 injectors in Male and Addu. Heroin is typically smoked rather than injected, and injecting remains relatively rare and mainly infrequent.[^33] Overdose rates have been correspondingly low (1 overdose death in 2007, 3 in 2008, 2 in 2009), though drug-related deaths by suicide increased (11 in 2009, including 9 by hanging and 3 by overdose, for a total of 19 drug-related deaths).[^34]

### 8.4.5 Trafficking Patterns

The Maldives lies proximate to both the Golden Crescent (Afghanistan-Pakistan-Iran) and Golden Triangle (Myanmar-Laos-Thailand) drug production regions. As an import-oriented economy with an international airport and numerous sea ports, it is potentially vulnerable as a transit point for large shipments.

The 2021 Situational Analysis identified significant shifts in trafficking patterns:

- **Source countries shifted** from South Asian countries to European countries.
- **Transport mechanisms shifted** from air and sea to mail.
- **Local production expanded**, with knowledge of locally produced drugs among users increasing.
- **Migrant workers** became increasingly involved in the drug trade as carriers.
- The main trafficking route historically was Trivandrum (India) to Male, with Bangladeshi nationals disproportionately represented among arrested carriers.[^35]

Maldives Customs Services data for 2010 showed nine drug seizures: one case of heroin (360 grams), four cases of cannabis (4,428 grams), and four cases of hashish (3,795 grams).[^36]

## 8.5 Drug Treatment and Rehabilitation Services

### 8.5.1 Overview of the Treatment System

The drug treatment and rehabilitation system in the Maldives operates through two parallel pathways: the **compulsory treatment system** (court-ordered) and the **voluntary treatment system**. Both pathways converge on the same set of institutions, though the entry points, legal obligations, and post-treatment monitoring differ significantly.

Prior to the 2011 Drug Act, the DTRC on Himafushi was the country's sole treatment facility. By 2021, ten facilities had been established across the country, of which seven were operational.[^37] The treatment infrastructure includes:

- **Drug Rehabilitation Centre (DRC)**, Himafushi (later relocated to K. Himmafushi)
- **Regional Drug Rehabilitation Centre**, Addu (intermittently operational)
- **Community Service Centres** in Male, Addu, and Fuvamulah
- **Detoxification centres** in S. Hulhumeedhoo and K. Villimale
- **Methadone Maintenance Therapy (MMT) clinic** in Male (at the DDPRS facility in Greenge)
- **Drop-in centres** and outreach services operated by NGOs

### 8.5.2 The Compulsory Treatment Pathway

The compulsory pathway operates as follows:[^38]

**Arrest**: If a person is found in possession of an illegal drug, a charge is filed and a urine test conducted at the police station. If illegal narcotic or psychotropic substances are detected, additional charges for drug use may be filed.

**Judicial System**: Once a case is filed with the Prosecutor General, the accused is sent to the lower court for hearing and verdict. There are three categories of drug offences: use, possession, and trafficking (depending on amount found). In most drug possession and use cases, a defence lawyer is not provided. If found guilty, the judge hands down a sentence based on the drug law. First-time offenders are eligible for transfer to the Drug Rehabilitation Centre, provided there are no pending cases within the judicial system.

**Prison**: The offender is sent to one of three prisons to serve their sentence. Parole can be applied for once one-third of the sentence has been served. Alternatively, a drug offender can apply to the Treatment Board for enrolment into the Drug Rehabilitation Centre.

**Drug Rehabilitation Centre**: Based on the Therapeutic Community modality, residents must complete a three-phase programme for 6-8 months and remain drug-free during their stay. They are monitored by regular urine testing. Positive urine tests result in removal and return to prison to finish the original sentence. Release from the DRC depends upon approval from the Treatment Board.

**Community Service Centre**: Release from the DRC is followed by an 11-month community service order. A system of regular signing-in, urine testing, and counselling constitutes the main components. If tested positive twice for drug use, the individual is sent back to the DRC for a second time.

**Reintegration**: Following completion of the CSC, the individual can resume unsupervised life outside the Criminal Justice System. Regular contact with recovery groups is encouraged, though a major barrier to reintegration is the difficulty of finding employment.

### 8.5.3 The Voluntary Treatment Pathway

The voluntary pathway follows a different entry point:[^39]

**Awareness and Decision**: Usually through a referral from a family member, peer, or contact with an NGO, an active drug user may decide to stop using drugs.

**Detoxification**: Depending on the type of drug dependency and severity, detoxification takes from seven days to one month. Two options are available: residential detoxification managed by the DDPRS in S. Hulhumeedhoo and K. Villimale (a semi-structured programme lasting 21 days), or self-detoxification at home without medical support. There is no follow-up after medical detoxification.

**Drug Rehabilitation Centre**: Voluntary entrants are subject to the same rules as those who enter through the Criminal Justice System.

**Methadone Maintenance Therapy**: A dispensing clinic located at the main DDPRS facility in Greenge, Male. Volunteers initially needed clearance from five different government departments to ensure no criminal cases were being processed before enrolment. This requirement was later reviewed and in most cases waived. Methadone is dispensed daily and flexible dosing is managed by the clinician. Counselling services are provided by both DDPRS counsellors and the NGO Journey. There are no time limits for staying in the programme.[^40]

**Reintegration**: A voluntary decision to leave the MMT programme is discussed with the clinician. If the individual does not have a criminal record, employment opportunities are available that are not available to those who completed mandatory treatment -- illustrating how the criminal justice pathway actively disadvantages recovery.

### 8.5.4 The Drug Rehabilitation Centre: Operational Detail

The DRC was established on Himafushi island in 1989 (some sources say 1997 for its formalised programme). By 2010, it had admitted 2,597 people, averaging about 207 per full operating year. Optimum capacity was 150 with a maximum of 250. A dormitory accommodated up to 20 women.[^41] Operating costs were estimated at $20-25 per person per day.

**Staffing**: A counsellor in charge of the facility, a senior administrator, seven counsellors, and security staff. Most counselling staff were young. The DRC also included a medical department comprising a doctor and two nurses.[^42]

**Treatment Methodology**: The DRC is based on the US Daytop Therapeutic Community (TC) modality, modified for Maldivian culture, beliefs, and Islamic principles. The programme is highly structured, aimed principally at behavioural modification. It is based on four structures and five pillars of TC, proceeding through a "primary phase" and a "re-entry phase."[^43]

**Primary Phase -- Six Stages**:

| Stage | Duration | Role | Activities |
|-------|----------|------|------------|
| 1 | 40 days | "Young Member" | Taught TC philosophies; attends life skills, religious classes, sports, recreation, literacy, and general education |
| 2 | 21 days | "Departmental Crew" | Cleaning, arranging tools, stationery, and belongings |
| 3 | 21 days | "Ramrods" | Put in charge of paperwork |
| 4 | 21 days | "Head of Department" (HOD) | Organises department activities |
| 5 | 21 days | "Co-coordinator of Department" (COD) | Coordinates with Staff on Duty to formulate action plans and arrange daily activities |
| 6 | 21 days | "Older Member in House" | Helps the Staff on Duty run all programmes and departments |

**Re-entry Phase**: Patients learn relapse prevention skills, problem solving, and decision making. The importance of self-help groups is stressed. Counsellors, the clinical supervisor, and the patient must collectively agree on readiness before release to the Community Service Centre.[^44]

### 8.5.5 The Community Service Centre ("Halfway House")

Upon release from the DRC, patients enter the Community Service Centre, operated under the Department of Rehabilitation Services. Monitoring includes regular check-ins with community centre counsellors and routine urine testing. Help is offered whenever needed, and the programme serves as a bridge between residential treatment and full community reintegration.[^45]

### 8.5.6 Critical Assessment of Treatment Services

The 2010 World Bank evaluation by Mortimore and Stimson identified fundamental deficiencies across the treatment system:[^46]

- **Treatment period too short**: The 6-8 month rehabilitation programme was considered insufficient, particularly given the absence of a structured re-entry programme.
- **No sustainable aftercare**: Even if patients were "cured," they returned to the same social conditions that contributed to their addiction, with no aftercare support.
- **Recycling through the system**: People were being "recycled" through prison and the treatment system with no clear exit.
- **Services operating below capacity**: Some services were not cost-efficient.
- **Lack of coordination**: Multiple agencies operated in silos, undermining treatment continuity.
- **Employment as the missing link**: The lack of job opportunities for recovering drug users was identified as the single greatest obstacle to reintegration across all services.
- **Administrative systems making innovation difficult**: Bureaucratic requirements (such as the five-department clearance for MMT enrolment) deterred voluntary participation.

The evaluation concluded with a stark assessment: "Many of the components of a good treatment and rehabilitation system are in place. There is a lack of coordination which has a negative impact on helping people become abstinent."[^47]

## 8.6 National Standards for Drug Treatment

### 8.6.1 Treatment Modalities

The Maldives has employed several treatment modalities, not always in a coordinated manner:

**Therapeutic Community (TC)**: The primary modality at the DRC, based on the US Daytop model, modified for Maldivian cultural and Islamic context. The TC relies on a structured, hierarchical community of peers for behavioural modification.

**Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)**: Counselling forms the core of the CBT approach used alongside the TC. Counselling helps the client recognise how drugs affect health and lifestyle, encouraging rejection of drugs and development of spiritual and psychosocial skills as alternatives.[^48]

**Methadone Maintenance Therapy (MMT)**: Introduced as a pilot in Male, the MMT programme represented the first pharmacological treatment for opioid dependence in the Maldives. The 2010 evaluation found "sufficient positive evidence now to roll out the methadone maintenance programme to other community sites."[^49] However, scale-up was slow, and by 2021 it remained limited.

**Detoxification**: Residential detoxification at two centres (S. Hulhumeedhoo and K. Villimale) follows a semi-structured 21-day programme. The World Bank evaluation recommended exploring mobile detoxification camps as a cost-effective alternative to static centres, and also recommended detoxification services in general hospitals.[^50]

### 8.6.2 Counselling and Human Resource Gaps

A persistent theme across all evaluations has been the acute shortage of qualified clinical staff. The 2006 thesis by Ageel identified tensions between NNCB policy and clinical practice, a lack of clinical staff in policy-making, and inadequate monitoring and training.[^51] The 2010 evaluation found that most counselling staff at the DRC were young and lacked professional qualifications. The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis highlighted that "low accessibility to services due to a severe lack of qualified human resources remains the biggest challenge and is the key area of concern at present."[^52]

Recommendations across multiple evaluations have called for:
- A specialised drug counselling curriculum developed jointly by the Ministry of Education and NGOs
- Professionalisation of counselling with career development pathways
- Employment of recovering drug users as peer counsellors and outreach workers
- A support mechanism for counsellors to prevent burnout

### 8.6.3 Waiting Times and Access Barriers

The 2021 Situational Analysis documented significant delays in the treatment pipeline:

- **Long waiting times** between drug court orders and assessment completion, and between assessment completion and treatment programme start.
- The mean number of days between sentencing and implementation of the sentence was documented, revealing systemic bottlenecks.
- NDA client files showed a growing backlog across years, with more individuals registered than could be accommodated in available treatment slots.[^53]

These delays have profound implications: individuals ordered to treatment but unable to access it remain in limbo -- neither punished nor treated -- creating a population of court-ordered but un-served substance users who continue using drugs while ostensibly in the system.

## 8.7 National Drug Agency -- Structure and Programs

### 8.7.1 Establishment and Mandate

The National Drug Agency was established under the **Drugs Act 17/2011** as the primary government body responsible for drug prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation in the Maldives. It replaced the earlier institutional arrangements under the Narcotics Control Board and the Department of Drug Prevention and Rehabilitation Services (DDPRS), which had operated within the Ministry of Health and Family.[^54]

The NDA Board includes representatives from multiple government agencies, providing a cross-sectoral governance structure. The 2021 Situational Analysis was jointly conducted by the NDA and WHO, with contributions from the NDA Board, Maldives Customs Services, Maldives Police Service, Drug Court, Juvenile Justice Unit, IGMH, Maldives Food and Drug Authority, Health Protection Agency, and NGOs Journey and Hands Together.[^55]

### 8.7.2 Divisions and Functions

Within the earlier DDPRS structure (which the NDA inherited and reorganised), there were two main operational divisions:[^56]

- **Division of Drug Prevention and Harm Reduction Services**: Responsible for Laboratory Services, the MMT, and Prevention and Awareness Services.
- **Division of Treatment and Rehabilitation Services**: Responsible for residential services (Drug Rehabilitation Centres) and Community Services.

### 8.7.3 Laboratory Services

The NDA operates laboratory facilities that conduct urine drug testing for the criminal justice system, treatment monitoring, and workplace testing. Between 2016 and 2020, more than 50,000 tests were carried out, with an average of 10,000 tests each year. The 2021 analysis noted that "new technological advancements are needed at NDA to test for new substances entering the market" -- a reflection of the shift toward synthetic drugs that existing testing protocols may not detect.[^57]

### 8.7.4 Prevention and Awareness

Prevention activities have historically been fragmented across multiple agencies. The Ministry of Education's School Health and Safety Section developed life skills curricula including drug awareness and education, supported by the Colombo Plan and UNICEF. However, the 2010 evaluation found that school counsellors lacked the skills and capacity to identify at-risk children, and there were no NGOs working directly with active young drug users.[^58]

The 2021 Situational Analysis recommended special attention for students in middle school (grades 6-9) and the transition period from O Level completion and beyond, with close monitoring of school dropouts and students not attending school -- populations disproportionately vulnerable to drug initiation.[^59]

### 8.7.5 NGO Partners

Several NGOs have played significant roles in drug treatment and prevention:

- **Journey**: The longest-established drug-focused NGO, operating drop-in centres, recovery support groups, and outreach services. Journey conducted its own rapid assessment in 2006.
- **Hand in Hand**: Provides services for drug users in Male.
- **Society of Women Against Drugs (SWAD)**: Operates a Drop-in Centre and co-established a "Youth Health Cafe" with UNFPA support, providing HIV prevention information, STI screening, and reproductive health services in a social setting acceptable to youth.
- **Society for Health Education (SHE)**: Active in Male.
- **Open Hand**: Operates in Fuvamulah.[^60]

A critical gap noted by the 2010 evaluation was that all NGO drug programmes were oriented toward recovery -- none were focused on engaging active drug users, creating a service gap for the very population most in need of harm reduction interventions.[^61]

## 8.8 Drug-Related Crime and Gang Violence

### 8.8.1 Drug Offences and the Criminal Justice System

Drug-related offences have become the dominant category of crime in the Maldivian justice system. By the early 2000s, drug cases constituted the most frequent type before the criminal courts, with a 200% increase noted in recent years.[^62] A 2004 prison review found that **47% of all prisoners were drug offenders** -- a staggering proportion that reflected both the high prevalence of drug use and the punitive orientation of the legal framework.[^63]

The Maldives imprisons its people at approximately **15 times the rate of India**, with most of those jailed being non-violent drug offenders whose incarceration raises the risks of radicalisation while doing nothing to diminish crime.[^64]

### 8.8.2 The Drug-Gang Nexus

The 2019 Youth Vulnerability study documented a deeply concerning overlap between drugs, gangs, and organised crime. Recruiters for criminal gangs target vulnerable groups in communities, particularly those in drug rehabilitation programmes or prison. An overlap has developed between criminal groups and extremists, with the prevalence of drugs, gangs, and unemployment stirring up disillusioned young men.[^65]

Drug users frequently turn to crime to fund their addiction. As the UNAFEI resource paper noted: "The need to use more and more drugs, coupled with the debilitating highs and lows, makes securing a paying job all the harder for users, at a time when they need more and more money to pay for their addiction. Many users turn to crime -- petty theft and robbery -- or turn to dealing."[^66]

The 2021 Situational Analysis highlighted several dimensions of the drug-crime nexus:

- **Human trafficking** among drug users, with users being forced into other illegal activities.
- **Sex trade** involvement among substance users, with documented percentages of both males and females engaging in transactional sex to fund drug use.
- **Involvement of migrant workers** in the drug trade, both as carriers and small-scale dealers.[^67]

### 8.8.3 The Dense Urban Environment

Male, the capital, with approximately 130,000-250,000 residents (depending on the counting method), is one of the most densely populated cities in the world. This extreme density makes it exceptionally difficult for former drug users to avoid re-encountering old drug haunts and associates. As the UNAFEI resource paper noted: "There is not a single street in Male where you will not see a person dependent upon drugs."[^68] This urban geography fundamentally undermines relapse prevention efforts and makes community-based recovery extremely challenging.

## 8.9 Harm Reduction Approaches and Debates

### 8.9.1 The Methadone Maintenance Debate

The introduction of Methadone Maintenance Therapy represented the most significant harm reduction initiative in the Maldives. The pilot programme in Male demonstrated positive results, and the 2010 World Bank evaluation recommended expansion to other sites, possibly determined by mapping and size estimation surveys of heroin use.[^69]

However, MMT faced significant resistance and misunderstanding:

- **Better explanation needed**: The evaluation urged that all stakeholders -- parliamentarians, government, treatment services, drug users, and parents -- needed to understand that MMT is a long-term intervention with both individual and community benefits.
- **Enrolment barriers**: The requirement for clearance from five government departments before voluntary enrolment deterred participation, though this was subsequently relaxed.
- **Counselling alignment**: Psychosocial counselling protocols needed to be developed that were consonant with the aims of MMT, rather than working at cross-purposes with harm reduction principles.[^70]
- **Influential champions needed**: The evaluation recommended identifying parliamentarians, religious leaders, prison authorities, and civil society leaders who could advocate for MMT as a public health priority.

### 8.9.2 HIV Prevention and Injecting Drug Use

Although the HIV epidemic in the Maldives remained in its early stages, the potential for spread through injecting drug use was identified as a critical concern. The 2010 evaluation made several harm reduction recommendations:

- People who continue to inject should receive explicit advice on avoiding blood-borne viruses, including on sterile injecting equipment and procedures.
- **Relaxation of pharmacy restrictions**: Any prohibition on pharmacists selling sterile equipment to people who inject should be relaxed, with consideration given to a social marketing programme using private sector pharmacies.
- **Voluntary confidential testing and counselling (VCT)** for HIV and HCV should be expanded, with careful attention to location and operation of VCT sites to ensure trust and confidentiality.
- **Contingency plans** should be prepared for the rapid introduction of needle and syringe programmes if the number of injectors or cases of HIV/HCV increased.[^71]

### 8.9.3 The Abstinence vs. Harm Reduction Tension

A fundamental tension pervades Maldivian drug policy between abstinence-based approaches (rooted in both the TC model and Islamic principles) and harm reduction approaches (rooted in public health pragmatism). The 2010 evaluation noted that urine testing was used as a punitive/control mechanism rather than a clinical tool -- positive tests resulted in removal from rehabilitation and return to prison, rather than triggering additional support. The evaluation urged that counsellors should have more flexibility to make case-by-case decisions, and that relapse during DRC and Community Service should be treated as an expected part of recovery rather than a reason for expulsion.[^72]

### 8.9.4 Alcohol: The Absent Dimension

The 2010 evaluation noted the complete absence of an alcohol awareness, harm reduction, treatment, and recovery programme in the Maldives. Despite alcohol prohibition under Islamic law, alcohol use was documented among drug users (12% reported alcohol as an initiation substance in the 2003 RSA). The absence of any programmatic response to alcohol use represents a significant gap in the substance abuse service continuum.[^73]

## 8.10 Drug Use and Youth Vulnerability

### 8.10.1 The Youth Vulnerability Study (2019)

The **Youth Vulnerability in the Maldives** study, published in February 2019 by the National Counter Terrorism Centre and UNDP, provided the most comprehensive examination of the intersection between drugs and youth marginalisation. Based on nationwide consultations with young Maldivians aged 18-34, the study found:[^74]

- Young people identified **drugs as a serious problem** in their communities, alongside gangs, crime, and corruption.
- **Drug treatment reaches too few people**, and the support given to them after they leave programmes does too little to help them stay off drugs.
- **Drugs and associated crimes**, imprisonment, and corruption were **eroding faith in government** and undermining communities.
- **Prisons and drug use** were identified as routes to extremism -- working to reduce the number jailed and cutting demand for drugs were considered the most worthwhile immediate steps.

### 8.10.2 Pathways to Vulnerability

The study identified several structural factors driving youth drug vulnerability:

**Education-employment mismatch**: Education is expensive, limited, and rarely matches the job market. Youth unemployment stands at 26%, and nearly two-thirds of young people felt they could only get a job through political connections.[^75]

**Social isolation**: 69% of young people felt social isolation was a serious problem. Only 39% agreed that community spirit was strong on their island. The dense urban environment of Male and the remoteness of outer atolls both produced isolation, though in different forms.[^76]

**Lack of recreational alternatives**: A major barrier identified was the absence of recreational and social mechanisms accessible to youth. Sports programmes, once the typical response to youth anomie, were evidently no longer sufficient.

**Political disillusionment**: Many young people were deeply disillusioned by politics, which they saw as dividing communities and families. The perception that advancement required political connections rather than merit was corrosive.

### 8.10.3 The School-to-Drug Pipeline

Multiple studies have documented the young age of drug initiation in the Maldives. The 2003 RSA found that half of all drug users started before age 17. The 2009 Global School-based Student Health Survey confirmed relatively high levels of drug use among school children aged 13-15 across the country.[^77] The 2021 Situational Analysis recommended targeting prevention at middle school students (grades 6-9) and the O Level transition period, with monitoring of school dropouts.[^78]

The education system's capacity to respond is limited. School counsellors lack skills and have heavy workloads. Teachers cannot identify at-risk children. There are no NGOs working directly with active young drug users. Children cannot access services provided by adult-oriented NGOs like Journey and SWAD.[^79]

### 8.10.4 The Island Factor

Children from outer atolls who come to Male at age 13 for further education, staying with relatives in overcrowded conditions, are particularly vulnerable. Away from parental supervision, in a dense urban environment saturated with drug availability, these young people face heightened exposure risk.[^80] Conversely, drug use on outer islands is complicated by the even more limited availability of treatment -- most services are concentrated in Male and Addu, leaving the majority of atolls without any local treatment option.

## 8.11 COVID-19 Impact on Substance Abuse Services

### 8.11.1 Disruption of Services

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly affected substance abuse services in the Maldives, though comprehensive documentation of the impact remains limited. The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis, conducted during the pandemic period, noted several key disruptions:

- The phenomenon of **online drug sales was quite possibly accelerated** by COVID-19, as contactless drug transactions and dark web markets expanded during lockdowns.[^81]
- Access to drugs became easier through new channels even as physical mobility was restricted.
- **Treatment programme continuity was disrupted** as residential facilities faced infection control challenges and community-based services were interrupted by lockdowns and movement restrictions.

### 8.11.2 Pandemic as Compounding Factor

The pandemic compounded pre-existing vulnerabilities for substance users:

- **Economic disruption**: The collapse of the tourism economy -- the Maldives' primary source of income -- eliminated many of the already-scarce employment opportunities available to recovering drug users.
- **Social isolation intensification**: Lockdowns deepened the social isolation that was already identified as a major risk factor for drug use among youth.
- **Mental health deterioration**: The pandemic worsened mental health conditions that frequently co-occur with substance use disorders.
- **Service access barriers**: Travel restrictions between islands made it even more difficult for users on outer atolls to access treatment services in Male.

### 8.11.3 Lessons for Service Resilience

The pandemic exposed the fragility of a treatment system concentrated in a few facilities in a few locations. The 2021 Situational Analysis's recommendation for systemic reforms -- including integrated data management, stakeholder collaboration, and decentralised service delivery -- were amplified by the pandemic experience.[^82]

## 8.12 Prison Radicalisation and Drug Offenders

### 8.12.1 The Prison Radicalisation Study (2022)

The **Prison Radicalisation in the Maldives** study, published in 2022 by Transparency Maldives and the Ministry of Home Affairs with USAID funding, was conducted by the International Center for the Study of Violent Extremism (ICSVE) led by Dr. Anne Speckhard. The five-month study (July-November 2021) included interviews with 20 prisoners at Maafushi high-security prison (17 men, 3 women), one former Maafushi prisoner in drug rehabilitation, and extensive stakeholder meetings.[^83]

### 8.12.2 The Drug-Prison-Radicalisation Nexus

The study documented a clear pathway from drug use through imprisonment to radicalisation:

**Demographics of the prison sample**: Mean age at first arrest was 21.56 years. Mean education was 8.2 years. Socioeconomic status was 64.7% poor/working class, 29.4% middle class, 5.9% upper class. Marital status was 41.2% single, 11.8% married, 47.1% divorced. Legal status was 64.7% serving limited sentences, 35.3% serving life sentences.[^84]

**Drug use as entry point**: Maafushi prison inmate interviews probed life and family history, experiences of drug abuse, drug peddling, and other criminality -- and explored how these experiences led to extremist contacts in prison and pathways out of drug addiction that could be replaced by adherence to extremist ideology and group membership.[^85]

**Mechanisms of radicalisation**: The study identified several prison-specific factors:

- **Overcrowding and understaffing**: Create chaotic, dangerous conditions where extremist groups can offer protection, purpose, and resources.
- **Lack of adequate rehabilitation programmes**: In the absence of meaningful programmes, extremist groups fill the vacuum with their own structure, identity, and sense of purpose.
- **Lack of respected imams**: Without credible religious leaders who can counter extremist scriptural interpretations, radicalised inmates operate unchallenged.
- **Grievances with the justice system**: Perceived unfairness of sentencing fuels resentment that extremist recruiters exploit.[^86]

### 8.12.3 From Drug Addict to Extremist

The study documented how drug offenders -- typically young men with limited education, broken families, and histories of social marginalisation -- found in prison an environment where extremist groups provided what society had failed to offer: belonging, purpose, and a coherent narrative explaining their suffering. The study noted that "a significant number [of those who joined extremist groups] were drug users, former prisoners, or people from troubled families."[^87]

The Youth Vulnerability study had earlier warned: "Recruiters, some of them brought in from abroad to preach on the islands, target vulnerable groups in communities, as gangs often do. Those in drug rehabilitation programmes or prison are frequent targets, while an overlap has developed between criminal groups and extremists."[^88]

### 8.12.4 Recommendations

The Prison Radicalisation study recommended:

- **Segregation** of violent and non-violent prisoners to prevent prisons from becoming places of radicalisation.
- **Reduction of prison populations** through parole and non-custodial sentences for drug users and non-violent offenders, including house arrest, island arrest, or electronic monitoring.
- **Prison-based rehabilitation programmes** specifically designed to counter radicalisation.
- **Adequate staffing and training** for prison officers.
- **Credible religious programming** with qualified imams who can counter extremist interpretations of Islam.[^89]

## 8.13 Research Evidence and Findings

### 8.13.1 The Evidence Base

Research on drug use in the Maldives, while growing, remains fragmented and episodic. The major studies include:

| Study | Year | Conducted By | Key Contribution |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Rapid Situation Assessment | 2003 | UNODC/NCB | First empirical assessment; established baseline data |
| Journey Rapid Assessment | 2006 | NGO Journey | Community-level data; 28% injection rate |
| Ageel thesis | 2006 | Academic | Policy-practice tensions; staff inadequacies |
| Drug Control Master Plan | 2008 | UNODC/GoM | First strategic framework |
| BBS | 2008 | UNDP | Injecting drug use; HIV/HCV risk data |
| GSHS | 2009 | WHO/MoE | School-based student drug use |
| World Bank Evaluation | 2010 | Mortimore & Stimson | Treatment system evaluation; 50+ recommendations |
| National Drug Use Survey | 2011-12 | UNODC/GoM | Population-level prevalence; 7,000+ users |
| GYTS | 2019 | WHO/GoM | 45.7% tobacco use among 13-15 year-olds |
| Youth Vulnerability Study | 2019 | NCTC/UNDP | Drug-gang-extremism nexus |
| NDA/WHO Situational Analysis | 2021 | NDA/WHO | Updated trends; 2016-2020 secondary data |
| Prison Radicalisation Study | 2022 | ICSVE/TM | Drug-prison-radicalisation pathway |

### 8.13.2 Key Research Findings Across Studies

**Escalating sophistication of drug markets**: From simple hashish smuggling in the 1970s to diversified synthetic drug markets using mail, dark web, and migrant carrier networks in the 2020s.

**Shifting user demographics**: Users are ageing (mean arrest age rising from 27.7 to 31.3 years between 2015 and 2020), suggesting an entrenched user population, while new initiation continues among youth.

**Treatment system inadequacy**: Despite legislative reform and institutional expansion, the treatment system remains characterised by insufficient capacity, chronic staffing shortages, long waiting times, high relapse rates, and the near-complete absence of aftercare and reintegration services.

**Criminalisation counterproductive**: The incarceration of drug users at massive scale has not reduced drug use but has created a pipeline into organised crime and violent extremism. The 2010 evaluation found that "too many drug users are in prison to no obvious gain to the community."[^90]

**Data fragmentation**: Stakeholders continue to work in silos, with no comprehensive integrated data management system. Each institution collects data in its own format for its own purposes, making it impossible to view the complete picture of drug use in the country.[^91]

### 8.13.3 Critical Gaps in Knowledge

Despite the accumulation of research over two decades, several critical gaps persist:

- **No updated population-based prevalence survey** since 2011-12, making it impossible to determine current prevalence with confidence.
- **No longitudinal outcome data** on treatment effectiveness -- it is unknown what proportion of DRC graduates achieve sustained recovery.
- **No cost-effectiveness analysis** of the mandatory versus voluntary treatment systems.
- **No systematic data on female drug use**, leaving half the population essentially invisible in drug policy.
- **No assessment of the 2011 Drug Act** and its effects on patterns of drug use, treatment uptake, and criminal justice outcomes.
- **No data on co-occurring mental health conditions** among substance users, despite the clear intersection identified in multiple studies.

### 8.13.4 The Implementation Gap

Perhaps the most consistent finding across all research is what might be termed the "implementation gap" -- the distance between what Maldivian law and policy promise and what is actually delivered on the ground. The Maldives has a Drug Act, a Drug Court, a National Drug Agency, treatment standards, and community service programmes. On paper, the system is comprehensive. In practice:

- Treatment centres operate below capacity while waiting lists grow, due to administrative bottlenecks and eligibility restrictions.
- Counsellors are under-qualified and unsupported.
- Aftercare is non-existent.
- Employment reintegration is blocked by stigma and criminal records.
- Outer atolls have no treatment access.
- The criminal justice system and the treatment system work at cross-purposes.
- Data is not shared across agencies.

As the 2019 Youth Vulnerability study concluded: "The Maldives has laws and institutions for better social protection, but they do not function adequately: implementing institutions should be re-invigorated and, where necessary, laws reformed to make them effective."[^92]

### 8.13.5 Toward an Evidence-Based Future

The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis outlined the path forward:

- **Comprehensive integrated data management** with real-time data from all relevant institutions.
- **New technologies** at NDA to test for emerging synthetic substances.
- **Targeted interventions** for vulnerable populations -- juveniles, elderly, migrants.
- **Attitudinal transformation** in families and communities toward substance use and recovery.
- **Minimisation of waiting times** between drug court orders, assessments, and treatment entry.
- **Stakeholder collaboration** between government and non-governmental organisations.[^93]

Whether the political will exists to implement these reforms remains the fundamental question. As the Youth Vulnerability study noted, "What is clear is that the Maldives does not lack awareness of these problems or the ability to solve them. There has instead been a lack of political will to implement reforms."[^94]
