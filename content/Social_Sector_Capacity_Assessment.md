# Capacity Assessments

---

## 1. Introduction

This assessment compiles the capacity gaps, resource constraints, and institutional weaknesses identified across the Maldivian social sector. It draws on the full body of evidence assembled in the Policy Encyclopedia – eighteen thematic chapters, seven appendices, three treaty body report drafts, and the Action Plans Analysis – as well as the Ministry of Social and Family Development's own internal capacity assessments and risk registers (2025), cross-referenced against sources including the UNICEF Maldives Social Sector Assessment (2022) and the Auditor General's Performance Audit on Government Initiatives to Alleviate Poverty (2025).

This is not a set of recommendations. Where the evidence identifies a specific gap – a missing service, an absent dataset, an insufficient workforce – that gap is documented here. The assessment covers the social sector broadly: child protection, gender equality and GBV response, disability inclusion, elderly care, mental health, substance abuse treatment, education, health systems, housing and poverty, social protection, and climate vulnerability as they intersect with social service delivery.

Evidence sources include government self-assessments (the MIPAA Voluntary National Survey 2021, the UNPRPD Situational Analysis 2024, ministerial capacity assessments), international assessments (UNICEF, WHO, World Bank, ADB), independent research (Rogers, Ali & Naeem 2025; Fulu 2007; ICSVE 2019), administrative data, treaty body reviews (CRC, CRPD, CEDAW), and the Auditor General's Office. Where multiple independent sources identify the same gap, this convergence is noted.

These are areas where targeted investment – in staffing, training, systems, coordination, data infrastructure, or fiscal resources – would strengthen the sector's capacity to deliver on its existing mandates.

### 2.2 Cross-Cutting Findings (Overall Capacity Assessment)

The ministry's Overall Capacity Assessment identified seven themes that recur across all five departments:

## 3. Workforce and Staffing

### 3.1 Social Work Workforce

The social work workforce is the most frequently cited capacity constraint across the Maldivian social sector. CFPS – the frontline agency for child protection, domestic violence response, and psychosocial support – carries average caseloads exceeding 160 cases per worker (UNICEF, 2022).[^1] At that volume, assessments are shallow, risk factors get missed, and the emotional intensity of the work combined with modest pay drives burnout and turnover that further reduces the available workforce.[^2] One ministry KI told the UNICEF assessment: "A lot of the cases get lost because of the caseload. We always give priority to the most urgent cases, so the other cases get lost."

The Action Plans Analysis calculated that delivering simultaneously on all active plans – NAPVAC, GEAP, NAPDI, the Ageing Policy, and the CFW recommendations – would require at minimum a tripling of the social work workforce, dedicated budget lines for each mandate area, and integrated data systems.[^3]

The geographic distribution of social workers compounds the numerical shortage. Social workers are concentrated in Male' and a handful of larger islands. The 19 Family and Children's Service Centres (FCSCs) cover only 20 of the country's 199 inhabited islands.[^4] Outer island FCSCs may have one or two social workers responsible for populations spread across multiple islands in an atoll, requiring sea travel for home visits and making emergency response impractical.[^5] Islands without FCSCs – the vast majority – depend on island council staff with no social work training.

The same pressure appears across independent assessments. The ICSVE prison radicalisation study (2019) found that stakeholders from the Ministry and social services agencies "expressed concerns regarding understaffing, sharing that their case and social workers are overburdened and unable to devote a proper amount of time to each of the children and families whom they oversee."[^6] The UNICEF Social Sector Assessment (2022) found that several FCSCs were not operational because the ministry had been unable to hire social workers or counsellors despite advertising vacancies.

The ministry's own internal capacity assessments (2025) report the same picture from inside: the Case Management department cites high caseloads and long working hours as drivers of burnout and attrition; the Prevention department estimates at least six additional staff are required for the vulnerability mapping alone; the Gender department reports insufficient staff for its project portfolio. Every assessed department reports being understaffed for its mandated workload.[^7]

### 3.2 Mental Health Professionals

The Maldives has very few locally trained psychiatrists and continues to rely heavily on expatriate professionals on short-term contracts (typically one to three years). This creates constant turnover that undermines continuity of care and institutional memory.[^8] As of 2025, the country has approximately four to five psychiatrists serving a population of over 500,000 – a ratio far below WHO minimum recommendations.

The reliance on expatriate psychiatrists compounds the shortage: they may not speak Dhivehi, requiring consultations in English or through interpretation, which can compromise diagnostic accuracy in a field where verbal communication is central to assessment. Short-term contracts provide limited opportunity to train local professionals or build institutional knowledge.[^9]

Counselling is unregulated. There is no accreditation system, so individuals providing "counselling" range from holders of postgraduate clinical psychology degrees to persons with short-course certificates, with no mechanism to distinguish between them or enforce standards.[^10]

The National Centre for Mental Health at IGMH is the only specialist mental health facility. Regional hospitals have begun establishing mental health units – the Raa Atoll Regional Hospital's Sidhdha wing being a notable example – but specialist psychiatric services outside Male' remain thin. A person in psychiatric crisis on a remote island may be days of travel from the nearest psychiatrist.[^11] Child and adolescent mental health services are almost entirely absent outside Male'.[^12]

### 3.3 Specialist Professionals

Several categories of specialist professional are either absent from the country or available only in very small numbers:

**Geriatric care.** The Maldives has zero geriatricians. No geriatric training programme exists in the country. The Ageing Policy identifies the need to incorporate geriatric and gerontology training into health professional curricula, but this has not been implemented. The Elderly department's own capacity assessment identifies "limited staff capacity within section on some high-level technical skills" as a high-priority gap.[^13]

**Sign language interpreters.** The number of qualified interpreters is insufficient to meet need. No national sign language curriculum or formal certification pathway has been established. Maldivian Sign Language has not been accorded legal recognition. Sign language interpretation is not systematically provided in public settings, including government press conferences, parliamentary proceedings, or emergency broadcasts.[^14]

**Forensic specialists.** No specialist forensic examination facilities exist outside Male'. A child victim of sexual abuse in Addu City – the largest atoll outside Male' – has access to an FCSC and police, but no specialist forensic examination facilities, no dedicated child-friendly interview rooms (in most cases), and no specialist therapeutic services.[^15]

**Special education teachers.** While at least one SEN teacher per school is the target, coverage remains insufficient for need. The inclusive education model requires teachers trained in differentiated instruction, assistive technology, and individualised education planning – skills that are not systematically developed in the teacher training pipeline.[^16]

**Disability assessors.** No systematic disability assessment capacity exists in outer atolls. The National Disability Registry depends on individuals travelling to Male' or assessment visits to atolls, which are infrequent.[^17]

**Palliative care.** The Maldives is classified under Group 1 in the WHO/WHPCA Global Atlas – "no known hospice-palliative care activity." No trained palliative care professionals, no palliative care policy, and no end-of-life care framework exists.[^18]

### 3.4 Professional Licensing and Standards

Social work has no mandatory licensing. Without a professional body or registration system, anyone with the requisite academic qualification can practise regardless of supervised placement experience or demonstrated competency with complex cases. There is no mechanism for ongoing professional development requirements, ethical enforcement, or accountability for malpractice.[^19]

The training pipeline feeds into this gap. MNU and a limited number of private institutions offer social work programmes, but graduate output falls short of demand. Senior positions require MNQF Level 9 or 10 qualifications that cannot always be filled domestically, and international recruitment faces barriers of language, cultural context, and willingness to work on remote islands.[^20]

## 4. Service Delivery and Geographic Coverage

### 4.1 The Male'-Atoll Service Divide

Social service delivery in the Maldives concentrates in Male'. Psychiatric care, forensic assessment, specialist disability services, legal aid, GBV shelters, children's residential care, and substance abuse rehabilitation are available only in Male' or in a small number of regional centres. For most of the population – spread across 187 inhabited islands in 26 atolls – reaching these services means travel by sea or domestic flight, often at considerable cost and time. The UNICEF assessment found that inter-island travel to access services "remains largely unaffordable for large segments of the Maldivian population."

The FCSC network provides the most distributed social service infrastructure, with 19 centres across the atolls, but that covers approximately 20 of 199 inhabited islands – about 10 per cent.[^21] FCSCs lack their own transport, so social workers depend on police vessels or public ferries to reach cases on other islands, with response times measured in days rather than hours.

The four-tier service architecture documented in the Child Protection chapter shows the gradient:

- **Tier 1 (Male'/Hulhumale'):** Full service concentration – CFPS headquarters, IGMH specialist services, legal aid, shelter, police FCPW, courts, all NGOs.
- **Tier 2 (regional hospital islands):** FCSC, regional hospital, police, magistrate court – but typically no specialist child protection, forensic, or therapeutic services.
- **Tier 3 (atoll capitals without regional hospitals):** FCSC and general services but no specialist facilities.
- **Tier 4 (all other inhabited islands):** No dedicated child protection service. The island council, WDC, IBAMA volunteers, and school teachers constitute the entirety of child protection infrastructure.[^22]

The same tiered reality applies across every social service domain, not only child protection.

### 4.2 Shelter and Safe House Capacity

GBV shelter capacity is limited to three ADB-funded shelters (Addu/Hithadhoo, Hulhumale', Raa Atoll) and FPA's transitional housing in Male'. These cover a fraction of need and provide time-limited accommodation with no pathway to independent housing.[^23] For a DV survivor, leaving an abusive partner means leaving the shared home, and without a shelter placement there may be nowhere else to go. FPA itself operates with three caseworkers in Male' for a city of over 250,000.[^24]

### 4.3 Residential Care and Alternative Care

Children's residential care is provided through the Fiyavathi system, restructured post-COVID into nine smaller units. Foster care remains underdeveloped as an alternative to institutional placement. Voluntary placements may become indefinite in the absence of case management capacity.[^25] No organisations advocate for care-experienced people – identified as one of the most significant gaps in the alternative care system.[^26]

The Addu elderly care facility has been announced under two successive administrations (Solih and Muizzu) without being completed, spanning at least five years of planning.[^27] A single facility – the Home for People with Special Needs in Guraidhoo – houses both elderly persons and persons with severe disabilities, serving a mixed population with different care needs.[^28]

No regulatory framework exists for private-sector elderly care facilities. No licensing standards, staffing ratios, or care quality benchmarks have been established for elderly care facilities, public or private.[^29]

### 4.4 Substance Abuse Treatment

The treatment system is characterised by insufficient capacity, chronic staffing shortages, long waiting times, high relapse rates, and near-complete absence of aftercare and reintegration services.[^30] There is no access to detoxification services for females.[^31] No alcohol treatment programme exists despite documented alcohol use among the substance-using population.[^32] Employment for recovering drug users – identified as the largest obstacle to reintegration – lacks any systematic programme.[^33]

Drug treatment facilities are predominantly concentrated in the Male' region. No development partner has a dedicated project for substance abuse treatment infrastructure, residential rehabilitation, or harm reduction services.[^34]

### 4.5 Home-Based and Community-Based Care

No formal home care programme exists for the elderly.[^35] The bedridden home visit programme – serving 67 patients in Greater Male' at the time of the 2022 study – is the only approximation. No food security strategy targets older persons; no meal delivery or nutritional support services exist for elderly persons with limited mobility on outer islands.[^36]

Community-Based Rehabilitation (CBR) for persons with disabilities is identified as a key priority, but dedicated staff to sustain and monitor the programme across 193 islands and 3 cities have not been approved due to hiring restrictions.[^37]

ADDA respite care services – enabling family caregivers temporary breaks – are available only in Male' and not replicated in outer atolls. No personal assistance scheme exists. Persons with disabilities in outer islands who require daily living assistance are entirely dependent on family caregivers, with no state-funded alternative.[^38]

### 4.6 Accessibility of Services

FPA services and shelter facilities are not fully accessible to women with sensory or physical impairments. Sign language interpretation is not systematically available at FPA intake points.[^39] Government websites have not been assessed against WCAG 2.1 accessibility standards. Public information on rights, services, legal procedures, and emergency alerts is not routinely available in Braille, Easy Read, audio, or Maldivian Sign Language.[^40]

Housing policy does not include accessible housing standards or an accessible housing allocation priority for persons with disabilities. Home modification grants are not systematically available.[^41]

## 5. Data, Research, and Evidence Systems

### 5.1 Missing Surveys and Outdated Baselines

Across multiple social policy domains, the evidence base relies on data that is either absent or significantly outdated:


| Domain                     | Most Recent Survey/Study         | Gap                                                                  |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Gender/GBV prevalence      | DHS 2016-17                      | 9 years old; no dedicated GBV prevalence study since                 |
| Drug use prevalence        | National Drug Use Survey 2011-12 | 14 years old; decade without updated population data                 |
| Adolescent mental health   | Global School Health Survey 2009 | 17 years old                                                         |
| Mental health (population) | Never conducted                  | No dedicated national mental health survey has ever been done        |
| Disability (functional)    | Census 2022 (WG-SS)              | First application of Washington Group methodology; no prior baseline |
| Elderly needs              | No dedicated survey              | No systematic needs assessment of elderly population                 |
| Anti-trafficking           | No prevalence study              | Data relies on case detection, not population estimates              |
| Child labour               | No dedicated survey              | No systematic child labour prevalence data                           |


The country has never conducted a dedicated national mental health survey. There is no mental health information system that systematically collects diagnostic data across health facilities.[^42] No systematic data exists on female drug use, leaving half the population invisible in drug policy.[^43] No longitudinal outcome data tracks treatment effectiveness for substance abuse – it is unknown what proportion of DRC graduates achieve sustained recovery.[^44]

### 5.2 Absent Registries and Databases

**Cancer registry.** Despite cancers being among the leading causes of death, no functional national cancer registry exists.[^45]

**Elderly registry.** No national registry of elderly persons exists, no standardised assessment of care needs, and no system for tracking health outcomes, social isolation, or abuse experienced by older persons.[^46]

**Centralised elderly database.** The Elderly department itself identifies the "lack of a centralized database on the elderly population" as a gap.[^47]

**Comprehensive disability registry.** The National Disability Registry covers only 25.6 per cent of eligible persons with disabilities. Coverage is lowest among women, older adults, those in Male', and those with sensory impairments. Psychosocial disability is substantially undercounted.[^48]

**Integrated case management.** CFPS operates the MCPD for child protection, but it does not integrate with police crime databases, NSPA's SPIS, or FPA's GBV database, limiting cross-sectoral case management.[^49]

### 5.3 Data Sharing and Siloed Systems

Drug policy monitoring suffers from the most fragmented data environment of any social policy domain. Each institution – NDA, police, customs, courts, corrections, health – collects data in its own format for its own purposes, making it impossible to view a complete picture.[^50]

There is no national integrated case management system for child protection – CFPS, police, prosecutors, and health systems each maintain separate records with limited data sharing.[^51] A child known to the police for exposure to domestic violence may not be known to CFPS; a child identified by a school as showing signs of neglect may not be referred to health services.

Gender-disaggregated data is not routinely collected across government systems. The Gender department identifies the "unavailability of regular and reliable gender-disaggregated data" as a major challenge.[^52]

### 5.4 Monitoring and Evaluation

The Ageing Policy has no dedicated monitoring framework. MPAO tracks pension coverage with precision, but this covers only one dimension of elderly wellbeing.[^53] The Gender department reports the absence of a monitoring and evaluation framework to track GEAP progress.[^54] No systematic monitoring assesses the impact of disability programmes.[^55]

No health technology assessment framework guides decisions on the introduction of new technologies, medicines, or interventions.[^56]

### 5.5 Data Protection

As government agencies digitise sensitive personal information – health records, welfare status, protection orders, disability assessments – the absence of comprehensive data protection legislation is a significant vulnerability. The consolidation of personal data across multiple government systems without adequate privacy safeguards raises concerns about misuse, unauthorised access, and discrimination.[^57]

## 6. Inter-Agency Coordination

### 6.1 The Structural Coordination Gap

MoSFD sets social policy but has no directive authority over the agencies it depends on to deliver it – police, prosecution, courts, health services, education. When a child protection case requires police response, a GBV referral requires health assessment, or a disability inclusion mandate requires action from the Ministry of Education, MoSFD can request but not compel. Coordination runs on relationships, MoUs, and goodwill.[^58]

The ministry's own capacity assessments identify this from every department. The Elderly department highlights "lack of clarity on the roles and responsibilities of different agencies" in providing care. DRW faces resistance from other ministries on Disability Act implementation. The Prevention department reports delays in receiving information from partner agencies needed for timely child protection interventions.[^59] Departments depend on counterparts in Health, Education, and Police who often do not prioritise social sector requests.[^60]

The ministry's Risk Register (2025) rates multi-sectoral project failure from uncoordinated inter-agency work as Critical (High likelihood, High impact).[^61]

### 6.2 Information Sharing Between Agencies

Without formal case conferencing, information about children at risk sits in separate agency systems that do not talk to each other. Police, CFPS, prosecutors, and health services each maintain their own records. Schools and CFPS have no established referral system, case conference mechanism, or review meetings despite the Education Act 2020 requiring child protection provisions.[^62] Police checks for school staff have not been implemented.[^63]

The information gap extends to enforcement: protection orders issued under the DVPA are not consistently shared with Maldives Correctional Service, so released offenders may return to households with active protection orders that Corrections is unaware of.[^64]

### 6.3 Donor Coordination

Managing donor coordination is itself a capacity demand. The Ministry of Finance coordinates development partners while MoSFD handles social sector engagement, but the UN system, World Bank, and ADB each operate under separate country frameworks with separate reporting requirements and results matrices. The World Bank's SAILS project designs unemployment insurance while UNICEF advocates for child-sensitive social protection and UNFPA generates population data that could inform targeting for both – but no coordination mechanism ensures these parallel efforts reinforce each other.[^65]

## 8. Training and Technical Expertise

### 8.1 Cross-Cutting Training Gaps

Specialised training is the second most cited gap (after staffing) in the ministry's Cross-Cutting Capacity Assessment, affecting all five departments.[^70]

**Policy analysis and development.** The Gender department identifies a "lack of technical expertise in policy analysis, development, and advocacy." The DRW department notes a gap in "technical capacity to review and draft legislation" and skills for conducting accessibility audits. The Elderly department lacks staff with experience in developing policy documents, public-facing materials, and data analysis.[^71]

**Trauma-informed care.** The Prevention and Case Management departments require training on trauma-informed care, handling complex cases, and working with vulnerable populations.[^72]

**Geriatric care and social gerontology.** The Elderly department requires training for staff in geriatric care – a specialism that does not currently exist in any Maldivian training programme.[^73]

**Gender-responsive budgeting.** The Gender department identifies a need for training in gender-responsive budgeting and gender-disaggregated data analysis.[^74]

**Sign language proficiency.** The DRW department identifies sign language training as a high-urgency need for its own staff.[^75]

### 8.2 Frontline Service Delivery Training

Case workers need TOT capacity and refresher sessions on parenting education delivery. Case supervision skills training is a high-priority gap. The Prevention department needs facilitator training for child rights awareness programmes.[^76]

A compounding problem: staff designated for policy and prevention work are consistently pulled into day-to-day case management because of staff shortages. The Prevention unit spends its time conducting awareness programmes rather than developing new prevention strategies. Gender department staff cannot focus on GEAP policy advocacy because of ongoing project administration.[^77] The ministry's Risk Register rates this "strategic stagnation and staff burnout" pattern as Critical.[^78]

## 9. Public Awareness and Help-Seeking

### 9.1 Awareness of Rights and Services

The MIPAA Voluntary National Survey (2021) rated "lack of public awareness" as the challenge that "always" affects elderly policy implementation – the highest severity rating given to any challenge in the survey. The government acknowledged that "limited budget and stakeholders to work" and "lack of data in the area creates gaps in designing interventions targeted at the issues."[^84]

The same gap recurs across domains: DRW identifies "low awareness among the general public and service providers about the rights of persons with disabilities"; Prevention reports a continuous need for child abuse prevention campaigns; Gender points to persistent gender stereotypes and low women's participation in leadership.[^85]

### 9.2 Stigma and Cultural Barriers

Mental health help-seeking runs into stigma rooted in cultural and religious interpretations that attribute mental illness to spiritual causes – *jinni* possession, *sihr* (sorcery). How Maldivians actually recognise, interpret, and respond to mental distress – the role of traditional healers, family decision-making, community attitudes – has never been systematically studied.[^86]

GBV reporting is suppressed by norms that treat domestic violence as a private family matter and stigmatise women who report. One in three women experienced DV (Fulu 2007), but the gap between prevalence and reporting rates is wide.[^87] A parallel pattern holds for substance abuse: 34.2 per cent of surveyed drug users had never sought treatment (DHS 2016-17), meaning more than a third of the drug-using population has had no contact with the treatment system at all.[^88]

### 9.3 The Small-Island Dynamic

On small islands, the same community closeness that provides informal support also discourages reporting. Everyone knows everyone, and the social cost of reporting abuse or family dysfunction – ostracism, family rupture, loss of standing – can outweigh the perceived benefit of formal intervention, especially when the nearest social worker is hours away by boat or the intervention is seen as ineffective.[^89]

## 10. Legal and Regulatory Gaps

### 10.1 Missing Legislation

Several pieces of legislation identified as necessary across multiple chapters and action plans have not been enacted:

| FGM/C criminalisation              | Not legislated despite CRC Committee recommendations | CRC Concluding Observations; Gender chapter; Action Plans Analysis |
| Social Work licensing/registration | No professional body or licensing framework          | Cross-Cutting Issues chapter                                       |
| Data protection legislation        | Absent                                               | Cross-Cutting Issues chapter                                       |
| Accessible housing standards       | Not established in law                               | CRPD chapter; Disability chapter                                   |


### 10.2 Enforcement and Implementation Gaps

Where legislation exists, enforcement gaps are widespread:

- The Gender Equality Act 2016 is enacted but the Gender department notes gaps in enforcement.[^90]
- The Disability Act 2010 is in force but multiple departments face challenges in getting other ministries to implement its provisions. Revision of the Act has been scheduled for 2026 in the legislative agenda.[^91]
- The Domestic Violence Prevention Act 2012 established protection orders, but enforcement depends on police compliance with order conditions and corrections sharing information – neither of which occurs consistently.[^92]
- The Anti-Trafficking Act is in force but the U.S. State Department consistently places the Maldives on the Tier 2 Watch List, citing inadequate enforcement, limited victim identification, and insufficient support services.[^93]

### 10.3 Regulatory Gaps

No regulatory framework exists for private elderly care facilities – no licensing, staffing ratios, or care quality benchmarks.[^94] No standardised accreditation system exists for counsellors.[^95] Residential care settings for persons with disabilities have not been subject to systematic independent monitoring against international standards.[^96]

## 12. Identified Risks

This section compiles the risks that affect the social sector's ability to deliver on its mandates. Some are structural and require long-term institutional reform; others are operational and can be mitigated through targeted investment or policy adjustment.

### 12.1 Fiscal and Sustainability Risks


| Risk                                                      | Detail                                                                                                                                                             |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Universal subsidy system is regressive                    | 43% of total subsidies benefit top 40% of households; ~2% of GDP spent on indirect subsidies to SOEs. Subsidy reform plan (April 2025) was rolled back.[^97]       |
| Social protection spending crowds out targeted programmes | Aasandha alone budgeted at MVR 1.9 billion (2024). Universal schemes consume fiscal space that could fund targeted interventions for the most vulnerable.[^98]     |
| NSPF stalled after development                            | National Social Protection Framework endorsed by Cabinet (October 2022), cost MVR 1.2 million to develop, but no implementation steps taken as of May 2025.[^99]   |
| Unemployment Insurance withdrawn                          | UI Bill withdrawn from Parliament; SAILS project restructured to remove UI components; USD 16 million reallocated.[^100]                                           |
| Electoral cycle disruption                                | Election cycles and campaign promises have driven major changes to social protection schemes, leading to sustainability challenges and policy discontinuity.[^101] |


### 12.2 Workforce Risks


| Risk                                            | Detail                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Burnout-driven attrition cycle                  | High caseloads, emotional intensity, personal safety risks (including death threats), limited supervision, and modest pay drive burnout and turnover, further depleting the workforce. Ministry Risk Register rates this Critical.[^102]                                       |
| Inability to recruit for remote islands         | Several FCSCs not operational because the ministry was unable to hire social workers or counsellors despite advertising vacancies (UNICEF 2022).[^103]                                                                                                                         |
| Strategic work displaced by operational demands | Staff designated for policy and prevention work are consistently pulled into day-to-day case management. The Prevention unit is absorbed by awareness programmes; the Gender department cannot focus on GEAP implementation. Ministry Risk Register rates this Critical.[^104] |
| Expatriate dependency in psychiatry             | The few local psychiatrists are supplemented by expatriate professionals on 1–3 year contracts. If contracts are not renewed or replacements delayed, psychiatric services face interruption.[^105]                                                                            |


### 12.3 Coordination and Governance Risks


| Risk                                    | Detail                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Multi-sectoral project failure          | No formal mechanism enforces inter-agency collaboration. Departments depend on goodwill of partner ministries who may not prioritise social sector requests. Ministry Risk Register rates this Critical.[^106] |
| Fragmented social protection governance | No single body oversees the full social protection system. Responsibilities fragmented across MoSFD, NSPA, MPAO, with no unified leadership. NSPF identifies this as a structural weakness.[^107]              |
| Data silos prevent integrated response  | CFPS, police, health, NSPA each maintain separate records. A child known to police for DV exposure may not be known to CFPS; a protection order may not reach Corrections.[^108]                               |
| Donor coordination gaps                 | UN system, World Bank, and ADB each operate under separate frameworks. Parallel efforts (SAILS, UNICEF social protection, UNFPA population data) are not coordinated through a single mechanism.[^109]         |


### 12.4 Service Delivery Risks


| Risk                                      | Detail                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Geographic inequity entrenched by default | Male'-centric service delivery is the default. Without explicit geographic equity targets, service expansion perpetuates existing concentration patterns.[^110]                                                 |
| Climate shocks on service continuity      | No assessment has examined how climate change may affect the government's ability to deliver social protection through damaged infrastructure, displaced staff, fiscal pressures, or increased caseloads.[^111] |
| Transport dependency for island response  | FCSCs lack their own transport; social workers depend on police vessels or public ferries. Response times to island cases measured in days, not hours. Weather compounds delays.[^112]                          |
| No poverty targeting infrastructure       | No social registry operational nationally. The 2019–2020 pilot was not rolled out. Without a registry, targeted programmes cannot identify who to reach.[^113]                                                  |


### 12.5 Data and Evidence Risks


| Risk                              | Detail                                                                                                                                                                    |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Policy based on outdated evidence | GBV data 9 years old; drug prevalence 14 years; adolescent mental health 17 years. Policy decisions rely on evidence that may no longer reflect current conditions.[^114] |
| No poverty research base          | No research has been conducted to identify the drivers and causes of poverty in the Maldives (Auditor General 2025).[^115]                                                |
| MoSFD programme data incomplete   | The Auditor General found that MoSFD datasets containing intervention details from programmes were incomplete.[^116]                                                      |


### 12.6 Legal and Political Risks


| Risk                                      | Detail                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Missing legislation stalls implementation | Mental Health Act, Elderly Act, FGM criminalisation, data protection law all pending. Without legal mandates, agencies lack authority to enforce standards or compel compliance.[^117] |
| Disability Act revision timing            | Scheduled for 2026 legislative agenda, but possible revisions to the agenda may delay it further.[^118]                                                                                |
| AGO and Parliament delays                 | All departments with bills-related projects identify likely delays in the Attorney General's Office review and Parliamentary process as a high-priority risk.[^119]                    |
| Zakat Bill not ratified                   | The Islamic charity system has potential to assist the poor but lacks a statutory framework to mandate contributions. The Zakat Bill has not been ratified.[^120]                      |
