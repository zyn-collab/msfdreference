# Part XII: Housing, Poverty, and Economic Vulnerability

## Introduction

The Republic of Maldives presents one of the most paradoxical development profiles in South Asia. With a GDP per capita of US$10,627 (2019), a Human Development Index score of 0.740, and a national poverty rate of just 5.4 percent, the country outperforms every regional peer on headline economic indicators.[^1] Yet beneath these aggregate figures lies a deeply fractured picture of economic opportunity, where geography dictates welfare, housing consumes the majority of household budgets in the capital, and a tourism-dependent economy leaves large segments of the population vulnerable to external shocks. The COVID-19 pandemic -- which contracted Maldives' real GDP by 33.6 percent in 2020, the sharpest decline in South Asia -- laid bare the structural fragilities that aggregate statistics conceal.[^2]

This Part examines the interconnected dimensions of poverty, housing, and economic vulnerability in the Maldives. It traces how the country's unique island geography, enclave economic model, and centralized development pattern have produced a distinctive set of challenges: a housing crisis in the Greater Male' Region driven by population concentration and land scarcity; persistent poverty in the atolls rooted in limited economic opportunity and infrastructure deficits; a labour market distorted by skills mismatches, gender barriers, and public-sector dependency; and a population whose proximity to the poverty line makes it acutely susceptible to economic disruption. The analysis draws on the 2019/20 Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES), the World Bank's 2022 Poverty Assessment, the Housing Development Corporation's 2017 Comprehensive Housing Market Study, the 2020 Rapid Livelihood Assessment, and the World Bank's 2021 Systematic Country Diagnostic Update, among other sources.

## 12.1 Poverty and Inequality -- Trends and Measurement

### The Poverty Measurement Framework

Official poverty statistics in the Maldives are derived from the Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES) series, administered by the Maldives Bureau of Statistics (MBS). Earlier rounds were conducted in 2002/03, 2009/10, and 2016, with the most recent -- HIES 2019/20 -- serving as the basis for current estimates. The Maldives has historically used a relative poverty line set at half the median of per capita expenditure, known as the Low Relative Poverty Line (LRPL). The national poverty line stands at 71.4 Maldivian rufiyaa (MVR) per person per day, equivalent to 2,172.6 MVR per month or 26,071.5 MVR per year. A High Relative Poverty Line (HRPL) of 142.8 MVR per day is also employed for sensitivity analysis. The internationally comparable upper-middle-income country (UMIC) poverty line, set at US$5.5 per person per day in purchasing power parity (PPP) terms, yields a national rate of 1.7 percent.[^3]

Due to methodological innovations introduced in HIES 2019 -- including tablet-based enumeration, revised food-item aggregation, and modified imputed rent questions -- welfare estimates from this round are not directly comparable with earlier surveys. The 2019 HIES was also truncated by COVID-19: fieldwork in three atolls (Noonu, Meemu, and Gnaviyani) was suspended entirely, and Male' coverage reached only 81 percent of planned enumeration blocks. The National Bureau of Statistics recalculated survey weights to adjust for these gaps, and validation checks confirmed national representativeness.[^4]

### National Poverty Rates and Geographic Distribution

At the national poverty line (71.4 MVR per person per day), the national poverty rate in 2019/20 was **5.4 percent**. This headline figure, however, masks a dramatic spatial divide:

- **Atolls**: 9.5 percent poverty rate
- **Male'**: 0.9 percent poverty rate

The poverty rate in atolls is thus approximately **ten times** that of Male'. The population split between Male' and the atolls is roughly 52-48 percent, but atolls contain **92 percent** of the country's poor population. Of the estimated 24,048 poor individuals nationwide, only 1,893 reside in Male', while 22,155 live in the atolls.[^5]

At the international UMIC poverty line (US$5.5 PPP per day), national poverty stands at 1.7 percent, driven entirely by poverty in atolls at 3.2 percent; there are no poor individuals in Male' by this benchmark. Under the High Relative Poverty Line, the national rate rises sharply to 43.9 percent, with 67.3 percent in atolls and 18.1 percent in Male' -- illustrating how a substantial share of the population lives well below the median standard of living even if technically above the absolute poverty threshold.[^6]

### Inequality: The Gini Coefficient and Expenditure Distribution

Despite relatively low poverty, Maldives exhibits significant inequality. The national Gini coefficient stands at **29.3**, which compares favorably with South Asian peers but conceals the nature of inequality between Male' and the atolls. When populations are measured separately, Male' and atolls show similar internal inequality (Gini of 25.2 and 24.2 respectively). The national figure rises to 29.3 only when the two populations are pooled -- signalling that **inequality between Male' and atolls is greater than inequality within either region**. Per capita median expenditure in the top quintile is four times that of the bottom quintile nationally.[^7]

The distribution of per capita expenditure in atolls is tightly clustered around the national poverty line, making atoll populations highly vulnerable to economic shocks. World Bank simulations demonstrate that an economic shock reducing all Maldivian households' annual budgets by just two months' worth of spending (16.67 percent) would **double** the national poverty rate to 11.2 percent and push nearly one in five atoll residents (19.6 percent) below the poverty line, while increasing Male' poverty only modestly to 1.9 percent.[^8]

### Atoll-Level Variation

The "atolls versus Male'" framing, while useful, oversimplifies a complex geography of deprivation. Poverty rates vary enormously across atolls, and distance from Male' is not a reliable predictor of poverty. Among the atolls with the highest poverty rates:

| Atoll | Poverty Rate (%) | Share of National Poor (%) |
|---|---|---|
| Raa | 18.9 | 15.4 |
| Gaafu Daalu | 15.6 | 11.2 |
| Thaa | 14.4 | 7.9 |
| Alif Alif | 14.2 | 4.6 |
| Haa Dhaalu | 12.9 | 13.7 |
| Haa Alif | 12.5 | 9.3 |

Meanwhile, some atolls located far from Male' maintain relatively low poverty rates: Baa at 1.7 percent, Laviyani at 2.1 percent, and Addu at 5.7 percent. These variations reflect differences in economic structure, tourism linkages, infrastructure, and migration patterns. Investment in public infrastructure is positively correlated with distance from Male' (correlation coefficient 0.6 with budget allocation), and more populous atolls tend to receive greater government support (correlation 0.89). However, the relationship between public investment and poverty reduction remains weaker, suggesting that budget allocations alone do not resolve underlying structural disadvantages.[^9]

## 12.2 Multidimensional Child Poverty

### Non-Monetary Poverty Measures

Maldives published its first Multi-Dimensional Poverty Index (MPI) in 2020, based on the 2017 Demographic and Health Survey. Unlike the monetary poverty line, the MPI classifies individuals as poor or non-poor based on deprivation across health, education, and standard of living indicators. The MPI reveals instructive differences between Male' and atolls. In Male', multidimensional poverty is driven primarily by **over-congestion and lack of access to healthcare**. In atolls, the drivers are fundamentally different: **low years of schooling, lack of access to safe drinking water, inadequate sewer systems, and limited healthcare access**.[^10]

### Children and Deprivation

The 2022 Child-Equity Census provides the most detailed picture of children's welfare across the country. Children face compounding disadvantages: they are disproportionately represented in larger households (which have higher poverty rates), they are more likely to live in overcrowded conditions, and their welfare is strongly mediated by the education and employment status of the household head.

Households with more than one child per adult are more than **twice as likely** to be poor, and such households are disproportionately found in atolls. Only about 10 percent of Maldivian households have a child-to-household-size ratio exceeding 0.5, but these families face poverty rates of 12 percent nationally, compared to 4.9 percent for households at or below the 0.5 ratio. The deprivation rate for such families reaches 13.9 percent in atolls and 12.5 percent in Male'.[^11]

The linkage between household head education and child poverty is stark. Households headed by individuals who have not completed primary education face a national poverty rate of **12.6 percent** and a deprivation rate of 39.9 percent in Male' -- nearly eight times the deprivation rate of households where the head has primary education or above. Nationally, having a household head with tertiary education is associated with a poverty rate approximately 30 times lower than that observed when the head has not completed primary school.[^12]

Children in atoll communities face particular risks from the combination of limited secondary and tertiary education facilities, health service gaps, water and sanitation deficits, and the economic marginality of primary-sector livelihoods (fisheries, small-scale manufacturing) on which their parents depend.

## 12.3 Housing Policy and the Housing Shortage

### The Constitutional and Policy Framework

The 2008 Constitution of the Republic of Maldives recognizes housing as a basic human right, placing its provision among the top obligations of the government. This constitutional commitment builds on a longer policy tradition: the National Housing Policy of 2008 (Ministry of Housing and Urban Development) identified six focus areas for addressing the country's housing needs -- adequate shelter for all, housing affordability, housing finance, land management, building standards, and institutional coordination. The Seventh National Development Plan (2007) noted that housing prices in Male' exceeded 12 times the average annual household income, a ratio that places home ownership beyond the reach of most residents.[^13]

The Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM) has repeatedly flagged housing as a major socioeconomic rights challenge. A 2008 HRCM rapid assessment reported that **85 percent of household income in Male' was spent on housing**, and that housing-related complaints constituted the third-largest category of economic, social, and cultural rights grievances received by the Commission.[^14]

### Historical Development of Housing Provision

Housing provision in the Maldives was traditionally a private, family-based activity: individuals constructed on inherited land or on plots allocated by the government. As Male' urbanized, the growing population was initially accommodated through land reclamation -- but within less than 20 years, the island had been reclaimed up to its physical reef boundary, increasing its area by over half its original size while still failing to meet demand.[^15]

By the late 1980s, the government recognized housing in Male' as a severe and worsening crisis. Two key responses followed:

**Villingili satellite settlement**: Developed on a nearby island to absorb overflow population, though it too quickly became constrained.

**Hulhumale' reclamation**: The most ambitious response, the government reclaimed land within the Hulhule'/Farukolhufushi lagoon beginning in 1997. The first phase was completed in 2003, with initial residents moving in 2004. Phase I delivered 280 residential units, 48 commercial units, a school, hospital, road network, and full utility infrastructure. An additional 222 residential land plots and 65 industrial plots were allocated. By 2016, Hulhumale's population exceeded 40,000.[^16]

**Housing Development Finance Corporation (HDFC)**: Established in March 2004 as a 100 percent government entity (later privatized to 49/51 government-private ownership), HDFC was created to provide housing finance on more favorable terms than commercial banks, which had historically offered only limited mortgage products to both developers and individual buyers.

**Legal framework reforms**: The Land Law of Maldives (2002) introduced condominium registration (strata titling), allowing individual registration of units within buildings. This enabled mortgaging and sale of individual apartments, opening the door for private developer participation in the housing market. The Land Law's condominium chapter allowed multiple owners to jointly develop a single plot, dramatically increasing housing density potential.[^17]

### The Persistent Gap

Despite these interventions, the housing crisis has not been resolved. The supply of new units for lower-income groups progressed very slowly until 2010, and much new upper-income housing has been developed by redeveloping existing low-rent stock -- reducing options for those with limited means. The housing market in Male' has remained predominantly a rental market: despite rapid construction in Hulhumale' and some ownership opportunities, the majority of Male' residents continue to rent. The government has continued to construct social housing and allocate land for various housing developments under the Hulhumale' Phase II reclamation project, but demand consistently outstrips supply.[^18]

## 12.4 Greater Male' Region -- Housing Market Analysis

### The 2017 Comprehensive Housing Market Study

The Housing Development Corporation (HDC), in collaboration with the Maldives National University and the Islamic University of the Maldives, conducted a comprehensive housing market study for the Greater Male' Region in 2017. The study, based on a survey of 672 local residents across Male', Hulhumale', and Villimale', provides the most detailed empirical picture of housing conditions, affordability, migration patterns, and housing preferences in the capital region.[^19]

### Demographic and Migration Profile

The study confirmed the powerful centripetal pull of the Greater Male' Region. Of surveyed participants, **57 percent** had moved from an outer island to Male'. The primary reasons for migration were education (78.6 percent), employment (46.2 percent), and medical needs (17.3 percent). The population within the Greater Male' Region was distributed across Male' (72.9 percent), Hulhumale' (14.35 percent), and Villimale' (12.74 percent).[^20]

Regarding residential mobility, 38.2 percent of respondents had been in their current location for 0-5 years, while 35.9 percent had been there for over 16 years -- suggesting both a significant recent-migrant population and a settled long-term community. Only 14 percent reported having moved within the Greater Male' Region, indicating limited intra-regional mobility.

When asked about their preferred place to live, responses revealed an interesting tension: while 35.9 percent preferred Male' and 33.7 percent preferred Hulhumale', only **17.1 percent** expressed a preference for returning to their home island. This suggests that the centralization of population is driven not merely by push factors in atolls but also by genuine preference for the services, opportunities, and connectivity available in the capital region.[^21]

### Housing Tenure and Conditions

The study found that **56.6 percent** of Greater Male' residents lived in rented apartments, **41.5 percent** in owned dwellings, and only **1.9 percent** in social housing. Among those who had migrated from islands, 39 percent were renters, while about 10 percent of Male'-origin residents also rented -- and a similar percentage of island-origin residents owned their dwellings. The extremely low social housing share (1.9 percent) reflects the minimal reach of government housing programs relative to need.[^22]

The most common monthly rent was MVR 10,000-15,000 (42.3 percent of renters), followed by below MVR 10,000 (27 percent) and MVR 15,000-20,000 (21.4 percent). The World Bank's 2022 Poverty Assessment found that approximately **74 percent** of households in Male' live in rented dwellings (compared to 5.3 percent in atolls), and renters in Male' spend about **37 percent** of their annual expenditure on rent alone, compared to 19 percent among atoll renters.[^23]

### Affordability

The housing market study's most concerning finding relates to affordability. Most renters earning less than MVR 10,000 per month (53.3 percent of individual respondents reported personal income below MVR 5,000) pay rent that consumes most or all of their income. The study concluded that "housing is not affordable for an average person" in the Greater Male' Region, and that rent or housing prices should ideally constitute only 15 to 20 percent of household income -- a benchmark that the vast majority of residents fail to meet.[^24]

The equilibrium market prices identified by the study were: less than MVR 10,000 per month for a one-bedroom unit; MVR 10,000-15,000 for a two-bedroom unit; and MVR 10,000-15,000 for a three-bedroom unit. Given that most households earn MVR 20,000-30,000 per month (25.6 percent of respondents), even a modest two-bedroom rental consumes 33-75 percent of household income.[^25]

Housing finance mechanisms compound the problem. According to housing financing authorities, people earning lower salaries do not qualify for social housing applications; simultaneously, low-income individuals cannot access housing loans. While they may theoretically apply for social housing if they fall within a certain income threshold, the supply of social housing units is grossly insufficient. The study recommended lowering prices and promoting housing across different market segments, introducing affordable mortgages with targeted subsidies, creating housing finance safety nets backed by annual government budget allocations, and facilitating cross-subsidization through public-private partnerships.[^26]

## 12.5 Population Concentration and Internal Migration

### The Urbanization Paradox

Maldives' urbanization pattern is among the most extreme in the world. With a total land area of less than 300 km2 spread across 1,192 islands (of which approximately 200 are inhabited), the country's population is heavily concentrated around Male' -- a city of less than two square kilometers that accommodates over one-third of the national population. The population density of Male' exceeds **65,000 persons per square kilometer**, ranking it fifth among localities in South Asia, surpassed only by Dhaka, Karachi, Kolkata, and Kathmandu. Unlike those cities, however, Male' is the country's only large urban agglomeration.[^27]

This extreme concentration is the product of decades of centralized development. Until recently, virtually all industrial, commercial, and social infrastructure was developed in and around Male'. Job opportunities, higher education, specialized healthcare, and government services are concentrated in the capital, triggering sustained migration from the atolls. The 2014 Population and Housing Census recorded a total population of 344,023, of which 38 percent lived in Male'. By 2019, the estimated population was 530,953, with the Greater Male' Region's share continuing to grow.[^28]

### Push and Pull Factors

Migration to Male' is driven by a combination of push factors in the atolls (limited employment, restricted education beyond lower secondary level, absence of specialized healthcare, inadequate infrastructure) and pull factors in the capital (employment in public and private sectors, higher education institutions, medical facilities, social networks). The housing market study found that 78.6 percent of migrants cited education as a reason for moving, 46.2 percent cited employment, and 17.3 percent cited medical needs.[^29]

The enclave nature of the economy reinforces this pattern. Revenue-generating tourist resorts are located on dedicated islands that are generally off-limits to Maldivian residents (except resort workers). Industrial islands are similarly segregated. Administrative islands -- where the general population lives -- are thus economically dependent on public-sector employment, fisheries, small-scale commerce, and remittances. In many smaller islands, economic activity is insufficient to sustain young, educated populations, who consequently leave for Male'.

### The National Spatial Plan

The government has recognized the unsustainability of continued Male'-centric development. The National Spatial Plan, adopted alongside the Strategic Action Plan for 2019-2023, envisions the development of regional hubs and subregional centers with basic services accessible within proximity. The plan aims to distribute development more equitably, reduce overcrowding in the capital, and provide viable alternatives to migration. Past administrations have focused heavily on the Greater Male' Region, and the Hulhumale' Phase II project represents the largest single effort to provide housing relief. However, critics argue that continued investment in Male'-adjacent reclamation merely reinforces centralization rather than creating genuine regional alternatives.[^30]

## 12.6 Household Income and Expenditure Surveys

### The HIES Framework

The Household Income and Expenditure Survey has been the primary instrument for measuring welfare in the Maldives since 2002/03. Each round (2002/03, 2009/10, 2016, and 2019/20) has incorporated improvements in methodology, sampling, and data collection. HIES 2019, administered from October 2019 to March 2020 (with a break in December 2019 and suspension in March 2020 due to COVID-19), was the first round conducted entirely on digital tablets, enabling real-time quality control. The survey's sampling frame was based on the 2014 Census, drawing 15 households from each enumeration block (approximately 65 households per block).[^31]

### Income Trends and Sources

HIES data reveal important shifts in household income composition between 2016 and 2019. The average annual per capita income for households in the poorest decile rose from 21,019 MVR in 2016 to 28,770 MVR in 2019, a substantial real increase. In 2016, wages and private transfers comprised 51 percent of annual income for the poorest decile; by 2019, these two sources accounted for 63 percent. Simultaneously, the contribution of self-employment to total household income declined from 25.9 percent in 2016 to 19.7 percent in 2019, while earnings from wages and salaries increased across the income distribution.[^32]

This shift from self-employment to wage employment was particularly pronounced in the atolls and among poorer households. By 2019, employment in each of the major atoll occupations -- agriculture, fisheries, manufacturing, education, trade, and administration -- had shifted from self-employment toward wage employment, with the largest increases in wage employment seen in fisheries and trading activities. The expansion of wage jobs in atolls allowed households to improve their quality of life, as reflected in widening asset bases, improved housing quality, and greater access to piped water and sewer connections even among poorer households.[^33]

### Expenditure Patterns

The structure of household expenditure differs sharply between Male' and the atolls. In Male', housing costs (primarily rent) constitute the single largest expenditure category for most households, particularly among renters. The World Bank's analysis of HIES 2019 data shows that renters in Male' spend approximately 37 percent of annual expenditure on housing, compared to 19 percent among the small number of atoll renters. When housing costs are excluded, the disparity in cost of living between Male' and atolls narrows considerably.[^34]

Food expenditure also reveals important welfare indicators. Spending on food "away from home" (restaurants, prepared food) across the wealth distribution increased between 2016 and 2019 in both Male' and atolls, a pattern generally associated with rising affluence. However, a concerning trend is the substitution of bottled water for rainwater and piped water, driven by concerns about safety and taste. This increases household costs and generates plastic waste, particularly in atolls where rainwater harvesting could provide a sustainable alternative.[^35]

## 12.7 Labour Market and Employment Challenges

### Structure of Employment

The Maldivian labour market is characterized by several distinctive features that shape poverty and vulnerability patterns. As of the 2016 HIES (the most recent pre-COVID labour data), overall unemployment was 6.2 percent nationally. Youth unemployment, however, was **15.9 percent** -- two and a half times the adult rate. The overall labour force participation rate was 60 percent, but this figure conceals a large gender gap: participation rates of 80 percent for men versus only 44 percent for women.[^36]

The government is the largest single employer, accounting for 43 percent of female employment and 38 percent of male employment. Public-sector jobs offer preferred working conditions and benefits, creating significant distortions: almost two-thirds of young women and over half of young men surveyed indicated they would prefer to wait for government or state-owned enterprise jobs rather than accept private-sector positions. About 27 percent of young men and 23 percent of young women were classified as NEET (not in employment, education, or training).[^37]

### The Tourism Enclave and Labour Market Segmentation

Tourism, contributing approximately 26 percent of GDP (2019), is the dominant economic sector but operates through an enclave model that limits its employment benefits for the broader population. Resorts are located on dedicated islands, typically employing a mix of Maldivian and foreign workers. The 2019 Resort Employees Survey recorded 44,954 employees across resorts, with locals representing 47 percent of the total -- but only 3 percent of total resort employees were female Maldivians. The 2014 Census showed that approximately 90 percent of tourism jobs are held by males, and foreigners account for about half of all tourism employment.[^38]

The requirement that at least 51 percent of a resort's workforce be Maldivian has improved local employment linkages, but significant barriers remain. The rapid growth of high-end tourism services has outpaced the Maldivian labour market's capacity to supply appropriately skilled workers. Employers frequently hire foreign workers for both high-skill positions (particularly in resorts) and low-skill positions (construction), while Maldivians often lack the technical, vocational, and soft skills required. A skills gap analysis based on the HIES 2016 found that 15 percent of women and 24 percent of men are overqualified for their low-skill occupations, while for high-skill occupations, nearly 44 percent of women and 62 percent of men are underqualified.[^39]

The guesthouse segment -- based on administrative islands rather than resort islands -- has emerged as a promising alternative that creates more direct economic linkages with local communities. Non-resort accommodation services grew from approximately 1 percent of GDP in 2015 to about 3 percent by 2019. However, this nascent sector was particularly devastated by COVID-19 and has been slower to recover than the established resort segment.[^40]

### Gender Dimensions

Gender disparities in the labour market are pronounced and structurally embedded. Women's labour force participation is less than half that of men (44 percent versus 80 percent), despite women generally achieving higher levels of education than their male counterparts. The low participation rate is driven by early marriage (more than 50 percent of women marry before age 20, and nearly 80 percent of women with children have their first child before 25), limited childcare options, transportation barriers (cited by almost half of young atoll women as a reason for declining job offers), and cultural norms that designate certain sectors -- particularly tourism and resorts -- as unsuitable for women.[^41]

Women who are employed tend to earn less and face greater economic insecurity. In the COVID-19 crisis, while the overall incidence of income shocks was similar for both genders, women were **twice as likely** to stop working entirely (3.4 percent of women versus 6.9 percent, compared to men's lower rates) and nearly three times as likely to experience prolonged work absence (5.1 percent for women versus 1.8 percent for men). In manufacturing, 36 percent of workers are women, but 22 percent of female manufacturing workers had stopped working or were on prolonged absence, compared to 8 percent of male colleagues.[^42]

## 12.8 COVID-19 Economic Impacts -- Livelihood Assessments

### The Scale of the Shock

The COVID-19 pandemic struck the Maldives with devastating economic force. Tourism -- the economy's backbone -- came to a complete standstill in April 2020 when borders closed on March 27. Tourist arrivals had already fallen by 63 percent in March 2020 and dropped to zero from April through mid-July. Total arrivals in 2020 amounted to only 555,494, approximately 67.4 percent below the 2019 level of 1.7 million. The World Bank projected a GDP contraction of 28 percent or more for 2020, and government revenue was expected to fall by 49 percent -- approximately US$1 billion less than the prior year.[^43]

The Rapid Livelihood Assessment, commissioned by the Ministry of Economic Development with UNDP support and published in August 2020, documented the crisis across three dimensions: macroeconomic impacts (Part I), employment effects (Part II), and impacts on micro, small, and medium enterprises (Part III). At the time of the assessment, 955 tourist establishments were in operation (155 resorts, 13 hotels, 629 guesthouses, 158 safari vessels), and the tourism industry employed more than one-fifth of the working population.[^44]

### Employment Disruption

The Government's online job portal, JobCenter.mv, became the primary avenue for reporting COVID-19-related employment impacts and seeking income support. By mid-2020, over 7,500 individuals had registered employment-related complaints. The economic disruption was not limited to tourism: services account for 72 percent of Maldivian employment (Census 2014), and the cascading effects of tourism shutdown rippled through transport, food and accommodation, retail, and construction.

The World Bank's phone surveys conducted in February and April 2021 provided systematic evidence of income shocks. Self-employed individuals were **three times** more likely than wage earners to report income losses. Self-employment rates are higher in primary (fisheries) and secondary (manufacturing) sectors, and two out of three self-employed individuals reside in atolls -- driving the higher incidence of income shocks in atoll communities. Among the self-employed in atolls, approximately 70 percent experienced a shock, compared to 54 percent of the self-employed in Male'.[^45]

Manufacturing experienced the most severe disruption, with approximately 70 percent of all workers in the sector reporting economic shocks, including complete suspension of activities. Manufacturing on atoll administrative islands is typically small-scale and dependent on either the local economy or linkages with nearby resorts; lockdowns and travel bans severed both connections. Fisheries workers faced a comparatively lower shock rate of about 5 percent in terms of complete work suspension, but experienced significant price and market access disruptions.[^46]

### Government Response and Income Support

The government announced a comprehensive Economic Recovery Plan (ERP) of US$162 million on March 20, 2020. Key measures included:

- **Income support**: A monthly allowance of MVR 5,000 (approximately US$324) to Maldivian citizens who lost their jobs or had livelihoods affected, disbursed through the JobCenter portal beginning April 22, 2020.
- **Utility relief**: Reduction of electricity and water bills for April and May 2020; lower petrol and diesel prices.
- **Price controls**: Imposed on food staples to prevent profiteering.
- **Debt moratoria**: On student, housing, and business loans until December 31, 2020.
- **Financing facilities**: Extended through Bank of Maldives and SME Development Finance Corporation to affected businesses.
- **Budget reallocation**: Cuts to senior executive salaries, cancellation of non-essential travel and deferred maintenance/renovation projects, freeing approximately MVR 1 billion for COVID response.[^47]

The World Bank-supported COVID-19 Income Support Project provided additional emergency framework for targeted income transfers. The SAILS (Strengthening Allocation of Income Support through Labour Market Services and Social Protection) Project subsequently built upon the emergency response to create more durable labour market services and social protection infrastructure. However, the fiscal impact was severe: the budget deficit for 2020 was projected at US$841 million, total public and publicly guaranteed debt reached 139.3 percent of GDP, and the current account deficit widened to an estimated 26.3 percent of GDP.[^48]

### Differentiated Impacts by Sector and Demographics

The livelihood assessments revealed critical disparities in vulnerability:

**By sector**: Tourism and hospitality workers faced the most immediate and severe impacts, but manufacturing, transport, retail, and construction all experienced significant disruption. Fisheries were relatively less affected in terms of employment, but faced market access and price volatility challenges.

**By age**: Youth were at greater risk of economic shocks than mid-career professionals, compounding already high pre-pandemic youth unemployment rates.

**By employment type**: Wage workers with formal contracts were relatively protected compared to self-employed individuals and informal workers, who lacked contractual protections and often could not access formal support mechanisms.

**By location**: Atoll residents were more exposed than Male' residents, owing to higher self-employment rates, greater dependence on primary-sector activities, and more limited access to government relief infrastructure.

**By gender**: Women faced extreme shocks including complete work stoppage and prolonged absence at rates two to three times those of men, despite similar overall incidence of some form of income shock.[^49]

## 12.9 Social Disparities Across Atolls and Islands

### The Geography of Disadvantage

The Maldives' development model has produced pronounced spatial disparities that cannot be reduced to a simple Male'-atolls binary. The World Bank's 2022 Poverty Assessment demonstrates that clusters of better-off and worse-off atolls exist within each region and zone, based on the National Spatial Plan's geographic framework.

In the **North Region** (283.6 km north-south distance), the Upper North zone contains Haa Alif (12.5 percent poverty), Haa Dhaalu (12.9 percent), and Shaviyani (5.6 percent). In the Lower North zone, Raa has the country's highest atoll poverty rate at 18.9 percent, while neighbouring Baa has only 1.7 percent and Laviyani just 2.1 percent. These adjacent atolls share similar geographic characteristics but have markedly different economic profiles, likely related to tourism development (Baa includes a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve with significant resort development).[^50]

In the **Central Region** (378.3 km north-south), Alif Alif (14.2 percent) and Thaa (14.4 percent) show high poverty alongside neighbours with rates below 3 percent (Alif Daalu at 3.1 percent, Daalu at 2.7 percent, Waavu at 2.8 percent).

In the **South Region** (269.7 km north-south), Gaafu Daalu stands out at 15.6 percent poverty with 11.2 percent of the national poor, while Addu -- the country's second-largest population center -- maintains a moderate 5.7 percent rate.

### Infrastructure and Service Delivery Gaps

The disparity in welfare across atolls is closely linked to infrastructure. Access to basic healthcare and education is universal at the primary level, but advanced facilities -- high schools, secondary healthcare, tertiary education -- are concentrated on atoll capital islands. Residents of smaller islands must travel, often by expensive and infrequent ferry or speedboat services, to access these facilities. Public ferry connections vary dramatically: all three atolls in the Upper North zone are connected by public ferries, but none of the four atolls in the Lower North zone share ferry links. In the Lower Central zone, only two of five atolls have inter-atoll ferry connections. The absence of affordable, reliable transport effectively cuts many island communities off from regional service hubs.[^51]

Water and sanitation represent another critical dimension. HIES data show that between 2016 and 2019, Maldivians increasingly substituted bottled water for rainwater and piped water, driven by concerns about safety. The incidence of piped water and sewer connections improved in atolls between the two survey rounds, but access remains markedly lower than in Male'. Toilet facilities in atolls have shifted away from open or basic systems toward flush toilets connected to septic tanks, but full sewer network connections remain rare outside the capital region.[^52]

### The Vulnerability Mapping Evidence

The 2019 Vulnerability Mapping of Laamu Atoll provides a microcosm of how multiple disadvantages intersect in atoll communities. The assessment documented the compounding effects of geographic isolation, limited economic diversification (dependence on fisheries and small-scale agriculture), inadequate infrastructure (particularly water, sanitation, and transport), gender-based barriers to employment, and exposure to climate risks. Vulnerable groups identified include single mothers, elderly persons living alone, persons with disabilities, and households dependent on fishing income during seasonal downturns. The intersectionality of these factors means that vulnerability in atolls is not merely economic but encompasses physical safety, health access, educational opportunity, and social inclusion.[^53]

## 12.10 Fisheries Communities and Economic Vulnerability

### The Fisheries Sector as Social Policy

The tuna fisheries of the Maldives occupy a unique position at the intersection of economic activity, social policy, food security, and cultural identity. The 2013 Socio-Economic Assessment of the Tuna Fisheries, conducted by the International Pole & Line Foundation (IPNLF) with the London School of Economics, framed the pole-and-line fishery not merely as an industry but as "a social and economic policy tool" that shapes the vulnerability, adaptability, and livelihood assets of island populations.[^54]

Fisheries constitute the most important primary economic activity on most inhabited islands and a key source of income for men in atolls with lower levels of formal education. Fish is a major staple of the Maldivian diet, and the country exports tuna products to European, South Asian, and East Asian markets. Primary industries including fisheries and agriculture contributed 4.6 percent of GDP in 2019, but this figure understates the sector's importance as a social safety net and employer of last resort for atoll communities where alternative formal employment is scarce.[^55]

### Vulnerability of Fishing Communities

Fishing communities face multiple sources of vulnerability:

**Market exposure**: The sector is heavily exposed to world market trends, particularly for skipjack tuna. Price volatility directly affects fisher incomes, which are typically calculated as shares of the catch value rather than fixed wages. When global prices decline or export markets contract -- as occurred during COVID-19 -- fishers absorb the full impact.

**Climate effects**: Changes in ocean temperature, monsoon patterns, and marine ecosystem health directly affect fish availability and catch volumes. The Maldives' tuna fishery is heavily dependent on the abundance of baitfish, which are sensitive to oceanographic conditions.

**Infrastructure deficits**: Returns from fisheries have remained low in atolls due to the **lack of storage and processing facilities** and limited capacity to convert raw catch into value-added products. Most fish is sold fresh or dried, with minimal value capture at the island level. Cold-storage facilities, processing plants, and efficient transport links to markets are concentrated in Male' and a few larger islands.[^56]

**Generational change**: Younger Maldivians in atolls increasingly aspire to service-sector employment (particularly government or tourism jobs), and fishing is viewed as a low-status occupation. This creates a potential labour shortage in the fishery and erodes the traditional knowledge base, even as alternative employment remains scarce in many islands.

### Fisheries and the COVID-19 Impact

The pandemic's impact on fisheries was complex. While fisheries were not subject to direct closure orders (unlike tourism and guesthouse operations), the cascade of restrictions disrupted market access, transport logistics, and export chains. The Rapid Livelihood Assessment noted that approximately 5 percent of fisheries workers experienced complete work suspension, but broader impacts -- reduced catches due to transport restrictions, inability to sell catch at normal prices, disruption of export logistics -- affected a much larger share of fishing communities. The fisheries sector is the most important primary economic activity and source of employment in almost all inhabited islands, so any disruption has immediate welfare consequences for atoll communities that lack alternative income sources.[^57]

## 12.11 Research Evidence and Policy Recommendations

### Profile of Poverty and Deprivation: Key Correlates

The accumulated evidence from HIES data, the World Bank Poverty Assessment, the Systematic Country Diagnostic, and sector-specific studies identifies a consistent set of factors associated with poverty and economic vulnerability in the Maldives:

**Household size and overcrowding**: Households with 10 or more members face a poverty rate of 12 percent, three times that of smaller households. Overcrowded households (more than three members per sleeping room) -- affecting 10.4 percent of the population -- are almost **twice as likely** to be poor, and in Male', overcrowded households have deprivation rates **eight times** higher than non-overcrowded ones.[^58]

**Education of household head**: Below-primary education among household heads is associated with 12.6 percent poverty nationally and 39.9 percent deprivation in Male'. Tertiary education reduces poverty to 0.4 percent nationally. The return to education is sharply non-linear: the welfare penalty for being poorly educated in Male' is much larger than in atolls, while the reward of tertiary education is larger in atolls.[^59]

**Employment type**: Self-employed household heads face a 5.5 percent poverty rate, more than double the 2.4 percent rate for wage-earning employees. Informal employment is associated with poverty rates twice those of formal employment. Primary-sector (fisheries, agriculture) and secondary-sector (manufacturing, construction) employment are both associated with higher poverty relative to tertiary-sector work.[^60]

**Disability and chronic disease**: 13.2 percent of household heads face disability and 35.5 percent live with chronic disease. Households with disabled heads have significantly higher poverty (9.6 percent) and deprivation rates (11.3 percent in Male', 8 percent in atolls).[^61]

**Female-headed households**: 42.7 percent of households nationally are female-headed. These households show a slightly higher poverty rate (6 versus 5 percent for male-headed) but are notably more likely to be deprived in Male' (7.5 percent versus 6.7 percent).[^62]

**Migration status**: Migrant household heads represent 52 percent of the population. Migration is associated with lower poverty in atolls (suggesting voluntary moves to better-off locations) but slightly higher deprivation in Male' (reflecting the costs and challenges of settlement in the capital).[^63]

### Policy Framework and International Partnerships

Multiple international development frameworks shape the policy response to housing, poverty, and economic vulnerability:

**The World Bank Systematic Country Diagnostic (2021)** identified three priority areas: (1) generating economic opportunities for a young and expanding workforce, (2) environmental sustainability and climate resilience, and (3) fiscal sustainability. Cross-cutting priorities include governance, labour market reform, and private-sector development. The SCD emphasized that Maldives' enclave tourism model has created limited linkages between the high-end resort sector and the broader population, and that the country must harness its demographic transition to generate productive employment.[^64]

**The Maldives SDG Roadmap (2022)** assessed post-COVID progress toward the Sustainable Development Goals and identified housing affordability, poverty reduction, decent work, and reduced inequalities as areas requiring accelerated action. The pandemic reversed or slowed progress on multiple SDG targets.[^65]

**The Islamic Development Bank Partnership Strategy (2022-2025)** prioritizes resilient and sustainable economic development, with attention to housing, employment, and social protection. The **EU Multiannual Indicative Programme (2021-2027)** focuses on green recovery and governance priorities, including economic diversification and social resilience.[^66]

**The ADB Country Diagnostic (2015)** identified geographic dispersion, extreme economic concentration in tourism, and climate vulnerability as the fundamental structural constraints, recommending infrastructure investment in regional hubs, skills development, and diversification of economic activity beyond Male' and the resort islands.[^67]

### Policy Recommendations from the Evidence Base

The convergent evidence from poverty assessments, housing studies, livelihood assessments, and country diagnostics points to several interconnected policy imperatives:

**1. Regional economic development and decentralization.** The extreme concentration of economic activity and population in the Greater Male' Region is a primary structural driver of both the housing shortage and atoll poverty. Effective implementation of the National Spatial Plan -- through genuine investment in regional hubs with secondary healthcare, higher education, transport connectivity, and private-sector enabling infrastructure -- is essential. Budget allocations must be guided not only by population size and distance but by demonstrated poverty rates and economic potential. Improved inter-atoll ferry connections are a prerequisite for regional hub strategies to function.[^68]

**2. Housing supply and affordability in Greater Male'.** The housing crisis requires expansion of social housing stock, introduction of affordable mortgage products with targeted subsidies for disadvantaged groups (low-income earners, single parents, persons with disabilities, elderly), continued development of Hulhumale' Phase II with explicit affordability targets, establishment of housing finance safety nets backed by annual government allocations, and regulatory mechanisms to prevent developer add-ons from inflating costs beyond approved price points. Cross-subsidization models through public-private partnerships should allocate a mandated percentage of new developments to lower-income housing.[^69]

**3. Labour market reform and skills development.** Closing the gap between educational attainment and labour market demands is critical. Expanding technical and vocational education in atolls, reducing skills mismatches that drive reliance on foreign labour, promoting private-sector employment through improved business climate and reduced public-sector wage premiums, and specifically targeting female labour force participation through childcare provision, flexible work arrangements, and transport solutions. The SAILS Project provides an institutional foundation for improved labour market services and social protection.[^70]

**4. Social protection and shock resilience.** The COVID-19 experience demonstrated both the vulnerability of the population to external shocks and the value of rapid response mechanisms. Building on the emergency income support infrastructure to create permanent, scalable social protection systems -- including unemployment insurance, expanded disability and elderly support, and climate-related disaster response funds -- would reduce the transmission of economic shocks to poverty. The finding that a two-month income shock could double the national poverty rate makes clear the urgency of building fiscal buffers and protection mechanisms.[^71]

**5. Fisheries sector modernization.** Protecting and modernizing the fisheries sector as both an economic activity and a social safety net requires investment in cold-storage and processing facilities at the atoll level, development of value-added fish products, improvement of transport links to export markets, establishment of minimum price guarantee mechanisms to buffer fishers against world market volatility, and environmental management to ensure long-term sustainability of fish stocks. The sector's role as the primary income source for many of the country's poorest communities makes fisheries policy inseparable from poverty policy.[^72]

**6. Data and monitoring infrastructure.** Several knowledge gaps were identified across the evidence base. The truncation of HIES 2019 (three atolls entirely missing) limits the granularity of current poverty estimates. Regular, complete HIES rounds with consistent methodology are essential for tracking welfare changes. The MPI should be updated with more recent data to complement monetary poverty measures. Labour market data collection should extend to resort and industrial islands, which are currently excluded from household surveys despite employing one-fifth of the workforce. Strengthening the capacity of the Maldives Bureau of Statistics to produce timely, disaggregated data is a foundational requirement for evidence-based social policy.[^73]

## Conclusion

The Maldives' development trajectory -- from one of the world's poorest countries in 1980 (GDP per capita US$268) to an upper-middle-income country approaching high-income status by 2019 (GDP per capita US$10,627) -- is a remarkable achievement built on the successful commercialization of a unique marine environment through high-end tourism. Yet this success has generated its own contradictions: an enclave economy that concentrates wealth while limiting broad-based employment; a housing crisis in the capital region that consumes the lion's share of household resources; persistent poverty in atolls that contain 92 percent of the nation's poor; a labour market defined by skills mismatches, gender exclusion, and public-sector dependency; and an economic structure so dependent on international tourism that a single pandemic could contract GDP by a third.

Addressing these challenges requires moving beyond the current development model's implicit assumption that the benefits of centralized, tourism-driven growth will trickle outward to atoll communities. The evidence presented in this Part demonstrates that they have not done so sufficiently, and that without deliberate policy intervention -- in regional development, housing affordability, labour market reform, social protection, and fisheries modernization -- a significant share of the Maldivian population will remain economically vulnerable, spatially marginalized, and inadequately housed, regardless of how the headline GDP and poverty statistics perform. The country's small size, geographic fragmentation, and climate vulnerability make these challenges simultaneously more acute and more tractable than in larger, more complex economies -- but only if policy design matches the specificity of the context.
