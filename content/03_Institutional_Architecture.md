## 3.1 Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD) -- Structure and Mandate

The ministry responsible for social policy in the Maldives has undergone repeated reorganisation across administrations, with its name, portfolio scope, and internal structure shifting with each change of government. Under the Muizzu administration (2023-present), the portfolio is held by the **Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD)**. Previous incarnations include the Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services (MoGFSS) under the Solih administration (2018-2023), the Ministry of Gender and Family under the Yameen administration (2013-2018), and the Ministry of Health and Family during the brief Waheed presidency (2012-2013).[^1] These name changes are not merely cosmetic; they reflect real shifts in which functions are bundled together, which deputy ministers hold what briefs, and which directorates have operational authority at any given time.

### Core mandate

Regardless of its name, the ministry has consistently held four primary mandates:

1. **Child protection and welfare**: Policy formulation and service delivery for children at risk, administered through the Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS) unit within the ministry.
2. **Gender equality and protection from gender-based violence**: Including oversight of the Family Protection Authority (FPA) as an arm's-length body, and coordination of the Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP).
3. **Social welfare for vulnerable groups**: Including services for the elderly, persons with disabilities, and other populations requiring state support.
4. **Community-level social service coordination**: Oversight of Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs) across atolls and liaison with the Local Government Authority (LGA).[^2]

### Internal structure

The ministry's internal organisation typically includes:

- **Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS)**: The operational unit responsible for case management of child protection and domestic violence cases at the national level. CFPS provides centralised management for all 20 atoll-level FCSCs and maintains specialised teams for assessment, investigation, and case coordination.[^3]
- **Policy Department**: Responsible for policy development, research coordination, and strategic planning across the ministry's mandate areas.
- **Social Protection and Services Division**: Coordinates with NSPA on allowance programmes and oversees community-level social work.
- **Gender Equality Division**: Staffs the GEAP Steering Committee and coordinates the Gender Focal Point network across all public institutions, as required by the Gender Equality Act 2016.[^4]

### Staffing and operational realities

The ministry's workforce is thin relative to its mandate. Social service workers in the Maldivian civil service are classified under the National Pay Commission's job classification system, with roles ranging from Chief Social Services Officer down to lead-level positions, with salaries denominated in MVR and structured with allowance steps.[^5] Attracting and retaining qualified social workers has been a persistent challenge. The first cohort of trained social workers emerged only in 2006-2007, when groups of school leavers received an Advanced Certificate through training programmes delivered by ministry staff and at the University of Kelaniya in Sri Lanka. A one-year Advanced Diploma in Social Service Work followed in 2007, eventually giving way to the full four-year undergraduate Social Work degree programme at the Faculty of Health Sciences at the Maldives National University.[^6] However, the supply of graduates has never matched demand across 20 atolls and roughly 200 inhabited islands.

A critical operational constraint is geographic dispersion. The ministry's mandate covers a population spread across approximately 35,000 square miles of ocean, on 199 inhabited islands. Central office staff in Male can set policy and manage databases, but direct service delivery depends on FCSC workers stationed in atoll capitals who may serve multiple islands with limited transport options.[^7] The result is a system where policy ambition consistently exceeds implementation capacity, particularly on remote islands.

### Coordination role

The ministry chairs or co-chairs several inter-agency coordination bodies, including the National Domestic Violence Coordinating Committee, the GEAP Steering Committee, and the Social Sector Coordination Committee at Deputy Minister level. The Strategic Action Plan 2019-2023 assigned the ministry a lead role in driving integrated social service delivery.[^8] In practice, coordination relies heavily on informal relationships between individual officers rather than institutionalised protocols, and the effectiveness of inter-agency work varies significantly depending on the personalities in post and the political priorities of any given administration.

## 3.2 National Social Protection Agency (NSPA)

### Establishment and mandate

The **National Social Protection Agency (NSPA)** was established in **2011** during the latter part of the Nasheed administration, created as the central body administering social protection cash transfer programmes in the Maldives. Its establishment was part of the institutional infrastructure needed to roll out the Aasandha universal health insurance scheme and to coordinate the growing number of social welfare programmes that emerged from the pension and welfare reforms of 2008-2011.[^9]

### Programmes administered

NSPA administers the following programmes:

| Programme | Monthly amount | Approximate recipients |
|---|---|---|
| Aasandha universal health insurance | Claims-based | All Maldivian citizens |
| Disability Allowance | MVR 2,000 | ~6,000 persons |
| Single Parent Allowance | Varies | Single-parent households |
| Foster Care Allowance | Varies | Foster families |
| Food Assistance Programme | In-kind/subsidy | Eligible households |

The Aasandha programme is by far the largest, with annual costs ranging from MVR 1-2.3 billion. Aasandha operations were later corporatised as Aasandha Company (Aasandha Pvt Ltd), a state-owned enterprise handling claims processing and provider reimbursements, while NSPA retained oversight of the broader social protection transfer portfolio.[^10]

### Social Protection Information System (SPIS)

NSPA manages the Social Protection Information System (SPIS), which holds records of all individuals receiving pensions, allowances, and subsidies. The SPIS is linked to the Department of National Registration's ID database to enable unique identification and prevent duplication of benefits. For example, if someone receives a disability allowance, the SPIS flags any overlapping claims, such as a foster parent allowance for the same child, for eligibility verification. The system also connects with the Maldives Pension Administration Office (MPAO) to adjust Basic Pension amounts against MRPS payments automatically.[^11]

NSPA has begun developing a "Single Registry" of poor and vulnerable households to better target programmes. A pilot social registry was conducted in 2019-2020 with World Bank support, collecting data on household income, assets, and demographics, though it has yet to roll out nationally.[^12]

### Coverage gaps

Despite NSPA's administrative reach, coverage remains uneven. Research by Hameed et al. (2022) found that only 25.6 per cent of people with disabilities receive the Disability Allowance, with lowest coverage among women, older adults, those in Male, and those with sensory impairments. Barriers include lack of information about the programme, perceptions of disability that do not match eligibility criteria, geographic factors, and stigma.[^13] The Single Parent Allowance, while important for households headed by divorced women, reaches only those who navigate the application process -- a process that requires documentation and administrative engagement that many vulnerable women find difficult to sustain, particularly on remote islands.[^14]

### Integration with the OneGov platform

The government's OneGov digital platform is an integration effort that allows different services to be accessed via one portal. Many social services have been onboarding to OneGov, including Zakat House services integrated in late 2024, enabling cross-referencing against NSPA's database of social assistance recipients. The long-term vision is that a citizen could log in with their eID and see all benefits they receive, apply for new ones online, and that agencies can cross-reference data with privacy maintained through role-based access.[^15]

## 3.3 Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS)

### Institutional identity

The Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS) is the operational unit within MoSFD (and its predecessor ministries) responsible for all child protection casework at the national level. It was established as the successor to the Unit for the Rights of the Children (URC), which the Ministry of Home Affairs created in 1992 as the secretariat for the National Council for Children's Rights.[^16] The URC's initial work consisted of counselling provided by volunteer counsellors trained through a two-week introductory course. Over time, the mandate expanded from counselling to statutory child protection intervention, but this expansion occurred without a corresponding growth in professional capacity, creating tensions between the counselling and social control functions of child protection work that persist to this day.[^17]

### Structure and functions

CFPS is centralised in Male and provides overall management for all Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs) situated across the 20 administrative atolls. CFPS consists of specialised teams -- for assessment, investigation, legal liaison, and case coordination -- whereas FCSCs remain generic teams handling all case types.[^18] This centralised-decentralised structure means that complex decisions about child removal, court applications, and inter-agency coordination funnel through Male even when the family in question lives on a remote island hundreds of kilometres away.

The Child Rights Protection Act (CRPA) 2019 gave CFPS the power to apply for judicial orders:

- **Interim Assessment Order** (Article 80): Obtained where an offence has been committed and investigation is required without parental consent. Both CFPS and the Maldives Police Service can apply.
- **Protection Order** (Article 90): A temporary order to remove a child and place them with a state-approved person.
- **Supervision Order** (Article 93): Allows CFPS or police to monitor a child with their parents daily to ensure safety.[^19]

Orders must be applied for within five days of taking a child into state care. When courts require assessments, the task is passed to CFPS workers on top of their existing caseload.[^20]

### Caseload crisis

CFPS workers carry caseloads that far exceed safe levels. UNICEF (2022) documented average caseloads of over 160 cases per worker, a figure that makes meaningful engagement with individual families effectively impossible and drives a reactive rather than preventive approach to child protection.[^21] The scrutiny of oversight bodies such as HRCM and the Children's Ombudsperson's Office compounds the pressure: these organisations impose ambitious monitoring objectives that are challenging to fulfil given staff capacity, leaving social workers in a permanent state of anxiety about accusations of negligence.[^22]

### The 1412 helpline

The toll-free 24-hour national child helpline (1412) was established with assistance from Child Helpline International and later given statutory basis in Article 76 of the CRPA 2019. The helpline provides a reporting channel for child abuse and neglect, but its effectiveness depends on the capacity of CFPS and FCSCs to respond to the cases it generates.[^23]

### Data system: the Maldives Child Protection Database (MCPD)

Launched in 2016, the MCPD is a digital case management system that links all FCSCs with central CFPS. Each child protection case is entered with unique identifiers, case notes, actions taken, and status updates. Authorised users can access and update cases, and supervisors can oversee workloads and trends. The MCPD has improved information flow significantly -- if a family moves between islands, the new FCSC can see the case history and ensure continuity of care.[^24] However, the system's utility is constrained by the fact that data entry depends on overloaded workers who may not always update records promptly, and the database does not integrate seamlessly with other agency systems such as police crime databases or NSPA's SPIS.

## 3.4 Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs) -- Atoll Level

### Network and coverage

FCSCs are the frontline social service delivery points across the Maldives, with one centre established in each of the 20 administrative atolls. They are managed under the overall direction of CFPS in Male but staffed locally and expected to serve all inhabited islands within their atoll's jurisdiction.[^25] The Decentralisation Act 2010 restructured governance by replacing atoll chiefs and island chiefs with elected atoll and island councillors, which had the effect of gradually placing some CFPS functions under local council authority.[^26]

### Staffing and capacity

Each FCSC is staffed by a small team of social workers and support staff -- typically ranging from two to five social workers depending on the atoll's population. These workers are generalists who handle child protection cases, domestic violence reports, elderly welfare, disability services, and family disputes. The workers who initially staffed FCSCs were school leavers who completed a one-year Advanced Certificate course, followed by deployment to islands as social work managers.[^27] Over time, the educational pipeline has improved with the availability of the four-year Social Work degree at MNU, but recruitment and retention remain difficult because many graduates prefer to work in Male rather than accept postings to remote atolls.

### Operational challenges

The geographic reality of FCSCs is the defining constraint on their effectiveness. An FCSC located in an atoll capital may be responsible for serving 10 to 15 inhabited islands, some accessible only by boat with journeys of several hours in favourable conditions. During rough weather seasons, travel between islands can be impossible for days or weeks, meaning that urgent child protection cases on outer islands may go unattended.[^28]

Rogers, Ali and Naeem (2025) identify notable differences between rural atoll FCSCs and urban areas. Urban regions, especially Male, have greater access to services due to better resource allocation and higher awareness. Rural and isolated islands face challenges in accessing services largely due to geographical isolation and resource limitations.[^29] The result is a two-tier system where families in Male have access to specialist teams, inter-agency coordination, and rapid court processes, while families on remote islands depend on one or two overworked generalist social workers who may also be embedded in the local social fabric in ways that compromise professional independence.

### Community Social Groups and IBAMA

To extend the reach of FCSCs into communities, the government established Community Social Groups (CSGs) in 2010, revisiting the initiative in 2016. These groups were supported by UNICEF and provided a community-led approach to addressing social problems and facilitating timely access to services. The CSGs were later revived under the IBAMA project ("you and I together"), developed by MoGFSS and UNICEF. IBAMA provides social protection and statutory services at the island level with an official mandate managed through the LGA via atoll and island councils.[^30] When functioning well, IBAMA groups provide a wraparound service -- school counsellors, health workers, and community representatives coming together to develop multi-sectoral support plans for vulnerable families. However, the quality and activity levels of these groups vary enormously across islands.

## 3.5 National Drug Agency (NDA)

### Establishment and evolution

The institutional response to drug use in the Maldives has evolved through several iterations. Drug trafficking and use emerged as a significant social problem from the mid-1970s, linked to increased tourism and international connectivity. The legislative response began with Law No. 17/77, and the Narcotics Control Board was established in 1997.[^31] The National Drug Agency (NDA) was later established as the primary body responsible for demand reduction, treatment, and rehabilitation services for substance use disorders.

### Scale of the problem

The drug problem in the Maldives is severe and disproportionately affects youth. UNODC reporting found that opiates (mainly heroin) and cannabinoids (mainly hashish) are the most frequently used drugs, with about 8 per cent of users reporting injection and half starting before age 17.[^32] Drug-related offences showed a 200 per cent increase over the period documented by the Ministry of Home Affairs, primarily affecting males aged 16-30.[^33] In 2013, the US Department of State reported that 47 per cent of prisoners were drug offenders, reflecting the scale of the crisis.[^34]

### Treatment and rehabilitation

NDA oversees both compulsory and voluntary drug treatment and rehabilitation systems. A World Bank-supported evaluation (2010) assessed the performance of these systems and found significant deficiencies: a lack of clinical staff involvement in policy formation, inadequate professional monitoring and training, and ethical issues in service delivery.[^35] Research by Ageel (2006) identified tensions between the formal policies of the national narcotics control system and actual clinical practice, with rehabilitation services constrained by legal frameworks that prioritised criminalisation over public health approaches.[^36]

The NDA/WHO Situational Analysis of Drugs (2021) provided the most comprehensive recent assessment of the drug landscape, drawing on data from police, customs, courts, correctional services, and health agencies to inform intervention strategies.[^37] However, services remain predominantly concentrated in the Male region, with limited treatment and rehabilitation capacity available in atolls.

### Intersection with child and family protection

Drug use is deeply intertwined with child protection and domestic violence systems. Case workers interviewed for vulnerability mapping research described marital rape by intoxicated partners occurring in front of children, and substance abuse as a factor in many of the most severe child protection cases.[^38] The NDA's focus on individual treatment often operates in a silo from CFPS's family-level interventions, creating coordination gaps when a parent's substance use is the primary driver of harm to children. The Strategic Action Plan 2019-2023 and the Maldives Police Service Strategic Plan 2024-2028 both identify drug trafficking and its downstream social effects as priority areas, but integrated service delivery models linking NDA, CFPS, and police remain underdeveloped.[^39]

## 3.6 Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM)

### Legal basis and independence

The Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM) was established as an independent statutory body with a constitutional mandate to promote and protect human rights. HRCM is empowered to investigate complaints, conduct inspections of detention facilities and care institutions, and make recommendations to government agencies. It reports publicly and submits shadow reports to UN treaty bodies.[^40]

### Role in social policy oversight

HRCM plays a significant oversight role in the social policy domain through several mechanisms:

- **Monitoring child protection**: HRCM and the Children's Ombudsperson's Office monitor child protection cases closely, requesting updates from social workers on individual cases and conducting child rights audits across atolls.[^41] While this oversight serves an important accountability function, Rogers, Ali and Naeem (2025) note that the ambitious objectives imposed by these organisations prove challenging to fulfil given staff constraints, with workers already overloaded with casework and "consequently constantly afraid of being accused of negligence."[^42]
- **Shadow reporting**: HRCM has submitted shadow reports to the CEDAW Committee (2021), the CRPD Committee (2023), and the CRC Committee, covering issues including gender-based violence, domestic violence, human trafficking, women with disabilities, sexual and reproductive health rights, and the effectiveness of the Gender Equality Act.[^43]
- **Baseline human rights surveys**: HRCM, in partnership with UNDP and FPA, has conducted the "Rights Side of Life" baseline human rights survey series (2005, 2011, 2019) examining public attitudes, awareness, and perceptions on human rights issues across regions and demographics.[^44]
- **Disability rights assessment**: HRCM conducted a baseline assessment of activities addressing the rights of persons with disabilities (2010) in partnership with UNDP, and submitted a 57-page CRPD shadow report (2023) identifying gaps between legislative commitments and actual implementation.[^45]

### Operational limitations

HRCM's effectiveness is constrained by its limited enforcement powers -- it can recommend but not compel action. Its monitoring activities, while valuable, add to the workload of already overstretched social service workers. The Commission's political independence has also been a subject of international concern, with successive US State Department human rights reports noting periods of judicial interference and restrictions on institutional independence.[^46]

## 3.7 Children's Ombudsperson

### Establishment

The office of the Children's Ombudsperson was established under the Child Rights Protection Act (CRPA) 2019, filling a gap identified over multiple CRC reporting cycles. The Ombudsperson operates as an independent office with a mandate to monitor, investigate, and advocate for children's rights.[^47]

### Functions and powers

The Children's Ombudsperson:

- Investigates complaints related to violations of children's rights
- Monitors government agencies' compliance with the CRPA
- Makes recommendations to government agencies and reports to the People's Majlis (Parliament)
- Conducts child rights audits and assessments across atolls
- Convenes inter-agency forums, including the 2022 Child Rights Symposium focused on promoting inter-agency coordination for child protection[^48]

### Operational reality

The Ombudsperson's office represents an important institutional innovation, but its effectiveness depends on the capacity of the agencies it oversees to respond to its recommendations. As Rogers, Ali and Naeem observe, the Ombudsperson and HRCM together create a significant oversight burden on CFPS workers who are already managing caseloads exceeding safe levels. The risk is that oversight drives defensive practice -- workers prioritising documentation and compliance over meaningful engagement with families -- rather than genuine improvement in child outcomes.[^49] The Ombudsperson's geographic reach is also constrained: conducting meaningful audits across 20 atolls and nearly 200 inhabited islands requires resources and travel capacity that a small office in Male may struggle to sustain.

## 3.8 Maldives Police Service -- Family and Child Protection Department

### Institutional structure

The Maldives Police Service (MPS) operates a dedicated **Family and Child Protection Department** (also referred to as the Family and Child Protection Wing, FCPW) responsible for investigating cases of domestic violence, child abuse, sexual offences against children, and related crimes. Under the MPS Strategic Plan 2024-2028, domestic violence is identified as a strategic priority alongside organised crime, drug trafficking, human trafficking, cybercrime, and terrorism.[^50]

### Operational role in case management

When a domestic violence or child abuse case is reported, the FCPW is typically the first formal state responder. Under the Domestic Violence Prevention Act (DVPA), police have obligations to attend, document incidents, and provide immediate safety measures. Under the CRPA, both CFPS and police can apply for Interim Assessment Orders and Protection Orders from the court.[^51] The MPS and MoSFD have an MoU for information sharing on GBV and child cases, and a Case Management Coordination Committee convenes monthly to reconcile data between police, FPA, and CFPS on reported cases and outcomes.[^52]

The FCPW maintains a database that tracks offenders, tagging individuals with any history of DV or child abuse complaints. When a new complaint is received, police can immediately see whether the accused has prior allegations, informing how vigorously the case is pursued.[^53]

### Challenges in practice

Despite these formal structures, the research evidence documents significant gaps between the FCPW's mandate and its operational practice:

- **DV as a "personal issue"**: Case workers and victims interviewed for vulnerability mapping research described police treating domestic violence as a personal matter between husband and wife, encouraging informal reconciliation rather than formal intervention. One respondent described police attending but telling the couple to "smooth it over."[^54]
- **Slow response times**: Women reported waiting a month or more for police to attend after filing a report. In one documented case, an eight-month delay in securing evidence from a suspect's phone allowed threats to continue until the victim withdrew the case: "If police had acted earlier, I wouldn't have withdrawn."[^55]
- **Breach of confidentiality**: In one extreme case documented in the research, police disclosed to an abuser the island to which his wife had fled, enabling him to follow and assault her there.[^56]
- **Information-sharing failures**: Protection order information was not always shared with Corrections, meaning that when an abuser was jailed, the prison did not know to monitor for continued harassment from detention.[^57]

These failures are not universal -- some respondents described prompt police response, proper documentation of injuries, and effective support in obtaining protection orders.[^58] The inconsistency is the problem: outcomes depend on individual officers, station capacity, and sometimes on the social standing of the parties involved.

## 3.9 Local Government Authority and Decentralised Service Delivery

### The Decentralisation Act and its evolution

The Decentralisation Act (Law No. 7/2010) fundamentally restructured governance by creating elected local councils at both atoll and island levels, replacing the appointed atoll and island chiefs. Three to four atolls were initially grouped into seven administrative provinces. The Act assigned local councils significant responsibilities, including coordination of social services at the island level.[^59] Subsequent amendments, particularly in 2019, expanded local council responsibilities further, including social protection coordination.

### The Local Government Authority (LGA)

The LGA oversees the functioning of island and atoll councils and provides administrative support, capacity building, and policy coordination. In the social policy domain, the LGA's significance lies in its role as the institutional bridge between central government ministries in Male and island-level service delivery.

### Impact on social service delivery

Decentralisation has had mixed effects on social policy implementation:

**Benefits**: Over time, the remoteness of islands has created subcultures unique to each island. Decentralisation, in principle, allows local councils to understand and respond to island-specific dynamics that central authorities in Male cannot easily grasp. This is particularly important for child protection, where, as Rogers, Ali and Naeem note, "complex politics permeates even in the provision of child protection activities" and the island dynamic specific to each island must be understood to properly manage cases.[^60]

**Challenges**: Decentralisation has created confusion about where authority lies for social protection functions. Some island councils have established proactive social committees, while others wait for national direction. The Decentralisation Act delegated responsibilities without always ensuring corresponding resources, skills, or institutional support at the local level. The result is significant variation in service quality across islands depending on the capacity and initiative of the local council.[^61]

IBAMA groups and Community Social Groups were designed to operate under the LGA framework, providing a structured mechanism for community-level social protection. Where they function effectively, they create a multi-sectoral support system at island level. Where council capacity is weak, they exist on paper but deliver little.[^62]

## 3.10 Council for the Protection of the Rights of the Child

### Legal basis

The Child Rights Protection Act (CRPA) 2019 mandated the creation of a **National Council for the Protection of the Rights of the Child (NCPRC)**. The Council was reconstituted in early 2020 and consists of senior officials from relevant ministries (Gender/Social Development, Education, Health, Youth), civil society representatives, and is appointed by the President.[^63]

### Functions

The NCPRC serves as a high-level coordination body designed to break inter-agency silos in child protection. Its functions include:

- Advising the government on child rights policy coordination
- Ensuring that when CFPS identifies an abused child, other agencies (Education, Health) fast-track necessary support, such as school transfers or mental health services
- Monitoring the overall implementation of the CRPA across government[^64]

At the atoll level, Child Protection Committees (CPCs) operate under the NCPRC umbrella. A CPC, often chaired by the atoll council or FCSC head, meets to coordinate services for vulnerable children locally, discussing anonymised cases and identifying gaps such as the absence of a trained counsellor in the atoll.[^65]

### Historical context

The NCPRC is the successor to the ministerial-level National Council for Children's Rights established after the Maldives ratified the CRC in 1991. That earlier body was chaired by the Minister of Home Affairs and made major decisions regarding children, including overseeing CRC implementation. Its successor, the NCPRC, was designed to be more operational and multi-sectoral rather than purely ministerial.[^66]

### Effectiveness

The Council's effectiveness is difficult to assess independently. It provides a necessary platform for cross-ministry discussion, but like many coordination bodies in the Maldives, its impact depends on the political will of its members and the frequency with which it actually convenes. The gap between the Council's mandate to ensure integrated child protection and the operational reality of fragmented service delivery remains significant. The Presidential Commission on Child Rights Violations, established in February 2020 in response to public concern over tragic child abuse cases, conducted joint audits of cases involving CFPS, the Police FCPW, and HRCM, highlighting coordination lapses that the NCPRC should theoretically have prevented.[^67]

## 3.11 Women's Development Committees (WDCs)

### Structure and mandate

Women's Development Committees (WDCs) are established at the island level across the Maldives to promote women's participation in local governance and community development. An assessment by Transparency Maldives (2015) examined the role, effectiveness, and challenges of WDCs in promoting women's participation, finding a mixed picture.[^68]

### Role in social protection

WDCs serve several functions in the social policy landscape:

- **Community welfare**: WDCs organise food packages, school supplies, and other material support for vulnerable families on their islands. In the absence of formal social protection mechanisms reaching every household, WDCs serve as an informal safety net, leveraging community solidarity.[^69]
- **Awareness-raising**: WDCs conduct awareness programmes on gender equality, domestic violence prevention, and children's rights.
- **Local coordination**: WDCs participate in community-level coordination, including through IBAMA groups, linking families to formal services.

### Challenges

The IFES qualitative assessment (2015) found that women in the Maldives are societally positioned primarily in the domestic sphere, with the double burden of work, household, and childcare limiting their participation in community activities. WDC members themselves often face these constraints, limiting the time and energy they can devote to committee work.[^70] WDC budgets are minimal, depending on island council allocations that vary widely. On some islands, the WDC is an active and influential institution; on others, it exists primarily on paper.

The Transparency Maldives assessment noted that WDCs' capacity to influence local decision-making remained limited despite their formal existence. Their recommendations to island councils are advisory rather than binding, and in conservative communities, women's committee work may face resistance from traditional power structures.[^71]

## 3.12 Community Social Groups (CSGs) and IBAMA

### Origins and design

Community Social Groups (CSGs) were initiated in 2010 by the Ministry of Health and Family and revisited in 2016. Supported by UNICEF, the CSGs provided a community-led approach to addressing social problems and facilitating timely access to services at the island level.[^72] The groups were designed to bring together community members -- teachers, health workers, religious leaders, council members -- to identify and respond to vulnerable individuals and families before problems escalated to the point of requiring formal statutory intervention.

### The IBAMA project

The CSG concept was revived and formalised under the IBAMA project ("you and I together" in Dhivehi), developed jointly by MoGFSS and UNICEF. IBAMA is a multi-sectoral community-based system for preventing and responding to violence against children, the elderly, and other vulnerable populations. It has an official mandate managed through the LGA via atoll and island councils, giving it legal standing under the Decentralisation Act and the CRPA 2019, which empowers community groups by granting them legal recognition.[^73]

### Operational model

When functioning as intended, an IBAMA group operates as follows: a concern about a child or vulnerable person is identified by a community member (neighbour, teacher, health worker). The IBAMA group convenes, assesses the situation, and either provides community-level support (referral to the WDC for material assistance, linking to health services) or refers the case to the FCSC for formal investigation. For families under supervision orders, IBAMA provides the wraparound service -- school counsellors and community social workers monitoring the family and implementing a multi-sectoral support plan.[^74]

### Limitations

The quality and activity levels of IBAMA groups vary enormously across islands. Islands with an active council, a capable FCSC social worker, and engaged community members can make the model work well. Islands where councils are passive, social workers are absent, or community members are reluctant to intervene see IBAMA groups that exist in name only. The small-island dynamic also cuts both ways: close-knit communities can provide genuine support, but they can also foster silence about abuse when the perpetrator is a powerful or well-connected community member, or when reporting would disrupt established social hierarchies.[^75]

## 3.13 Key International Partners: UNICEF, UNDP, UNFPA, WHO, World Bank, ADB

### UNICEF

UNICEF has been the single most influential international partner in the development of child protection systems in the Maldives. Following the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, UNICEF expanded its programmes and created a focused child protection programme, building on its former combined education and child protection programme. UNICEF provided financial and technical support for the development of policies, procedures, and the drafting of the Child Rights Protection Law to replace the limited 9/91 legislation. UNICEF also supported the drafting of the Juvenile Justice Act (enacted 2019), the establishment of the 1412 child helpline, the development of the MCPD case management database, the IBAMA community-based protection system, and the establishment of CSGs.[^76]

UNICEF's Situation Analysis of Children and Adolescents (most recently by Haarr, 2025) provides the most comprehensive periodic assessment of child outcomes, covering demographics, mortality trends, immunisation, nutrition, early childhood development, adolescent health, education access, violence against children, and institutional capacity gaps.[^77] UNICEF Maldives has also co-produced statistical analyses using Census 2014 and Census 2022 data to inform child-focused policymaking.[^78]

The scale of UNICEF's influence reflects a structural reality: NGOs and civil society are largely absent in the provision of child protection services in the Maldives, making child protection activities dominated by the state with a very active role from UNICEF. As Rogers, Ali and Naeem observe, the system has absorbed the model of England's child protection framework (a needs-based system) through UNICEF-facilitated technical support, despite the UN's own rights-based approach.[^79]

### UNDP

UNDP's engagement in social policy centres on governance strengthening, human rights, and vulnerability reduction. Key contributions include:

- The Maldives Human Development Report 2014, focusing on vulnerability and inequality

## 3.14 Civil Society Organisations in Social Policy

### Structural characteristics

Civil society in the Maldives is relatively underdeveloped compared to the state's role in social service delivery. Rogers, Ali and Naeem (2025) observe that "NGOs and civil society are largely absent in the provision of services; hence child protection activities are dominated by the state."[^86] This creates a landscape where a small number of organisations play outsized roles in filling specific gaps that the state has not yet addressed.

### Key organisations

**Advocating for the Rights of Children (ARC)**: An NGO focused on child protection advocacy, ARC works on child safeguarding policy development and training. Rogers, Ali and Naeem suggest that the introduction of a family support mechanism through partnership with an NGO such as ARC could greatly benefit CFPS, providing preventive family support that the statutory system currently lacks capacity to deliver.[^87]

**Family Legal Clinic (FLC)**: Provides free legal advice and representation in family law matters, including divorce, custody, maintenance, and domestic violence protection orders. FLC is Male-based but conducts remote consultations by phone. It is the primary source of free family law counsel for vulnerable women and its capacity constraints mean that if it were to scale back, the state lacks an immediate alternative.[^88]

**Hope for Women**: Operates safe house/shelter services for women fleeing domestic violence. Shelter capacity is limited to a small number of women at a time, and services are heavily Male-centric. Plans for regional shelters are under discussion.[^89]

**Society for Health Education (SHE)**: Works on sexual and reproductive health rights, submitting UPR joint submissions highlighting barriers to SRH services access and noting that an estimated 52 per cent of youth are deprived of SRH information.[^90]

**Transparency Maldives**: While primarily an anti-corruption organisation, Transparency Maldives has produced significant research relevant to social policy, including the Assessment of Women's Development Committees (2015), the PRIME (Promoting Resilience in Maldives) programme for at-risk youth, and research on corruption vulnerabilities in the health sector.[^91]

**Maldivian Red Crescent**: Operates nine branches nationwide providing humanitarian services including emergency preparedness, health and social care, social inclusion programmes for vulnerable groups (including foreign migrants), and youth development. The Red Crescent's geographic reach across atolls gives it a presence that most other CSOs lack.[^92]

### Supplementary role and limitations

Civil society contributions are critical but structurally fragile. NGOs such as FLC and Hope for Women depend on donor funding that fluctuates year to year. The government provides small grants to some organisations, but integration of civil society contributions into a sustainable service delivery model remains incomplete. The Strategic Action Plan 2019-2023 called for strengthening public-private partnerships in social service delivery, but implementation has been uneven.[^93]

On remote islands, NGO presence is minimal. Atoll communities often rely on their own initiative through WDCs or ad hoc charity. The cultural norm of community helping -- known as "gah'therikan" -- remains strong, with islanders collectively raising funds through Facebook groups or island Viber groups for urgent cases. This communal safety net, while informal, is an important supplement to formal systems for families who might otherwise fall through gaps.[^94]

### Zakat and Islamic charitable mechanisms

The Maldives Zakat House, operating under the Ministry of Islamic Affairs, collects and distributes zakat (Islamic obligatory charity) to eligible categories as prescribed by Islamic law. Zakat distributions are generally modest -- one media report noted that each qualifying person received approximately MVR 1,500 from Fitr zakat in 2023, sufficient for roughly one month's groceries but not a sustained intervention.[^95] In late 2024, Zakat House services were being integrated into the OneGov platform, enabling cross-referencing with NSPA's database and potentially improving targeting. The government has recognised zakat as a complement to formal social protection, not a substitute, but in remote communities where state programmes do not fully reach, zakat and community charity may be the only available support for families in crisis.[^96]

## Cross-cutting analysis: Institutional fragmentation and coordination gaps

### The coordination problem

The institutional architecture described in this chapter reveals a system that is comprehensive in scope but fragmented in execution. A family facing multiple challenges -- for example, a single mother who is a domestic violence survivor with a disabled child -- must navigate separate applications for each form of assistance: police/FPA for protection, NSPA for the single parent allowance, NSPA again for the disability allowance, Zakat House for emergency support, and counselling from MoSFD. There is no single case manager for the whole family. Frontline workers do their best to refer to each other, but integration relies on informal coordination rather than a unified service portal.[^97]

### Geographic inequality

The availability and quality of services varies dramatically by location. A woman in Male has access to CFPS specialist teams, the Family Court, FLC's legal services, Hope for Women's shelter, and multiple police stations with FCPW officers. A woman on a remote island may have access to a single overworked FCSC social worker, a Magistrate Court without specialised family law training, and police officers who treat domestic violence as a personal matter. Shelters are entirely Male-centric, and while plans for regional shelters exist, none had been established at the time of writing.[^98]

### Capacity versus mandate

Laws enacted since 2012 -- the DVPA, the CRPA, the Gender Equality Act, the Juvenile Justice Act -- have set ambitious duties for implementing agencies. Mandatory response to all DV reports, counselling for all perpetrators, court-ordered assessments within tight timescales, caseloads of over 160 per social worker -- these mandates were designed for a system with significantly more human resources than currently exist. The result is a gap between what the law requires and what the system can deliver, with overloaded workers triaging rather than providing comprehensive support.[^99]

### Data fragmentation

While data systems exist (MCPD, FPA's GBV database, police crime databases, NSPA's SPIS), interoperability remains incomplete. It is still challenging to track a family's holistic progress across sectors. Monitoring tends to be programme-specific rather than family-centric -- NSPA monitors allowance uptake, FPA monitors DV case resolution, CFPS monitors child protection cases -- lacking a unified outcomes framework for family wellbeing. This hinders strategic targeting and early identification of multi-problem families who might need intensive support.[^100]

### The trajectory

Despite these challenges, the trajectory is broadly positive. The system demonstrated resilience during COVID-19, when agencies coordinated to maintain essential services including 24/7 hotlines, shelter access, and emergency cash transfers. Public awareness of available help is rising, with more people reporting DV and seeking allowances than a decade ago, indicating growing trust that support exists.[^101] The government's stated ambition to move toward integrated social service delivery -- piloting one-stop centres, developing a unified social registry, onboarding services to OneGov -- represents a recognition that the current fragmented architecture, while functional, falls short of what vulnerable families need. The question is whether institutional reform can keep pace with the legislative ambition that has characterised the past decade of Maldivian social policy.
