# Summary: Child Protection and Child Rights

This summary distils the key points from the full encyclopedia chapter into a slide-based format. Each module covers a major theme, with slides on the left and explanatory notes on the right. It is designed for readers who want a structured overview without reading the entire chapter.

---

## Module 1: History


---

## Slide 1: Pre-1991 – No Formal System

- No child protection legislation existed before 1991
- Child welfare governed by extended family networks, Islamic family law (*fiqh*), and island chief (*kadheeb*) adjudication
- The state had no institutional role in protecting children from harm within families
- Community norms suppressed disclosure: family honour, *satr* (concealment of faults), social proximity of small island communities
- **Islaahiya reform facility (1979):** First institutional care – housed delinquent youth and children needing care together without differentiation between offenders and victims
- No concept of mandatory reporting, no definitions of abuse, no trained social workers, no referral pathways

---

## Slide 2: 1991-2006 – Symbolic Foundations

**CRC ratification (11 February 1991)**
- Reservations on Article 14(1) (freedom of religion) and Article 21 (adoption – not permitted under Islamic law as practised in the Maldives)
- The Article 21 reservation has lasting operational consequences: adoption cannot be used as a permanent care solution

**Law 9/91 – First child protection statute**
- Declaratory only: no definitions of abuse, no mandatory reporting, no enforcement mechanisms, no penalties
- Remained the sole child protection law for **28 years** (until CRPA 2019)

**Unit for the Rights of Children (URC), est. 1992**
- Initially in Ministry of Home Affairs (security framing), moved to Ministry of Youth, Women and Social Welfare in 1993
- Operated 1992-2006 with minimal staff, no case management, no reach beyond Male', no legal mandate to investigate or intervene
- Functioned as CRC reporting secretariat, not a service provider

---

## Slide 3: 2004-2008 – UNICEF Expansion and First Social Workers

**2004 Indian Ocean tsunami:** 82 dead, ~21,000 displaced. Catalysed major UNICEF expansion – brought international child protection models, standards, and funding into the Maldives at a scale not previously seen.

**2006 Child Protection Procedure Manual**
- Modelled on England's "Working Together to Safeguard Children"
- First attempt to systematise child protection practice
- Key areas of misfit with Maldivian context:
  - English model assumes nuclear family structure; Maldives has extended family networks with children routinely moving between households
  - Assumes inter-agency information-sharing architecture; Maldives had none
  - Assumes statutory authority for social workers; Maldives had none
  - Assumes forensic police capacity; Maldives had none outside Male'

**First social workers trained (2006):** One-year Advanced Certificate via University of Kelaniya, Sri Lanka. Advanced Diploma followed, then four-year BSW – establishing the first formal professional pathway.

---

## Slide 4: 2008-2013 – Democratic Transition, 1412 Helpline, and the 2009 Case

**2008 democratic transition:** New Constitution, institutional restructuring. Child protection moved under Ministry of Health and Family.

**Child Helpline 1412 established (2008):** First dedicated, toll-free, 24-hour reporting channel. Partnership with Child Helpline International.

**2009 baby death case – system failure:**
- Infant killed by mother's boyfriend (violent shaking)
- Mother known to drug services for 7 years
- No risk assessment conducted for the child
- Drug services and child protection did not communicate
- No protective intervention despite years of contact
- Post-incident review recommended: clinical supervision for social workers, inter-agency coordination protocols, systematic risk assessment
- Pattern mirrors UK serious case reviews (Victoria Climbie, Peter Connelly) – but without institutional capacity for systematic reform response

---

## Slide 5: 2010-2018 – FCSCs, Decentralisation, and Digital Case Management

**Decentralisation Act (2010):** Transferred responsibilities to local councils, creating tension between centralised child protection standards and decentralised governance.

**Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS) established centrally**
- Network of **20 Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs)** across atolls
- First physical child protection presence outside Male'
- Each FCSC: typically 2-5 social workers covering ~10 inhabited islands
- Ministry operational on 20 of 199 inhabited islands – leaving vast majority without direct access

**Key milestones:**

| Year | Development |
|---|---|
| 2010 | Community Social Groups (CSGs) initiated – volunteer-based island coverage |
| 2015 | Child protection focal points placed in schools |
| 2016 | Maldives Child Protection Database (MCPD) launched – first digital case management connecting all FCSCs |
| 2018 | National Child Helpline Policy finalised |

---

## Slide 6: 2019 – Child Rights Protection Act

**CRPA (Act 19/2019)** – enacted 14 November 2019, replacing Law 9/91 after 28 years:
- Comprehensive definitions of abuse (physical, sexual, emotional, neglect)
- Mandatory reporting obligations (all persons; enhanced for specified professionals)
- Three categories of court orders: Interim Assessment (Art. 80), Protection (Art. 90), Supervision (Art. 93)
- 5-day statutory timeline for court order applications after emergency removal
- Children's Ombudsperson established as independent oversight body
- Children's Rights Council created
- Minimum marriage age set at 18 (though Family Act inconsistency unresolved)
- Child labour prohibited below age 16

**Juvenile Justice Act (Act 18/2019)** – enacted same day:
- Age of criminal responsibility raised from 10 to 15
- Death penalty for juveniles prohibited (5 juveniles were sentenced to death 2013-2015)
- Rehabilitation emphasis mandated
- Judicial flogging of juveniles NOT abolished

---

| Year | Development |
|---|---|
| 2020 | Education Act – compulsory education until 16, corporal punishment banned in schools |
| 2020 | Alternative Care Regulation (2020-R-69) and Reintegration Regulation (2020/R-30) issued |
| 2021 | Combined 6th/7th CRC Periodic Report submitted |

**Current system capacity:**
- 20 FCSCs operational across all atolls
- Average social worker caseload: 70-80 cases (international standard: 25-30)
- 2,936 cases received by MoSFD in 2023, of which 1,278 (43.5%) were violence against children
- 187 children in alternative care (91% in residential institutions)
- 18 registered foster carers caring for 16 children

## Slide 8: Recurring Structural Issues

Five patterns that recur throughout the system's history and continue to shape current operations:

1. **Long gaps between legislation and implementation.** Law 9/91 existed for 28 years without meaningful enforcement. The CRPA's implementation remains incomplete 7 years after enactment.

2. **Imported models without adaptation.** The 2006 Procedure Manual (England), the BSW curriculum (Sri Lanka), and the CRPA framework (England's Children Act via UNICEF) all assumed institutional infrastructure that didn't exist. The system has "absorbed the system of England which is a needs-based system" through UNICEF-facilitated technical support, despite the UN's own rights-based approach (Rogers, Ali & Naeem 2025).

3. **Crises as reform catalysts.** The tsunami (2004), the baby death case (2009), high-profile CSA cases – each drove reform. Proactive reform without a triggering crisis has been rare.

4. **The Male'-atoll gap.** Institutional capacity concentrates in Male' while the majority of the population (and the majority of vulnerability) is dispersed across atolls.

5. **Institutional instability.** Child protection has moved between ministries repeatedly. The parent ministry has been renamed and restructured multiple times. Each reorganisation disrupts relationships, data systems, and institutional memory.

## Module 2: Legal Framework


---

## Slide 1: Constitutional Provisions

**Article 35 of the 2008 Constitution:**
- (a) Every child has the right to protection from abuse and maltreatment, including all forms of physical and mental violence
- (b) Right to education, social security, and care and assistance from the state
- (c) Right to a name and nationality from birth
- State obligation to provide special protection to children without families

**Article 10:** No law contrary to any tenet of Islam shall be enacted. Creates operational tensions in three areas: the zina/sexual abuse boundary for adolescent victims, the marriage age threshold, and corporal punishment.

**Article 36:** Rights of children including right to non-discrimination and right to receive education. Reinforces Article 35 protections.

---

## Slide 2: Child Rights Protection Act 2019 – Scope and Definitions

**Act 19/2019**, enacted 14 November 2019. Replaced Law 9/91 after 28 years. Developed over a decade with UNICEF technical support, drawing on England's Children Act framework and Asia-Pacific comparative legislation.

**Age definition (Article 4):** A child is any person who has not attained age 18. Supersedes older laws with varying age thresholds. Applies regardless of puberty status, marital status, or employment.

**Abuse definitions (Article 133):**

| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| Physical violence | Lacerations, bruises, cuts, scars, fractures, bone damage, joint dislocation, bleeding, blood vessel rupture, burns, blisters, changes in physiological functioning or consciousness, damage to hair or teeth |
| Sexual abuse | Any sexual act toward, against, or exhibited to a child, whether by a family member or otherwise |
| Exploitation | All inhumane acts with negative impact on physical/mental wellbeing and growth, for any kind of benefit |
| Negligence | Acts or omissions by parents/guardians/caregivers that could or would cause physical or mental harm |

**Best interests principle (Article 6):** Primary consideration in all decisions affecting children – applies to courts, government agencies, institutions, and private entities.

**Children's participation (Article 7):** Views of children must be considered in all decisions affecting them, commensurate with age and maturity.

---

## Slide 3: CRPA – Mandatory Reporting and Court Orders

**Mandatory reporting:** All persons have a duty to report known or suspected child maltreatment. Enhanced obligations for specified professionals (teachers, health workers, social workers, police).

**Three categories of court orders:**

| Order | Article | Function | Use Case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Interim Assessment Order | Art. 80 | Temporary placement during assessment | Emergency removal – child in immediate danger |
| Protection Order | Art. 90 | Conditions on care, may include removal | Longer-term intervention after assessment |
| Supervision Order | Art. 93 | Child remains in family under CFPS monitoring | Least intrusive – monitoring without removal |

**5-day statutory timeline:** Application for court order must be filed within 5 days of taking a child into state care. Safeguard against indefinite informal removal without judicial oversight.

**Good faith protection:** Staff acting in good faith under the CRPA are protected from liability when making difficult decisions, provided procedures are followed.

---

## Slide 4: CRPA – Institutional Framework

**Institutions established or mandated by the CRPA:**

| Institution | Function |
|---|---|
| Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS) | Centralised case management; oversees all 20 FCSCs |
| Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs) | Atoll-level service delivery |
| Children's Ombudsperson | Independent oversight; complaint investigation; system monitoring |
| National Council for Child Rights Protection | Policy coordination; multi-agency oversight |
| IBAMA community groups | Island-level volunteer child protection networks |

**Additional provisions:**
- Minimum marriage age set at 18 (Family Act inconsistency unresolved – see Slide 7)
- Child labour prohibited below age 16 (aligned with Education Act 2020 compulsory education requirement)
- Prohibition on child participation in hostilities or armed conflict
- Framework for community-based child protection through IBAMA volunteer networks

---

## Slide 5: Juvenile Justice Act 2019

**Act 18/2019**, enacted same day as CRPA (14 November 2019).

| Provision | Pre-JJ Act | Post-JJ Act |
|---|---|---|
| Age of criminal responsibility | 10 years | 15 years |
| Death penalty for juveniles | Permitted (5 sentenced 2013-2015) | Prohibited |
| Primary orientation | Punitive | Rehabilitation and reintegration |
| Separate detention | Not guaranteed | Required |
| Judicial flogging | Permitted | **Not abolished** |

**Rehabilitation outcomes (2010-2015):** 10 of 89 juveniles completed rehabilitation programme – 11.2% completion rate. The law's rehabilitation mandate outpaces the system's capacity to deliver.

**Flogging gap:** Children convicted of zina (extramarital sexual activity) can still be sentenced to judicial flogging. Approximately 85% of persons sentenced to flogging in the Maldives are women and girls. CRC Committee has repeatedly called for abolition.

## Slide 7: Unresolved Legal Inconsistencies

**1. Marriage age:**
- CRPA: 18 minimum
- Family Act Section 4(b): marriage below 18 permitted with court approval
- Islamic jurisprudence: puberty as threshold of adulthood, not chronological age
- Current child marriage rate: 2.2% (ADB 2025)
- Inconsistency flagged by both CRC Committee and CEDAW Committee; remains unresolved

**2. Zina and child sexual abuse:**
- CRPA defines sexual abuse but does not explicitly address the situation where a child above puberty who has been sexually abused is also potentially liable for zina under Islamic criminal law
- Child victims have been prosecuted for zina – re-victimisation within the system
- CRC Committee has called for explicit protection of child sexual abuse victims from zina prosecution
- Rogers, Ali & Naeem (2025): this is a "grey area" that leaves practitioners without clear guidance

**3. Corporal punishment:**
- International standards (CRC): prohibition of all corporal punishment in all settings
- Islamic hudud: corporal punishment for specific offences
- Maldivian law: banned in schools (Education Act 2020), not abolished in judicial or home settings
- Judicial flogging of children for zina remains legal

**4. Adoption:**
- CRC Article 21 reservation: adoption not permitted under Islamic law as practised
- Available permanent care options limited to foster care, kafalah, and institutional care
- No legal mechanism for permanent transfer of parental rights

## Slide 8: CRC Committee Recommendations

Recurring recommendations from the Committee on the Rights of the Child to the Maldives:

| Recommendation | Status |
|---|---|
| Resolve CRPA/Family Act marriage age inconsistency | Unresolved |
| Abolish judicial flogging of children | Not implemented |
| Protect child sexual abuse victims from zina prosecution | Not implemented |
| Raise age of criminal responsibility above 15 | Not implemented |
| Strengthen mandatory reporting implementation | In progress |
| Reduce social worker caseloads to international standards | Not implemented |
| Ensure periodic review for all children in alternative care | Partially implemented |
| Establish disaggregated data collection on violence against children | In progress (MCPD) |
| Withdraw CRC reservations (Art. 14, 21) | Not withdrawn |

These recommendations are public, referenced by development partners in programme design, and form the basis for shadow report assessments by HRCM and CSOs.

---

## Slide 9: Operational Guidance for Staff

**When handling cases, the legal framework provides:**

- **Authority:** CRPA gives legal standing to investigate, assess, and recommend interventions
- **Obligations:** Mandatory reporting, 5-day court order timeline, best interests assessments, documentation
- **Limits:** Cannot remove a child indefinitely without court order; must consider child's views; must follow procedural requirements
- **Protections:** Good faith actions under the CRPA are protected from liability

**When encountering the legal grey areas (zina/CSA boundary, marriage age, corporal punishment):**
- Document reasoning in the case file
- Consult supervisor before taking action
- Seek guidance from Children's Ombudsperson where appropriate
- Structural legal ambiguities cannot be resolved at the caseworker level – the task is to protect the child within the framework as it exists while flagging gaps through institutional channels

## Slide 2: MoSFD and CFPS

**MoSFD** carries 39 responsibilities across four domains. Name has changed with each government (MoGFSS under Solih, MoGF under Yameen, MoHF under Waheed). Each rename reflects portfolio shifts and institutional culture changes that affect child protection resource allocation.

**Budget trajectory:** MVR 180 million (2015) → MVR 420 million (2024), a 133% nominal increase. FCSC operational budget: MVR 38 million (2024). DJJ budget: MVR 22 million (2024).

**CFPS structure:** Centralised in Male' with specialised teams for assessment, investigation, legal liaison, and case coordination. Complex decisions (child removal, court applications, inter-agency coordination) funnel through Male' even when the child is on a remote outer island.

**Case management process (CFPS SOPs, 2021):**
1. Referral and intake
2. Initial assessment (24 hours urgent, 72 hours non-urgent)
3. Full assessment and investigation (joint with police where criminal)
4. Case planning
5. Intervention and service provision
6. Monitoring and review
7. Case closure

**MCPD (Maldives Child Protection Database):** Launched 2016, links all 24 FCSCs with unique case identifiers. Pilot digital case management deployed in selected FCSCs 2023, national rollout planned 2026.

## Slide 3: Caseload and Staffing Levels

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Child protection social workers | 61 |
| Child population | 128,208 |
| Average potential caseload per worker | ~160 |
| Average active caseload per worker | 70-80 |
| International recommended maximum | 25-30 |

**Documented consequences (UNICEF 2022; Rogers, Ali & Naeem 2025):**
- Reactive crisis response displaces preventive family support
- Assessments are often cursory; risk factors missed
- Burnout and turnover driven by emotional intensity, death threats from gang-connected families, limited supervision, modest remuneration
- Sustained follow-up and monitoring capacity severely limited
- Oversight bodies (HRCM, Children's Ombudsperson) impose monitoring objectives difficult to fulfil at current staffing, creating anxiety about negligence accusations and driving defensive documentation over genuine welfare improvement

## Slide 4: FCSCs – Atoll-Level Service Delivery

**24 FCSCs operational** (December 2024) – one per administrative atoll plus Addu City and Fuvahmulah.

**Functions:** Receives referrals from all sources; conducts initial assessments; manages cases across ALL social service domains (child protection, DV, elderly, disability, family disputes); coordinates with local police, schools, health facilities; feeds data into MCPD.

**Geographic constraint:** Each FCSC covers 10-15 inhabited islands, some accessible only by multi-hour boat journeys. During rough weather, travel between islands impossible for days or weeks. Ministry presence on 20 of 199 inhabited islands – children on the remaining 179 islands depend on informal community mechanisms, IBAMA volunteers, and remote reporting via 1412.

**Workforce:** Initially staffed by school leavers with one-year certificates (Kelaniya, Sri Lanka, 2006). Four-year BSW now available at MNU. Graduate supply has never matched demand across 20 atolls. Many graduates prefer Male' postings over remote atoll deployments.

---

## Slide 5: Department of Juvenile Justice

**Established 2020** under Ministry of Home Affairs. Budget: MVR 22 million (2024).

**Facilities:**

| Facility | Population | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Imaduddin School for Boys | Male juvenile offenders | Secure facility; education, vocational training, psychosocial support |
| Fiyavathi Rehabilitation Centre | Female juvenile offenders + girls needing protective care | Dual function raises rights concerns: non-offending vulnerable girls housed with offenders |
| National Reintegration Centre | Children exposed to extremist ideologies | Only 3 successful reintegrations to date |

**Diversion programme:** Hundreds of children diverted from prosecution (2020-2022) through counselling, family conferencing, community service.

**Juvenile Court (Male'):** Specialist court with child-friendly procedures – separate waiting areas, informal seating, public excluded, specialist-trained judges, child-appropriate interview rooms. Constraint: children from outer islands must travel to Male' for proceedings.

---

## Slide 6: Independent Oversight Bodies

**Children's Ombudsperson (est. 2021 under CRPA):**
- Receives and investigates complaints about children's rights violations
- Monitors CRPA compliance by government agencies
- Reports to People's Majlis
- Conducts child rights audits across atolls
- Convened 2022 Child Rights Symposium on inter-agency coordination

**Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM):**
- Constitutional body monitoring all human rights
- Conducts investigations, publishes annual reports
- Submits shadow reports to CRC, CEDAW, CRPD committees
- Monitors detention facilities including juvenile facilities

**Family Protection Authority (FPA):**
- Independent agency under DVPA 2012
- Manages DV cases – overlap with CP when children witness or experience DV
- 642 DV cases attended at peak (2016)
- Creates coordination requirements between FPA and CFPS

---

## Slide 7: Police and Prosecution

**MPS Family and Child Protection Wing (FCPW):**
- Investigates DV, child abuse, sexual offences against children
- Database tags individuals with DV/child abuse history for rapid checking
- MoU with MoSFD for information sharing
- Case Management Coordination Committee meets monthly

**Documented operational issues:**
- DV sometimes treated as personal matter; police encouraging informal reconciliation
- Response time delays: documented cases of weeks-to-months between report and attendance
- Evidence collection delays allowing threats to continue until victim withdraws
- Limited forensic capacity outside Male'

**Prosecutor General's Office:**
- Only 3% of DV cases reported to police reach prosecution
- Only 14% of violence against women cases referred by police reach prosecution
- Conviction rate for VAW cases at court: 16.7% (2016-2019)
- The "criminal justice funnel" means the vast majority of reported cases result in no legal consequence for the perpetrator

---

## Slide 8: Coordination Mechanisms and Their Limitations

**Existing coordination mechanisms:**
- Multi-Disciplinary Teams (MDTs) at atoll level – bringing together social workers, police, health, education
- Case Management Coordination Committee (monthly, MPS-MoSFD)
- Child Protection Committees at atoll level
- School-based child protection focal points (since 2015)

**Documented coordination failures (Rogers, Ali & Naeem 2025; UNICEF 2022):**
- No overarching coordination architecture – multiple parallel mechanisms without integration
- MDTs function inconsistently across atolls – depend on local relationships rather than systematic protocols
- Information sharing between agencies remains ad hoc despite MoUs
- The 2009 baby death case pattern (agencies in contact with family but not communicating with each other) has not been structurally resolved
- School focal points lack clear referral pathways to FCSCs
- No formal case conferencing protocols are consistently applied across all FCSCs
- Political interference in casework documented – local power dynamics influencing case outcomes, particularly where alleged perpetrators hold community authority

---

## Slide 9: IBAMA – Community-Based Child Protection

**IBAMA (community protection groups):**
- Volunteer networks at island level, established under CRPA framework
- Evolved from earlier Community Social Groups (CSGs, 2010)
- Intended function: early identification and referral of child protection concerns, community-level support, bridging gap between FCSCs and islands without social service presence

**Limitations:**
- Volunteers, not professionals – no case management authority
- Training quality and consistency varies significantly across islands
- Volunteer motivation difficult to sustain without compensation or formal recognition
- On some islands, IBAMA groups are inactive or exist on paper only
- Small island dynamics mean IBAMA members may be related to or socially connected to families involved in cases – compromising independence

**Coverage gap context:** IBAMA operates on selected islands; the remaining 179 islands without FCSC presence have no formal child protection mechanism of any kind.

Child protection vulnerability is not evenly distributed. Key geographic patterns:

## Slide 4: Mandatory Reporting Implementation

**Legal obligation:** All persons must report suspected child maltreatment (CRPA). Enhanced obligations for teachers, health workers, social workers, police.

**Implementation reality:**

| Indicator | Status |
|---|---|
| Professional awareness of reporting obligation | Variable – higher in Male', lower in atolls |
| Training on mandatory reporting | Provided to some professionals; not universal |
| Reporting rate | Low relative to estimated prevalence |
| Anonymous reporting mechanism | 1412 accepts anonymous reports |
| Consequences for failure to report | Legally prescribed but not enforced in practice |

**Barriers to reporting:**
- Small island social dynamics – reporter's identity easily inferred

- Satr norms – cultural expectation of concealing family faults

## Slide 2: What the Fulu (2006/07) Study Found

The Maldives Study on Women's Health and Life Experiences remains the most methodologically rigorous study on violence prevalence in the Maldives. WHO multi-country methodology; nationally representative sample.

**Findings directly relevant to child protection:**
- 12.2% of women aged 15-49 reported childhood sexual abuse (16.3% in Male')
- ~1 in 3 women experienced physical or sexual intimate partner violence
- Qualitative data documented community norms around honour ideology, satr, and the suppression of disclosure
- Children growing up in violent households – while not the study's primary focus – face direct and indirect exposure to violence
- The study established empirical evidence for patterns that were previously anecdotal or suspected

**Limitation:** The study is now nearly 20 years old. No comparable national prevalence study has been conducted since. The evidence base for CSA prevalence relies on a single study from 2006/07.

---

## Slide 3: What Rogers, Ali & Naeem (2025) Found

The first academic treatment of Maldivian child protection within an international comparative framework (Bristol University Press, Policy Press).

**Key findings:**
- The Maldivian CP system has "absorbed the system of England which is a needs-based system" through UNICEF-facilitated technical support – despite the UN's own rights-based approach
- The CRPA drafting process drew on England's Children Act framework and Asian-Pacific comparative legislation via Defence for Children International
- The system has both strengths (legal framework, MCPD database, FCSC network) and structural weaknesses (caseload crisis, coordination gaps, implementation lag)
- The Children's Ombudsperson creates a monitoring tension: ambitious oversight objectives that overstretched workers cannot fulfil, driving defensive documentation over genuine welfare improvement
- The zina/CSA grey area for adolescent victims remains unresolved and creates real risk of re-victimisation within the system

---

## Slide 4: UNICEF Situation Analyses

UNICEF has produced multiple situation analyses (most recent: 2021, updated 2025). These are the most comprehensive assessments of the child protection system.

**Key documented findings:**
- Average caseworker caseload: >160 potential cases, 70-80 active (vs. 25-30 recommended)
- Reactive crisis response displaces preventive family support
- Inter-agency coordination remains ad hoc despite formal mechanisms
- The FCSC generalist model – handling all social service domains with the same team – prevents specialisation
- Training quality and supervision for social workers are inconsistent
- Data systems improving (MCPD) but outcome tracking absent
- Alternative care heavily institutionalised (91%)

## Slide 4: Implementation Challenges for Action Plans

**Recurring implementation problems across successive plans:**

1. **Unfunded mandates:** Plans set ambitious targets without secured budget allocation. Activities depend on donor funding (primarily UNICEF) that is neither permanent nor guaranteed.

2. **No accountability mechanism:** Plans lack binding enforcement. If targets are missed, there are no formal consequences for implementing agencies.

3. **Monitoring without data:** Plans require progress reporting, but the data systems to track progress (particularly outcome data) do not exist for many indicators.

4. **Multi-agency coordination requirements:** Plans assign responsibilities to multiple ministries and agencies (MoSFD, MoE, MPS, PGO, MoH, FPA) without authority over those agencies. MoSFD cannot compel the police to improve response times or the PGO to increase prosecution rates.

5. **Atoll-level implementation gap:** Plans are designed centrally in Male'. Translation into atoll-level action depends on FCSC capacity, local council engagement, and community buy-in – none of which is assured.

6. **Plan succession without evaluation:** New plans are developed before previous plans are systematically evaluated. The NSAP-CP 2020-2024 expired without a published evaluation of what was achieved.

## Slide 5: CFPS Standard Operating Procedures (2021)

The SOPs define the operational process that social workers should follow. Key elements:

| SOP Stage | Specification |
|---|---|
| Referral and intake | All referrals logged; source documented; initial screening conducted |
| Initial assessment | 24 hours for urgent cases; 72 hours for non-urgent |
| Full assessment | Joint with police where criminal conduct suspected; home visit; child interview; family interview |
| Case planning | Intervention plan developed; multi-disciplinary input where available |
| Intervention | Safety planning, referral, placement, counselling, court order application as needed |
| Monitoring | Regular follow-up at defined intervals |
| Case closure | Requires supervisor approval; closure criteria met |

**Gap between SOP and practice:** SOPs assume caseloads of 25-30. At actual loads of 70-80, 24-hour urgent assessments may become 48-72 hours, monitoring intervals stretch, and documentation is compressed. The SOPs represent the standard the system aims for; actual practice is shaped by resource constraints.

## Slide 6: The Alternative Care Regulatory Framework

**Alternative Care Regulation 2020-R-69:**
- Governs all placements of children outside their family: residential institutions, foster care, kinship care (kafalah)
- Requires assessment of child's needs, matching with placement, regular review
- Establishes standards for residential care facilities (staffing, physical conditions, programming)
- Mandates periodic review of all children in care to assess whether placement remains appropriate

**Reintegration Regulation 2020/R-30:**
- Governs return of children from alternative care to family/community
- Requires reintegration plan, family assessment, graduated return, post-reintegration monitoring

**Implementation status:** Regulations exist on paper. Implementation varies across facilities. Regular periodic review of all children in care is not consistently achieved at current staffing levels.

## Slide 7: The Coordination Architecture – Mandated Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Function | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Multi-Disciplinary Teams (MDTs) | Atoll-level case discussion bringing police, health, education, social work | Functioning inconsistently across atolls |
| Case Management Coordination Committee | Monthly MPS-MoSFD data reconciliation | Operational in Male' |
| Atoll-level Child Protection Committees | Multi-agency coordination at atoll level | Variable – some active, some dormant |
| National Council for Child Rights Protection | Policy-level coordination | Established under CRPA; meets periodically |
| School CP Focal Points | Early identification and referral from schools | In place; referral pathways inconsistent |

**The fundamental coordination problem:** No single agency has authority over all child protection actors. MoSFD sets policy but cannot direct police, prosecution, courts, or health services. Coordination depends on relationships, MoUs, and goodwill – not structural authority. ## Slide 2: The CRC Reporting Cycle

**How it works:**
1. Government submits periodic report (combined 6th/7th submitted October 2021)
2. Civil society / HRCM submit shadow reports providing independent assessment
3. CRC Committee reviews reports, requests additional information
4. Committee conducts constructive dialogue with government delegation
5. Committee issues Concluding Observations with specific recommendations
6. Government expected to implement recommendations and report on progress in next cycle

**What the report must cover:**
- Legislative developments (CRPA, JJ Act, Education Act)
- Institutional developments (FCSCs, Ombudsperson, DJJ)
- Budget allocation for children
- Data on violence against children, alternative care, juvenile justice, education, health
- Specific responses to previous Concluding Observations

**Operational implication for staff:** Data from your casework, FCSCs, and the MCPD feeds into CRC reports. Accurate data collection and case documentation directly supports treaty compliance.

| ARC | NGO advocacy | Community-level CP concerns |
| International partners | Technical input | UNICEF Situation Analysis used as supplementary information |

Shadow reports matter because they provide the Committee with an independent check on government claims. Where government reports emphasise legislative progress, shadow reports document implementation gaps. The dynamic creates pressure for honest government reporting.

---

## Slide 7: The Universal Periodic Review (UPR)

The UPR is a UN Human Rights Council mechanism in which every UN member state's human rights record is reviewed periodically by other states.

**CP-relevant UPR recommendations to the Maldives:**
- Strengthen child protection legislation and implementation
- Address child marriage and FGM/C
- Improve juvenile justice and abolish corporal punishment
- Protect children from sexual exploitation and trafficking
- Strengthen data collection on violence against children

**Government response:** The Maldives has accepted most UPR recommendations in principle. Implementation tracking is conducted through national mechanisms and reported in subsequent UPR cycles.

**Staff implication:** UPR recommendations, like CRC recommendations, create a public record of commitments. They are referenced by development partners, donors, and civil society in advocacy and programming.

## Slide 8: Reporting Requirements and Data Flow

**What treaty reporting requires from the system:**

| Data Required | Source | Current Availability |
|---|---|---|
| VAC case volumes by type, age, sex, atoll | MCPD / FCSC records | Available (improving since 2016) |
| Alternative care statistics (type, duration, outcomes) | CFPS records | Volume available; outcomes not tracked |
| Juvenile justice data (diversions, sentences, rehabilitation) | DJJ / courts | Partially available |
| Budget allocation for children | MoSFD finance | Available but CP-specific allocation difficult to disaggregate |
| Child labour data | MoSFD / MoEYS | Limited |

**The data quality circle:** Treaty bodies request data → government must provide it → this creates incentive for data system improvement → better data enables better policy. Accurate case documentation at the FCSC level directly supports this cycle.

## Module 9: Implementation Realities


---

## Slide 1: The Policy-Practice Gap

The Maldives has a modern legislative framework (CRPA 2019, JJ Act 2019, DVPA 2012), a national action plan (NAPVAC 2024-2028), and a service delivery network (24 FCSCs, 1412 helpline, MCPD database). On paper, the system has the components of a functioning child protection framework.

In practice, the gap between what the framework prescribes and what the system delivers is substantial. This module examines where and why implementation falls short.

---

## Slide 2: Workforce Constraints

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Child protection social workers | 61 |
| Average active caseload | 70-80 (recommended: 25-30) |
| Average potential caseload | ~160 |
| BSW programme established | 2006 (first graduates ~2010) |
| Professional social work in Maldives | <20 years old |
| Graduate supply vs demand | Consistently below demand; graduates prefer Male' over atoll postings |
| Turnover drivers | Emotional intensity, death threats (gang-connected families), limited supervision, modest pay |
| Clinical supervision | Inconsistent; not available in all FCSCs |
| Career progression | Limited within the social work profession |

The workforce constraint is a primary implementation barrier. Every other gap – assessment quality, follow-up frequency, coordination, documentation – is downstream of insufficient staff.

---

## Slide 3: The Criminal Justice Funnel

Cases progressively drop out at each stage of the criminal justice process:

| Stage | Attrition |
|---|---|
| Incident occurs | Unknown prevalence (no national study since 2006/07) |
| Reported to system | Under-reported (cultural barriers, fear, satr, island dynamics) |
| Police investigation | Delays, informal reconciliation, evidence collection failures |
| Referred to prosecution | Only 14% of VAW cases reach PGO from police |
| Prosecution filed | Only 3% of DV cases reported to police are prosecuted |
| Conviction | 16.7% conviction rate for VAW cases at court (2016-2019) |
| Sentence served | Sentencing data incomplete |

**The practical consequence:** The vast majority of violence against children and women results in no legal consequence for the perpetrator. Child protection intervention cannot rely on the criminal justice system as a reliable backstop. Staff must work with families knowing that prosecution is unlikely even in cases with clear evidence.

---

## Slide 4: The Geographic Implementation Gap

**What Male' has:**
- CFPS headquarters with specialised teams
- IGMH with forensic capacity
- Juvenile Court
- Family Court
- FPA with dedicated DV staff
- Police FCPW
- Legal aid (limited)
- Residential care facilities
- Children's Ombudsperson office

**What a typical outer island has:**
- Health centre with 1-2 nurses (no doctor)
- Primary/secondary school
- Island council
- No social worker, no police investigator, no forensic capacity, no court, no shelter, no psychologist

**What this means in practice:** A child sexually abused on an outer island during monsoon season may wait days for a social worker to arrive by boat, weeks for forensic examination in Male', months for police investigation, and years for prosecution – if prosecution occurs at all. The perpetrator likely remains on the same island throughout.

---

## Slide 5: Coordination Failures in Practice

**Documented patterns (Rogers, Ali & Naeem 2025; UNICEF 2022):**

- **Agencies working in silos:** Health, education, police, and social services each hold partial information about the same family without systematic sharing – the 2009 baby death case pattern
- **MDTs functioning inconsistently:** Some atolls have active multi-disciplinary teams; others have them on paper only. Effectiveness depends on personal relationships between local professionals, not institutional protocols
- **School-FCSC disconnect:** School focal points identify concerns but referral pathways are unclear or unused. No systematic case conferencing between education and child protection
- **FPA-CFPS overlap:** DV cases involving children require coordination between FPA (DV mandate) and CFPS (CP mandate). No joint protocol systematically applied
- **Police response variability:** Some police officers take child protection reports seriously; others treat them as family matters and encourage informal resolution

**Political interference:** Documented cases where local power dynamics – island councillors, atoll officials, politically connected families – influence case handling. On an island of 500 people, the alleged perpetrator may be the island chief's relative.

---

## Slide 6: Alternative Care Implementation

**The institutional care default:**
- 91% of 187 children in alternative care are in residential institutions
- Only 18 foster carers registered nationally
- No foster carer recruitment, training, or support programme at scale
- No specialised therapeutic foster care
- Kafalah (Islamic guardianship) exists culturally but not as a formalised CP pathway

**Why the system defaults to institutions:**
- Institutions exist and have beds; foster homes must be found, assessed, and matched
- No professional foster care support infrastructure
- Community reluctance to take in unrelated children (stigma, cost, disruption)
- Kinship care is common but often informal and unmonitored – quality varies
- The system lacks the assessment and matching capacity that family-based alternatives require

**Leaving care:** Children exit institutional care at 18 with no systematic transition support. No leaving care policy or programme exists. This transition point is a documented vulnerability – young adults without family support, vocational skills, or housing entering the community unprepared.

---

## Slide 7: Mandatory Reporting – Implementation Status

**Legal obligation:** Clear (CRPA – all persons).

**Implementation reality:**

| Factor | Status |
|---|---|
| Professional training on reporting obligations | Inconsistent – some professions trained, others not |
| Public awareness of reporting obligation | Low – particularly in atolls |
| Enforcement of failure-to-report provisions | No documented prosecutions for failure to report |
| Anonymous reporting mechanism | 1412 accepts anonymous reports |
| Reporter protection | Legal provision exists; practical protection on small islands uncertain |
| Reporting rate relative to estimated prevalence | Very low |

**Why reporting remains low despite the legal obligation:**
- Social cost of reporting on a small island (identity easily inferred)
- Fear of retaliation where perpetrator has authority or connections
- Satr norms – cultural obligation to conceal family faults
- Perception that reporting makes things worse ("nothing will happen" or "the family will be destroyed")
- Children's dependence on the very adults they would report against
- No enforcement: failure to report has not been prosecuted, so the obligation lacks practical consequences

---

## Slide 8: Data and Documentation in Practice

**MCPD (operational since 2016):**
- Links all 24 FCSCs with unique case identifiers
- Enables national case volume tracking and disaggregation
- Pilot digital case management in selected FCSCs (2023)

**Implementation gaps:**
- Data entry depends on caseworker time – overloaded workers may delay or abbreviate entries
- Outcome data not tracked (case opened → case closed, but no follow-up on child wellbeing post-intervention)
- Data quality varies across FCSCs
- No systematic data linkage between MCPD (MoSFD), police records (MPS), health records (hospitals), or education records (MoEYS)
- The Family Assessment Form (2020) standardised intake but form completion quality is inconsistent

**The consequence:** The system knows how many cases it handles but cannot demonstrate what happens to children after intervention. Policy decisions are made without outcome evidence.

---

## Slide 9: Staff Wellbeing and Professional Support

Child protection social work is emotionally demanding, physically isolating (for atoll-based staff), and professionally under-supported.

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| Clinical supervision | Not consistently available; some FCSCs have no supervisor |
| Peer support | Limited – small teams, geographic isolation |
| Vicarious trauma | Recognised but not systematically addressed |
| Personal safety | Death threats from gang-connected families documented |
| Remuneration | Modest relative to caseload demands and professional risk |
| Career progression | Limited pathways within social work; many leave for better-paying roles |
| Professional recognition | Social work as a profession is still establishing its identity and status |

The caseload crisis and these professional support gaps are not separate problems. They interact: overloaded workers without supervision burn out faster, make more errors, and leave sooner – further depleting the workforce.

## Module 10: Reform Agenda


---

## Slide 1: Where the System Stands – Summary Assessment

| Dimension | Status |
|---|---|
| Legal framework | Strong (CRPA 2019, JJ Act, DVPA) – major gaps: marriage age inconsistency, flogging, zina/CSA boundary |
| Institutional architecture | Exists (CFPS, 24 FCSCs, Ombudsperson, DJJ) – coordination weak, authority fragmented |
| Workforce | Critically insufficient – 61 workers for 128,000 children; 70-80 caseloads |
| Data systems | Improving (MCPD since 2016) – outcome tracking absent |
| Alternative care | Heavily institutionalised (91%); foster care minimal (18 carers nationally) |
| Criminal justice response | Weak – 3% DV prosecution rate; 16.7% conviction rate |
| Geographic coverage | 20 FCSCs for 199 islands; most islands have no social service presence |
| Prevention | Underdeveloped – system is almost entirely reactive |
| Evidence base | Thin – no national prevalence study since 2006/07; no outcome data |
| International compliance | Mixed – legislation improving; implementation lagging across most CRC recommendations |

---

## Slide 2: Five Priority Reform Areas

Based on the evidence reviewed in this course, five reform areas would have the greatest operational impact:

**1. Workforce expansion and professional development**
- Double the child protection social worker workforce from 61 to 120+ over 5 years
- Establish clinical supervision as mandatory in all FCSCs
- Create career progression pathways within social work
- Introduce retention incentives for atoll postings (housing, salary supplements, professional development)
- Ensure all FCSCs have at least one qualified supervisor

**2. Family-based alternative care development**
- Establish a national foster carer recruitment, training, and support programme
- Formalise kafalah as a CP pathway with assessment and monitoring standards
- Set target: reduce institutional care from 91% to 50% within 5 years
- Develop leaving care support programme for young people exiting care at 18

**3. Legal framework harmonisation**
- Amend Family Act to align marriage age at 18 without exception
- Legislate explicit protection for child sexual abuse victims from zina prosecution
- Abolish judicial flogging of children
- Complete the Ageing Bill (relevant to cross-cutting elderly-child care dynamics)

**4. Coordination reform**
- Establish enforceable information-sharing protocols (not just MoUs) between MoSFD, MPS, PGO, health, education
- Mandate and resource functioning MDTs in all atolls
- Create integrated case conferencing protocols applied consistently across FCSCs
- Address the structural problem: no single agency has authority over all CP actors

**5. Evidence and accountability**
- Commission a national child abuse prevalence study (no study since 2006/07)
- Implement outcome tracking in the MCPD – measuring what happens to children post-intervention
- Establish a child death review mechanism
- Evaluate IBAMA effectiveness systematically
- Publish evaluations of action plans before designing successors

## Slide 3: What the CRC Committee Expects Next

The next CRC reporting cycle will assess progress on the outstanding recommendations. The government will need to demonstrate:

- Resolution of the CRPA/Family Act marriage age inconsistency
- Progress on abolishing corporal punishment of children in all settings
- Steps to protect CSA victims from zina prosecution
- Evidence of caseload reduction toward international standards
- Progress on disaggregated data collection (MCPD improvements)
- Evidence of periodic review for all children in alternative care
- Strengthened mandatory reporting implementation
- Budget data showing increasing investment in child protection

**Staff implication:** The data, case records, and documentation you produce will form the evidentiary basis for the government's next CRC report. Quality documentation demonstrates system progress; gaps in documentation undermine the government's reporting credibility.
