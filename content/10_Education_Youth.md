# Part X: Education and Youth Development

Education and youth development constitute the foundational infrastructure of social policy in the Maldives. In a nation where more than 40% of the population is aged 18-34, the stakes of youth policy are high: whether the system equips young people with the knowledge, skills, and resilience to navigate a rapidly changing economy has consequences for unemployment, substance abuse, gang recruitment, and radicalisation.[^1] The Maldives has achieved near-universal primary enrolment, high literacy rates, and expanding higher education access -- accomplishments that place it among the most educationally advanced nations in South Asia. Yet beneath these headline statistics lie deep structural fractures: geographic inequity between Male and the outer atolls, chronic skills mismatches with the labour market, a vocational training system widely perceived as second-class, high youth NEET rates, and an education system that young Maldivians themselves describe as having failed to prepare them for either employment or adult life.[^2] This chapter examines the full landscape of education and youth development policy, from the legislative framework of the Education Act 2020 through the frontline realities of atoll schools, higher education expansion, TVET reform, youth vulnerability, violent extremism, and the unfinished agenda of sexual and reproductive health education.

## 10.1 Education Act 2020 and Governance Framework

### The Legislative Foundation

The Education Act (Law No. 24/2020), ratified by President Ibrahim Mohamed Solih on 10 November 2020, is the most comprehensive legislative framework for education in the Maldives. Prior to its passage, the education sector operated under a patchwork of executive regulations, presidential decrees, and ministerial policies without a single overarching statute. The Act establishes education as a fundamental right and codifies the state's obligation to provide free, compulsory education to all children between the ages of 4 and 16 -- spanning from pre-primary (Lower Kindergarten) through the completion of lower secondary education (Grade 10).[^3]

Key provisions of the Education Act include:

- **Right to free education**: Every Maldivian child is entitled to free education from pre-primary through secondary level at government schools, with the state bearing the costs of tuition, textbooks, and basic learning materials.
- **Compulsory attendance**: Parents and guardians are legally obligated to ensure children aged 4-16 attend school. Failure to comply can result in legal proceedings, though enforcement mechanisms remain underdeveloped in practice.
- **Inclusive education mandate**: The Act requires schools to accommodate children with disabilities and special educational needs, prohibiting discrimination in admissions and mandating reasonable adjustments to the learning environment.
- **Curriculum standards**: The Ministry of Education is empowered to set national curriculum standards, approve textbooks, and establish assessment frameworks. The National Institute of Education (NIE) serves as the technical arm for curriculum development.
- **Teacher qualifications**: Minimum qualification standards for teachers are established, with the Act requiring all teachers to hold at least a diploma-level qualification, a standard that many serving teachers in outer atoll schools have not yet achieved.
- **School governance**: The Act provides for school boards with community representation, though their actual decision-making authority varies considerably between government schools in Male and those on remote islands.

### Governance Architecture

The governance of education in the Maldives has been subject to frequent ministerial restructuring. As of the period under review, the primary institutional actors include:

**Ministry of Education (MoE)**: Responsible for pre-primary, primary, and secondary education policy, curriculum development, teacher management, and school administration. The MoE directly operates all government schools and supervises community schools. It houses several key divisions including the Policy Planning and Research Division, the Quality Assurance Department (QAD), and the National Institute of Education.[^4]

**Ministry of Higher Education (MoHE)**: Established as a separate ministry to oversee tertiary education, the Maldives Qualifications Authority (MQA), higher education financing (including the First Degree Scheme and loan programmes), and TVET coordination. The MoHE also manages the Maldives National Skills Development Authority (MNSDA), which regulates all TVET programmes.

**Maldives Qualifications Authority (MQA)**: Responsible for quality assurance in higher education and TVET, maintaining the Maldives National Qualifications Framework (MNQF), registering programmes, and accrediting institutions. The MNQF spans 10 levels, from Certificate 1 (Level 1) through doctoral qualifications (Level 10).[^5]

**Quality Assurance Department (QAD)**: Located within the MoE, responsible for school inspections, teacher performance evaluations, and monitoring compliance with national standards. The Education Sector Analysis 2019 found that the QAD was significantly under-resourced, conducting infrequent inspections and lacking the capacity to provide meaningful follow-up support to underperforming schools.[^6]

### Implementation Realities

The gap between the Education Act's progressive provisions and frontline implementation is substantial. Several structural challenges persist:

First, the Act's compulsory education provision faces enforcement difficulties on outer islands where some families withdraw children (particularly boys) for economic activity or send them to Male for secondary education, creating de facto school dropout that goes unrecorded. Second, the inclusive education mandate has outpaced the availability of trained special education teachers, accessible infrastructure, and diagnostic services, particularly outside Male. Third, the teacher qualification requirements highlighted a workforce where a significant proportion of atoll-based teachers held qualifications below the stipulated minimum, creating a tension between legal requirements and operational necessity. The MoE's response has been to implement in-service training programmes while granting transitional exemptions, but the pace of upskilling has been slow.[^7]

The frequent restructuring of education governance -- with higher education responsibilities moving between combined and separate ministries across different administrations -- has created institutional instability. Policy continuity suffers when incoming governments reorganise ministerial responsibilities, reassign staff, and adjust priorities. The Education Sector Analysis 2019 specifically recommended that education governance reform should transcend political cycles, calling for a long-term national education plan developed through broad-based consultation.[^8]

## 10.2 Historical Evolution of the Education System

### Edhuruge Origins and the Transition to Universal Schooling

The transformation of Maldivian education from informal coral-stone learning houses to a modern national system is one of the most substantial social transitions in the country's history. For centuries, education in the Maldives was delivered through the *edhuruge* system -- small, home-based schools typically run by a learned community member, often focused on Quran recitation, basic Arabic literacy, and rudimentary arithmetic. The *edhuruge* served a deeply localised function: each island had its own learning tradition, and there was no standardised curriculum, no formal certification, and no concept of universal access.[^9]

The first modern school in the Maldives, Majeediyya School, was established in Male in 1927, introducing English-medium instruction and a curriculum modelled on the colonial education systems of neighbouring South Asia. For decades, Majeediyya remained the only school offering a modern education, accessible almost exclusively to Male's elite families. The outer atolls remained reliant on the *edhuruge* system well into the second half of the twentieth century.

### The Expansion Phase (1960s-1990s)

President Ibrahim Nasir's government (1968-1978) initiated the first systematic effort to extend modern education beyond Male, establishing government schools on atoll capitals. However, the pace of expansion was limited by the fundamental geographic challenge of the Maldives: 1,190 islands spread across 26 atolls, with most inhabited islands having populations too small to sustain a full-cycle school. The response was a tiered system where smaller islands had primary-only schools, atoll capitals offered education through lower secondary, and only Male and a handful of larger islands provided higher secondary education.

President Maumoon Abdul Gayoom's government (1978-2008) oversaw the largest expansion of the education system to date. By the late 1990s, the government had achieved near-universal primary enrolment -- a remarkable feat for a small island developing state. Key milestones included:

- Establishment of Atoll Education Centres (AECs) on every atoll capital, providing education from primary through lower secondary level.
- Introduction of community schools on smaller islands, often with government subsidies but community management.
- Adoption of the British-style examination system (GCE O-Levels and A-Levels through Edexcel and Cambridge) as the national assessment framework.
- Expansion of teacher training through the Institute for Teacher Education (now the Faculty of Education at MNU).

### Post-2008 Reforms

The democratic transition of 2008 brought new attention to education quality as distinct from mere access. President Mohamed Nasheed's government (2008-2012) initiated curricular reforms and expanded preschool education. The National Curriculum Framework was revised to incorporate competency-based learning, life skills, and local content. However, political instability -- including the contested transfer of power in 2012 -- disrupted implementation.

Subsequent governments continued expanding access while grappling with quality deficits. The Education Sector Plan 2019-2023, developed with UNESCO technical assistance, set ambitious targets for improving learning outcomes, reducing geographic disparities, strengthening TVET, and integrating ICT into instruction. The passage of the Education Act in 2020 provided the legal backbone for many of these aspirations.[^10]

### The COVID-19 Disruption

The COVID-19 pandemic, beginning in March 2020, forced the closure of all schools for extended periods and exposed the digital divide in Maldivian education. While schools in Male relatively quickly transitioned to online learning platforms, students on outer islands faced severe connectivity challenges. Many atoll islands lacked reliable internet access, students did not have devices, and teachers were untrained in remote instruction. The pandemic reinforced what the Education Sector Analysis had already identified: the Maldivian education system's deep dependence on physical classroom delivery and its limited preparedness for alternative modes of learning.[^11]

## 10.3 Primary, Secondary, and Higher Secondary Education

### Structure of the System

The Maldivian education system follows a 7-3-2 structure after pre-primary:

| Level | Grades | Key Stage | Age Range |
|-------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Pre-Primary | Nursery, LKG, UKG | Foundation | 3-6 |
| Primary | Grades 1-6 | KS 1 & KS 2 | 6-12 |
| Lower Secondary | Grades 7-10 | KS 3 & KS 4 | 12-16 |
| Higher Secondary | Grades 11-12 | KS 5 | 16-18 |

Students sit for the GCE O-Level examinations at the end of Grade 10 and GCE A-Level examinations at the end of Grade 12, using international examination boards (primarily Edexcel and Cambridge). The reliance on international examination systems means Maldivian students are benchmarked against global standards, but it also means the curriculum is shaped by external requirements rather than national priorities.[^12]

### Enrolment and Access

The Maldives has achieved near-universal primary education. Gross Enrolment Rates (GER) at the primary level have consistently exceeded 95%, and Net Enrolment Rates (NER) are similarly high. The achievement is remarkable given the geographic dispersion of the population across approximately 187 inhabited islands. As of 2018, the country operated 213 schools (both government and community) serving approximately 73,000 students from pre-primary through higher secondary.[^13]

However, enrolment figures mask important disparities:

**Geographic attrition**: While primary enrolment is near-universal, significant drop-off occurs at the lower and higher secondary levels. Many islands have schools that terminate at Grade 7 or Grade 10, requiring students to migrate to atoll capitals or Male to continue their education. This migration imposes financial and social costs that disproportionately affect families on smaller, more remote islands. The Education Sector Analysis 2019 documented that enrolment drops notably between primary and lower secondary, and again between lower secondary and higher secondary.[^14]

**Gender dynamics**: At the primary level, enrolment is roughly equal between boys and girls. However, at higher levels, gender dynamics shift. Girls tend to outperform boys academically and have higher completion rates at the secondary level. Boys are more likely to drop out, particularly in the outer atolls, where employment in fishing, tourism, or construction provides an alternative income path. At the higher education level, women significantly outnumber men -- by 2019, 59% of locally enrolled higher education students were female.[^15]

**The Male concentration effect**: The consolidation of quality education in Male has been a persistent concern. Almost all schools offering a full range of O-Level and A-Level subjects are concentrated in the capital. Students from outer atolls who wish to access these subjects must relocate to Male, often living with relatives in overcrowded conditions or in unsupervised arrangements. This concentration has widened rather than narrowed over time, as investment in Male schools has outpaced investment in atoll schools.[^16]

### Learning Outcomes

Despite high enrolment, learning outcomes reveal significant quality deficits. The National Assessment of Learning Outcomes (NALO), conducted in 2016 for Grades 4 and 7, produced concerning results:

- In Grade 4 Mathematics, the national average score was 477 (on a 500-centred scale), with significant variation across atolls. Several atolls scored well below the national average, indicating systematic geographic inequality in learning outcomes.
- In Grade 4 English, results showed similar atoll-level disparities, with Male students consistently outperforming those from outer atolls.
- In Grade 7, the pattern repeated: students in Male and nearby Kaafu Atoll achieved markedly higher scores than those in more remote atolls.
- Dhivehi language assessments showed less geographic variation, but still revealed that a substantial proportion of students were not meeting expected competency levels.[^17]

The O-Level pass rates tell a similarly mixed story. While the proportion of students passing five or more subjects (the traditional benchmark for secondary completion) has improved over time, many students -- particularly in science subjects -- achieve grades that do not meet university entry requirements. The GCE O-Level results from the Keyodhoo School National Examination showed considerable year-on-year variation, with some schools experiencing pass rates below 30% in core subjects.[^18]

### Teacher Quality and Distribution

The teacher workforce is central to the education system and faces several persistent challenges. As of 2018, approximately 8,700 teachers served in government and community schools. Key challenges include:

**Qualification gaps**: The Education Sector Analysis found that a significant proportion of teachers, especially in atoll schools, held qualifications below the diploma level that the Education Act would later mandate. While Male schools could recruit degree-holding graduates, atoll schools often relied on teachers with certificate-level qualifications or, in some cases, untrained community members filling gaps.

**Expatriate dependence**: The system relies heavily on expatriate teachers, particularly for secondary-level science and mathematics instruction. In 2018, approximately 30% of secondary teachers in some atolls were expatriates, primarily from India and Sri Lanka. While these teachers fill critical gaps, high turnover rates, language barriers, and inconsistent quality have been persistent concerns.

**The urban-rural divide**: Qualified Maldivian teachers overwhelmingly prefer to work in Male, where living conditions, professional development opportunities, and social amenities are superior. Despite incentive schemes (including atoll allowances), recruitment and retention of qualified teachers in outer atolls remains a persistent human resource challenge in the education sector.

**Multi-grade teaching**: On smaller islands with limited student populations, teachers must simultaneously instruct multiple grades in a single classroom. The Education Sector Analysis dedicated an entire section to multi-grade teaching, finding that while it is a practical necessity, most teachers have received no specific training in multi-grade pedagogies, leading to compromised learning outcomes.[^19]

## 10.4 Higher Education -- MNU, IUM, and Private Institutions

### Institutional Overview

Higher education in the Maldives has expanded dramatically since the establishment of the first local university in 2011. As of September 2023, the country's registered Higher Education Institutions (HEIs) comprised two public universities, two public colleges (College of Defence and Security Studies; National College of Policing and Law Enforcement), and twelve private colleges.[^20]

**The Maldives National University (MNU)**: Established by Act of Parliament in 2011, MNU is the country's oldest and largest university, evolved from the former Maldives College of Higher Education. MNU operates multiple faculties covering education, health sciences, engineering, hospitality and tourism, Islamic studies, law, arts, and social sciences. It maintains campuses and outreach centres across several atolls, though the majority of programmes are offered in Male. MNU has been the primary institution training Maldivian teachers, nurses, and public administrators.

**The Islamic University of Maldives (IUM)**: Established in 2015 (evolved from the earlier College of Islamic Studies), IUM offers programmes in Islamic studies, education, law (Shariah), Arabic language, and social sciences. IUM has attracted significant enrolment, particularly in religion and theology, which consistently ranks among the most popular fields of study at the national level.

**Private colleges**: Twelve private colleges, including Villa College (one of the largest), Cyryx College, MAPS College, Mandhu College, Avid College, Maldives Business School, and others, offer programmes ranging from certificate to bachelor's degree level. Several operate in partnership with international universities (particularly from Malaysia, the UK, and Australia), offering franchise or validated degree programmes. Villa College, for instance, offers programmes validated by international partner institutions alongside its own MQA-accredited qualifications.[^21]

### Enrolment Trends

Higher education enrolment has grown substantially over the period 2019-2023, driven significantly by the government's First Degree Scheme:

| Year | Local Enrolments | New Graduates | Dropout | Academic Staff |
|------|-----------------|---------------|---------|---------------|
| 2019 | 27,421 | 11,254 | 2,691 | 869 |
| 2020 | 26,825 | 7,245 | 1,991 | 2,146 |
| 2021 | 26,733 | 12,837 | 4,066 | 1,867 |
| 2022 | 29,016 | 6,093 | 2,897 | 2,395 |

Several patterns emerge from these data. First, female students consistently outnumber males in higher education -- by 2022, 64% of locally enrolled students were female and only 36% male, a gender gap that has widened over time. Second, dropout rates are concerning: in 2019, one in every ten students dropped out; in 2021, the figure was three in every twenty. The overwhelming majority of dropouts are male (88% in 2019), suggesting that higher education attrition is heavily gendered. Third, the academic staff base remains small relative to enrolments, with heavy reliance on part-time faculty (reaching 60% part-time in 2021).[^22]

### The First Degree Scheme

The government's most significant higher education intervention has been the "Free First Degree" programme, launched in 2019. Under this scheme, the government covers the full tuition costs for any Maldivian citizen pursuing their first bachelor's degree at a registered local institution. Between 2019 and 2022, the scheme enrolled 18,880 students and created 1,693 scholarship opportunities. The policy drove a 62% increase in bachelor's-level enrolments and represented a substantial public investment -- part of a total MVR 2.70 billion spent on higher education over the period, encompassing the First Degree Scheme, loan programmes, and scholarships.[^23]

The scheme has been praised for democratising access to higher education, particularly for students from lower-income families and outer atolls who previously could not afford university fees. However, critics have raised concerns about quality dilution (as institutions rapidly expanded to absorb demand), the sustainability of fiscal commitments, the mismatch between degree programmes and labour market needs, and the absence of outcome-tracking mechanisms to assess whether graduates are securing relevant employment.

### Fields of Study

The distribution of higher education enrolments by field reveals significant patterns. The most popular fields of study between 2019 and 2022 were:

- **Management and administration**: Consistently the largest field, with 3,968 enrolments in 2019 rising to 6,441 in 2022.
- **Religion and theology**: The second-largest field, with 635 enrolments in 2019 peaking at 4,780 in 2020, reflecting strong demand for Islamic studies.
- **Nursing and midwifery**: Growing from 1,521 in 2019 to 3,803 in 2022, driven by health sector workforce needs.
- **Teacher training**: Combined enrolments in teacher training with and without subject specialisation totalled approximately 2,730 in 2019 and 3,642 in 2022.
- **Law**: Growing significantly from 1,318 in 2019 to 1,437 in 2022.
- **Accounting**: Stable at around 1,000-1,600 annually.

Fields with notably low enrolments included philosophy and ethics (single digits), fine arts, environmental sciences, and literature and linguistics. The low enrolment in environmental sciences is particularly concerning given the Maldives' acute vulnerability to climate change. Similarly, the limited enrolment in building and civil engineering (57 in 2019, 99 in 2022) reflects the continued reliance on expatriate labour for construction -- a sector that is one of the country's largest employers of foreign workers.[^24]

### Higher Education Loan Scheme

Since 2019, six loan schemes were announced, providing 3,720 loan opportunities of which 2,942 were awarded. The loan utilisation grew from 249 awarded (of 405 opportunities) in 2019 to 1,275 awarded (of 1,275 opportunities) in 2023 -- indicating both increasing demand and improved utilisation rates. The scheme supports students pursuing education both locally and abroad, with recipients studying in over 40 countries.[^25]

### Enrolment by Qualification Level

The MNQF-based breakdown of enrolments reveals an important structural shift. Between 2019 and 2022, enrolments at Level 7 (bachelor's degree) grew from 8,028 to 13,266, while enrolments at Level 9 (master's degree) grew from 1,922 to 2,681. Meanwhile, lower-level certificate enrolments (Levels 1-3) showed more variable patterns. This shift toward degree-level qualifications reflects the First Degree Scheme's impact but also raises questions about whether the workforce genuinely requires this level of academic qualification, or whether the economy's actual needs -- particularly in technical and vocational areas -- remain underserved.[^26]

## 10.5 Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET)

### The Persistent "Second-Class" Perception

TVET has been extensively studied and diagnosed, yet reform has been slow. The ILO's 2012 TVET Assessment, a comprehensive evaluation using the Interagency Working Group (IWG) framework, identified a fundamental cultural barrier: TVET is perceived as a second-class option by Maldivian youth and their families, a pathway for those who have "failed" in the academic stream rather than a valued route to skilled employment.[^27]

This perception is not irrational. The Maldivian education system has been structured around academic achievement, measured primarily by GCE O-Level and A-Level results, with success defined as university entry. Students who do not achieve the requisite grades are effectively streamed into TVET by default rather than by informed choice. The result is a TVET system that receives the least academically prepared students, reinforcing the perception that it is for those who cannot succeed elsewhere.

Research by Sunny and Ismail (2023) confirmed the persistence of this perception. Their survey of 200 Maldivian respondents found that while 81.7% had heard of TVET, less than half (45.2%) understood that it could lead to better employment opportunities. Experts interviewed for the study identified seven strategies to improve TVET engagement: promoting TVET through electronic media, launching a national campaign, strengthening partnerships with industry, branding TVET more effectively, holding skills competitions, improving career guidance in schools, and ensuring TVET curricula align with industry demand.[^28]

### Institutional Framework

The TVET system involves multiple institutional actors:

**Maldives National Skills Development Authority (MNSDA)**: The primary regulatory body responsible for developing and regulating all TVET programmes, updating curricula, introducing new courses, conducting examinations, and awarding certificates. More than 117 public institutions and 12 private institutions are registered with MNSDA as training providers.[^29]

**Maldives Polytechnic (MP)**: Created by Presidential Decree in 2010 under Article 116 of the Constitution, MP is the national government institution for technical and vocational education and training. It operates campuses and outreach centres across multiple atolls and offers programmes from Certificate 1 through Advanced Diploma. However, the Polytechnic has faced persistent challenges including limited equipment, insufficient workshop facilities, and difficulty recruiting qualified instructors, particularly for specialised technical fields.[^30]

**TVET Authority (TVETA)**: Responsible for enforcing quality assurance in all TVET providers -- public, private, and community-based -- through the development of National Vocational Standards, skills validation, and recognition of non-formal and informal learning.

**Employment Sector Councils (ESCs)**: Intended to bridge the gap between training providers and industry by providing sector-specific guidance on skills needs. In practice, the ILO Assessment found that ESCs were largely inactive, meeting infrequently and lacking the capacity or authority to meaningfully influence TVET programme design.[^31]

### The Skills Mismatch

The Maldivian economy's demand for technical skills far exceeds the TVET system's capacity to supply them. The tourism industry alone employs tens of thousands of workers in roles requiring technical competencies -- water desalination plant operation, air conditioning and refrigeration, marine engineering, electrical systems, culinary arts, hospitality management -- yet the majority of these positions are filled by expatriate workers. The ILO Assessment noted that all resorts require engineers and technicians for their infrastructure, but training in these areas was insufficient to meet demand.[^32]

TVET enrolment data show the scale of the gap. Total TVET enrolment in 2018 was approximately 1,762 across all institutions (853 in public institutions, 423 in private institutions, and 486 at the Maldives Polytechnic), with a significant gender imbalance: males substantially outnumbered females in most technical programmes. The TVET participation rate as a proportion of the youth population remained extremely low compared to TVET systems in comparable economies.[^33]

### Programmes and Pathways

TVET in the Maldives is offered from Certificate 1 through Advanced Diploma, mapped to Levels 1-5 of the MNQF. Current training areas include welding, machining, refrigeration and air conditioning, automotive and marine repair, electrician training, carpentry, IT, office management, beauty and fashion, and culinary arts. Apprenticeship programmes exist but are limited in scope and industry engagement.[^34]

The BTEC (Business and Technology Education Council) programme, offered through lower secondary schools, provides an alternative pathway for students who may not be inclined toward the purely academic O-Level track. BTEC enrolment grew steadily from 2014, reaching schools across several atolls by 2018. However, the programme has faced implementation challenges including teacher unfamiliarity with competency-based assessment, limited practical facilities, and uncertain articulation pathways to higher-level qualifications.[^35]

### The HUNARU Programme and MEERY Project

Several targeted programmes have attempted to address youth employment through TVET. The HUNARU programme, a flagship skills training initiative launched by the President, targeted 8,500 unemployed youth between 2011 and 2012 to increase their employability through short-term vocational training. While HUNARU reached a significant number of young people, evaluations questioned the quality and labour market relevance of the training provided.[^36]

More recently, the Maldives: Enhancing Employability and Resilience of Youth (MEERY) Project, a US$20 million World Bank-funded initiative, has targeted TVET reform, small and medium enterprise development, and youth employment. MEERY represents a more systematic approach than earlier ad hoc programmes, emphasising institutional capacity building, industry engagement, and outcome-based programming. However, the project's long-term impact depends on sustained government commitment beyond the project cycle.[^37]

## 10.6 Inclusive Education and Special Needs

### The Policy Commitment

The Education Act 2020 mandates that all schools must accommodate children with disabilities and special educational needs (SEN), prohibiting discrimination in admissions. The Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010) similarly guarantees the right to education for persons with disabilities. The Maldives is a signatory to the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and has committed to Sustainable Development Goal 4's principle of inclusive and equitable quality education.

### The Implementation Gap

The reality of inclusive education falls far short of these commitments. The Education Sector Analysis 2019 dedicated a full section to the SEN programme, identifying fundamental gaps:

**Identification and diagnosis**: There is no national system for early identification and assessment of learning disabilities, autism spectrum disorders, or other developmental conditions. Children with less visible disabilities often go undiagnosed and unsupported throughout their schooling. The few diagnostic services that exist are concentrated in Male.[^38]

**Trained personnel**: The number of trained special education teachers is insufficient. Most schools, particularly in the atolls, have no SEN-trained staff. Regular classroom teachers have received little or no training in differentiated instruction, classroom management for diverse learners, or basic SEN awareness.

**Physical accessibility**: School infrastructure across the atolls was not designed with accessibility in mind. Multi-storey buildings without lifts, narrow doorways, and inaccessible sanitation facilities effectively exclude wheelchair users and those with mobility impairments. While new school construction projects increasingly incorporate universal design principles, the existing stock of school buildings remains largely inaccessible.

**Curriculum and assessment**: The national curriculum and assessment system makes limited provision for alternative assessment methods. Students with learning disabilities who cannot perform in timed written examinations may receive some accommodations, but the system is not designed for genuine differentiated assessment.

**Stigma and exclusion**: Cultural attitudes toward disability remain a significant barrier. Families on smaller islands may keep children with visible disabilities at home rather than send them to school, and teachers in some communities lack the awareness and training to create genuinely inclusive classroom environments. Children with intellectual disabilities are particularly vulnerable to exclusion.[^39]

### Promising Initiatives

Despite these challenges, several initiatives have sought to advance inclusive education. The MoE has established SEN units in some Male schools, staffed with specialist teachers and providing individualised support. The Faculty of Education at MNU offers modules in special and inclusive education, gradually building the pipeline of trained teachers. International development partners, including UNICEF, have supported inclusive education pilot programmes. However, the scale of these initiatives remains inadequate relative to the magnitude of need, and the atolls remain severely underserved.[^40]

## 10.7 Education Quality, Access, and Equity

### Geographic Equity

The defining challenge of Maldivian education is geographic inequity. The archipelagic geography -- 187 inhabited islands spread across 871 km from north to south -- creates inherent difficulties in providing uniform educational quality. But the current level of disparity goes well beyond what geography alone necessitates, reflecting decades of underinvestment in atoll education and the gravitational pull of Male as the centre of economic and social life.

The Youth Vulnerability in the Maldives study (UNDP/NCTC, 2019) captured the frustration of young people on outer atolls: "Almost all young people on the outer atolls complained about the quality and variety of education, saying that many sciences were not taught, schools lacked equipment and laboratories, and there was no career guidance. Half of those questioned said their education had neither helped them get a job nor prepared them well for adult life."[^41]

Specific dimensions of the equity gap include:

**Subject availability**: Outer atoll schools offer a limited range of O-Level and A-Level subjects compared to Male schools. Science subjects -- chemistry, physics, biology with laboratory components -- are frequently unavailable on smaller islands due to lack of equipment and qualified teachers. Students who aspire to study sciences must migrate to Male or atoll capitals, a barrier that disproportionately affects those from lower-income families.

**School facilities**: The Education Sector Analysis documented significant disparities in school infrastructure across atolls. While Male schools are relatively well-equipped (though often overcrowded), atoll schools frequently lack science laboratories, computer rooms, libraries with adequate collections, sports facilities, and in some cases, basic amenities like reliable electricity and internet connectivity.

**ICT integration**: Technology in education has been identified as a potential equaliser, but the digital divide mirrors the broader geographic divide. The Education Sector Analysis found that ICT integration in Maldivian schools varied dramatically, with some schools in Male having smart boards and computer labs while schools on remote islands lacked functional computers entirely. Connectivity remains a fundamental constraint: while Male and atoll capitals typically have broadband access, smaller islands may have only intermittent mobile data coverage.[^42]

### Education Spending

Public spending on education represents a significant share of government expenditure but faces efficiency challenges. The Education Sector Analysis found that education spending as a proportion of GDP was comparable to regional peers but that the composition of spending was heavily skewed toward recurrent costs -- particularly salaries -- with limited allocation for quality improvement, learning materials, or infrastructure maintenance. The per-student spending varies significantly across atolls, with smaller schools on remote islands having higher per-student costs (due to small class sizes and fixed overheads) but often lower quality outcomes.[^43]

Household spending on education is substantial and regressive. Families bear costs for school supplies, uniforms, additional tutoring, and -- for those sending children to Male for secondary education -- accommodation and living expenses. These costs disproportionately burden families on outer atolls, who face the additional expense of inter-island transport and Male accommodation. The Education Sector Analysis estimated that household spending on education represented a significant proportion of income, particularly for lower-income quintiles.[^44]

### The Out-of-School Population

While the Maldives has achieved near-universal primary enrolment, the out-of-school population is not negligible, particularly at the secondary level. The Education Sector Analysis applied the UNESCO Five Dimensions of Exclusion model to identify children and youth at risk of being out of school. Key at-risk groups include:

- Children with disabilities who are kept at home
- Boys in the outer atolls who leave school for employment in fishing or construction
- Children from families experiencing domestic violence or substance abuse
- Adolescent girls who drop out due to early marriage or pregnancy
- Children who migrate to Male for education but find themselves in unstable living situations

The global trend of increasing out-of-school adolescents is evident in the Maldives, where the number of out-of-school youth at the upper secondary level is significantly higher than at the primary level. The ESA identified several categories of out-of-school children including those who never enrolled, those who enrolled but dropped out, and those who are enrolled but at risk of dropping out due to chronic absenteeism or disengagement.[^45]

## 10.8 Life Skills Education and Adolescent Development

### Life Skills in Schools

The gap between what Maldivian schools teach and what young people need to navigate adult life has been a recurring theme in research and youth consultations. The national curriculum includes life skills components, but their implementation is inconsistent and often superficial. Life skills education in the Maldivian context is expected to cover critical thinking, decision-making, emotional regulation, interpersonal communication, conflict resolution, financial literacy, and health-related behaviours -- yet in practice, schools prioritise examination-oriented instruction at the expense of these competencies.[^46]

Research by Nasheeda (2023) on life skills education in the Maldives identified two critical factors determining programme effectiveness: programme duration and real-world connection. Short-term, standalone life skills sessions were found to have minimal lasting impact, while programmes that were integrated into the regular curriculum over sustained periods and connected to students' actual lived experiences showed more promising outcomes. The study recommended that life skills education must move beyond isolated workshops to become a sustained, embedded component of schooling, with teachers trained in facilitative rather than didactic pedagogies.[^47]

### Adolescent Health and Behaviour

The Global School-Based Student Health Survey (GSHS), conducted in the Maldives in 2009, provided one of the few systematic assessments of adolescent health behaviours. The survey revealed concerning patterns in physical activity, dietary habits, mental health indicators, and substance use initiation. While the data is now significantly dated, it remains one of the only nationally representative surveys of adolescent health in the Maldives, highlighting the critical need for updated data collection.[^48]

The lack of structured support networks in schools compounds adolescent vulnerability. The Youth Vulnerability study reported that "there is no structured network of support in schools" and that "the few counsellors they have are not qualified." Young people reported that their education focused on syllabus content and examination preparation, with no meaningful attention to personal development, emotional wellbeing, or preparation for the social challenges they would face as adults. As one young person stated: "Education doesn't focus on values. They teach a syllabus but nobody pays any attention to behaviour."[^49]

### Career Guidance

The near-total absence of career guidance in Maldivian schools is one of the most consistently cited gaps in the education system. The UNDP Youth Vulnerability study found that "careers guidance was almost entirely lacking, as was vocational training, even though the country suffers from some skills shortages." Young people reported feeling directionless upon completing their education, uncertain about what employment opportunities existed or how to access them. Girls were channelled toward teaching or nursing; boys toward fishing or tourism. Systematic career guidance -- covering the full range of employment options, skills requirements, and educational pathways -- remained essentially absent from the education system, leaving students to make consequential decisions about their futures with minimal information.[^50]

The study recommended school-based career guidance backed by a research unit that could help balance job demand with education, and encourage young people into new opportunities. Some Island Councils had brought in advisors for short consultations, but these were ad hoc and unsustained.

## 10.9 Youth Employment and NEET (Not in Education, Employment, or Training)

### Youth Unemployment

Youth unemployment in the Maldives is a major social policy challenge. As of International Youth Day 2021, the country had 257,215 persons aged 18-35, with a labour force participation rate of 69.9% and an unemployment rate of 7.3% among this cohort. However, these headline figures understate the severity of the problem. The actual youth NEET rate -- encompassing those who are neither in education, employment, nor training -- is substantially higher, particularly among young women and those on outer atolls.[^51]

The Youth Vulnerability study (2019) reported that "a quarter of young Maldivians are out of work," while simultaneously "more than 100,000 foreigners are working on the islands legally and perhaps another 30,000 illegally." This paradox -- high youth unemployment alongside massive reliance on imported labour -- points to fundamental structural failures in the education-to-employment transition. The economy generates jobs, but the education system does not produce graduates with the skills or orientation to fill them. Tourism, construction, and fishing -- the three largest employment sectors -- depend heavily on foreign workers, while Maldivian graduates cluster in public administration, teaching, and healthcare.[^52]

### Structural Barriers

Several interlocking barriers perpetuate youth unemployment:

**The corruption-employment nexus**: The Youth Vulnerability study placed corruption at the top of young people's concerns, ahead of education, drugs, unemployment, housing, gangs, and violent extremism. The connection to employment is direct: 67% of respondents believed that "you could only get a job if you had the right connections." On many islands, the government is the primary employer, and civil service positions are allocated based on political loyalty rather than qualification. "Corruption puts people off studying. Why bother getting a higher degree when it doesn't help you get a job?" asked one participant. This perception actively undermines educational motivation.[^53]

**The geographic concentration of opportunity**: Economic opportunities are overwhelmingly concentrated in Male and on resort islands. Outer island residents face a stark choice: remain in a community with minimal employment options, or migrate to Male where they face housing costs that consume much of their income. The lack of decentralised economic opportunity creates a brain drain from the atolls and concentrates population pressure on an already congested capital.

**Skills mismatch**: Education is misaligned with job market demands. "Many people are now educated beyond the point that they are looking for an entry level job in tourism or fishing. But few have the vocational or technical qualifications for managerial and engineering jobs, which are in great demand in resorts and which are mostly filled by foreigners."[^54]

**Gender barriers**: Women face additional employment barriers including workplace harassment, discrimination in hiring, limited opportunities in male-dominated sectors (tourism, fishing, construction), and cultural expectations around family responsibilities. The Youth Vulnerability study found that "women across the country complained about being asked about their marital status, their children, and their clothing in interviews, saying this was routine." Young women entering the workforce reported systematic workplace harassment that was treated as normal.[^55]

### The Entrepreneurship Gap

Self-employment and entrepreneurship represent a potential pathway out of the unemployment trap, but face their own barriers. Young people interviewed for the Youth Vulnerability study complained about the difficulty of starting businesses: bank loans required collateral (typically land or property) that young people did not have, advice on business plan development was unavailable, and affordable commercial space was impossible to find, particularly on outer islands. "Very little has been done to inculcate an entrepreneurial mindset in people: government jobs are still favoured by many despite the political challenges they present."[^56]

## 10.10 Youth Vulnerability and Risk Factors

### Overlapping Risk Factors

Youth vulnerability in the Maldives cannot be understood through any single lens. The UNDP/NCTC Youth Vulnerability in the Maldives study (2019) -- the most comprehensive assessment of its kind -- documented how multiple risk factors intersect and reinforce one another. A young man on an outer island with limited education, no employment prospects, inadequate housing, and exposure to drug networks faces a compound vulnerability that no single intervention can address. The study's polling of 173 respondents identified seven priority challenges facing young Maldivians, ranked in order of concern:

1. Corruption
2. Education
3. Drugs
4. Unemployment
5. Housing
6. Gangs
7. Violent jihad (ranked last, placed in last position by 60% of respondents)

The ordering is significant: young Maldivians themselves see systemic governance failures (corruption, education) as more pressing than the security-focused concerns (gangs, extremism) that dominate international discourse about the Maldives.[^57]

### Drug Use and Substance Abuse

Drug use has emerged as one of the most destructive forces in the lives of young Maldivians. The nexus between unemployment, social isolation, gang recruitment, and substance abuse creates a self-reinforcing cycle. The Youth Vulnerability study found that "most of those interviewed pointed to drugs as a serious problem in their communities, along with gangs and crime." Drug treatment programmes were criticised as reaching "too few people" with "too little" post-treatment support. The overlap between drug rehabilitation programmes, prison populations, and gang recruitment creates pathways into further vulnerability rather than recovery.[^58]

### Gang Involvement

Gangs in Male and Addu City represent a significant threat to youth safety and development. The Youth Vulnerability study documented that gangs provide what the formal system fails to deliver: "identity, protection, income, and support." Gangs flourish in environments characterised by family breakdown, poverty, academic failure, and unemployment. "Early academic failure and inflexible education systems can worsen the problems and encourage young people to join," the study noted. Almost all respondents from Male and Addu felt that gangs were a problem -- "ubiquitous and encountered almost daily." The link between gangs and politicians, with allegations that gangs are used during elections and enjoy impunity in return, further erodes young people's faith in institutions.[^59]

### Social Isolation and Mental Health

The study found that 69% of respondents felt social isolation was a serious problem for young people, while only 39% agreed that community spirit was strong on their island. This finding is notable in a traditionally communal society and suggests rapid social change is fraying the community bonds that once served as protective factors. The combination of social isolation, untreated mental health conditions, substance abuse, and lack of meaningful engagement creates fertile ground for recruitment into destructive behaviours, whether gang membership, drug dealing, or extremist networks.[^60]

## 10.11 Youth and Violent Extremism

### Context and Scale

The Maldives has attracted disproportionate international attention for its perceived vulnerability to violent extremism. A number of Maldivian men and women are believed to have travelled to Syria to join armed groups, primarily Jabhat al-Nusra (later Hayat Tahrir al-Sham), an organisation aligned with Al Qaeda. Some are believed to have been killed; others remain unaccounted for. Their presence in the Middle East led to concerns that the Maldives was becoming a "breeding ground for jihadis."[^61]

However, the Youth Vulnerability study provided crucial nuance to this narrative. When respondents were asked to rank their concerns, violent extremism was consistently placed last -- ranked seventh by the young people themselves, with 60% placing it in last position. Only 10% of respondents believed that people on their islands supported extremism. The study cautioned against an overstatement of the extremism threat, noting that "the lack of nuance in the analysis of these issues in the Maldives has created a distorted perception of threats and an overstatement of support for violence."[^62]

### Drivers and Pathways

The study identified several factors that create vulnerability to radicalisation, while also noting factors that counter the escalation of extremism:

**Drivers of vulnerability**: Limited educational and employment opportunities; social exclusion and disillusionment with democratic politics; exposure to extremist rhetoric through social media and visiting preachers; the overlap between prison populations, drug networks, and extremist recruiters; and the use of religious conformity as a political tool.

**Countering factors**: No active violent conflict within the Maldives; no history of organised violent conflict in past decades; government presence on all inhabited islands; no religious minorities to scapegoat; and the fact that Salafi views, while gaining a foothold, remain in the minority, with most Maldivians committed to the values of peace, non-violence, and equality. Indeed, more than 80% of young people surveyed were optimistic about the future, rejected violence, and wanted to remain in the Maldives.[^63]

### The National Action Plan on P/CVE

The National Action Plan on Preventing and Countering Violent Extremism (NAP P/CVE) 2020-2024, developed by the National Counter Terrorism Centre, provides the policy framework for addressing radicalisation. The NAP identifies education as a key intervention area, calling for critical thinking education, interfaith understanding, civic engagement, and community-level resilience building. However, teachers have reported feeling "frightened and threatened talking about these subjects" -- a significant barrier to implementing deradicalisation education in the classroom.[^64]

The study's recommendation was clear: the most effective counter to extremism was not security-focused deradicalisation but addressing the underlying drivers -- corruption, unemployment, education quality, housing, and social exclusion -- that create vulnerability in the first place.

## 10.12 Sexual and Reproductive Health Education

### The Contested Terrain

Sexual and reproductive health (SRH) education in the Maldives occupies deeply contested terrain between public health imperatives and conservative social and religious norms. Despite the country's Constitutional commitment to the right to health, and its obligations under international human rights conventions, access to comprehensive SRH education remains severely limited, particularly for adolescents and young people.

### The HRCM Assessment

The Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM) produced a 2016 assessment on the accessibility of SRH education, identifying significant gaps. Key findings included:

- SRH education in schools was limited to basic biological reproduction within the science curriculum, with no comprehensive coverage of contraception, STI prevention, consent, gender-based violence, or sexual rights.
- Teachers reported discomfort and inadequate training in delivering SRH content, often skipping or minimising these topics.
- Cultural and religious sensitivities created an environment where parents, community leaders, and some school administrators actively opposed SRH education, viewing it as incompatible with Islamic values.
- Young people reported having to seek information through the internet, peers, or informal channels, leading to misinformation and increased risk-taking behaviour.[^65]

### Health Outcomes

The consequences of inadequate SRH education are reflected in health data. The Maldives faces challenges with adolescent pregnancy, sexually transmitted infections, and limited awareness of reproductive rights among young people. The Global School-Based Student Health Survey (2009) provided some baseline data on adolescent health behaviours, but the age of this data means that current patterns are poorly understood. The lack of updated, nationally representative data on adolescent sexual health is itself an indicator of the policy silence around these issues.[^66]

### The Way Forward

Advocacy for improved SRH education has come primarily from international organisations (UNFPA, WHO), the HRCM, and civil society groups. Progress has been incremental: some elements of "health and wellbeing" education have been integrated into the national curriculum, and teacher training has included limited SRH components. However, the fundamental tension between comprehensive, evidence-based SRH education and prevailing social norms remains unresolved. Young people -- particularly young women -- continue to enter adulthood with limited understanding of their reproductive health, rights, and available services.[^67]

## 10.13 Research Findings on Education and Youth

### Key Evidence Synthesis

The body of research on education and youth in the Maldives, drawn from government analyses, international assessments, and academic studies, converges on several critical findings:

**1. The education system produces qualifications but not capabilities.** Young Maldivians increasingly hold formal qualifications -- driven by the First Degree Scheme's expansion of university access -- but employers consistently report skills deficits. The mismatch between educational outputs and labour market requirements is a central structural weakness of the system. The economy needs technicians, engineers, hospitality professionals, marine biologists, and skilled tradespeople; the education system produces generalists in management, administration, and religious studies.[^68]

**2. Geographic inequity is a defining inequity in the system.** The gap in educational quality between Male and the outer atolls is not merely a resource allocation problem but a structural consequence of national development patterns that have concentrated investment, infrastructure, and opportunity in the capital. Atoll students receive inferior instruction, in worse facilities, with fewer subject choices, from less qualified teachers, and face higher costs to access secondary and higher education. This geographic inequity is a primary driver of internal migration, social dislocation, and youth vulnerability.[^69]

**3. TVET reform remains incomplete despite decades of diagnosis.** Every major assessment since at least the ILO's 2012 evaluation has identified the same problems: TVET is perceived as second-class, underfunded, disconnected from industry, and institutionally fragmented. While initiatives like MEERY and the Maldives Polytechnic expansion show intent, the fundamental challenge of transforming social attitudes toward vocational education and integrating TVET into the mainstream education pathway has not been achieved.

**4. Youth disengagement is systemic, not individual.** The Youth Vulnerability study decisively demonstrates that the challenges facing young Maldivians -- unemployment, substance abuse, gang involvement, social isolation, and vulnerability to extremism -- are not the result of individual moral failures but of systemic policy failures in education, housing, employment, governance, and justice. Young people ranked corruption and education as their top concerns, above drugs, gangs, and extremism, indicating a sophisticated understanding of root causes.[^70]

**5. The gender paradox in education requires attention.** Women significantly outperform men in the education system -- comprising 59-64% of higher education enrolments -- yet face worse employment outcomes due to labour market discrimination, harassment, and cultural barriers. Meanwhile, young men are dropping out at higher rates, with 88% of higher education dropouts being male, suggesting a disengagement of young men from formal education that has serious implications for their long-term prospects.

**6. Data systems are inadequate for evidence-based policymaking.** The Education Sector Analysis dedicated an entire chapter to the weaknesses of the Maldives Education Management Information System (MEMIS) and the broader data ecosystem. School census data was incomplete, learning outcome assessments infrequent, and longitudinal tracking of student pathways essentially non-existent. Without robust data, education policy operates more on assumptions than evidence.[^71]

**7. Education reform must transcend political cycles.** The repeated reorganisation of education governance with each change of government -- splitting and merging ministries, replacing key officials, and adjusting priorities -- undermines institutional memory and policy continuity. The Education Sector Analysis and Youth Vulnerability study both called for long-term education plans developed through broad consultation and insulated from electoral politics.[^72]

### The PRIME Programme and At-Risk Youth

Transparency Maldives' 2021 assessment documented the PRIME programme, an initiative targeting at-risk youth through mentorship, skills development, and civic engagement. PRIME represents one of the few sustained efforts to reach young people who have already disengaged from formal education and employment. The programme's approach -- combining practical skills with personal development and community connection -- aligns with the research evidence on what works for disconnected youth. However, its scale remains limited relative to the size of the at-risk population.[^73]

### Government Training Needs

The Statistics Booklet 2019-2023 identified 12,724 training requirements in government sectors for 2021-2023, with 7,511 at MQA Level 5 and above. By September 2023, 9,275 individuals had been trained at MQA Level 5 and above, surpassing the overall target. However, 19 of the individual fields still needed to meet their targets, and the focus was shifting to training the remaining individuals within two years. This data indicates that while higher education expansion has been substantial, strategic alignment between training investment and sectoral needs remains a work in progress.[^74]

### Toward an Integrated Youth Policy

The accumulated evidence points toward the need for an integrated youth policy that addresses education, employment, housing, health, and civic participation as interconnected domains rather than siloed ministerial responsibilities. Young Maldivians themselves have been clear about what they need: quality education accessible on their home islands; career guidance that connects learning to employment; vocational training that is respected and leads to real jobs; housing they can afford; an economy where merit matters more than connections; and a political system that includes rather than exploits them.

The Youth Vulnerability study concluded with a blunt assessment: "What is clear is that the Maldives does not lack awareness of these problems or the ability to solve them. There has instead been a lack of political will to implement reforms." This finding -- that the binding constraint on education and youth development is political commitment rather than technical knowledge or fiscal capacity -- is a central conclusion across the body of research.[^75]
