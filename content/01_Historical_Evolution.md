# PART I: HISTORICAL EVOLUTION OF SOCIAL POLICY IN THE MALDIVES

---

## 1.1 Pre-Independence Social Structures (Pre-1965)

The Maldives has been an independent nation-state for most of its recorded history, with a sultanate system governing the archipelago for centuries. Social welfare in the pre-modern era was organised almost entirely through Islamic institutions and kinship networks.

**Traditional social protection mechanisms:**

- **Zakat (obligatory almsgiving):** As a 100% Sunni Muslim nation, the Maldives relied on zakat as the primary institutionalised form of wealth redistribution. Zakat funds were collected and distributed locally, typically through island kadheeb (chiefs) and mosque committees, providing support to the poor, orphans, debtors, and travellers.[^1]

- **Extended family and island community networks:** The geographically dispersed nature of the Maldives – with populations spread across approximately 200 inhabited islands out of 1,192 total – created tight-knit island communities where informal social support was the norm. Families bore primary responsibility for care of children, the elderly, and persons with disabilities. The concept of "institutional care" was virtually non-existent.[^2]

- **Island-level governance:** Each inhabited island was governed by a kadheeb (island chief), appointed by the sultan, who served as the local administrator, dispute resolver, and overseer of community affairs. Social disputes – including family matters, child custody, and property inheritance – were resolved through a combination of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) and customary practice (urf).[^3]

- **Matrilineal traditions:** Unlike many South Asian Muslim societies, the Maldives historically exhibited matrilocal marriage patterns, where husbands moved to the wife's family island or household. Women held property rights and inheritance rights (as guaranteed under Islamic law), and female participation in fishing-adjacent economic activities (such as fish processing) was substantial. This gave Maldivian women a degree of economic agency unusual in the region, though political power remained firmly male-dominated.[^4]

> *→ **Extended Coverage:** For comprehensive analysis of historical women's status, matrilineal traditions, and the evolution of gender relations in Maldivian society, see **Part V: Gender, GBV, and Women's Empowerment, §5.76–§5.90** of this encyclopedia.*

**Colonial period and British protectorate (1887–1965):**

Under the British protectorate agreement (1887), the Maldives retained internal self-governance while Britain managed external defence and foreign affairs. The British had minimal interest in social policy intervention. Key developments during this period include:

- **1927:** The first modern school, Majeediyya School, was established in Malé, marking the beginning of formal education beyond traditional religious schools (edhuruge). For boys only initially.
- **1944:** Aminiya School opened for girls in Malé – one of the earliest formal educational institutions for women in the Indian Ocean region.
- **1952–1953:** The First Republic briefly replaced the sultanate but lasted only one year before the monarchy was restored. No significant social legislation was passed.
- **1953:** The first constitution of the republic was promulgated, though it was short-lived.
- **1960s:** A few health centres and schools were established in atoll capitals with minimal coverage.

**The political system of the ancient kingdom** was characterised by a hierarchy of chiefs and nobles serving under the sultan, with island-level governance carried out by kadheeb who wielded considerable local power. Social stratification was based on birth, with noble families (beys and kilegefaanu) enjoying privileges, and commoners having limited social mobility. The caste-like raivaru system, though less rigid than South Asian caste systems, influenced marriage patterns and social standing.[^5]

## 1.2 Early Republic: The Nasir Era (1968–1978)

On 11 November 1968, following a national referendum, the Maldives became a republic for the second time, with Ibrahim Nasir becoming the first president of the modern republic.

**Key social policy developments:**

- **Centralisation of governance in Malé:** Nasir consolidated political authority in the capital, weakening atoll-level governance structures. This pattern of extreme centralisation would shape social service delivery challenges for decades.

- **Basic education expansion:** The government began establishing primary schools in atoll capitals, though coverage remained extremely limited. The vast majority of children on outer islands received only Quran school (edhuruge) education.

- **Health infrastructure:** The Central Hospital in Malé (later renamed Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital, IGMH, in 1995) was the only significant medical facility. Outer islands had no modern healthcare. Traditional medicine (dhivehi beys) remained the primary health intervention for most of the population.

- **No formal social protection:** There was no pension system, no disability allowance, no unemployment insurance, and no social welfare agency. The family unit remained the sole safety net.

- **Migration to Malé begins:** Nasir's centralisation policies and the opening of the first tourist resort (Kurumba Village, 1972) began a pattern of internal migration to Malé that would accelerate dramatically in subsequent decades, creating extreme urban density and straining social infrastructure.[^6]

- **1974:** The first vocational training centre was established in Malé.

## 1.3 The Gayoom Era: Foundations of Modern Social Policy (1978–2008)

Maumoon Abdul Gayoom came to power in 1978 and governed for 30 years – the longest-serving leader in Maldivian history. His era saw the construction of most foundational social institutions.

### Early Gayoom Period (1978–1990)

- **Education drive:** Gayoom declared a national commitment to building at least one primary school on every inhabited island. By the mid-1980s, primary education coverage had expanded dramatically. The "Community School" model was introduced, where island communities contributed to school construction.
  - 1979: First A-Level (higher secondary) centre opened at Majeediyya School in Malé (boys)
  - 1981: Higher secondary offered at Aminiya School (girls)
  - 1984: Teacher Training Centre established in Malé
  - 1987: Tourism Training School established

- **Health system construction:** A network of atoll hospitals and regional health centres was gradually established:
  - 1978–1990: Health centres established in all 20 atoll capitals
  - 1985: First atoll-level hospital opened in Addu City
  - Regional hospitals gradually established in Kulhudhuffushi, Ungoofaaru, Gan, and Thinadhoo

- **UN engagement begins:** The Maldives joined the United Nations in 1965 and began engaging with international social development frameworks:
  - 1984: Ratified CEDAW (Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women), though with reservations on Articles 7(a) and 16
  - 1989: Work began on aligning with CRC (Convention on the Rights of the Child)

### The 1990s: Institutional Foundations

This decade saw the creation of the first dedicated social welfare institutions:

- **1991:** The Maldives ratified the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC), with reservations on Article 14(1) (freedom of religion) and Article 21 (adoption), based on Islamic law. The same year, the first child protection law was enacted – **Law on the Protection of the Rights of the Child (Law 9/91)**. This was largely declaratory, lacking enforcement mechanisms and operational definitions, but it established the legal principle that children have rights under Maldivian law.[^7]

> *→ **Extended Coverage:** For comprehensive analysis of the CRC ratification, the origins of Law 9/91, and the full institutional history leading to the CRPA 2019, see **Part IV: Child Protection and Child Rights, §4.1–§4.18** of this encyclopedia.*

- **1992:** The **Unit for the Rights of the Children (URC)** was established within the Ministry of Home Affairs, serving as the secretariat for the newly created **National Council for Children's Rights** (chaired by the Minister of Home Affairs). This marked the beginning of formal state-level child protection work. The URC began providing basic counselling services using volunteer counsellors given 2-week introductory courses.[^8]

- **1993:** First reporting cycle to the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child. Growing recognition of gender inequality led to the URC being moved to the **Ministry of Youth, Women and Social Welfare**.

- **1994:** The National Social Protection Programme was still non-existent. The elderly, disabled, and destitute relied entirely on family support or Islamic charity.

- **1996:** Second CRC reporting cycle. Efforts to draft a comprehensive Child Rights Protection Act began, with support from Defence for Children International and UNICEF. The **Maldives College of Higher Education (MCHE)** was established in 1998, consolidating multiple training centres.

- **1997:** First national A-Level examinations held at the Centre for Higher Secondary Education (CHSE) in Malé.

- **Late 1990s:** Emergence of drug use as a visible social problem, particularly among youth in Malé. Heroin and cannabis use began increasing, though systematic data was not yet collected. The government response was primarily punitive, with drug offenders imprisoned under existing penal code provisions.[^9]

### Early 2000s: Gathering Momentum

- **2000:** The **Maldives Accreditation Board (MAB)** was created as the first post-secondary education regulatory body.

- **2001:** Repeal of Chapter 3, Section 72 of the Maldives Penal Code Law 1/66, which had contained provisions for mandatory reporting of crimes. This created a gap in the legal framework for reporting child abuse that would not be addressed until the CRPA 2019.[^10]

- **2002–2003:** The first National Drug Use Survey was conducted, revealing the scale of substance abuse. Growing public concern about youth drug use drove calls for dedicated drug treatment services.

- **2003:** First National Narcotics Control Bureau established. Early drug rehabilitation efforts began, characterised by significant "practice dilemmas" – the tension between Islamic morality-based approaches and evidence-based harm reduction models.[^11]

- **2004:** The **Indian Ocean Tsunami** (26 December 2004) devastated the Maldives. Though the death toll (82 confirmed dead, 26 missing) was lower than in neighbouring countries, the economic and social impact was enormous:
  - 14 islands completely destroyed and evacuated permanently
  - 69 islands suffered severe damage
  - Approximately one-third of the population directly affected
  - Economic damage estimated at 62% of GDP
  - The disaster exposed the extreme vulnerability of atoll communities and catalysed international development assistance
  - UNICEF significantly expanded its programmes in the Maldives, creating a dedicated child protection programme (previously combined with education)
  - The disaster relief effort brought considerable international attention to social vulnerabilities in the Maldives[^12]

- **2005–2006:** Post-tsunami reconstruction drove significant institutional development:
  - **2005:** Social workers' roles expanded significantly. UNICEF and VSO provided technical support for developing policies and procedures.
  - **2006:** Groups of school leavers received training through programmes at the Ministry of Gender and Family and at the University of Kelaniya (Sri Lanka) – a 1-year Advanced Certificate course. These trained social workers were deployed to Family and Children Service Centres on the atolls.[^13]
  - **2006:** A child protection procedure manual was developed, drawing on England's "Working Together" guidance (DfE, 2003). However, the imported Western framework created practical tensions with local religious and cultural practices, particularly around parental disciplinary authority.[^14]
  - **2006:** The **Maldives Study on Women's Health and Life Experiences** was published – the first national survey of domestic violence. It revealed that approximately one in three women had experienced physical and/or sexual violence from an intimate partner.[^15]

> *→ **Extended Coverage:** For comprehensive analysis of GBV prevalence data beginning with this 2006 survey, the DVPA 2012 operational framework, and the full FPA response system, see **Part V: Gender, GBV, and Women's Empowerment, §5.1–§5.20** of this encyclopedia. For the social work profession history and the 2006–2007 training initiatives, see **Part IV: Child Protection and Child Rights, §4.59**.*

- **2007:** The government introduced the 1-year Advanced Diploma in Social Service Work, which later gave way to the full 4-year Bachelor of Social Work degree programme at the Faculty of Health Sciences.[^16]

- **2007:** A new Constitution was ratified by a Constituent Assembly, establishing a multi-party democratic system, separation of powers, an independent judiciary, and a comprehensive bill of rights including social and economic rights.

## 1.4 Democratic Transition and Social Reform (2008–2012)

The 2008 presidential election – the first multi-party democratic election in Maldivian history – brought Mohamed Nasheed (MDP) to power, defeating Gayoom. The change in government brought significant shifts in social policy direction.

### The Nasheed Administration (2008–2012)

**Universal health insurance – Madhana:** The Nasheed government introduced the Madhana health insurance scheme, later renamed **Aasandha** under subsequent governments. This represented the single largest social protection expansion in Maldivian history – providing universal health insurance coverage to all citizens.

**Social protection expansion:**
- **Introduction of the Senior Citizen Allowance:** A non-contributory monthly pension of MVR 2,000 for all citizens aged 65 and above, later increased to MVR 5,000. This was the Maldives' first universal cash transfer programme and transformed elderly welfare.
- **Disability Allowance:** A monthly allowance for persons with disabilities registered with the NSPA.
- **Single Parent Allowance:** Monthly cash transfers to single parents (overwhelmingly mothers) to support child-rearing costs.
- **Foster Parent Allowance:** Payments to families providing foster care for children in state protection.

**Institutional restructuring:**
- Child protection was moved to the **Ministry of Health**, under the **Department of Gender and Family Protection Services**. This shifted the framing toward a rights-based health perspective and saw improved case management.[^17]
- The toll-free 24-hour **National Child Helpline (1412)** was established with assistance from Child Helpline International.

**Decentralisation Act 2010 (Act 7/2010):**
- Replaced the centralised atoll chief system with elected local councils
- Three to four atolls were grouped into seven administrative provinces
- **Child and Family Protection Services (CFPS)** was centralised to manage all **Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs)** situated across the 20 atolls
- The Act had profound implications for social service delivery, delegating some powers to local councils while maintaining central oversight through CFPS[^18]

**Community Social Groups (CSGs):**
- Initiated in 2010 by the Ministry of Health and Family
- Provided a community-led approach to addressing social problems and facilitating timely access to services
- Supported by UNICEF
- Later revived under the **IBAMA** ("you and I together") project – a multi-sectoral community-based system for preventing and responding to violence against children, the elderly, and other vulnerable populations[^19]

**Climate advocacy:** Nasheed gained international attention for climate advocacy, including an underwater cabinet meeting highlighting sea-level rise threats, drawing global attention to the Maldives' climate vulnerability and its social dimensions.

**Political crisis and removal:**
On 7 February 2012, Nasheed resigned under pressure from political opponents, police, and military – in what he described as a coup. Vice President Mohamed Waheed Hassan assumed the presidency. Despite political turmoil, the social protection programmes introduced under Nasheed were not reversed, establishing them as politically difficult to dismantle.

## 1.5 Political Instability and Policy Continuity (2012–2018)

### The Waheed Interim and Yameen Administration

**Waheed Hassan (2012–2013):** Maintained existing social programmes. The period was characterised by political instability and contested legitimacy.

**Abdulla Yameen Abdul Gayoom (2013–2018):**

- **Aasandha expanded:** The Madhana/Aasandha universal health insurance system was significantly expanded under the Yameen government, with the scheme eventually covering nearly all medical expenses at designated providers. Aasandha expenditure grew rapidly, becoming one of the largest items in the national budget.

- **Disability Act implementation:** The **Disability Act 2010 (Act 8/2010)** began to be more actively implemented, with the establishment of a **National Registry of Persons with Disabilities** and the allocation of disability allowances.

- **Domestic Violence Prevention Act 2012 (Act 3/2012):** Enacted during the transition period, this law for the first time criminalised domestic violence as a distinct offence, established protection orders, and mandated service provision for victims. However, implementation challenges remained severe, including lack of shelters, limited police training, and cultural resistance to reporting.[^20]

- **Sexual Offences Act 2014 (Act 17/2014):** Modernised the legal framework for sexual crimes, though tensions between Islamic legal principles (particularly around zina – extramarital sex) and child protection imperatives remained unresolved.[^21]

- **Social Protection Act 2014:** Formalised the various cash transfer programmes established under Nasheed.

- **Gender Equality Act 2016 (Act 18/2016):** Enacted to promote gender equality across public and private spheres, though enforcement remained weak.

- **Prevention of Human Trafficking Act 2013 (Act 12/2013):** Addressed growing concerns about trafficking of migrant workers (primarily from Bangladesh, India, and Sri Lanka) in the construction, domestic work, and fishing sectors.

- **National Social Protection Agency (NSPA):** Established/strengthened as the dedicated agency for administering social protection programmes including Aasandha, disability allowance, single parent allowance, foster parent allowance, and food/utility subsidies.

- **Islamic University of Maldives (IUM):** Established by Act in 2015, upgrading the former College of Islamic Studies.

- **Maldives Qualifications Authority (MQA):** Reconstituted in 2010 from the former Maldives Accreditation Board, with expanded regulatory mandate.

- **Housing mega-projects:** The Yameen government launched massive reclamation and housing projects, particularly Hulhumalé Phase II, to address the acute housing shortage in the Greater Malé region.

- **Fiscal strain:** Rapid expansion of social protection spending (particularly Aasandha) created significant fiscal pressure, raising questions about long-term sustainability.[^22]

## 1.6 The Solih Administration: Consolidation and Expansion (2018–2023)

Ibrahim Mohamed Solih (MDP) won the 2018 presidential election on a platform emphasising justice, good governance, and social development.

**Key social policy developments:**

- **Child Rights Protection Act 2019 (Act 19/2019):** The most comprehensive child protection legislation in Maldivian history, replacing the limited Law 9/91. The CRPA:
  - Defined categories of child abuse (physical violence, sexual abuse, exploitation, negligence) in alignment with the CRC
  - Established the **Council for the Protection of the Rights of the Child** with multi-sectoral representation
  - Created the **Children's Ombudsperson** position
  - Mandated reporting of child abuse (Article 126) with criminal penalties for failure to report (2 years imprisonment; 5 years if failure motivated by financial gain)
  - Established judicial orders for intervention: Interim Assessment Order (Article 80), Protection Order (Article 90), Supervision Order (Article 93)
  - Gave legal recognition to community protection groups
  - Incorporated "Responsibilities and duties of a child" (Article 36 a-h) to address cultural tensions around children's rights and parental authority[^23]

> *→ **Extended Coverage:** For comprehensive analysis of the CRPA 2019 – its provisions, the CFPS case management system, mandatory reporting enforcement, and implementation gaps – see **Part IV: Child Protection and Child Rights, §4.1–§4.18** of this encyclopedia.*

- **Juvenile Justice Act 2019 (Act 18/2019):**
  - Set the age of criminal responsibility at 15 years
  - Prioritised rehabilitation over retribution
  - Prohibited the death penalty for juveniles
  - However, did not abolish flogging, raising concerns especially for child victims of sexual exploitation aged 13+[^24]

> *→ **Extended Coverage:** For comprehensive analysis of the Juvenile Justice Act 2019, pre-2019 systemic failures, and the current juvenile justice architecture, see **Part IV: Child Protection and Child Rights, §4.72** and **§4.77** of this encyclopedia.*

- **Education Act 2020 (Act 24/2020):** Established compulsory education until age 16 and codified students' rights.

- **National Social Protection Framework 2022–2026:** A comprehensive policy document guiding social protection reform, developed with World Bank support. Focused on improving targeting, reducing leakage, and building an integrated social registry.

- **Strategic Action Plan (SAP) 2019–2023:** Included extensive social sector goals across education, health, social protection, child protection, gender equality, and decentralisation.

- **COVID-19 response (2020–2021):**
  - Income support programmes for workers affected by lockdowns
  - Expanded Aasandha coverage for COVID-related treatment
  - Rapid livelihood assessments (with UNDP) revealed severe economic impacts, particularly on tourism-dependent communities, women, and informal sector workers
  - GBV cases increased during lockdown periods
  - Mental health impacts emerged as a significant concern[^25]

- **World Bank Social Protection System Review (2022):** Comprehensive assessment of the Maldives' social protection architecture, identifying fragmentation, targeting challenges, and fiscal sustainability concerns.

- **Social Registry feasibility study (2020):** World Bank-supported assessment of establishing a unified social registry and benefit management system to improve targeting and reduce duplication.

## 1.7 The Muizzu Administration: Current Directions (2023–Present)

Mohamed Muizzu (PNC/PPM) assumed the presidency in November 2023 with a platform emphasising national sovereignty, infrastructure development (particularly housing), and Islamic values.

**Early social policy directions:**

- **Ministry restructuring:** The social welfare ministry was renamed the **Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD)**, signalling a focus on family-centred approaches.

- **Housing as social policy:** The Muizzu government has emphasised housing construction – particularly in Hulhumalé and Gulhifalhu – as a central plank of social policy, responding to the chronic housing shortage that affects virtually all Maldivian families.

- **Continued social protection programmes:** Cash transfer programmes (senior citizen allowance, disability allowance, single parent allowance) have been maintained, though there are ongoing discussions about targeting reforms and fiscal sustainability.

- **Strategic Plan 2024–2028:** Sets out medium-term priorities including social protection reform, child protection strengthening, and addressing youth vulnerability.

- **NSPA reforms:** Ongoing efforts to improve social protection administration, including digital transformation and data integration initiatives.
