# CRPD Obligations
## Combined Initial and Second Periodic Report to the United Nations Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

**State Party:** Republic of Maldives
**Report Type:** Combined Initial and Second Periodic Report
**Document Reference:** CRPD/C/MDV/2 (Draft)
**Reporting Period:** 2010–2025
**Date of Preparation:** March 2026
**Ministry Responsible:** Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD)

---

> *Note to Encyclopedia readers: This document is a draft periodic report prepared in the format and style of official State Party reports submitted to the UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD Committee) under CRPD/C/2/3 reporting guidelines. It is intended as a comprehensive policy reference and advocacy resource. It incorporates data from the 2022 Population and Housing Census, the National Disability Registry, the Global Disability Fund's 2025 Situational Analysis, and the outcomes of the Maldives' initial CRPD review at the Committee's 33rd Session (August 2025).*

## PART I: INTRODUCTION

### 1. State Party Information

1. The Republic of Maldives ratified the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) on 5 April 2010. The Optional Protocol to the Convention has not been ratified. Maldives submitted its initial report (CRPD/C/MDV/1) in 2024, which was reviewed by the CRPD Committee during its 33rd Session (11–29 August 2025). This combined document serves as the State Party's second periodic report and responds to the Concluding Observations adopted by the Committee in August 2025.

2. The Maldives is a small island developing state consisting of 1,192 coral islands grouped in 26 natural atolls, of which 187 are inhabited. The country's geographic dispersion presents structural challenges for the equitable delivery of social services, including services for persons with disabilities. The capital, Malé, houses approximately 38% of the national population of around 521,000 persons but concentrates the majority of specialised disability services.^[Maldives Population and Housing Census 2022, Maldives National Statistical Bureau, 2023, https://statisticsmaldives.gov.mv]

3. The reporting period covered in this document is 2010 to 2025, encompassing the initial and second periodic reporting cycles. Where relevant, the report notes distinct sub-periods: the pre-legislative period prior to 2010; the early implementation phase of 2010–2018; and the consolidation phase of 2019–2025.

### 2. Consultation Process

4. This report was prepared under the coordination of the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD), with inputs from the Agency for Disability and Different Ability (ADDA), the National Social Protection Agency (NSPA), the Ministry of Education, the Ministry of Health, the Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM), and the Attorney General's Office.

5. The State Party acknowledges the importance of meaningful participation of organisations of persons with disabilities (OPDs) in the preparation of periodic reports, consistent with Article 4(3) of the Convention. Consultations were conducted with disability civil society organisations during 2024–2025, building on the multi-stakeholder process used in the preparation of the 2025 Situational Analysis on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, which was developed in partnership with UNDP Maldives and the UN Partnership to Promote the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNPRPD).^[Situational Analysis of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in the Maldives, UNDP/UNPRPD/MoSFD, January 2025, https://maldives.un.org/sites/default/files/2025-01/Situation-Analysis-of-the-Rights-of-Persons-with-Disabilities-in-the-Maldives-country-report-1.pdf]

6. The State Party acknowledges that the 2025 Situational Analysis identified a significant gap in the systematic participation of persons with psychosocial disabilities in consultation processes. This group remains among the most marginalised, and their exclusion from formal consultation processes is a challenge the State Party commits to addressing in the next reporting cycle. Similarly, women and girls with disabilities were noted to be substantially underrepresented due to restrictions imposed by families.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, CRPD 33rd Session — Maldives, August 2025, https://www.ungeneva.org/en/news-media/meeting-summary/2025/08/experts-committee-rights-persons-disabilities-appreciate-efforts]

### 3. Overview of the Disability Situation in Maldives

7. According to the 2022 Population and Housing Census, which applied the Washington Group Short Set (WG-SS) methodology, 24,401 persons in Maldives live with a disability, representing approximately 7% of the resident population. Of these: 55% are women; 65% live outside the capital Malé in atolls and outer islands; and the majority are of working age (18–59 years).^[Maldives Population and Housing Census 2022, Maldives National Statistical Bureau, 2023]

8. The National Disability Registry (NDR), managed by the National Social Protection Agency (NSPA) and formally launched on 5 January 2023, recorded 10,701 registrants as of December 2022, rising to 13,656 by late 2024 and 14,566 by July 2025. The gap between Census estimates (24,401) and NDR registrations (14,566) reflects both registration barriers and the NDR's historical linkage to financial assistance eligibility, which has discouraged registration by those with mild or moderate disabilities.^[NSPA Disability Registry Data, cited in CRPD/C/MDV/1 State Party Report, 2024; UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]

9. The dominant disability categories registered in the NDR include physical/mobility impairments, visual impairments, hearing impairments, and intellectual disabilities. Persons with psychosocial (mental health-related) disabilities remain largely absent from administrative data due to gaps in census methodology and the social stigma associated with mental health conditions.^[Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, May 2025, https://globaldisabilityfund.org/new/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/maldives-country-brief.pdf]

10. The disability profile of Maldives is shaped by its developmental context. Non-communicable diseases (NCDs), consanguineous marriages in geographically isolated communities, inadequate prenatal care in outer islands, and limited rehabilitative services have historically contributed to disability prevalence. Climate vulnerability — including flooding, storm surges, and saltwater intrusion affecting freshwater supplies — disproportionately affects persons with disabilities who have fewer resources to evacuate or adapt.

## PART II: IMPLEMENTATION OF SPECIFIC ARTICLES

---

### ARTICLES 1–4: GENERAL PROVISIONS, DEFINITIONS, GENERAL OBLIGATIONS, AND PRINCIPLES

#### Pre-2019 Situation

11. Prior to 2010, Maldives had no dedicated legislation on disability rights. The dominant paradigm was charitable and welfare-based, with provision centred on the Reehendi shelter for destitute persons, which also accommodated persons with severe disabilities in conditions that did not meet rights-based standards. Disability was conceptualised primarily through a medical model as a personal impairment requiring institutional care, rather than as a condition arising from the interaction between impairment and attitudinal and environmental barriers.

12. The enactment of the Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010) — formally titled the Act for the Protection of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Provision of Financial Assistance — marked the first recognition in Maldivian law of disability as a rights-based concern. The Act adopted language broadly consistent with the social model of disability, recognising the obligations of the State to provide reasonable accommodation, equal opportunities, and non-discrimination in employment and access to services.^[Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010), Republic of Maldives, 2010]

13. However, the Act retained elements of the medical/welfare model, defining disability primarily through medical categorisation, and its implementation through the period 2010–2018 was characterised by weak institutional capacity, limited budgetary allocation, and absence of monitoring mechanisms. The Regulation on Protection of Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Financial Assistance (2011/R-3) established operational procedures for disability certification and allowance payments but did not create robust enforcement mechanisms.^[Regulation 2011/R-3 on Protection of Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Financial Assistance]

#### 2019–2025 Developments

14. The period 2019–2025 has seen incremental but meaningful progress toward alignment with Convention principles. Key legislative developments include:

- **Regulation on Determination and Registry of Persons with Disabilities (2021/R-54)**: This regulation reformed the disability assessment process, establishing clearer criteria for disability determination and laying the foundation for the National Disability Registry (NDR), formally operationalised in January 2023.^[Regulation 2021/R-54 on Determination and Registry of Persons with Disabilities]
- **Employment Regulation (2024/R-9)**: A dedicated regulation on providing employment opportunities for persons with disabilities, encouraging their hiring across civil service and state-owned enterprises (SOEs).^[Employment Regulation 2024/R-9, Republic of Maldives; UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]
- **Education Act (2021)**: Includes provisions for inclusive education and special educational needs, building on the 2013 Inclusive Education Policy.^[Education Act, Republic of Maldives, 2021]

15. The Disability Act (8/2010) is currently undergoing a comprehensive participatory review, announced before the Committee during the initial report examination in August 2025, to address identified gaps and bring domestic legislation closer to full CRPD compliance.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, CRPD 33rd Session — Maldives, August 2025]

16. The State Party acknowledges that the definition of disability in domestic law does not yet fully reflect the Convention's interactive model (Article 1), and that the prohibition of discrimination on grounds of disability in the Constitution (Article 20) has not been supplemented by robust statutory anti-discrimination provisions with accessible complaints mechanisms and effective remedies.

### ARTICLE 5: EQUALITY AND NON-DISCRIMINATION

#### Pre-2019 Situation

17. The Constitution of the Maldives (2008) prohibits discrimination on several grounds, including "physical or mental disability" (Article 20). Article 36 provides that the State shall endeavour to provide persons with disabilities with such programmes as would enable them to lead normal lives. These provisions constituted the foundational non-discrimination framework prior to the CRPD ratification.

18. However, the Constitution's non-discrimination clause was not accompanied by implementing legislation that defined direct and indirect discrimination, provided for reasonable accommodation as a legal obligation, or established accessible complaint mechanisms for persons with disabilities. The Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM), established under Law No. 6/2006, received disability-related complaints but lacked enforcement powers beyond recommendations, and disability complaints formed only a small proportion of its caseload.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

19. No standalone anti-discrimination law has been enacted during the reporting period. Anti-discrimination provisions for persons with disabilities remain dispersed across the Disability Act (2010), the Employment Act (2/2008), and constitutional guarantees. This legislative fragmentation means there is no comprehensive, sector-spanning anti-discrimination statute that covers access to goods and services, healthcare, education, and public life — areas the Committee has consistently identified as requiring explicit statutory protection.

20. The State Party acknowledges the CRPD Committee's concern, expressed during the August 2025 review, that policy implementation remains weak and legal protections are not effectively enforced.^[Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, May 2025] HRCM has produced disability-focused monitoring reports and receives individual complaints but cannot issue binding remedies. The State Party commits, in the next legislative cycle, to reviewing options for establishing a more effective anti-discrimination enforcement mechanism.

21. The concept of reasonable accommodation remains underdeveloped in Maldivian law. While the Disability Act obliges employers to provide "suitable conditions" for persons with disabilities, there is no statutory definition of reasonable accommodation, no clarity on the threshold of disproportionate burden, and no procedural guidance for workplace accommodation requests.

### ARTICLE 6: WOMEN WITH DISABILITIES

#### Pre-2019 Situation

22. Prior to 2019, there was no policy or legislative instrument specifically addressing the intersectional situation of women with disabilities. Disability policy treated persons with disabilities as a homogeneous group, while gender policy did not integrate disability dimensions. Women with disabilities were effectively invisible in both systems.

23. Research and anecdotal evidence indicated that women with disabilities faced compounded disadvantage: higher rates of exclusion from education and employment; greater dependence on family caregivers (predominantly female family members); elevated vulnerability to gender-based violence (GBV) with reduced access to protection services; and institutional barriers to accessing justice. These concerns were not reflected in systematic data collection.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

24. The 2022 Census data confirmed the gender dimension of disability: 55% of the 24,401 persons with disabilities identified are women. Despite this demographic reality, there remains a significant absence of strategies, policies, and disaggregated data specifically addressing women with disabilities — a concern explicitly noted by the CRPD Committee during the August 2025 review.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, CRPD 33rd Session — Maldives, August 2025]

25. A notable economic inclusion initiative was introduced in 2024: a five-year financial scheme of MVR 100 million to support women entrepreneurs, with 25% of allocations reserved for women with disabilities.^[CRPD/C/MDV/1 State Party Report and UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025] This constitutes the most significant gender-disability intersectional measure of the reporting period.

26. The Family Protection Authority (FPA), established in 2012 under the Prevention of Domestic Violence Act (3/2012), provides services for survivors of domestic violence, including women with disabilities. However, FPA services and shelter facilities are not fully accessible to women with sensory or physical impairments, and capacity for disability-responsive case management remains limited. Sign language interpretation is not systematically available at FPA intake points.

27. The State Party recognises the Committee's finding that the lack of gender perspectives in disability laws and the absence of disability perspectives in gender policies constitutes a structural gap requiring attention in the next policy cycle.

### ARTICLE 7: CHILDREN WITH DISABILITIES

#### Pre-2019 Situation

28. Prior to 2018, children with disabilities had limited access to specialised educational and developmental support, particularly outside Malé. The Child Rights Act (9/1991) and subsequent child protection legislation did not specifically address the rights of children with disabilities as a distinct category requiring additional safeguards. Children with severe or complex disabilities were frequently kept at home without access to education, therapy, or social participation.

29. The dominant practice was to place children with intellectual and developmental disabilities in "special units" attached to mainstream schools, where they were educated in segregated settings with minimal interaction with the broader school community. This approach, while providing some educational access, fell short of the Convention's vision of genuinely inclusive education.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

30. The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA) was established in 2018, providing specialised early intervention and educational services for children on the autism spectrum primarily in Malé. This was a significant institutional development, though it raised questions about the balance between specialised provision and inclusive education.^[Maldives Institute for Autism, established 2018]

31. The Department of Inclusive Education within the Ministry of Education has intensified efforts to mainstream children with special educational needs (SEN) into mainstream classrooms, supported by SEN-trained teachers. As of 2024, every mainstream school has at least one teacher with SEN training, and the role of "inclusive ambassador" has been introduced.^[UNICEF Maldives, Disability-Inclusive Education Practices in Maldives, 2022, https://www.unicef.org/maldives/media/2936/file/Disability-Inclusive%20Education%20Practices%20in%20Maldives.pdf]

32. In 2024, the Ministry of Education, in partnership with UNICEF and the World Bank, mobilised USD 9.5 million through the Global Partnership for Education (GPE) under the "THAIBA" Education Transformation Agenda, with Equity and Inclusion as a priority reform area. This grant explicitly targets children with disabilities as among the most vulnerable learners.^[UNICEF Maldives, Towards More Inclusive and Equitable Education, 2024]

33. Despite these advances, significant challenges persist. Findings from a 2024 study on inclusive education implementation confirmed that teacher training gaps, inadequate teacher-student ratios, insufficient physical accessibility of school buildings, and attitudinal barriers remain major obstacles.^[Implementation of Inclusive Education in a Selected School in Malé, Maldives: Teachers' Experiences, ResearchGate, 2024, https://researchgate.net/publication/383514987] Children with disabilities in outer islands continue to face severely limited access to SEN support, as specialised teachers and resources remain concentrated in larger urban schools.

34. The Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS), the front-line child protection authority, does not systematically screen for disability when receiving referrals of children at risk, and disability-specific case management protocols for child protection have not been developed. Children with disabilities — particularly those with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities — are at elevated risk of abuse and neglect, yet this vulnerability is not adequately reflected in child protection systems.

### ARTICLE 8: AWARENESS-RAISING

#### Pre-2019 Situation

35. Disability awareness activities prior to 2019 were largely limited to International Day of Persons with Disabilities (3 December) commemorations, consisting of public events in Malé with limited reach to outer atolls, and media features that generally reflected medical or charitable framings of disability.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

36. The 2024 Employment Regulation (R-9/2024) includes a mandate for sensitisation programmes targeting employers in the civil service and private sector. As of August 2025, over 350 persons with disabilities had been placed in employment through government awareness and facilitation initiatives.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, CRPD 33rd Session — Maldives, August 2025]

37. ADDA conducts public awareness programmes on disability rights and conducts training for frontline service providers. However, these activities are not sustained, systematically evaluated, or consistently resourced.

38. The 2025 Situational Analysis identified that social stigma — particularly around mental health conditions and intellectual disabilities — remains a major barrier to school enrolment, employment, and social participation. Families continue to conceal the disability status of household members, particularly girls and women, which contributes to under-registration and exclusion. Disability awareness curricula are not systematically integrated into school education.

### ARTICLE 9: ACCESSIBILITY

#### Pre-2019 Situation

39. Maldives had no building accessibility standards or regulations during the pre-2010 period. The Disability Act (2010) included broad obligations for physical accessibility of public buildings and transport, but these were not accompanied by enforceable standards, a compliance timeline, or designated responsibility for enforcement.

40. The geographic character of the Maldives — a dispersed archipelago where islands are connected primarily by inter-island ferry services and seaplanes — creates structural accessibility challenges unrelated to infrastructure design. Ferry landings, in particular, involve small boats and uneven surfaces that are inaccessible to wheelchair users and persons with mobility impairments.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

41. **Physical Accessibility**: No accessible infrastructure standard has been enacted as binding legislation during the reporting period. Local councils vary widely in their approach to accessibility, with some upgrading public spaces in response to ADDA guidance and some civil society advocacy, while the majority have made no accessibility investments. The 2025 Situational Analysis confirmed that public services including schools, health centres, government offices, and transport infrastructure remain largely inaccessible to persons with physical, visual, or hearing impairments.^[Situational Analysis, UNDP/UNPRPD/MoSFD, January 2025]

42. **Transport**: The Male-Hulhule bridge (Sinamale Bridge, 2018) and the expanded Malé road network have not been designed to accessible standards throughout. Public ferry services lack wheelchair boarding ramps and accessible onboard facilities. No national transport accessibility policy exists. For the 65% of persons with disabilities living outside Malé, transport inaccessibility effectively constitutes a de facto barrier to accessing health, education, employment, and justice services.^[2022 Census data; Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025]

43. **Information and Communication Accessibility**: Sign language interpretation services are critically limited. There is no government-funded sign language interpretation service for general public access. Braille resources and audio-description services for public information are absent or negligible. Government websites are not systematically tested for WCAG compliance. Maldives Sign Language lacks formal recognition as an official language.^[Situational Analysis, 2025]

44. **Notable Progress**: In the 2023 presidential election, visually impaired voters were able to cast their ballots independently and confidentially using a designated tactile template — a historic first for Maldives, demonstrating that accessible solutions are achievable when political commitment exists.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, CRPD 33rd Session — Maldives, August 2025]

### ARTICLE 10: RIGHT TO LIFE

45. The Constitution of Maldives guarantees the right to life to every person. No specific legislative provision addresses the right to life of persons with disabilities in the context of medical decision-making, denial of treatment, or emergency situations.

46. The State Party notes that access to Aasandha (the universal health insurance scheme) provides all persons with disabilities with financial coverage for essential healthcare, including emergency and acute care. Persons registered in the NDR receive additional health-related benefits. However, the quality and physical accessibility of healthcare services — particularly in outer islands — remain barriers to the effective realisation of the right to health and associated survival interests of persons with disabilities.^[Aasandha Health Insurance Scheme, Ministry of Finance and Aasandha Maldives]

47. The State Party is reviewing its mental health framework in light of the CRPD Committee's observations, and acknowledges concerns that involuntary hospitalisation procedures in the Mental Health Act — where enacted — may affect the right to life and security of persons with psychosocial disabilities.

### ARTICLE 11: SITUATIONS OF RISK AND HUMANITARIAN EMERGENCIES

#### Pre-2019 Situation

48. The Maldives is among the most climate-vulnerable countries in the world, with 80% of its land area less than one metre above sea level. Disasters including coastal flooding, storms, and freshwater crises disproportionately affect persons with disabilities, who have fewer resources for self-protection and evacuation.

49. Prior to 2019, disaster management frameworks did not include disability-specific provisions. The National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) did not maintain disaggregated data on persons with disabilities in affected areas, and local emergency response plans did not address the specific needs of persons with disabilities.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

50. The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015–2030) — to which Maldives is a signatory — calls for disability-inclusive disaster risk reduction. The State Party has taken initial steps to integrate disability considerations into disaster management policy, including acknowledgement in national climate adaptation strategies that persons with disabilities are among the most vulnerable groups.^[Situational Analysis, 2025]

51. However, the 2025 Situational Analysis found that legal inconsistencies and gaps in coordination continue to prevent a systematically disability-inclusive emergency response. Evacuation plans at the island council level rarely address the needs of persons with mobility, visual, or cognitive impairments. The NDR is not currently integrated with the NDMA's emergency preparedness planning, meaning that first responders do not know the location of persons with disabilities in their jurisdiction during a disaster.^[Situational Analysis, 2025; Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025]

52. The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022) revealed significant disability inclusion gaps in emergency response. Persons with disabilities faced barriers to accessing health information in accessible formats, were not prioritised in early vaccination rollouts in some atolls, and caregivers who depended on ADDA respite services were left without support during facility closures.

### ARTICLE 12: EQUAL RECOGNITION BEFORE THE LAW

#### Pre-2019 Situation

53. Maldivian civil law has historically relied on substituted decision-making models, primarily through guardianship arrangements, for persons determined to lack legal capacity. Persons with intellectual disabilities, persons with severe psychosocial disabilities, and older persons with dementia have been subject to family-controlled guardianship without independent judicial oversight, periodic review, or effective right of appeal.

54. Under Maldivian family law, persons with "mental incapacity" as determined by a court may be placed under permanent guardianship, extinguishing their right to make independent decisions regarding property, marriage, contracts, medical treatment, and movement.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

55. No legislative reform of the guardianship framework has been undertaken during the reporting period. The State Party acknowledges that the existing guardianship system is not consistent with Article 12 of the Convention, which requires that all persons with disabilities enjoy legal capacity on an equal basis with others, and that support mechanisms replace substituted decision-making.^[CRPD General Comment No. 1 (2014) on Equal Recognition Before the Law, CRPD/C/GC/1]

56. The Penal Code (9/2014) introduced some reforms relevant to legal capacity, but these do not comprehensively address supported decision-making. The ongoing review of the Disability Act (2010) provides an opportunity to introduce provisions for supported decision-making aligned with CRPD Article 12 and General Comment No. 1.

57. The State Party commits to engaging with the Law Reform Commission, the Attorney General's Office, and OPDs to develop a supported decision-making framework as a priority legislative action in the 2026–2028 period.

### ARTICLE 13: ACCESS TO JUSTICE

#### Pre-2019 Situation

58. Access to justice for persons with disabilities in Maldives was severely limited prior to the reporting period. There were no systematic provisions for procedural accommodations in court proceedings, no sign language interpretation services at courts, no Braille or accessible format legal documents, and no legal aid scheme specifically addressing the needs of persons with disabilities.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

59. The Legal Aid Act (2016) established a legal aid framework in Maldives administered through the Attorney General's Office, but it does not include disability-specific provisions or outreach strategies for persons with disabilities in outer islands.^[Legal Aid Act, Republic of Maldives, 2016]

60. The State Party acknowledges that procedural accommodations in court proceedings — including sign language interpretation, accessible court buildings, and modified hearing formats — are not yet routinely provided. The Maldives courts system has not adopted a policy on disability accommodation in legal proceedings.

61. The geographic concentration of courts in Malé and in atoll capital islands creates a further barrier to justice access for persons with disabilities in small outer islands, who face compounded barriers of physical inaccessibility, transport costs, and unavailability of disability support services.

62. The State Party notes that no conviction records exist of cases prosecuted for disability-based discrimination or abuse of persons with disabilities, suggesting severe under-reporting or barriers to reporting and access to justice.

### ARTICLE 14: LIBERTY AND SECURITY OF PERSON

63. The Maldives Penal Code (9/2014) provides general protections for individual liberty. However, the State Party acknowledges that provisions permitting the involuntary hospitalisation of persons with "mental illness" — under health and public order frameworks — remain in force and have not been reviewed for compatibility with CRPD Article 14.

64. The CRPD Committee's General Comment No. 1 and its Guidelines on article 14 (2015) have clarified that disability — including psychosocial disability — may never serve as a justification for deprivation of liberty. The State Party is committed to reviewing relevant provisions to eliminate disability-based grounds for involuntary detention as part of the mental health legislative reform process currently underway.

65. Persons with disabilities detained in correctional facilities do not receive disability-specific accommodation in practice. No prison accessibility standards exist, and disability identification at admission to the Maldives Correctional Service is not systematic.

---

### ARTICLE 15: FREEDOM FROM TORTURE AND CRUEL, INHUMAN OR DEGRADING TREATMENT

66. The Maldives Constitution and the Maldives Penal Code (9/2014) prohibit torture and ill-treatment. The State Party is party to the Convention Against Torture (CAT).

67. The State Party acknowledges concern that residential care settings for persons with disabilities — including those managed under ADDA — have not been subject to systematic independent monitoring. The physical conditions and treatment standards in these settings have not been evaluated against international standards for the prohibition of cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment. The HRCM has monitoring authority but does not routinely inspect disability care facilities.

68. The State Party commits to establishing a systematic monitoring mechanism — consistent with the OPCAT framework — for all residential settings accommodating persons with disabilities, inclusive of settings managed by both government and civil society organisations.

---

### ARTICLE 16: FREEDOM FROM EXPLOITATION, VIOLENCE, AND ABUSE

#### Pre-2019 Situation

69. Persons with disabilities — particularly women and children with disabilities — face elevated risks of exploitation, violence, and abuse in the Maldivian context. Social and familial confinement of persons with disabilities, limited personal autonomy, dependence on informal care arrangements, and absence of accessible reporting mechanisms collectively heighten this vulnerability.

70. The Sexual Offences Act (17/2014) and the Prevention of Domestic Violence Act (3/2012) provide general protections but do not include disability-specific provisions that account for the particular vulnerabilities of persons with disabilities in reporting and prosecuting violence.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

71. No formal safeguarding framework for adults with disabilities has been enacted during the reporting period. ADDA provides limited case management for persons with disabilities experiencing exploitation or neglect, but lacks the legal mandate, staffing capacity, and geographic reach for a comprehensive adult safeguarding function.

72. The FPA manages cases of domestic violence, including cases involving persons with disabilities. However, FPA shelter facilities are not accessible to persons with physical disabilities, and sign language services for deaf survivors of violence are not systematically available.^[Situational Analysis, 2025]

73. Children with disabilities are addressed under the child protection framework managed by CFPS under MoSFD. The Child Protection Act (2019) provides a rights-based framework for child protection, but does not contain specific provisions on safeguarding children with disabilities, and CFPS case workers lack systematic disability-awareness training.

### ARTICLE 17: PROTECTING THE INTEGRITY OF THE PERSON

74. The State Party is not aware of any forced sterilisation, forced contraception, or forced medical procedures specifically targeting persons with disabilities being carried out by state institutions. However, the absence of any systematic monitoring, data collection, or legislative prohibition specific to these practices means that the State Party cannot provide assurance that such practices do not occur in informal or private healthcare settings.

75. The legal framework does not contain explicit provisions prohibiting medical procedures on persons with disabilities without free and informed consent. Guardianship provisions under civil law allow substitute decision-makers to authorise medical procedures, which raises concerns about the integrity of consent processes for persons under guardianship.

---

### ARTICLE 18: LIBERTY OF MOVEMENT AND NATIONALITY

76. Persons with disabilities in Maldives enjoy the right to Maldivian nationality on an equal basis with others. No documented cases of nationality denial on grounds of disability have been recorded.

77. Freedom of movement is constitutionally guaranteed. However, the practical freedom of movement of persons with physical, visual, and cognitive disabilities is severely constrained by the inaccessibility of transport infrastructure, as detailed under Article 9 above. Persons with disabilities in small outer islands are, in practice, unable to travel to access services in Malé or atoll capitals without substantial informal support arrangements.

### ARTICLE 19: LIVING INDEPENDENTLY AND BEING INCLUDED IN THE COMMUNITY

#### Pre-2019 Situation

78. Community-based living for persons with disabilities was severely limited prior to the reporting period. Institutional provision — centred on the Reehendi shelter — housed persons with severe disabilities in congregate settings. Most persons with disabilities lived with families, with no formal personal assistance, home-based support, or independent living support services available.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

79. ADDA provides limited respite care services in Malé, enabling family caregivers of persons with significant support needs to take temporary breaks from caring. These services are not replicated in outer atolls. No personal assistance scheme exists in Maldives. Persons with disabilities in outer islands who require assistance with daily living are entirely dependent on family caregivers, with no state-funded alternative.

80. The Disability Allowance (MVR 2,000 per month), administered by NSPA, provides income support but is insufficient on its own to enable independent living, particularly given the high cost of living in Malé. The allowance was increased from a tiered rate (MVR 2,000 for severe, MVR 1,000 for moderate) to a flat rate of MVR 2,000 for all registered persons with disabilities in 2019.^[NSPA Disability Allowance Scheme; Regulation 2011/R-3]

81. The 2025 Situational Analysis found that the concept of independent living as articulated in Article 19 — including the right to choose where and with whom one lives — is not embedded in national disability policy. Most formal provision for persons with disabilities is centred on institutional or family care models, with no strategic plan for transition to community-based alternatives.

82. The 2019 confirmation that disability allowance would not be withdrawn upon gaining employment was a critical policy reform, removing a disincentive to work that had effectively trapped many persons with disabilities in welfare dependency.^[NSPA/MoSFD Policy Announcement, 2019; Maldives Policy Think Tank, "Breaking the Implementation Gap", 2024]

### ARTICLE 20: PERSONAL MOBILITY

83. Assistive technology provision in Maldives remains severely inadequate. Wheelchairs, crutches, hearing aids, white canes, and other mobility devices are provided through ADDA and some NGO programmes, primarily in Malé. No systematic national programme for assistive technology assessment and provision exists, and outer island access is minimal.

84. Aasandha health insurance provides limited coverage for some assistive devices through the health system, but coverage gaps exist for non-medical assistive technologies and for maintenance and replacement. The cost of importing assistive technologies to outer islands creates additional barriers.

85. Sign language interpretation training is provided through a limited programme, but the number of qualified interpreters in the country remains insufficient to meet need. A national sign language curriculum and formal certification pathway have not been established.

### ARTICLE 21: FREEDOM OF EXPRESSION AND INFORMATION AND OPINION

86. Maldives Sign Language is used by the deaf community but has not been accorded legal recognition as an official language. Sign language interpretation services are not systematically provided in public settings, including at government press conferences, parliamentary proceedings, or public emergency broadcasts.

87. The State Party acknowledges that public information — including information on rights, social services, legal procedures, and emergency alerts — is not routinely available in accessible formats including Braille, Easy Read, audio formats, or Maldivian Sign Language. Government websites have not been assessed against WCAG 2.1 accessibility standards.

88. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the absence of Maldives Sign Language interpretation in public health communications left deaf persons without timely access to critical health information, illustrating the concrete consequences of this gap.

---

### ARTICLE 24: EDUCATION

#### Pre-2019 Situation

89. Prior to 2013, children with disabilities were largely educated in segregated special units within mainstream schools, with limited integration into regular classrooms. The Special Education Needs (SEN) framework, developed through the National Institute of Education, provided a basic structure for SEN teacher training and student assessment, but lacked an explicitly rights-based orientation.

90. Access to education for children with severe or multiple disabilities outside Malé was extremely limited. Many children with disabilities in outer islands did not attend school, with families citing lack of appropriate support, inaccessibility of buildings, attitudinal barriers, and lack of transport as the primary reasons.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

91. The Education Act (2021) codified inclusive education as a legal obligation of the State, building on the 2013 Inclusive Education Policy. The Department of Inclusive Education within the Ministry of Education leads implementation.^[Education Act, Republic of Maldives, 2021; Department of Inclusive Education, ied.gov.mv]

92. As of 2024, every mainstream school in Maldives has at least one teacher with SEN training. "Inclusive ambassador" roles have been introduced at school level. SEN teachers conduct disability assessments upon school entry and develop individual learning plans.^[UNICEF Maldives, Disability-Inclusive Education Practices in Maldives, 2022]

93. The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA), established in 2018, provides specialised early intervention and schooling for children with autism spectrum conditions in Malé, including a centre-based programme and outreach support.

94. GPE funding (USD 9.5 million, 2024) under the THAIBA Agenda explicitly prioritises children with disabilities as among the most at-risk learners. The three GPE grants — including a USD 3 million Multiplier grant with the World Bank — will fund inclusive education system transformation through 2028.^[UNICEF Maldives, Towards More Inclusive and Equitable Education, 2024]

95. Despite this progress, significant gaps remain:

| Challenge | Status (2025) |
|---|---|
| SEN teachers per school | At least 1 per school, but insufficient for need |
| Accessible school buildings | Partial in Malé; very limited in outer atolls |
| Children with disabilities out of school | Data not disaggregated nationally; estimated high |
| Tertiary education (MNU/MIT) disability support | Minimal; no systematic disability support services |
| SEN teacher-to-student ratio | No national standard set |
| Psychosocial/intellectual disability inclusion | Limited; MIA serves autism only |

96. Higher education institutions — including Maldives National University (MNU) and Maldives Institute of Technology (MIT) — do not have systematic disability support services, accessible facilities, or reasonable accommodation policies for students with disabilities. This constitutes a significant barrier to educational achievement and career development for students with disabilities who successfully complete secondary school.

### ARTICLE 25: HEALTH

#### Pre-2019 Situation

97. Aasandha, the universal health insurance scheme introduced in 2012, provided a critical enabler of healthcare access for persons with disabilities by removing the financial barrier to healthcare at the point of use. Prior to Aasandha, persons with disabilities faced cost barriers to accessing healthcare, particularly specialist and hospital services. However, Aasandha's introduction did not address barriers of geographic remoteness, physical accessibility of health facilities, or the availability of disability-competent health professionals.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

98. Aasandha continues to provide universal health coverage, and persons with disabilities are included within this universal scheme. No specific disability health package or additional entitlement exists beyond the universal coverage.^[Aasandha Universal Health Insurance Scheme, Ministry of Finance]

99. Healthcare facilities in outer islands — regional hospitals and health centres — remain largely inaccessible for persons with physical disabilities. Ramps, accessible bathrooms, and disability-adapted examination equipment are absent in most primary care settings outside Malé.

100. Mental health services represent a particular gap. The limited number of psychiatrists, psychologists, and counsellors in Maldives — concentrated in Malé — means that persons with psychosocial disabilities in outer islands have virtually no access to community-based mental health support. Telepsychiatry programmes have been piloted but are not systematically funded or scaled.

101. Rehabilitation services — physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and speech and language therapy — are similarly concentrated in Malé. The Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) provides the primary rehabilitation service. No systematic community rehabilitation programme exists, and persons with newly acquired disabilities outside Malé have no access to timely rehabilitation services.

102. The State Party notes that the interaction between disability and non-communicable diseases (NCDs) requires attention. The high prevalence of NCDs in Maldives — including diabetes (with associated complications of visual impairment and limb amputation), cardiovascular disease, and obesity — contributes to acquired disability, and persons already living with disabilities face particular risks from NCD co-morbidities. NCD prevention and management programmes do not yet systematically incorporate disability considerations.

### ARTICLE 26: HABILITATION AND REHABILITATION

103. ADDA operates vocational training workshops in Malé that provide skills training and some employment placement support for persons with disabilities. Programmes include arts and crafts production, basic computing skills, and other vocational skills. Participation is primarily by persons with physical disabilities; persons with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities are substantially underrepresented in vocational rehabilitation.^[ADDA Programme Information, Agency for Disability and Different Ability, MoSFD]

104. No community-based rehabilitation (CBR) programme exists at the national level. The WHO CBR Guidelines, endorsed by the CRPD Committee, provide a framework for inclusive community-level rehabilitation that Maldives has not yet adopted.

105. Physiotherapy services through IGMH are the primary habilitation resource. Paediatric habilitation for children with developmental disabilities is provided through limited outpatient services. The absence of early intervention programmes for children under five with developmental delays or disabilities in outer islands represents a critical missed opportunity, as early intervention is most effective when delivered in the early years.

### ARTICLE 27: WORK AND EMPLOYMENT

#### Pre-2019 Situation

106. The Employment Act (2/2008) prohibits discrimination in employment on the basis of disability and requires employers to provide suitable conditions for persons with disabilities. In practice, enforcement was minimal during the period 2010–2018. The unemployment rate among persons with disabilities was not tracked by official statistics. Persons with disabilities in employment were predominantly in the informal economy or in family businesses.

107. A significant disincentive to employment existed in the design of the Disability Allowance: prior to 2019, persons with disabilities who gained formal employment risked having their allowance reduced or withdrawn. This created a poverty trap and was a widely cited barrier to labour market participation.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

108. The 2019 policy reform confirming that the Disability Allowance would not be reduced upon entering formal employment was a landmark change, removing a structural barrier to work.^[MoSFD/NSPA Policy Announcement, 2019; plus.mv, "Govt Confirms Disability Allowance Will Continue Alongside Employment", 2019]

109. Employment Regulation 2024/R-9, establishing a framework for providing employment opportunities for persons with disabilities in civil service and state-owned enterprises, represents the most significant employment policy development of the reporting period. The regulation establishes a registry of job-seeking persons with disabilities and creates a technical advisory structure.^[Employment Regulation 2024/R-9; UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]

110. As of August 2025, over 350 persons with disabilities had been placed in employment through government-facilitated programmes under the civil service and SOE framework.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]

111. The MVR 100 million women's entrepreneurship fund (2024) with 25% reserved for women with disabilities is an additional employment support measure.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]

112. Challenges remain:

| Indicator | Status |
|---|---|
| Official unemployment data disaggregated by disability | Not collected |
| Workplace reasonable accommodation framework | Not established in law |
| Private sector disability hiring incentives | None enacted |
| Supported employment programme | Not established |
| Vocational training for persons with intellectual/psychosocial disability | Minimal |
| Outer island employment opportunities for PWDs | Very limited |

### ARTICLE 28: ADEQUATE STANDARD OF LIVING AND SOCIAL PROTECTION

#### Pre-2019 Situation

113. The Disability Allowance, established under Regulation 2011/R-3, provided a tiered cash transfer: MVR 2,000 per month for persons with severe disabilities and MVR 1,000 per month for persons with moderate disabilities. Receipt was contingent on registration in the NDR (or its predecessor registry), which was not fully operationalised until 2023. As a result, many eligible persons with disabilities did not receive the allowance.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

114. From 2019, the Disability Allowance was standardised to MVR 2,000 per month for all registered persons with disabilities, regardless of severity classification. This simplified the eligibility criteria and increased support for those previously in the moderate category.^[NSPA Disability Allowance Scheme; Regulation 2011/R-3 as amended]

115. The 2025 Situational Analysis estimated that only 25.6% of persons with disabilities identified in the 2022 Census receive the Disability Allowance. This coverage gap has multiple causes:

- **Registry linkage**: Receipt requires NDR registration, and barriers to registration (geographic remoteness, documentation requirements, lack of awareness) reduce uptake.
- **Disability type exclusions**: The NDR's focus on physical and sensory disabilities has historically excluded persons with psychosocial or mild cognitive disabilities.
- **Assessment access**: Medical assessment for disability classification must be conducted by authorised assessors, who are concentrated in Malé.^[NSPA/NDR data; Situational Analysis, 2025]

116. Local council budgeting for disability inclusion is inconsistent. Some councils allocate specific disability budgets; others subsume disability expenditure in general welfare lines. Most state institutions allocate no budget for accessibility improvements.^[Situational Analysis, 2025; Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025]

117. Housing policy does not include accessible housing standards or an accessible housing allocation priority for persons with disabilities. Home modification grants for persons with disabilities are not systematically available.

---

### ARTICLE 29: PARTICIPATION IN POLITICAL AND PUBLIC LIFE

118. The 2023 presidential election milestone — visually impaired voters independently casting ballots using a tactile template — is the most significant disability-inclusive democracy development of the reporting period.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025]

119. Despite this achievement, systemic participation gaps persist. Persons with disabilities are substantially underrepresented in elected office at all levels: national Parliament (People's Majlis), Atoll and City Councils, and Island Councils. No disability quota or reserved seat provision exists in electoral law.

120. Voting stations and election materials are not fully accessible. Physical inaccessibility of polling stations — steps, narrow doorways, inaccessible booths — remains a barrier for voters with mobility impairments in most locations outside the 2023 pilot. Election information is not routinely available in sign language or Braille.^[Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025]

121. Persons with disabilities lack representation in government, policy-making bodies, and the senior civil service. The technical advisory committee established under Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 was criticised by disability advocates for initially lacking meaningful representation of persons with disabilities in its composition.^[Maldives Policy Think Tank, "Breaking the Implementation Gap", 2024, https://maldivespolicythinktank.com/breaking-the-implementation-gap-a-call-for-transformative-change-in-disability-rights-in-the-maldives/]

### ARTICLE 30: PARTICIPATION IN CULTURAL LIFE, RECREATION, LEISURE AND SPORT

122. Cultural and recreational facilities in Maldives — including the National Museum, public parks, sport facilities, and community centres — are not systematically accessible to persons with disabilities.

123. ADDA organises some recreational activities for persons with disabilities registered with the agency in Malé. These include sports events (adapted sports) and cultural participation activities around national holidays and International Day of Persons with Disabilities.

124. Maldives has participated in the South Asian Federation (SAF) Games, but has not systematically promoted Paralympic-level disability sport. No national adapted sports policy or programme exists.

125. Access to cultural life — including film, theatre, and literary materials in accessible formats — is severely constrained by the absence of audio description, captioning, sign language-interpreted performances, or accessible cultural venues.

### ARTICLE 31: STATISTICS AND DATA COLLECTION

#### Pre-2019 Situation

126. Prior to the 2011 Census, Maldives had no nationally standardised method for counting persons with disabilities. Administrative data from service-providing agencies (Reehendi, Ministry of Social Development) recorded only those receiving services, dramatically undercounting the disability population.

127. The 2011 Population and Housing Census was the first national census to apply the Washington Group Short Set questions for disability measurement. However, analysis of 2011 census disability data for policy purposes was limited.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

128. The 2022 Population and Housing Census again applied WG-SS methodology, generating the most robust national disability prevalence estimate to date: 24,401 persons (7% of population).^[Maldives Population and Housing Census 2022, MNSB, 2023]

129. The NDR, formally launched 5 January 2023, provides ongoing administrative data on registered persons with disabilities. Data points in the NDR include: disability type, geographic location, level of support need, and allowance receipt status. However:

- The NDR's coverage (14,566 as of July 2025) represents approximately 60% of the census-estimated disability population.
- WG-SS does not capture psychosocial or intellectual disabilities with sufficient sensitivity; as a result, these groups are systematically undercounted in both census and NDR data.
- Local council disability data collection is inconsistent and not systematically aggregated.
- No outcome data is collected on education, employment, health, or social participation of persons with disabilities.^[Situational Analysis, 2025; NDR data, NSPA]

130. **Summary of Key Disability Statistics (2025)**:

| Indicator | Data | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Estimated disability prevalence | 24,401 (7% of population) | 2022 Census (WG-SS) |
| NDR registrations (July 2025) | 14,566 | NDR/NSPA |
| Disability Allowance coverage | ~25.6% of Census-estimated PWDs | NSPA/Situational Analysis |
| Gender ratio among PWDs | 55% women | 2022 Census |
| Living outside Malé | 65% of PWDs | 2022 Census |
| Employed through civil service/SOE initiative | 350+ (cumulative to 2025) | MoSFD |
| Psychosocial disability data | Not available in census | Data gap |
| Disability poverty rate | Not measured | Data gap |

131. The State Party commits to the following data improvements in the next reporting cycle:
- Developing a comprehensive National Disability Data Strategy, aligned with Washington Group recommendations and SDG disability-disaggregated data standards.
- Integrating NDR data with education, health, and employment administrative data systems to enable outcome tracking.
- Introducing psychosocial disability measurement in administrative data collection, in partnership with the mental health sector.
- Regularising local council disability data reporting to ADDA and NSPA.

### ARTICLE 32: INTERNATIONAL COOPERATION

132. Maldives has received substantial support from international partners in advancing disability rights during the reporting period:

- **UNDP/UNPRPD**: Led the comprehensive Situational Analysis on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in Maldives, published January 2025, providing the first evidence-based national assessment of disability barriers and opportunities.^[Situational Analysis, 2025]
- **UNICEF Maldives**: Provided technical and financial support for inclusive education initiatives, including co-financing of the 2022 UNICEF Disability-Inclusive Education Practices study and the 2024 GPE grant application.^[UNICEF Maldives, Education]
- **World Bank**: Partner in the GPE Multiplier grant (USD 3 million) for THAIBA inclusive education transformation.
- **Global Disability Fund**: Supported the Maldives country brief (2025) as part of its UNPRPD programme inception activities.^[Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025]
- **International Labour Organization (ILO)**: Provided technical input on disability-inclusive employment frameworks.

133. The State Party notes that international cooperation support has been primarily concentrated in Malé and focused on policy-level activities. Technical assistance for outer island disability service capacity-building has been limited. The State Party calls on international partners to increase support for decentralised disability inclusion programmes.

134. Maldives participates in SAARC (South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation) frameworks on disability, though regional coordination on disability policy implementation remains nascent.

### ARTICLE 33: NATIONAL IMPLEMENTATION AND MONITORING

#### Pre-2019 Situation

135. The Disability Act (2010) designated the Ministry responsible for social affairs as the national focal point for disability policy. In practice, implementation responsibility was fragmented across multiple ministries with no clear coordination mechanism and no national disability action plan with measurable targets.

136. The Adult Disability Care Centre (ADCC) — the predecessor of ADDA — served as the primary service delivery agency but had limited regulatory or coordination functions. Monitoring of the Disability Act's implementation was not systematically conducted.

#### 2019–2025 Developments

137. **Focal Ministry**: The Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD) is the designated focal ministry for disability rights implementation under Article 33(1).

138. **Coordination Mechanism**: The Disability Council serves as the central coordinating body for disability-related policies and initiatives across government.^[UN Geneva Meeting Summary, August 2025] The Disability Council's mandate, composition, and operational effectiveness require strengthening to fulfil the inter-ministerial coordination function envisaged by Article 33(1).

139. **Service Delivery Agency**: ADDA (Agency for Disability and Different Ability), restructured from the former Adult Disability Care Centre, serves as the primary operational agency for disability services, including vocational training, awareness raising, assistive device provision, and limited community services.

140. **Independent Monitoring**: The Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM), established under Law No. 6/2006 and constitutionally mandated as an independent national human rights institution (NHRI), serves as the independent monitoring mechanism under Article 33(2). HRCM has the mandate to receive complaints, investigate human rights violations, and produce thematic monitoring reports. HRCM has conducted disability-focused monitoring activities and produced reports on the rights of persons with disabilities.^[HRCM Disability Monitoring, hrcm.org.mv]

141. HRCM's capacity for disability monitoring is constrained by limited budgetary resources, insufficient disability expertise among staff, and the geographic reach challenges common to all Maldivian institutions. Outer island monitoring — essential given that 65% of persons with disabilities live outside Malé — is particularly limited.

142. **National Action Planning**: As of 2025, a Disability Strategic Action Plan for 2025–2030 is under development. The National Strategic Action Plan (SAP) for 2025–2030 includes disability inclusion as a focus area but does not specify dedicated disability targets or indicators.^[Situational Analysis, 2025; Global Disability Fund, Maldives Country Brief, 2025] The State Party commits to finalising the Disability Strategic Action Plan with measurable, time-bound targets disaggregated by disability type, gender, and geographic location.

143. **OPD Participation**: Organisations of persons with disabilities (OPDs) are not formally integrated into the Article 33(3) civil society participation mechanism. OPD capacity in Maldives remains limited, and OPDs are not systematically funded to participate in monitoring processes. The State Party commits to establishing a formal OPD consultation mechanism, including support for OPD capacity-building, particularly for organisations representing persons with psychosocial and intellectual disabilities who are currently most excluded from policy processes.

## PART III: PRIORITY COMMITMENTS FOR THE NEXT REPORTING CYCLE (2026–2030)

144. Building on the Concluding Observations of the CRPD Committee (33rd Session, August 2025) and the findings of the 2025 Situational Analysis, the State Party identifies the following priority commitments:

### Legislative Reform
1. Complete the comprehensive review and amendment of the Disability Act (8/2010) to bring it into full compliance with CRPD standards, including: (a) an interactive definition of disability; (b) statutory reasonable accommodation obligations; (c) explicit prohibition of forced institutionalisation on disability grounds; (d) supported decision-making provisions replacing substituted decision-making.
2. Enact a comprehensive anti-discrimination law with accessible complaints mechanisms and effective remedies.
3. Ratify the Optional Protocol to the CRPD, enabling individual communications and inquiry procedures.
4. Review mental health legislation to eliminate disability-based grounds for involuntary detention.

### Policy and Programme
5. Finalise and publish the Disability Strategic Action Plan 2025–2030 with measurable targets and budgetary allocations.
6. Develop and implement a national community-based rehabilitation programme.
7. Establish a personal assistance scheme enabling persons with significant support needs to live independently in their communities.
8. Scale Disability Allowance coverage to reach a minimum of 80% of the Census-estimated disability population by 2028 through simplified registration, outreach, and mobile assessment services.
9. Develop and implement a gender and disability intersectional policy framework.

### Data and Monitoring
10. Develop a National Disability Data Strategy aligned with international standards.
11. Integrate NDR with education, employment, and health administrative data.
12. Establish a systematic independent monitoring mechanism for disability care and residential settings.
13. Formally constitute OPD participation in the Article 33(3) monitoring framework.

### Accessibility
14. Enact binding accessibility standards for public buildings, transport infrastructure, and digital information, with a compliance timeline and designated enforcement authority.
15. Formally recognise Maldivian Sign Language as an official language and expand sign language interpreter training and employment.
