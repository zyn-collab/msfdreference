# Summary: Elderly Care and Population Ageing

This summary distils the key points from the full encyclopedia chapter into a slide-based format. Each module covers a major theme, with slides on the left and explanatory notes on the right. It is designed for readers who want a structured overview without reading the entire chapter.

---

## Module 1: History


---

## Slide 1: The Newest Domain

Elderly care is the newest and least developed of the ministry's four domains:
- **No dedicated elderly legislation exists** (unlike CRPA for children, DVPA for DV, Disability Act for PWDs)
- The first dedicated policy instrument was the **National Elderly Policy (2018)**
- The first senior citizens' centre (Naadhee) opened in **2023**
- An **Ageing Bill** is being prepared but has not been enacted
- The pension system (2009) was the first formal state mechanism reaching the elderly
- The system is being built as the demographic cliff approaches

**By contrast:** The child protection system dates to 1991, the disability framework to 2010, and the DV framework to 2012. Elderly policy is a decade behind the other domains.

---

## Slide 2: Traditional Care and Its Erosion

**Traditional model:**
- Extended family networks provided housing, food, and daily care for elderly parents
- The elderly held high social status in island communities — respected as community elders
- Islamic values reinforced filial obligation — caring for parents viewed as a religious duty
- This model functioned when: families lived on the same island, multiple generations shared housing, and communities were stable

**What's eroding the model:**
- **Urbanisation:** Young adults migrate to Male' for education/employment, leaving elderly parents behind on home islands
- **Housing:** 60% of Greater Male' population rents; overcrowded apartments cannot accommodate elderly parents
- **Economic pressure:** Dual-income households cannot spare a caregiver for elderly parents
- **Divorce:** High divorce rate (5.52 per 1,000) disrupts inter-generational care arrangements
- **Social norm change:** Younger generations increasingly view elderly care as a state rather than family responsibility
- **Geographic dispersal:** Elderly parents and adult children may live on different islands or in different atolls

**Result:** The traditional care system is weakening at precisely the moment demographic pressure is accelerating. The formal care system is not growing fast enough to replace it.

## Slide 5: The Demographic Cliff

**UNFPA projections (65+ Maldivian population):**

| Year | 65+ Population | Old-Age Dependency Ratio | Ageing Index |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2014 | 16,377 | 7 | 17 |
| 2030 | 31,783 | 11 | 30 |
| 2040 | 48,432 | 14 | 49 |
| 2050 | 74,516 | 20 | 75 |
| 2054 | 88,963 | 24 | 90 |

**By 2054, the 80+ cohort alone (19,028) will be larger than the entire 65+ population in 2014.** This has profound health system implications — the 80+ group has the highest rates of multi-morbidity, cognitive decline, functional dependency, and need for long-term care.

**The three demographic thresholds:**
- Ageing society (7% aged 65+): **2031** — 5 years away
- Aged society (14%): **2044**
- Super-aged society (21%): **2052**

The 21-year transition from ageing to super-aged is among the shortest projected globally. France: 115 years. Sweden: 85 years. Japan: 36 years. Maldives: 21 years.

## Slide 7: Recurring Structural Issues

1. **No legislation:** Unlike the other three domains, elderly rights have no dedicated statute. The Ageing Bill is in preparation but not enacted.
2. **The pension is the entire system:** The basic pension (MVR 5,000/month) is effectively the only universal elderly welfare mechanism. Beyond the pension, services are minimal.
3. **Zero specialist workforce:** No geriatricians, no geriatric nurses, no geriatric social workers. GPs with "special interest" provide what geriatric care exists.
4. **WHO Group 1 palliative care:** No hospice, no palliative care policy, no end-of-life care guidelines. NCDs cause 85% of all deaths.
5. **Family erosion without replacement:** Traditional family care weakening; formal care not growing fast enough to compensate.
6. **The retirement pension gender gap:** 78% male, 22% female beneficiaries. Most elderly women rely solely on the basic pension.

## Module 2: Legal Framework


---

## Slide 1: The Legislative Gap

**No dedicated elderly legislation exists.** The elderly are the only one of the ministry's four populations without a specific rights-based statute:

| Domain | Legislation | Year |
|---|---|---|
| Children | Child Rights Protection Act | 2019 |
| DV | Domestic Violence Prevention Act | 2012 |
| Disability | Disability Act | 2010 |
| **Elderly** | **None — Ageing Bill in preparation, not enacted** | — |

Elderly rights are derived from general constitutional provisions (Articles 17, 21, 23, 35) and from policy frameworks (not legislation). The pension is the only statutory mechanism reaching elderly universally (Pension Act 2009).

---

## Slide 2: The National Elderly Policy (2018)

**First dedicated policy instrument.** Six fundamental rights:
1. Right to life with dignity (freedom from discrimination)
2. Right to health
3. Right to work
4. Right to property and inheritance
5. Right to social security
6. Right to freedom from violence

**Four core principles:**
- Protection of Rights
- Independence and Participation
- Health and Well-being
- Preparatory Measures (building capacity before ageing reaches critical mass)

Drawn from: UN Principles for Older Persons (1991), Constitution (2008), UN Decade of Healthy Ageing (2021-2030), SDGs.

---

## Slide 3: The National Ageing Policy (2024) and Action Plan (2025-2028)

**National Ageing Policy 2024:** Updated policy framework replacing the 2018 policy. Detailed goals and strategies across care, health, economic security, and social participation.

**National Action Plan on Ageing 2025-2028:** Current operational framework (successor to the expired Plan of Action 2019-2023, which had 34 priority actions).

**The Ageing Bill:** Being prepared. When enacted, it would give elderly rights legal force for the first time. Status: in preparation, not yet before Parliament.

**Implementation record of the 2019-2023 Plan:** The Plan expired without a published evaluation of what was achieved. Plan succession without evaluation is a recurring pattern across all four ministry domains.

## Slide 5: MIPAA and the 2021 Voluntary National Survey

**MIPAA** (Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing, 2002): Voluntary framework (not binding treaty). Maldives submitted 2021 Voluntary National Survey.

**Key self-disclosures from the MIPAA Survey (Q13-Q40):**
- No legal capacity or supported decision-making measures for elderly (Q33)
- No elderly involvement in health services development (Q18.3)
- No data or resources allocated for elderly participation (Q18.3)
- Maldives NOT participating in the Titchfield Group on Ageing Statistics (Q40)
- MPAO maintains database with segregated data on 65+ (Q37)
- Free transport for elderly 65+ on government buses/ferries confirmed (Q26.3)
- Social workers allocated to vulnerable elderly through FCSCs (Q30.2) — but FCSC generalist staff
- Ranveyla campaign: nationwide awareness and health screening programme (Q10)

---

## Slide 6: Elder Abuse — Legal Gaps

**270 elder abuse cases reported 2018-2021** — males constitute >50% of victims (unusual pattern internationally where women predominate).

**Legal protection gaps:**
- No dedicated elder abuse legislation
- DVPA covers DV between family members (including elderly) but:
  - Designed for IPV context, not elder neglect/financial abuse
  - FPA is GBV-focused, not elderly-focused
  - Elderly abuse by non-family caregivers falls outside DVPA scope
- No mandatory reporting specifically for elder abuse
- No specialised elder abuse investigation protocol
- Financial exploitation of elderly (pension diversion by family) not specifically criminalised

The 270 reported cases (2018-2021) represent only cases entering the formal system. Actual prevalence is unknown — no elder abuse prevalence study exists.

---

## Slide 7: The Domestic Violence Act and Elderly

The DVPA covers violence in "family relationships" which includes elderly persons experiencing violence from family members. When an elderly person experiences DV:

- Report to FPA or FCSC
- DVPA protection orders available
- Criminal assault prosecuted under Penal Code

**Gaps specific to elderly:**
- No elder-specific risk assessment tools
- FPA caseworkers not trained in elder abuse dynamics (dependency, cognitive decline, financial exploitation)
- No elder-specific shelter or safe housing
- Economic dependency of elderly on the very family members who may be abusive
- On outer islands, the elderly person and the perpetrator share the same island with no alternative housing

## Slide 2: The Pension System as the Only Universal Mechanism

The basic pension (MVR 5,000/month for all citizens 65+) is the only mechanism that reaches ALL elderly universally. Everything else — health screening, home visits, social centres, care facilities — reaches only a fraction.

**Pension time series (basic pension beneficiaries):**

| Year | Beneficiaries | Paid out (MVR m) |

The growth from 15,035 to 22,023 beneficiaries (2011-2023) reflects population ageing. This will accelerate dramatically as the 65+ population grows from 20,553 to 88,963 by 2054.

---

## Slide 3: Guraidhoo Facility — The Only Residential Care

**Home for People with Special Needs (HPSN) Guraidhoo:**
- The only government residential care facility housing elderly alongside persons with disabilities and mental health conditions
- Not elderly-specific — mixed population creates care challenges
- 173 beds (119 psychiatric, 46 geriatric as of 2010 baseline)

**UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention findings:** Raised concerns about conditions and the legal basis for confinement. Some residents transferred to the facility by families without adequate consent procedures.

**New elderly care facility announcements:** Multiple announcements have been made over the years (Hulhumale', Addu) but implementation has been slow. The chronology of announcements vs actual construction reveals a pattern of promise and delay.

## Slide 4: Home and Community-Based Care

**Home visit programme for bedridden individuals:**
- Government programme providing home-based care support
- 1,215 bedridden individuals nationally (MoSFD data)
- Taj & Suzana (2023) study assessed satisfaction with the programme — generally positive but coverage limited

**Naadhee senior citizens' centre (Male'):**
- Opened 2023 — first dedicated social centre for elderly
- Located in Male'
- UNFPA Hattah programme (launched July 2024): nutrition and physical activity classes for 55+ at Naadhee; community events on N. Kudafari

**N. Kudafari:** Highlighted as a model island for community-based ageing with dignity

**Ranveyla Campaign:** Nationwide awareness and health screening programme targeting elderly — health checks, awareness about elderly rights, community engagement

**Community-based gaps:**
- No day care services for elderly outside Male'
- No respite care for elderly caregivers
- No community-based dementia support
- No structured volunteer visiting programme for isolated elderly
- No meals-on-wheels or food delivery for housebound elderly

## Slide 7: The Ministry's Elderly Caseload

- Cases categories: physical abuse, neglect, financial exploitation, abandonment

## Module 5: Programs and Services


---

## Slide 1: The Pension System in Detail

**Old-Age Basic Pension (OABP):**
- MVR 5,000/month for ALL Maldivian citizens 65+
- Universal, non-contributory — citizenship + age = eligibility
- 22,023 beneficiaries (2023): F 11,040, M 10,983
- Must reside in Maldives; not in full-time state care or incarcerated
- Deductions: 50% of MRPS retirement pension amount; 100% of other state-funded pensions exceeding MVR 5,000

**MRPS (contributory pension):**
- 14% of pensionable wage (7% employee + 7% employer)
- Employees aged 16-65 required to participate
- Can claim from age 55 if sufficient funds
- Fund: MVR 25.5 billion (Q1 2025); 192,057 members
- Retirement pension beneficiaries (2023): 1,869 (F: 411, M: 1,458)
- Retirement pension expenditure: MVR 165 million (2024)

**Pre-2009 system:** Only public service employees had pensions (50% salary after 20 years; 100% after 40 years). Required uninterrupted service — structural gender bias against women who interrupted careers for childcare.

## Slide 2: Pension Fiscal Sustainability

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Total pension expenditure (2024) | MVR 1.8 billion |
| Basic pension share | MVR 1.3 billion (72%) |
| Retirement pension share | MVR 165 million (9%) |

**The fiscal trajectory:** As the 65+ population grows from 22,023 (current beneficiaries) to potentially 90,000+ by 2054, basic pension costs will quadruple at current rates — from MVR 1.3 billion to MVR 5+ billion annually. No fiscal plan for this escalation has been published.

---

## Slide 3: Guraidhoo — Residential Care

**Home for People with Special Needs (HPSN):**
- Located on Guraidhoo island
- Government residential facility — houses elderly, persons with disabilities, and mental health patients together
- Not purpose-built for elderly care
- UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention raised concerns about: conditions, legal basis for confinement, consent procedures
- 36 elderly persons at one point in state care after families did not collect them

**What Guraidhoo represents:** The institutional care model that no strategic plan exists to replace with community-based alternatives.

---

## Slide 4: Home Visit Programme and Bedridden Care

**Government home visit programme:**
- Serves 1,215 bedridden individuals nationally
- Staff visit homes to provide basic care support, medication monitoring, and referrals
- Taj & Suzana (2023) Home Visit Study: caretakers generally satisfied but identified gaps in coverage and frequency

**What the programme covers:** Basic care support, medication assistance, health monitoring, referral to hospital when needed

**What it doesn't cover:** Continuous care, night-time support, specialised nursing, rehabilitation, social stimulation, respite for family caregivers

---

## Slide 5: Naadhee and Community Services

**Naadhee Senior Citizens' Centre (Male'):**
- Opened 2023 — first dedicated social centre for elderly
- Social activities, health education, community engagement
- UNFPA Hattah Programme (launched July 2024): nutrition classes, physical activity, cooking with local ingredients (moringa, turmeric)

**Ranveyla Campaign:**
- Nationwide awareness and health screening
- Health checks: blood pressure, blood sugar, weight, eyesight, dental screening
- Community awareness about elderly rights and available services
- Conducted at harbours, marketplaces, public spaces

**Aged Care Maldives (NGO, est. 2008):**
- Free health screening events (reached 874+ elderly through Atmosphere Foundation partnership)
- Home healthcare: bedridden care, assisted bathing, tube feeding, catheterisation, injections, physiotherapy referrals
- Exercise/yoga programmes focusing on strength, mobility, stability
- Volunteer companions: regular face-to-face or telephone conversations for isolated elderly
- Caregiver training workshops

**What doesn't exist:**
- Day care for elderly (outside Naadhee in Male')
- Meals-on-wheels or food delivery for housebound elderly
- Community-based dementia support
- Respite care for family caregivers (outside Male')
- Structured social visiting programmes for isolated elderly in atolls

## Slide 2: The Moosa Research (2016/2019) — Wellbeing and Social Connectedness

**Methodology:** 393 elderly persons (65+) surveyed across 31 islands. Custom instrument using capabilities approach. Five life domains: health, social connectedness, economic status, access to goods/services, ability to conform with social norms.

**Key findings:**
- **Health and social connectedness** were the most important determinants of wellbeing, each with large impact
- Overall social connectedness had a **29% shared contribution** with wellbeing
- Family centrality: collectivist social arrangements remain important despite geographic dispersal
- Structured community-based social activities for older people are **irregular and occasional**
- Formal voluntary associations working with older people are **non-existent in the islands** (except Male')
- Elderly maintain high social status in small island communities — tendency NOT to include them in some activities as it may appear disrespectful
- Despite low social engagement beyond family, different types of social engagement positively affect wellbeing

**Implication:** Policy interventions that strengthen social connectedness — community centres, visiting programmes, structured activities — would have measurable wellbeing impact.

---

## Slide 3: The Nazra (2018) Study — Urban Elderly Experience

**Qualitative study of elderly persons in Male'.** Key findings:

- **Depression and loneliness** documented among Male' elderly — urban proximity to services does not prevent social isolation
- **Confinement** in apartments — elderly confined to small living spaces in overcrowded Male' housing
- **Pension forms**: elderly persons reported that family members must fill out pension forms because "I cannot do it myself" — reflecting the educational profile (72% primary only; 1 in 5 never attended school)
- **Healthcare access challenges** despite Male' location — navigating hospital systems, understanding medications, accessing specialists
- **Social status decline** — the traditional respect for elderly is eroding in the urban context; younger generations have different priorities

---

## Slide 4: The Azeez et al. (2020) Study — Depression and Sociability

**Quantitative study of depression and loneliness among urban elderly.**

- Depression prevalence documented among Male' elderly (specific rates in the study)
- Sociability deficit — elderly in Male' are physically close to others but socially disconnected
- Contributing factors: migration away from home islands, loss of community context, apartment confinement, death of age-cohort peers, family members working long hours

**The urban paradox:** Male' elderly have geographic access to hospitals, social centres, and services — but they experience some of the highest rates of depression and loneliness documented in any Maldivian population. Proximity to services ≠ social connection.

---

## Slide 5: The HelpAge Pension Case Study (2023)

**Comprehensive analysis of the Maldivian pension system's sustainability.**

- Documented the pension system's role as the primary poverty reduction mechanism for the elderly
- Analysed fiscal trajectory as the beneficiary population grows
- Identified the double-pensioning problem (some recipients receiving both basic pension and other state pensions)
- Recommended: fiscal sustainability planning, reformed contribution structures, and expanded coverage of the contributory MRPS

**MMA and UNESCAP warnings:** Both the Central Bank (MMA) and UNESCAP's Social Protection Readiness Assessment (2023) identified pension fiscal sustainability as a concern. The pension system's current affordability does not guarantee future affordability as the 65+ population quadruples.

## Slide 3: Implementation Challenges

Same pattern as the other three domains, plus elderly-specific challenges:

1. **No legislative backing:** Policy without legislation means the Ageing Bill's absence undermines implementation
2. **No dedicated agency:** Unlike FPA (DV), ADDA (disability), or CFPS (child protection), elderly care has no dedicated institutional home with operational capacity
3. **MIPAA is voluntary:** The international framework for elderly policy (MIPAA) has no binding reporting cycle or Committee, creating weaker external accountability than CRC/CEDAW/CRPD
4. **Fiscal sustainability risk:** The pension system (90%+ of elderly expenditure) faces long-term affordability questions as beneficiaries quadruple
5. **No specialist workforce:** Cannot implement geriatric care policy without geriatricians — and training a geriatrician takes a decade
6. **The timing paradox:** The system needs to be built NOW (before the demographic cliff) but political urgency is low because the elderly population is currently small (5%)

## Module 8: International Obligations

## Slide 1: MIPAA Framework

**Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing (2002):** Voluntary (not binding) international framework for elderly policy.

**Key differences from CRC/CEDAW/CRPD:**

| Feature | CRC/CEDAW/CRPD | MIPAA |
|---|---|---|
| Legal status | Binding treaties | Voluntary framework |
| Reporting obligation | Periodic reports required | Voluntary national surveys |
| Monitoring body | Committee with review authority | No dedicated Committee |
| Concluding Observations | Issued with specific recommendations | None |
| Shadow reporting | CSOs submit independent assessments | No formal mechanism |
| Accountability | Public, tracked, diplomatically significant | Low external accountability |

**Implication:** Elderly policy lacks the external accountability mechanism that drives reform in child protection, gender, and disability. There is no "MIPAA Committee" to pressure the government.

## Slide 2: The Geographic Gap

**Male':** MPAO, Naadhee centre, Ranveyla screenings, Aged Care Maldives home healthcare, IGMH (GPs with interest), FPA (for elder DV), Medica Maldives (private geriatric consultations)

**Outer islands:** The pension arrives by bank transfer. Everything else is absent or minimal:
- Health centre with 1-2 nurses (no geriatric training)
- FCSC generalist staff (2-5 per atoll, handling all 4 domains)
- Maybe periodic Ranveyla health screening visit
- No day care, no social centre, no rehabilitation, no dementia support, no respite care
- Family caregiver with no formal training or support

**What happens when an elderly person on an outer island needs care:**
- Minor health issue: health centre nurse
- Major health issue: boat to atoll hospital (hours), or evacuation to Male' (expensive, complex)
- Dementia: family manages alone; no diagnosis, no treatment, no support
- Bedridden: home visit programme if coverage reaches the island
- Abuse: FCSC if staff trained (generalist); police if criminal
- End of life: no palliative care; family manages death at home or hospital transfer

## Slide 3: The Family Erosion Reality

The traditional family care model is weakening through multiple channels simultaneously:

| Driver | Effect on Elderly |
|---|---|
| Youth migration to Male' | Elderly parents left on home island without daily care support |
| Housing density in Male' | Apartments cannot accommodate elderly parents (overcrowding) |
| Dual-income households | No family member available as full-time caregiver |
| High divorce rate (5.52/1,000) | Disrupted inter-generational care arrangements |
| Changing social norms | Younger generations see elderly care as state responsibility |
| Cost of living | Economic pressure leaves no margin for unpaid caregiving |

**The "not collected" pattern:** Elderly persons brought to facilities for medical treatment whose families do not return to collect them — documented abandonment.

**The Moosa finding:** Despite family erosion, family remains central to elderly wellbeing — 29% shared contribution of social connectedness to wellbeing. The solution is not to replace families but to support them.

| Indicator | Reality |
|---|---|
| Geriatricians | 0 nationally |
| Geriatric wards | None at any hospital |
| Palliative care | WHO Group 1 — no known activity |
| Dementia services | No strategy; no memory care; no screening protocol |

| Atoll rehabilitation | None — physiotherapy/OT Male'-only |
| Polypharmacy management | No geriatric pharmacy guidelines |
| Fall prevention | No programme |
| Geriatric mental health | No specialised service |

**The clinical gap:** When an elderly patient presents with complex multi-morbidity, cognitive decline, polypharmacy issues, and functional deterioration — the geriatric syndrome — no part of the Maldivian health system is equipped to manage them as a geriatric patient. They are managed as a series of individual conditions by different specialists without integration.

## Slide 5: Elder Abuse — What We Know

**270 cases reported to MoSFD (2018-2021).** Males >50% of victims.

**Types documented:** Physical abuse, neglect, financial exploitation (pension diversion), abandonment

**Why elder abuse is underreported:**
- Economic dependency on perpetrators (who may be the caregiver)
- Physical inability to access reporting mechanisms (immobility, cognitive decline)
- No dedicated elder abuse reporting channel (1412 focuses on children)
- Social norms against reporting family members
- Geographic isolation — on an island of 300 people, everyone knows
- Fear of losing what care they have — reporting may make the situation worse if the abuser is also the caregiver

**Legal gaps:** No elder abuse-specific legislation, no mandatory reporting for elder abuse specifically, no specialised investigation protocol, no elder-specific shelter or safe housing

---

## Slide 6: The Workforce Problem

The elderly care workforce gap is the most fundamental constraint:

- **Training a geriatrician takes ~10 years** post-medical school — even if training started today, the first Maldivian geriatrician would not be practicing until ~2035
- **No geriatric nursing specialisation** exists in Maldivian nursing education
- **No geriatric social work** training or specialisation
- **FCSC staff** who handle elderly cases are generalists with no ageing-specific training
- **Health centre nurses** on outer islands have no geriatric assessment skills
- **Caregivers** (family members) receive no formal training or support

The entire elderly care system depends on a workforce that has not been trained for elderly care.

## Slide 2: Five Priority Reform Areas

**1. Enact the Ageing Bill**
- Give elderly rights legal force for the first time
- Include: right to dignity, right to health, right to adequate standard of living, right to freedom from abuse
- Establish a dedicated elderly care mandate with institutional authority
- Mandate elder abuse reporting and investigation protocols
- Create accessibility requirements for elderly-relevant services

**2. Build the specialist workforce**
- Fund geriatric medicine training for 5-10 Maldivian doctors (start immediately — 10-year pipeline)
- Establish geriatric nursing specialisation at MNU
- Create elderly-specific training module for all FCSC staff
- Develop caregiver training programme for atoll-level family caregivers
- Recruit elderly care specialists from abroad as interim measure

**3. Develop palliative care capacity**
- Target: WHO Group 1 → Group 3a (isolated provision) within 5 years
- Train palliative care nurses and doctors
- Establish palliative care unit at IGMH
- Develop end-of-life care guidelines
- Address palliative care needs for the 85% of deaths caused by NCDs

**4. Expand community-based care**
- Establish senior citizens' centres in all 5 regional hospital islands (from 1 in Male' to 6)
- Expand home visit programme beyond 1,215 bedridden
- Develop caregiver respite services in each atoll capital
- Pilot meals-on-wheels or food delivery for housebound elderly
- Create structured volunteer visiting programme for isolated elderly
- Develop community-based dementia support pilot

**5. Plan for the pension fiscal cliff**
- Commission fiscal sustainability analysis through 2054
- Develop options for: contribution rate adjustments, retirement age, means-testing, or revenue allocation
- Communicate the fiscal trajectory publicly and transparently
- Ensure any reform protects the most vulnerable (elderly women relying solely on basic pension)
