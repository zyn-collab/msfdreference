# PART I: INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Reporting Period and Process

The Republic of Maldives ratified the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) on 1 July 1993. The Maldives entered a reservation to Article 16 of the Convention, to the extent that its provisions are inconsistent with the principles of Islamic Sharia, reflecting the constitutional status of Islam in Maldivian law and public life.^[Republic of Maldives CEDAW Ratification and Reservations, UN Treaty Collection, 1993, https://treaties.un.org/Pages/ViewDetails.aspx?src=TREATY&mtdsg_no=IV-8&chapter=4&clang=_en]

This Seventh Periodic Report covers the period from 2019 to 2025. The previous Concluding Observations issued by the CEDAW Committee following the Sixth Periodic Report are contained in document CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, adopted in November 2021.^[CEDAW Committee, Concluding Observations on the Sixth Periodic Report of Maldives, CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, November 2021, https://tbinternet.ohchr.org/_layouts/15/treatybodyexternal/Download.aspx?symbolno=CEDAW%2FC%2FMDV%2FCO%2F6&Lang=en] The Maldives takes note of those Concluding Observations and has structured this report to address the Committee's priority concerns, including the status of the reservation to Article 16, women's political participation, the prosecution of gender-based violence (GBV), and the situation of women in the outer atolls.

This report was prepared under the leadership of the Gender Equality Division of the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD), with inputs from the Family Protection Authority (FPA), the Ministry of Education, the Ministry of Health, the Maldives Police Service, the Attorney General's Office, the Elections Commission, and the Department of National Planning. Women's Development Committees (WDCs) from across the twenty-one administrative atolls contributed community-level perspectives, particularly on the situation of island women.^[Ministry of Social and Family Development, Internal consultation records for 7th CEDAW Report preparation, 2024–2025]

## 1.2 National Consultation Process

In 2024, MoSFD conducted a structured national consultation process to inform this report. Consultations took place in Male' and in six atolls (Addu, Laamu, Raa, Shaviyani, Haa Dhaal, and Gaafu Alifu), selected to ensure geographic representation of the northern, central, and southern atoll chains, as well as the urban capital. Civil society organisations including the Maldivian Democracy Network (MDN), the Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM), and local women's rights groups participated. The Gender Equality Division convened a dedicated technical working group to review draft sections, cross-reference statistical data, and verify programme implementation details.^[Ministry of Social and Family Development, National Consultation Report for 7th CEDAW Periodic Report, 2024]

## 1.3 Key Developments Since the Sixth Periodic Report (2019)

The period 2019–2025 has been marked by significant legislative and institutional progress on gender equality, alongside persistent structural challenges in political representation, economic participation, and GBV accountability. Key developments include:

- **Amendment to the Sexual Offences Act (17/2014) in 2021**, explicitly criminalising marital rape for the first time in Maldivian law.^[Sexual Offences Act Amendment, Republic of Maldives, 2021; see also HRCM, Annual Report 2021]
- **Introduction of a 33% women's quota for local council elections**, enacted before the April 2021 local government elections. This resulted in a significant increase in women's participation in local governance.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, Local Council Elections Report, 2021]
- **Launch of the AIM Project** (Accountability and Integration Matters, 2022–2025), a joint EU-UNDP-MoSFD initiative to strengthen GBV accountability throughout the justice system.^[UNDP Maldives, AIM Project Documentation, 2022, https://www.mv.undp.org]
- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021)** disproportionately impacted women's employment and economic security, with women two times more likely than men to have stopped work during the pandemic, and only 15% of emergency relief loans disbursed to women.^[Maldives COVID-19 Gender Assessment, MoSFD/UN Women, 2021]
- **Updated National Action Plan on Gender Equality (NAPGE)** launched in 2022, incorporating gender-responsive budgeting (GRB) requirements across ministries.
- **Census 2022** yielding updated labour force and demographic data disaggregated by sex, providing improved baseline data for gender equality monitoring.^[Maldives Population and Housing Census 2022, National Bureau of Statistics]

Despite this progress, the Committee's priority concerns — low political representation, high GBV rates and low prosecution, the Article 16 reservation, FGM/C (female genital mutilation/cutting) not yet criminalised, and the labour force participation gap — remain substantially unresolved and are addressed in detail in Part II of this report.

## ARTICLE 1: DEFINITION OF DISCRIMINATION

### Pre-2015 Situation

Prior to 2016, the primary instrument addressing discrimination in Maldivian law was the Constitution of 2008, which provides in Article 17 that everyone is equal before the law, and in Article 20 that no person shall be discriminated against on the basis of sex, among other grounds.^[Constitution of the Republic of Maldives, 2008, Articles 17 and 20] While the constitutional prohibition was significant, it operated without a comprehensive statutory definition of discrimination against women aligned with CEDAW's two-limbed definition (direct and indirect discrimination, in public and private spheres). Enforcement relied heavily on constitutional litigation, which was not readily accessible to women, particularly those in outer atolls.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

The Gender Equality Act (Act 18/2016), enacted on 30 June 2016, provides the most comprehensive domestic law definition of gender discrimination.^[Gender Equality Act, Act 18/2016, Republic of Maldives, 2016] The Act defines gender discrimination as any distinction, exclusion, or restriction made on the basis of gender that has the purpose or effect of nullifying or impairing the recognition, enjoyment, or exercise of human rights and fundamental freedoms by persons of any gender, in the political, economic, social, cultural, civil, or any other field. This definition substantially mirrors CEDAW Article 1 and covers both direct and indirect discrimination.

Importantly, the Gender Equality Act extends the prohibition of discrimination to the private sector, including employment and business activities, going beyond the public sphere obligations that had existed under constitutional law. The Act also obligates the state to take affirmative steps to achieve substantive equality, not merely formal equality.

However, implementation of the Gender Equality Act has been uneven. The Act created obligations for government institutions to submit Gender Equality Action Plans, and introduced gender-responsive budgeting (GRB) as a mandatory process for all state ministries. As of 2024, compliance with GRB requirements remains inconsistent, with MoSFD's internal audit finding that fewer than 40% of ministries had submitted gender-disaggregated budget analyses for fiscal years 2022–2023.^[Ministry of Social and Family Development, Gender Equality Act Implementation Review, 2024]

The reservation to Article 16 continues to limit the definition's reach in matters of marriage and family law, where principles of Islamic personal status law may be applied by courts in ways that effectively distinguish between the rights of men and women. The Maldives acknowledges the Committee's recommendation to review or withdraw the reservation to Article 16 and notes the ongoing public debate on this question, including within the context of Islamic jurisprudence.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 13]

## ARTICLE 2: POLICY MEASURES

### Pre-2015 Situation

Prior to 2012, Maldives had limited statutory measures specifically targeting gender discrimination. The principal instruments governing women's legal status were family law provisions derived from Islamic jurisprudence, the Labour Relations Act (later replaced by the Employment Act 2/2008), and a patchwork of social protection programmes. The establishment of the Ministry of Gender and Family (precursor to MoSFD) provided an institutional anchor, but without dedicated legislation, enforcement capacity was negligible.

The Domestic Violence Prevention Act (Act 3/2012) represented a landmark shift, establishing, for the first time, civil protection orders for DV victims, regulatory oversight through the newly constituted Family Protection Authority (FPA), and mandatory reporting obligations for health, education, and social sector professionals.^[Domestic Violence Prevention Act, Act 3/2012, Republic of Maldives, 2012] The Sexual Offences Act (Act 17/2014) followed, providing for criminal penalties for sexual violence including rape, sexual harassment, and exploitation of children.^[Sexual Offences Act, Act 17/2014, Republic of Maldives, 2014]

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

The period 2016–2025 saw substantial expansion of the policy framework:

**Gender Equality Act (18/2016):** As noted under Article 1, this Act is the comprehensive gender equality legislation. It mandates Gender Equality Action Plans for all government bodies, introduces GRB, formalises the mandate of Women's Development Committees, and prohibits gender-based discrimination in employment, education, goods and services, and public activities.^[Gender Equality Act, Act 18/2016, 2016]

**Prevention of Family Violence Act (2020):** This Act strengthened and expanded the 2012 DVPA framework, broadening the definition of family violence, extending protection to relationships outside formal marriage, and clarifying duties of law enforcement agencies in responding to DV disclosures.^[Prevention of Family Violence Act, Republic of Maldives, 2020]

**Amendment to the Sexual Offences Act (2021):** A critical development — the explicit criminalisation of marital rape, addressing a longstanding gap. The amendment removed the marital exception from the definition of rape, aligning Maldivian law with international human rights standards.^[Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act, Republic of Maldives, 2021]

**Anti-Human Trafficking Act (Act 12/2013):** Remains in force and provides for victim identification, criminalisation of trafficking for sexual exploitation and labour exploitation, and coordination mechanisms across immigration, police, and social services.^[Anti-Human Trafficking Act, Act 12/2013, Republic of Maldives, 2013]

**Employment Act (Act 2/2008):** Contains provisions on non-discrimination in employment on grounds of sex, maternity protections (60 days paid maternity leave), and anti-harassment provisions. The Act applies to both formal and informal sector workers, though enforcement capacity for the informal sector remains limited.^[Employment Act, Act 2/2008, Republic of Maldives, 2008]

**Implementation Gaps:** Despite this framework, significant implementation gaps persist. The conviction rate for DV cases that reach the court system was 16.7% for the period 2016–2019.^[Family Protection Authority, Annual Statistics 2019] Only approximately 3% of DV/GBV cases reported to police are ultimately submitted for prosecution.^[AIM Project Baseline Assessment, UNDP/MoSFD, 2022] These figures indicate systemic barriers at multiple stages of the justice pathway — victim willingness to pursue cases, police recording practices, prosecutorial decision-making, and judicial interpretation. The AIM Project (2022–2025) is specifically designed to address these bottlenecks through capacity development, case management reform, and multi-agency coordination protocols.^[UNDP Maldives, AIM Project Midterm Review, 2024]

The National Action Plan on Gender Equality (NAPGE), updated in 2022, provides the overarching policy framework, setting targets in political participation, economic empowerment, GBV response, and institutional capacity. Progress against NAPGE targets is reported annually to the Majlis (Parliament), though the HRCM has noted that monitoring mechanisms remain weak and that data collection for gender-disaggregated indicators across sectoral ministries is inconsistent.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Annual Report 2023]

## ARTICLE 3: GUARANTEES OF HUMAN RIGHTS AND FUNDAMENTAL FREEDOMS

### National Machinery for Gender Equality

**Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD):** The principal national machinery responsible for gender equality policy. The Gender Equality Division within MoSFD serves as the CEDAW focal point, coordinates GRB implementation, manages the NAPGE, and maintains liaison with international treaty bodies. Budget allocations to MoSFD, including the Gender Equality Division, have remained modest relative to the scope of its mandate, with staffing of fewer than twenty dedicated gender specialists for national-level programming.^[MoSFD Annual Report 2024]

**Family Protection Authority (FPA):** An independent regulatory body established under the DVPA 2012. The FPA receives, investigates, and refers DV complaints; issues protection orders; oversees a network of safe houses; and publishes quarterly GBV statistics. As of 2024, the FPA operated with three dedicated DV caseworkers for the Greater Male' area, which contains approximately 40% of the national population.^[Family Protection Authority, Capacity Assessment, 2023] This staffing level is widely recognised as critically insufficient for the volume of cases (696 cases in 2021; 537 cases in 2024).^[FPA Quarterly Statistics, 2021 and 2024]

**Women's Development Committees (WDCs):** Established under the Decentralisation Act and formalised by the Gender Equality Act (18/2016), WDCs operate in each of the approximately 270 inhabited islands. They serve as the frontline community-level body for gender equality, receiving complaints, providing referrals, and facilitating women's participation in local decision-making. WDC members are elected by island communities. However, WDCs operate without dedicated budgets in most islands, depend on volunteer time, and have limited authority to compel action by island councils or higher administrative tiers.^[UNDP Maldives/MoSFD, WDC Capacity Assessment, 2022]

**Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM):** The national human rights institution, established under Act 6/2006. The HRCM monitors compliance with CEDAW and other treaties, receives complaints, and publishes thematic reports, including on GBV, women's rights, and discrimination.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, mandate and functions, https://hrcm.org.mv]

**Gender-Responsive Budgeting (GRB):** Introduced as a mandatory process under the Gender Equality Act (18/2016), GRB requires all government ministries to analyse budget allocations for gender impacts and report against gender-disaggregated indicators. Implementation has been supported by UNDP and UN Women through capacity building. As of 2024, progress is uneven across ministries.^[MoSFD, GRB Implementation Report, 2024]

## ARTICLE 4: TEMPORARY SPECIAL MEASURES

### Pre-2015 Situation

Prior to 2021, there were no statutory or constitutional temporary special measures (TSMs) specifically mandating women's representation in political or public institutions. Constitutional provisions guaranteeing equality were interpreted by the courts and relevant commissions as prohibiting differential treatment, including affirmative quotas, in some contexts, creating a legal tension with CEDAW Article 4.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**33% Women's Quota for Local Councils (2021):** The most significant TSM introduced in the reporting period was the enactment of a mandatory 33% women's quota for local government (island and atoll council) elections, ahead of the April 2021 local council elections.^[Local Government Act Amendment, Republic of Maldives, 2021] This measure resulted in a marked increase in women's representation in local governance: whereas women held approximately 4% of local council seats prior to 2021, the 2021 elections produced women occupying approximately one-third of council positions.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, 2021 Local Council Elections Report] This is the most successful TSM yet implemented in Maldives and demonstrates the effectiveness of legislative quotas in contexts where voluntary measures have failed to produce change.

**National Parliament (People's Majlis):** No statutory quota for women's representation in Parliament exists. The 2024 People's Majlis elections resulted in only 4 women elected out of 87 seats (4.6%), the lowest female parliamentary representation in the Maldives' democratic history.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, Parliamentary Election Results, 2024] The Maldives acknowledges the Committee's 2021 recommendation to introduce temporary special measures for parliamentary representation^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 31] and notes that legislative proposals for a parliamentary quota have been tabled but have not yet achieved passage.

**Cabinet and Senior Public Service:** Women constituted 4 of 22 Cabinet members (18.2%) as of 2024.^[President's Office, Cabinet Composition, 2024] Women remain underrepresented at all senior levels of the public service. MoSFD has implemented mentorship and leadership training programmes for women in the civil service, but targeted TSMs for senior appointments have not been formalised.

**Judicial Appointments:** Women are significantly underrepresented in the judiciary. The Judicial Service Commission has acknowledged this gap but no formal TSM for judicial appointments has been adopted. As of 2024, fewer than 15% of judges across all courts are women, and no woman has served on the Supreme Court bench.^[Judicial Service Commission of Maldives, Annual Report 2024]

**SDFC Loans and Economic Empowerment:** The Social Development Finance Corporation (SDFC) has not introduced a formal gender quota for loan allocation, resulting in only 20% of SDFC loans going to women despite women constituting approximately half of the population.^[SDFC Annual Report 2023] The Maldives considers gender-targeted financial products a potential TSM for women's economic empowerment and is reviewing options in this regard.

## ARTICLE 5: SEX ROLES AND STEREOTYPES

### Pre-2015 Situation

Traditional gender roles in Maldivian society have long positioned women primarily as caregivers and domestic managers, with men as primary breadwinners and public decision-makers. These roles were reinforced through educational curricula, religious discourse, and media representations. Prior to 2016, there was no systematic national effort to review educational materials for gender stereotyping or to address gender norms through public communication strategies.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Education Sector:** The Ministry of Education conducted a curriculum review in 2018–2019 that included a gender audit of textbook content. The audit identified persistent gender stereotyping in illustrations, narrative examples, and role assignments across primary and secondary materials. Revised national curriculum standards issued in 2020 include gender-sensitive content guidelines.^[Ministry of Education, National Curriculum Review Report, 2020] However, implementation at the school level varies, and teacher training on gender-responsive pedagogy has been limited in scale.

**Media and Advertising:** The Gender Equality Act (18/2016) includes provisions prohibiting gender-discriminatory advertising and media content that denigrates or demeans persons on the basis of gender. Enforcement through the Maldives Broadcasting Commission has been selective, and no formal gender monitoring mechanism for broadcast media has been established.^[Maldives Broadcasting Commission, Regulatory Framework, 2023]

**Domestic and Care Work:** The DHS 2016–17 data indicates that 49% of women outside the labour force cite domestic responsibilities or childcare as the primary reason for non-participation.^[Maldives Demographic and Health Survey 2016–17, National Bureau of Statistics/Ministry of Health, 2018] This reflects the persistence of the expectation that women bear the primary responsibility for unpaid care work. No national policy on recognition, reduction, or redistribution of unpaid care work has been adopted.

**COVID-19 and Gender Norms:** The pandemic (2020–2021) reinforced traditional gender roles: women were disproportionately responsible for home-based schooling and caregiving, leading many to exit the labour force. The COVID-19 Gender Assessment (MoSFD/UN Women, 2021) found women were two times more likely than men to have stopped work during the pandemic, with significant concentration in tourism-adjacent hospitality and retail sectors.^[MoSFD/UN Women, COVID-19 Gender Assessment Maldives, 2021]

**Religious Discourse:** Islamic religious discourse, delivered through formal Friday sermons coordinated by the Ministry of Islamic Affairs and through broadcast media, continues to reflect a range of perspectives on gender roles, some of which reinforce traditional subordination of women in family decision-making. The Ministry of Social and Family Development has begun working with the Ministry of Islamic Affairs to develop gender-sensitive religious messaging, particularly in the context of DV prevention.^[MoSFD, DV Prevention Communication Strategy, 2023]

**Female Genital Mutilation/Cutting (FGM/C) and Cultural Norms:** The persistence of FGM/C (13% overall prevalence per DHS 2016–17) reflects deep-seated cultural and religious norms, though the dramatic intergenerational decline (38% prevalence in women aged 45–49; 1% in women aged 15–19) demonstrates that norm change is possible and is occurring.^[Maldives DHS 2016–17, Chapter on Women's Health, 2018] The Maldives addresses FGM/C in detail under Article 12.

## ARTICLE 6: TRAFFICKING AND EXPLOITATION

### Pre-2015 Situation

Maldives is primarily a destination country for labour migration, with a large foreign workforce (estimated at 150,000–200,000 predominantly Bangladeshi, Indian, Sri Lankan, and other South Asian workers). The risk of trafficking — particularly labour trafficking of migrant workers and, to a lesser extent, sexual trafficking — was recognised before the enactment of specific anti-trafficking legislation. No formal national referral mechanism for trafficking victims existed prior to 2013.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Anti-Human Trafficking Act (12/2013):** This Act criminalises trafficking in persons for sexual exploitation, forced labour, organ trafficking, and other purposes. It provides for victim identification, protection orders, and government-funded victim assistance. The Act establishes coordination between the Maldives Police Service, immigration authorities, and MoSFD for case management.^[Anti-Human Trafficking Act, Act 12/2013, 2013]

**Prevalence and Vulnerability:** Women and girls constitute the majority of identified trafficking victims for sexual exploitation purposes. Foreign women in the sex trade — largely operating in Male' and resort islands — represent a key population. Migrant domestic workers, though a smaller sector in Maldives compared to other South Asian contexts, are also at risk of labour exploitation. The intersection of irregular migration status and trafficking vulnerability means that many victims do not come forward due to fear of deportation.^[US Department of State, Trafficking in Persons Report — Maldives, 2023, https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-trafficking-in-persons-report/maldives/]

**Family Protection Authority and GBV Intersection:** The FPA's mandate includes cases where trafficking intersects with domestic abuse situations, including women trafficked into forced marriages or exploitative domestic arrangements. Data sharing between FPA, police, and immigration remains a structural challenge, as noted in the AIM Project baseline review.^[AIM Project Baseline Assessment, UNDP/MoSFD, 2022]

**Resort Sector and Exploitation Risks:** The tourism resort sector, which is geographically isolated (resorts on uninhabited islands), employs a predominantly male Maldivian workforce and a significant number of foreign workers. Women working in resorts (only 3% of Maldivian female workers are employed in resorts)^[Census 2022, Labour Force Module] face particular vulnerabilities related to isolation, limited access to complaint mechanisms, and social stigma attached to women working in hospitality environments. Cases of sexual harassment and exploitation in the resort sector are believed to be significantly underreported.

**Capacity Gaps:** The anti-trafficking response is hampered by limited victim identification capacity among front-line officials (immigration, police, healthcare workers), insufficient shelter facilities for trafficking victims (the Government shelter in Male' has limited capacity), and inadequate legal aid for trafficking survivors.^[HRCM, Report on Trafficking Situation in Maldives, 2022]

**International Cooperation:** Maldives is a party to the ASEAN Convention Against Trafficking in Persons and cooperates bilaterally with origin countries (Bangladesh, India, Sri Lanka) on case referrals. However, formal Memoranda of Understanding with major sending countries for anti-trafficking cooperation are not yet fully operational.^[Attorney General's Office, International Cooperation on Trafficking, 2023]

## ARTICLE 7: POLITICAL AND PUBLIC LIFE

### Pre-2015 Situation

Women's participation in formal political life in Maldives has historically been very low. The 2008 democratic transition and multiparty elections did not produce a corresponding increase in women's electoral representation. In the 2014 parliamentary elections, women held approximately 5.9% of Majlis seats — 6 out of 85 seats — a figure that, while modest, was higher than the outcomes produced by subsequent elections.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, 2014 Parliamentary Election Results]

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**People's Majlis (Parliament):** The 2024 People's Majlis elections produced a historically low outcome for women's parliamentary representation: 4 women elected out of 87 seats, representing 4.6% of the legislature.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, 2024 Parliamentary Elections Results] This places Maldives among the lowest-ranking countries globally for women's parliamentary representation. No statutory quota for parliamentary elections exists. Political parties have not adopted voluntary quotas or candidate selection targets for women. The Maldives acknowledges this as a critical failure and notes that the Committee's 2021 recommendation to introduce temporary special measures for parliamentary elections has not yet been acted upon.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 31]

**Cabinet:** Women constituted 4 of 22 Cabinet ministers (18.2%) as of 2024, holding portfolios in social development, education, environment, and youth.^[President's Office, Cabinet, 2024] While this represents an improvement over some earlier periods, Cabinet composition fluctuates significantly with changes of government, and no institutional mechanism ensures sustained representation.

**Local Councils:** The introduction of the 33% women's quota for local council elections in 2021 produced a structural breakthrough. The 2021 local council elections resulted in women holding approximately one-third of all island and atoll council seats, compared to approximately 4% prior to the quota.^[Elections Commission of Maldives, 2021 Local Council Elections Report] Women council members report challenges in being heard within council deliberations, having limited access to resources, and facing community resistance to their authority, highlighting that formal representation must be accompanied by substantive empowerment.^[WDC Survey, MoSFD, 2022]

**Judiciary:** Women are significantly underrepresented in the Maldivian judiciary. No woman has served on the Supreme Court. Women comprise fewer than 15% of judges across all courts. The Judicial Service Commission has not adopted TSMs for gender-balanced appointments.^[Judicial Service Commission, Annual Report 2024]

**Civil Society and Community Leadership:** Women play active roles in civil society organisations, including in gender rights advocacy, environmental activism, and community development. Women's leadership in civil society has been an important driver of legislative reform in the reporting period.

**Key Barriers:** Research by the Maldivian Democracy Network (MDN) identifies the following as principal barriers to women's political participation: absence of childcare support for candidates, campaign financing constraints, gender-based harassment during campaigns (including online harassment), family and community expectations that women remain in domestic roles, and the small size of the candidate pool due to limited women in senior party positions.^[Maldivian Democracy Network, Women's Political Participation Study, 2023]

## ARTICLE 8: INTERNATIONAL REPRESENTATION

Maldives has made incremental progress in women's representation in its diplomatic and international relations workforce. Women hold senior positions in several diplomatic missions, including ambassador-level appointments. However, women remain underrepresented at the senior levels of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs' career diplomatic service. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs has not published disaggregated data on the gender composition of its diplomatic corps, which the Maldives acknowledges is a monitoring gap.^[Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Diplomatic Service Overview, 2024]

Women play increasing roles in Maldives' engagement with international organisations, including through the permanent mission to the United Nations in New York and Geneva. Maldives has supported gender-equality resolutions within the UN General Assembly and has engaged actively with CEDAW and other human rights treaty mechanisms. No formal gender parity target or TSM for international representation has been adopted.^[Ministry of Foreign Affairs, UN Engagement Record, 2024]

## ARTICLE 9: NATIONALITY

The Constitution of Maldives and the Citizenship Act provide for the transmission of Maldivian citizenship through both the mother and the father, in principle placing women and men on equal footing with respect to the transmission of nationality to children born to Maldivian parents.^[Constitution of the Republic of Maldives 2008; Citizenship Act, Republic of Maldives] Maldivian women married to foreign nationals can transmit Maldivian citizenship to their children, as can Maldivian men.

However, practical barriers have been identified with respect to Maldivian women married to non-Maldivian men residing in the country. The spousal visa framework and residency regulations are structured in ways that can create administrative dependency and instability, particularly in cases of marriage breakdown. These issues are being reviewed by the Department of Immigration.^[Department of Immigration and Emigration, Residency Framework Review, 2023]

Children born outside of wedlock face specific legal status challenges in Maldivian law, which is informed by Islamic jurisprudence on legitimacy. The Maldives acknowledges that this is an area requiring further review to ensure non-discriminatory outcomes for mothers and children in such situations.^[HRCM, Report on Children's Rights, 2023]

## ARTICLE 10: EDUCATION

### Pre-2015 Situation

Maldives achieved near-universal primary school enrolment for both boys and girls in the 1990s and 2000s, a significant achievement for a geographically dispersed archipelagic state. Secondary school completion rates, however, were historically low and unequal. In the outer atolls, the absence of secondary schools on most islands (only 22 of 188 inhabited islands had secondary schools as of the mid-2000s) meant that children, including girls, either boarded in Male' for secondary education or did not complete schooling. Boarding arrangements in Male' were more accessible to boys historically due to safety concerns for girls.^[Ministry of Education, Education Statistical Yearbook, 2010]

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Enrolment and Achievement:** Maldives has largely achieved gender parity in primary and secondary school enrolment. In fact, girls consistently outperform boys at the secondary level, and women substantially outnumber men in higher education: women constitute 59–64% of higher education enrolees.^[Maldives Higher Education Ministry, Statistical Report, 2023] The Maldives National University and private institutions report that women represent the majority of graduates across most faculties, including law, social sciences, business, and health sciences.

**Reverse Gender Gap:** A distinctive feature of Maldivian education is the reverse gender gap at secondary and tertiary levels: 88% of secondary school dropouts are male.^[Ministry of Education, Dropout Monitoring Data, 2023] Boys disproportionately leave school early to enter the labour market, particularly in fishing and construction, while girls remain in school longer. This paradox — women outperforming men in education but underperforming in labour force participation and political representation — reflects structural barriers that operate beyond the education system.

**Outer Atoll Access:** Geographic access to quality secondary and tertiary education remains unequal. Students from islands without secondary schools must travel to atoll capitals or Male', incurring costs and safety risks. Girls from conservative island communities may face family resistance to inter-island travel for education. Scholarship programmes and boarding facilities have expanded, but geographic inequality in education quality persists.^[Ministry of Education, Atoll Education Access Report, 2022]

**Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET):** Women are underrepresented in TVET programmes, particularly those leading to skilled trades in construction, marine engineering, and information technology. These sectors offer higher wages and employment stability, and the gendered streaming of TVET limits women's access to higher-earning occupational pathways.^[Ministry of Higher Education, TVET Gender Analysis, 2023]

**Gender-Sensitive Curricula:** As noted under Article 5, curriculum reform is ongoing. Life skills education, including content on healthy relationships, sexual and reproductive health rights, and gender equality, has been introduced in limited form but is not systematically embedded across grades or geographies.^[Ministry of Education, Life Skills Curriculum Review, 2021]

**Teacher Workforce:** Women constitute the majority of teachers at primary level but are underrepresented among school principals and senior educational administrators, reflecting a broader pattern of women's underrepresentation in leadership positions.^[Ministry of Education, Teacher Workforce Statistics, 2023]

## ARTICLE 11: EMPLOYMENT

### Pre-2015 Situation

Female labour force participation rates have historically been low in Maldives, particularly by comparison with female educational attainment levels. Prior to 2008, formal data on labour force participation by sex were limited. The Employment Act (Act 2/2008) introduced foundational protections including non-discrimination provisions and maternity leave entitlements, but enforcement mechanisms were not robust.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR) Gap:** The Census 2022 confirmed a large and persistent gender gap in labour force participation: female LFPR is 44%, compared to male LFPR of 80%.^[Maldives Population and Housing Census 2022, National Bureau of Statistics] This gap is among the largest in the South Asian region for a middle-income country. The primary self-reported barrier for women outside the labour force is domestic and childcare responsibilities (cited by 49% of non-participating women).^[DHS 2016–17] Affordable, accessible childcare infrastructure is severely limited, particularly outside Male'.

**Resort Sector Exclusion:** Tourism constitutes approximately 28% of GDP and is the largest formal employment sector. However, Maldivian women are almost entirely excluded from the resort sector workforce: only 3% of Maldivian female workers are employed in resorts.^[Census 2022, Labour Force Module] This exclusion is attributable to multiple factors: social and family resistance to women working in geographically isolated resort environments, the perceived moral risk of women in mixed-gender hospitality settings, the physical demands of resort work (particularly in mechanical, dive, and outdoor roles), and recruitment practices that effectively favour men for entry-level roles that women might otherwise fill. The resort sector exclusion has significant economic consequences, as resort jobs typically offer higher wages and benefits than other formal sector employment available to women.

**Domestic Work:** Domestic work — encompassing cleaning, cooking, and childcare services — employs a significant but uncounted proportion of women, both Maldivian and foreign. Domestic workers are not fully covered by the Employment Act's protections, leaving women in this sector outside formal minimum wage, leave entitlement, and anti-harassment frameworks.^[Employment Relations Authority, Domestic Worker Vulnerability Assessment, 2022]

**Maternity Leave and Protections:** The Employment Act provides for 60 days of paid maternity leave, funded by the employer.^[Employment Act, Act 2/2008, Section on Maternity Leave] This provision applies to the formal sector. The absence of a state-funded maternity insurance scheme means that small employers — who employ many women — face direct financial disincentives when hiring women of childbearing age, contributing to informal discrimination in recruitment. No paternity leave entitlement is currently mandated in law, limiting the redistribution of childcare responsibilities.

**Gender Pay Gap:** No official gender pay gap estimate is produced by Maldives statistical agencies. The National Bureau of Statistics' labour module in Census 2022 collected wage data, and disaggregated analysis is expected to be published in 2025. Anecdotal evidence and sectoral analysis suggest that women are concentrated in lower-wage occupations (teaching, nursing, clerical roles) while men dominate higher-wage sectors (fishing, construction, tourism operational roles).^[National Bureau of Statistics, Labour Force Analysis Discussion Paper, 2024]

**COVID-19 Impact:** The pandemic disproportionately affected women's employment. Women were two times more likely than men to stop work during the pandemic.^[MoSFD/UN Women, COVID-19 Gender Assessment, 2021] This reflected both sectoral concentration (women in hospitality and retail which closed) and increased domestic care burdens. Access to COVID-19 relief loans was also heavily skewed: only 15% of emergency business relief loans were disbursed to women.^[SDFC, COVID-19 Relief Loan Data, 2021] This disparity may be attributable to women's lower rates of formal business registration, collateral ownership, and financial institution access.

**Women with Disabilities:** Women with disabilities face compounded labour market exclusion. The LFPR for women with disabilities is estimated at 28%, compared to 44% for women overall.^[National Bureau of Statistics, Disability Module, Census 2022] Physical inaccessibility of workplaces, employer discrimination, and intersecting social stigma on both gender and disability grounds drive this outcome.

**Informal Sector:** A significant proportion of women's economic activity occurs in the informal sector — home-based crafts (lacquer work, mat weaving, traditional crafts), small trade, and food preparation — which is not captured in LFPR statistics. These activities are often not recognised as "work" by respondents or enumerators and generate income without the legal protections of formal employment.^[MoSFD, Women's Economic Participation Qualitative Study, 2022]

## ARTICLE 12: HEALTH

### Pre-2015 Situation

Maldives made dramatic progress in maternal and infant health indicators in the 1990s–2000s, reducing maternal mortality from among the highest in the South Asian region to significantly lower levels through infrastructure development, community health worker deployment, and improved primary care access. However, reproductive health services and comprehensive sexual health education remained limited, and data on FGM/C were not systematically collected.

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Maternal Mortality:** Maldives' maternal mortality ratio (MMR) has followed a volatile trajectory: 69 per 100,000 live births in 2006, falling to 13 per 100,000 in 2012, rising sharply to 60 per 100,000 in 2021 (linked partly to COVID-19 pandemic disruptions to obstetric services), and returning to 34 per 100,000 in 2022.^[Ministry of Health, Health Statistics Annual Report, 2022; WHO/UNICEF/UNFPA, Maternal Mortality Estimates] The 2021 spike is attributed to disruptions in antenatal care access during the pandemic, including women unable to travel to tertiary facilities in Male' for complicated deliveries. The return to lower levels by 2022 reflects restoration of services. Skilled birth attendance is high in Male' (virtually 100%) but remains lower in outer atolls, where some deliveries occur without skilled attendants due to transportation challenges and the absence of midwives on smaller islands.^[Ministry of Health, Obstetric Care Review 2022]

**Contraception and Reproductive Health:** Modern contraceptive prevalence is low at 27% (DHS 2016–17), with an unmet need for family planning of 29%.^[DHS 2016–17, Reproductive Health Chapter, Ministry of Health/NBS, 2018] This means more than a quarter of women who wish to avoid pregnancy are not using effective contraception. Barriers include limited availability of a full range of contraceptive methods in outer atolls, stigma around contraception access for unmarried women (even though unmarried sex is criminalised under Islamic law), provider attitudes, and limited counselling capacity. Emergency contraception is not consistently available at atoll-level pharmacies or health centres.

The Maldives has not legalised abortion; termination of pregnancy is not permitted under law except in cases of risk to the life of the mother.^[Penal Code of Maldives, Provisions on Abortion] This restricts reproductive choices for women and girls and means that pregnancies resulting from rape or incest must be carried to term in the absence of qualifying medical circumstances. The Maldives acknowledges the Committee's concern on this matter.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 37]

**Female Genital Mutilation/Cutting (FGM/C):** The DHS 2016–17 recorded an overall FGM/C prevalence of 13% in women aged 15–49.^[DHS 2016–17, FGM/C Chapter] The data reveal a pronounced intergenerational decline: prevalence among women aged 45–49 is 38%, falling to 1% among women aged 15–19. This indicates significant norm change in younger generations and suggests that FGM/C is being abandoned without formal legal prohibition in many communities. Nevertheless, FGM/C remains a practice affecting some girls, and the Maldives has not yet criminalised it. The practice in Maldives typically takes the form of a minor nick or incision (Type IV WHO classification in most documented cases), but the Maldives acknowledges that any form of FGM/C constitutes a violation of girls' bodily integrity.

The CEDAW Committee's 2021 Concluding Observations called upon Maldives to explicitly criminalise FGM/C.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 39] This recommendation has been under review by MoSFD in consultation with the Ministry of Islamic Affairs. Public health campaigns have been conducted through the Maldives Health Protection Agency and community health workers to discourage the practice. The Maldives commits to further legislative review.

**Mental Health:** Women are disproportionately affected by depression and anxiety disorders, including domestic violence-related PTSD. Mental health services remain severely limited: the Maldives has one psychiatrist for every 100,000 people, predominantly based in Male'.^[Ministry of Health, Mental Health Gap Analysis, 2023] Women GBV survivors have very limited access to trauma-informed counselling. The FPA's caseworkers provide some counselling support but are not trained as clinical mental health professionals. The AIM Project includes a component on psychosocial support services for GBV survivors.^[AIM Project Implementation Plan, 2022]

**Aasandha Universal Health Coverage:** The Aasandha scheme, Maldives' national health insurance programme, provides coverage for inpatient and outpatient services at IGMH (Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital, Male') and partner hospitals. Coverage extends to all citizens including those in atolls for referral care, but the cost and logistics of inter-island travel to access specialist services present barriers, particularly for women with young children, for elderly women, and for women with disabilities.^[Aasandha Annual Report 2023]

**Substance Use and Women:** While substance abuse has been documented primarily among young men, the intersection of DV and substance use — where perpetrators' substance abuse is a risk factor for escalated violence — is poorly addressed by current rehabilitation and DV response systems. Women affected by partners' substance abuse have limited specific service pathways.^[FPA, DV Risk Factor Analysis, 2022]

## ARTICLE 13: ECONOMIC AND SOCIAL BENEFITS

### Social Protection Framework

**Aasandha (Universal Health Coverage):** As noted under Article 12, Aasandha provides health insurance to all Maldivian citizens. Women benefit from this scheme on formally equal terms, but practical access barriers (transportation, childcare, information) can disadvantage women in outer atolls.^[Aasandha Annual Report 2023]

**Social Protection Allowances:** The Maldives maintains a system of targeted social allowances administered by MoSFD:
- *Disability Allowance:* Monthly cash transfer to persons with disabilities; women with disabilities are entitled to receive this benefit on equal terms with men, though the intersection of disability and gender creates compounded service access barriers.^[MoSFD, Social Protection Statistics, 2023]
- *Single Parent Allowance:* Targeted at single parents, predominantly women, providing monthly support for child-rearing costs.
- *Elderly Allowance:* Universal allowance for citizens above 65, providing a basic income floor. Women constitute the majority of recipients due to longer life expectancy.^[National Social Protection Agency, Annual Statistics, 2023]

**Pension:** The Maldives Retirement Pension Scheme (MRPS), administered by the Maldives Pension Administration Office, provides for individual account accumulation based on employment contributions. Given women's lower formal sector participation and shorter careers (due to caregiving interruptions), women's pension accumulations are structurally lower than men's, creating old-age economic vulnerability.^[Maldives Pension Administration Office, Annual Report 2023] A non-contributory pension for those without formal employment records exists but at a lower value than the contributory scheme.

**SDFC Loans:** The Social Development Finance Corporation (SDFC) provides micro and small business loans to Maldivians. As of 2023, only 20% of SDFC loans were disbursed to women.^[SDFC Annual Report 2023] This reflects women's lower rates of formal business registration, limited collateral ownership (land and property predominantly registered in male names), and lower financial literacy rates, as well as potentially demand-side factors including women's self-selection out of formal business activities. The Maldives is reviewing gender-responsive lending criteria and collateral requirements.

**COVID-19 Relief and Economic Recovery:** As noted under Article 11, only 15% of COVID-19 emergency relief loans went to women, highlighting structural exclusion of women from formal financial systems that is not overcome by gender-neutral criteria in emergency response programmes.^[SDFC COVID-19 Relief Data, 2021]

**Sports and Cultural Life:** Women's access to public sporting facilities and recreational infrastructure is unequal, particularly in outer atolls. Cultural norms restrict women's participation in team sports and public exercise in some communities. The Maldives Football Association has taken steps to expand women's football, though funding and facilities remain unequal.^[Ministry of Youth, Sports and Community Empowerment, Gender Report, 2023]

## ARTICLE 14: RURAL AND ISLAND WOMEN

### Geographic and Service Context

The Maldives consists of approximately 1,192 coral islands of which 188 are inhabited, spread across a north-south extent of approximately 820 kilometres.^[National Bureau of Statistics, Census 2022] This geographic reality fundamentally shapes the lives of women outside Male', creating significant inequalities in access to economic opportunities, justice, health, education, and social services.

### Pre-2015 Situation

Prior to the decentralisation reforms of 2010 and the formalisation of WDCs, island women had very limited formal mechanisms for voicing concerns or accessing government services. Social services were predominantly Male'-centric. Women in atolls relied on extended family networks, traditional community structures, and, in extremis, the goodwill of island chiefs (Katheebs).

### Current Situation (2015–2025)

**Women's Development Committees (WDCs):** The 270 WDCs across inhabited islands, formalised under the Gender Equality Act (18/2016), are the primary formal channel for island women's engagement with the governance system. WDC members are elected from island communities and serve as focal points for gender equality, DV referrals, and women's empowerment activities.^[Gender Equality Act 18/2016; MoSFD, WDC National Review, 2022]

However, WDCs operate under significant resource constraints: no dedicated recurrent budget is allocated to most WDCs; members are volunteers with no stipend; WDCs have no authority to require action from island councils or government ministries; and capacity (including basic knowledge of DV legislation and referral pathways) varies greatly.^[UNDP/MoSFD, WDC Capacity Assessment, 2022] A 2022 assessment found that fewer than 40% of WDC members had received any formal training from MoSFD in the preceding two years.^[ibid.]

**Health Access:** Atoll women face significant barriers to maternal and reproductive health services. Most islands have Basic Health Units (BHUs) or Health Centres staffed by general nurses and health officers, but without obstetricians, gynaecologists, or midwives. Pregnant women in later stages must travel — often by speedboat, sometimes in adverse weather — to atoll hospitals or Male' for delivery. This creates safety risks and imposes significant costs.^[Ministry of Health, Atoll Health Services Review, 2022] Women in atolls have lower rates of antenatal care visits (fewer than the WHO-recommended eight contacts) and lower contraceptive coverage.^[DHS 2016–17]

**Economic Participation:** Women in outer atolls are predominantly engaged in informal economic activities: weaving, handicrafts, small-scale food production and trade, and subsistence activities. Access to formal employment is limited by the absence of large employers outside Male' and the non-availability of the resort sector to women (resorts are on uninhabited islands and generally employ from Male' or through the foreign workforce). SDFC loan access is further limited in atolls by lower financial literacy, greater distances to service points, and lower awareness of available products.^[MoSFD, Atoll Women's Economic Needs Assessment, 2022]

**Justice Access:** Women in atolls seeking protection orders, legal aid, or judicial remedies for DV or family law matters face severe access barriers. Lower courts in atolls often lack trained judges, and the FPA — with its caseworkers concentrated in Male' — is not accessible by phone or in person to most atoll women. Legal aid services, where they exist, are predominantly in Male'.^[AIM Project Baseline Assessment, 2022] The HRCM maintains regional offices in some atolls, providing some complaint-receiving capacity.

**Fertility and Education:** Women in atolls have higher average fertility than women in Male' (total fertility rate of approximately 2.8 in atolls vs approximately 1.8 in Male' as of Census 2022).^[National Bureau of Statistics, Census 2022 Demographic Profiles] Earlier marriage, earlier first birth, higher parity, and lower educational attainment levels among atoll women reflect the compounding disadvantages of limited services.

**Atoll-Level DV Response:** The FPA's geographic reach is extremely limited. In practice, DV response in atolls depends on local police, island council members, WDC members, and health workers — few of whom have received specialised DV response training. Atoll hospital staff are the most common first point of contact for DV survivors in outer atolls but are not systematically trained in DV identification, safety planning, or referral protocols.^[FPA, DV Response Assessment — Outer Atolls, 2023]

## ARTICLE 15: LEGAL EQUALITY

### Access to Courts and Legal Processes

Women in Maldives have formal equality before the law, including the right to initiate litigation, appear as witnesses, and receive equal weight as parties in civil proceedings.^[Constitution of the Republic of Maldives 2008, Article 42] However, several structural factors limit substantive legal equality in practice.

**Islamic Law and Evidence:** In proceedings conducted under Islamic personal status law — including family law matters before the Family Court — the evidentiary framework drawn from classical Sharia jurisprudence may assign different weight to testimony by men and women in specific contexts. While this is not universally applied in modern Maldivian court practice, it creates uncertainty and disadvantage for women in family law proceedings.^[HRCM, Legal Framework Analysis on Women's Rights, 2022]

**Legal Aid:** Legal aid services in Maldives are limited in capacity and almost entirely Male'-based. The Legal Aid Commission, established in 2016, provides civil and criminal legal aid but has insufficient staff and budget to meet demand.^[Legal Aid Commission of Maldives, Annual Report 2023] Women seeking legal redress in DV, divorce, or custody matters in atolls typically have no access to legal aid; those in Male' may face long waiting periods.

**Flogging Sentences:** The Maldives retains judicial corporal punishment (flogging) under its Penal Code for certain offences, including extramarital sex (zina). Women constitute 85% of those receiving flogging sentences.^[HRCM, Report on Corporal Punishment, 2022] This profound gender disparity in sentencing outcomes reflects deeply discriminatory application of laws on extramarital sexual conduct, where women are far more likely than men to be identified, prosecuted, and convicted. The Maldives acknowledges this as a severe legal inequality and notes that the CEDAW Committee has raised this as a priority concern.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 51] This issue is closely tied to the intersection of Islamic criminal law principles and gender discrimination. No legislative reform on flogging sentences has been enacted in the reporting period.

**Access to Courts in Atolls:** The court system in atolls has limited judicial capacity, and magistrate courts handle a range of family, civil, and criminal matters. Women seeking protection orders, divorce, or other family law remedies in atolls may face delays, inadequately trained judges, and social pressure not to pursue legal action.

**AIM Project:** The AIM Project (2022–2025) includes a justice sector reform component specifically aimed at improving GBV case management within the prosecution and court system, including developing standard operating procedures for GBV case handling by the Maldives Prosecution Service (MPS), improving evidence-gathering protocols by police, and building judicial capacity on gender-sensitive adjudication.^[AIM Project Implementation Plan, 2022]

## ARTICLE 16: MARRIAGE AND FAMILY LIFE

### Reservation and Its Scope

The Maldives entered a reservation to Article 16 of the Convention at the time of ratification (1993), to the extent that Article 16's provisions are inconsistent with Islamic Sharia. This reservation reflects the constitutional position of Islam as the state religion and the application of Islamic personal status law to matters of marriage, divorce, custody, and inheritance.^[Republic of Maldives CEDAW Reservation, UN Treaty Collection, 1993]

The Maldives notes the CEDAW Committee's 2021 recommendation to review and withdraw this reservation, and acknowledges the Committee's view that this reservation is incompatible with the object and purpose of the Convention.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 13] The Maldives respectfully notes that this reservation reflects a deeply held constitutional and religious position and that any change requires broad national consultation and would need to be consistent with the Islamic character of the State as enshrined in the Constitution.^[Constitution of the Republic of Maldives 2008, Articles 2, 9, 10]

### Minimum Age of Marriage

The minimum legal age of marriage in Maldives is 18 years for both males and females, with exceptions possible through court order.^[Family Law Act, Republic of Maldives] Despite this legal minimum, the ADB 2025 data indicates a child marriage rate of 2.2% before age 18.^[Asian Development Bank, Gender Data Analysis — Maldives, 2025] This suggests that child marriages continue to occur, likely in rural island contexts where court supervision of marriages is less rigorous. The Maldives is committed to eliminating child marriage and has strengthened registration requirements.

### Polygamy

Polygamous marriage is permitted under Maldivian law subject to judicial approval and fulfilment of conditions drawn from Islamic jurisprudence (including the ability to treat wives equitably). Statistics indicate 165 polygamous marriages in 2015 and 157 in 2016.^[Family Court of Maldives, Marriage Registration Statistics, 2016] While these numbers are relatively low in absolute terms, polygamy structurally disadvantages women in terms of economic security, custody rights, and marital stability. The Maldives has not introduced restrictions beyond existing court approval requirements.

### Divorce

Maldives has one of the highest divorce rates in the world, reflecting both the ease of divorce under Islamic law and cultural norms.^[National Bureau of Statistics, Vital Statistics, 2022] Islamic law gives husbands the unilateral right to divorce (talaq) without court involvement, while women must apply through court (khul' divorce, with potential forfeiture of financial rights, or faskh where fault is established). This procedural asymmetry means women's divorce processes are slower, more expensive, and more uncertain in outcome.

The Family Court has introduced improved case management procedures to reduce delays in women-initiated divorce proceedings. However, women in divorce proceedings continue to face pressure from families and communities to reconcile rather than pursue legal remedies, and the social stigma of divorce falls more heavily on women.^[HRCM, Gender and Family Law Report, 2023]

### Marital Rape

The most significant legal development in the reporting period regarding marriage and family law is the 2021 amendment to the Sexual Offences Act (17/2014), which explicitly criminalised marital rape.^[Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act, Republic of Maldives, 2021] Prior to this amendment, the marital exception in the original Act meant that non-consensual sexual intercourse within marriage was not actionable as rape. The 2021 amendment removed this exception, aligning Maldivian law with international standards.

Enforcement of the marital rape provisions remains limited. Prosecutions under the new provision are rare, reflecting victim underreporting (due to stigma, fear of family consequences, economic dependence on spouse, and limited awareness of the new law), police reluctance to treat intra-marital sexual violence as a criminal matter, and prosecutorial challenges in building cases without corroborating evidence.^[AIM Project Baseline Assessment, 2022]

### Custody and Guardianship

Under Maldivian family law, mothers are generally awarded care (hadhanah) of young children after divorce, with fathers retaining guardianship (wilayah) rights over children's religious, educational, and major life decisions. As children age, custody arrangements can be contested and courts may award custody to fathers. The financial implications of custody arrangements — including the adequacy of child maintenance payments from non-custodial fathers — significantly affect women's post-divorce economic security.^[Family Court of Maldives, Custody Statistics, 2022]

### Inheritance

Islamic inheritance rules apply in Maldives. Daughters inherit one-half of a son's share. This gender-differentiated inheritance right is covered by the reservation to Article 16 and reflects the intersection of Maldivian family law with Islamic jurisprudence. Women's lower inheritance entitlements contribute to lower asset ownership and reduced collateral for financial access.^[HRCM, Women's Property Rights Analysis, 2021]

### GBV Statistics and DV Case Trends

The trajectory of DV reporting since the enactment of the DVPA 2012 is instructive:

| Year | Cases Reported to FPA | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| 2013 | 19 | First year of FPA operation |
| 2014 | 149 | Growing awareness of DVPA |
| 2015 | 438 | Significant increase in reporting |
| 2016 | 642 | Peak reporting year |
| 2017 | 539 | Slight decline |
| 2021 | 696 | New peak; post-COVID reporting surge |
| 2024 | 537 | Slight decline; capacity constraints |

^[Family Protection Authority, Quarterly Statistics 2013–2024]

The overall increase from 19 to 537–696 cases represents primarily an increase in reporting rather than an increase in violence prevalence, reflecting growing community awareness and (relative) trust in the FPA mechanism. However, DV prevalence data from the DHS 2016–17 indicates that 1 in 3 women experience violence in their lifetime, and 1 in 4 experience intimate partner violence — figures that dwarf the number of formally reported cases.^[DHS 2016–17, Violence Against Women Chapter]

**GBV cases reported to MoGFSS/MoSFD:** 2021: 494 cases (87% female victims); January–September 2022: 378 cases (89% female victims).^[MoSFD, GBV Case Statistics, 2022] The high female share of victims confirms the gendered nature of reported violence.

**DV Prosecution Rate:** Only approximately 3% of DV/GBV cases reported to police are ultimately submitted to the Prosecutor General's office for prosecution.^[AIM Project Baseline Assessment, 2022] Of those that reach court, the conviction rate was 16.7% for the period 2016–2019.^[FPA, DV Justice Pathway Analysis, 2019] These figures indicate catastrophic attrition in the GBV justice pathway — the gap between prevalence, reporting, police action, prosecution, and conviction represents one of the most serious implementation failures in the Maldives' gender equality framework.

# PART III: PRIORITY AREAS FROM CEDAW CONCLUDING OBSERVATIONS (2021) AND PROGRESS

## 3.1 Summary of Progress Against CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6 Recommendations

The CEDAW Committee issued its Concluding Observations on the Sixth Periodic Report of Maldives (CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6) in November 2021.^[CEDAW Committee, CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, November 2021] The following summarises progress against the principal recommendations:

### Reservation to Article 16

**Recommendation:** The Committee recommended that the Maldives review and withdraw its reservation to Article 16 with a view to withdrawing it.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 13]

**Progress:** The reservation has not been withdrawn. The Maldives has initiated internal consultations within MoSFD and with the Ministry of Islamic Affairs on the scope and implications of the reservation, but no legislative or constitutional action has been taken. The Maldives acknowledges that this remains a critical concern and commits to continued review.

### Women's Political Participation

**Recommendation:** The Committee recommended the adoption of temporary special measures to increase women's participation in parliament and other decision-making bodies.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 31]

**Progress:** The 33% local council quota (2021) represents meaningful progress at the local level. At parliamentary level, women's representation has declined to a historic low of 4.6% (4/87) in the 2024 elections. No parliamentary quota has been enacted. This is an area requiring urgent legislative action.

### Gender-Based Violence

**Recommendation:** The Committee recommended measures to address the low rate of prosecution and conviction in GBV cases, improve the capacity of the FPA, ensure adequate shelter services, and criminalise FGM/C.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 23, 39]

**Progress:** The AIM Project (2022–2025) is the primary vehicle for GBV justice system reform. The 2021 marital rape amendment was a significant legislative achievement. FPA staffing remains critically insufficient (3 caseworkers in Male' for a city of 250,000+). Safe house capacity has not significantly expanded. FGM/C has not been criminalised. GBV prosecution rates remain at approximately 3%.

### Flogging of Women

**Recommendation:** The Committee expressed deep concern about the disproportionate application of flogging sentences to women and recommended the repeal of corporal punishment.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 51]

**Progress:** No legislative reform on flogging has occurred. Women continue to constitute 85% of flogging sentence recipients. This is identified as an area of urgent concern.

### FGM/C Criminalisation

**Recommendation:** Explicitly criminalise FGM/C.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 39]

**Progress:** Not yet enacted. Public health campaigns have been strengthened. Interministerial review is ongoing. The Maldives commits to completing this review within the next reporting period.

### Economic Empowerment

**Recommendation:** Address the labour force participation gap, increase women's access to credit and financial services, address the gender pay gap, and improve maternity protection.^[CEDAW/C/MDV/CO/6, para. 43]

**Progress:** The LFPR gap (44% female vs 80% male) has not substantively narrowed. SDFC loan access remains at 20% to women. No official gender pay gap measurement has been produced. Paternity leave has not been legislated. Childcare infrastructure expansion has been limited.

## 3.2 Areas Requiring Urgent Attention

The Maldives identifies the following as requiring urgent attention in the coming reporting period:

1. **Parliamentary representation:** Legislative enactment of a parliamentary quota or equivalent TSM to reverse the decline to 4.6% women's representation, the lowest in Maldivian history.

2. **GBV prosecution pathway:** Systemic reform of police, prosecutorial, and judicial handling of GBV cases to dramatically increase the 3% prosecution submission rate and 16.7% conviction rate. This requires sustained investment in the AIM Project's reforms and their institutionalisation beyond the project period.

3. **FPA capacity:** Significant expansion of the FPA's staffing and geographic reach, including deployment of DV caseworkers to atoll hospitals and regional centres, not only Male'.

4. **FGM/C legislation:** Criminalisation of FGM/C in line with CEDAW Committee recommendations, accompanied by sensitisation of communities and Islamic religious leaders on the health and human rights dimensions.

5. **Flogging reform:** Urgent legislative review of the discriminatory application of flogging sentences, with a view to abolishing judicial corporal punishment.

6. **Women's economic inclusion:** Targeted interventions to increase women's LFPR, including affordable childcare expansion, SDFC gender-responsive lending products, and measures to open the resort sector to women workers.

7. **Atoll women's services:** Dedicated investment in WDC capacity, atoll-level DV response training, and geographic expansion of legal aid and reproductive health services.

8. **Gender pay gap:** Production of official gender pay gap data and legislative steps to address identified occupational segregation and pay differentials.

9. **Marital rape enforcement:** Training of police and prosecutors on the 2021 marital rape provisions and development of evidence-based prosecution strategies.

10. **Mental health and psychosocial support:** Expansion of trauma-informed counselling for GBV survivors, decentralised from Male' to atoll facilities.

## 3.4 Commitments for Next Reporting Period

The Maldives commits to the following specific actions for the period leading to the Eighth Periodic Report:

1. Complete legislative review of FGM/C criminalisation and, if criminalisation is enacted, implement community sensitisation programmes.
2. Table and seek passage of a parliamentary election quota or equivalent TSM for women's representation in the People's Majlis.
3. Expand FPA staffing by a minimum of ten DV caseworkers, deployed to regional atoll centres.
4. Produce official gender pay gap data from Census 2022 wage data and publish by 2026.
5. Conduct a review of the legal provisions on flogging, with reference to CEDAW obligations and the disproportionate impact on women.
6. Legislate paternity leave entitlements to begin redistributing childcare responsibilities.
7. Develop a national affordable childcare policy to address the structural barrier to women's LFPR.
8. Fully implement the AIM Project's GBV justice reform protocols within the Maldives Police Service and Prosecutor General's Office, and institutionalise them beyond the project period.
9. Produce comprehensive gender-disaggregated monitoring data across all NAPGE indicators and submit to the Majlis annually.
10. Commission an updated national DHS or equivalent survey to produce current GBV prevalence data (the 2016–17 DHS remains the most recent comprehensive source).

---

*The Republic of Maldives submits this Seventh Periodic Report in a spirit of transparency and commitment to the progressive realisation of women's rights under the Convention. The Maldives acknowledges the significant gaps that remain between its legal framework and the lived realities of women, particularly those in outer atolls, those affected by GBV, those with disabilities, and those excluded from political and economic participation. The Government remains committed to working with the CEDAW Committee, civil society, and international partners to close these gaps.*
