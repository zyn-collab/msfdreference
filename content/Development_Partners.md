## Section 1: Overview of Development Partner Engagement

### 1.1 The Architecture of International Engagement

The Maldives' social policy architecture has been substantially shaped by international development partners since the country's earliest interactions with the United Nations system. As a small island developing state (SIDS) with a population of approximately 521,000, limited domestic technical capacity, and a social service workforce stretched thin across nearly 200 inhabited islands, the Maldives has relied extensively on multilateral and bilateral partners for policy design, technical assistance, capacity building, and direct financing of social programmes. This dependence is not incidental; it is structural. The child protection system was modelled on English frameworks imported through UNICEF technical assistance.[^1] The social protection architecture was assessed and benchmarked using the Asian Development Bank's Social Protection Index methodology.[^2] The national prevalence study on domestic violence was conducted using WHO multi-country methodology with UNFPA support.[^3] The pension reform of 2009 drew on World Bank advisory work. The disability situational analysis was conducted under the UN Partnership on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNPRPD).[^4] In virtually every domain of social policy examined in this encyclopedia, international partners have been present -- not merely as funders but as architects of the frameworks within which Maldivian policy operates.

This appendix provides a comprehensive mapping of development partner engagement in the Maldivian social sector, covering the period from approximately 2010 to the present. It documents projects by partner, assesses thematic coverage and gaps, and offers a critical analysis of what this engagement has achieved and where it has fallen short.

### 1.2 The UN Sustainable Development Cooperation Framework (UNSDCF) 2022--2026

The overarching coordination mechanism for UN system engagement in the Maldives is the United Nations Sustainable Development Cooperation Framework (UNSDCF) 2022--2026, which succeeded the earlier UN Development Assistance Framework (UNDAF). The UNSDCF was signed by the Government of Maldives and the UN Country Team (UNCT) and serves as the blueprint for the UN Development System's support to the Maldives in achieving the 2030 Agenda.[^5]

The Framework is organised around four main outcome areas:

1. **Livelihoods and Productivity** -- inclusive economic growth, employment, and skills development.
2. **Human Development and Wellbeing** -- equitable access to quality social and protection services, health, education, and nutrition.
3. **Natural Resource Management, Climate Resilience and Environmental Sustainability** -- climate adaptation, disaster risk reduction, and environmental governance.
4. **Transformative Governance, Justice and Human Rights** -- rule of law, anti-corruption, gender equality, and civic participation.[^6]

The social sector outcome states that "by 2026, people in the Maldives, especially the most vulnerable and marginalized, benefit from increased access to and use of quality, equitable, inclusive and resilient social and protection services, and have enhanced relevant skills and live fulfilled lives with wellbeing and dignity."[^7] This outcome is served by the collective programming of UNICEF, UNFPA, UNDP, WHO, ILO, UN Women, and other resident and non-resident agencies.

### 1.3 The Common Country Analysis (CCA) 2020

The Maldives Common Country Analysis, published in March 2020 by the UN Resident Coordinator's Office (UNRCO), provides the analytical foundation for the UNSDCF. The CCA examined the political, human rights, economic, social, and environmental context, assessed progress toward the 2030 Agenda and SDGs, and identified vulnerable groups being left behind -- including women, children, youth, migrants, persons with disabilities, and atoll populations.[^8] The CCA's identification of financing gaps, institutional weaknesses, and geographic disparities informed the subsequent programming decisions of all UN agencies operating in the Maldives.

## Section 2: Partner-by-Partner Project Inventory

### 2.1 World Bank Group

#### Engagement History

The World Bank has been a major development partner in shaping the Maldives' social protection architecture. The relationship intensified following the 2004 tsunami, the 2008 democratic transition, and -- most dramatically -- the COVID-19 pandemic, which contracted Maldives' real GDP by 33.6 percent in 2020.[^9] As of early 2025, the World Bank programme in the Maldives includes ten projects financed by the International Development Association (IDA), one regional project, and one IDA guarantee operation, for a total net commitment of approximately US$217 million across all sectors.[^10]

The World Bank's social sector engagement in the Maldives operates through three primary channels: (i) investment lending for specific projects, (ii) analytical and advisory work (including the Human Capital Review, Systematic Country Diagnostic, and poverty assessments), and (iii) policy dialogue and technical assistance embedded within project implementation.

#### Narrative Assessment

The World Bank's COVID-19 response represents its most impactful social sector intervention. The Income Support Allowance programme, supported by the US$34.4 million COVID-19 Emergency Income Support Project, provided up to MVR 5,000 per month (approximately US$322) to approximately 23,000 workers who lost jobs or income during 2020, with payments delivered for up to nine months.[^12] A citizen engagement mechanism embedded in the project allowed beneficiary feedback, which the World Bank documented as having improved targeting and reduced exclusion errors.[^13]

The MEERY project has achieved notable results in youth employability: as of November 2024, disbursement stood at US$14.1 million (72 percent of the total budget), 111 new businesses had been registered by youth receiving project support, and 70 of those businesses were women-owned -- exceeding the project target of 40 women-owned businesses.[^14] The project's focus on tourism, construction, ICT, and agriculture reflects the sectors where youth employment opportunities are concentrated, though it does not directly address the social work and caregiving workforce shortages that constrain social service delivery.

The SAILS project, approved in May 2022 with US$24 million in IDA financing, represents the most structurally significant World Bank investment in Maldivian social protection. It aims to establish a comprehensive social insurance and active labour market programme, moving beyond emergency cash transfers to build permanent institutional infrastructure for income security and employment services.[^15] The project supports NSPA's social registry development and the integration of benefit management systems -- addressing the fragmentation identified throughout this encyclopedia.

The Atoll Education Development Project received additional financing of US$2.7 million from the Global Partnership for Education in October 2024, bringing total financing to US$12.775 million. The project focuses on mathematics, science, and English language teaching at the secondary level, with particular attention to outer atoll education outcomes.[^16]

The World Bank's analytical work has been equally influential. The 2021 Systematic Country Diagnostic Update assessed development challenges centred on job creation, private sector growth, and sustainability.[^17] The 2022 Poverty Assessment documented the stark Male-atoll divide in poverty (0.9 percent vs. 9.5 percent) and the extreme vulnerability of atoll populations to economic shocks.[^18] The Human Capital Review launched in early 2024 identified poor-quality education and low female employment rates as the main barriers to human capital acceleration.[^19] The National Social Protection Framework 2022--2026 was developed with World Bank and UNICEF technical assistance.[^20]

#### Detailed Project Profiles

The World Bank maintains an active portfolio of 10 projects in the Maldives (8 IDA projects, 1 IDA guarantee, and 1 regional project) with a total net commitment of approximately US$193.83 million. The engagement is governed by the Country Partnership Framework (CPF) 2023--2027, which focuses on three pillars: improving resilience to shocks, increasing access to economic opportunities, and improving human capital outcomes.[^98]

##### COVID-19 Emergency Income Support Project (P174014)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA
**Project ID:** P174014
**Period:** June 2020 -- December 2022 (originally closing June 2022; extended via restructuring)
**Budget:** US$34.4 million total (original US$12.8 million: IDA Credit US$6.4 million + IDA Grant US$6.4 million; Additional Financing of US$21.6 million approved January 2021)
**Status:** Completed
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Finance; National Social Protection Agency (NSPA)

**Objectives and Components:**

The project development objective was to mitigate the impact of the COVID-19 crisis on workers and their families and to strengthen the capacity of social protection programmes for future emergency response. The project had two broad components:

- **Component 1: Income Support Allowance (ISA).** This component financed the Government's COVID-19 Income Support Allowance programme, providing temporary cash transfers of up to MVR 5,000 (approximately US$322) per month for up to nine months to workers who had lost employment or experienced income reduction due to the pandemic. The ISA was administered through the NSPA and Jobcentre registration systems. By late 2020, approximately 17,000 workers had received disbursements totalling US$10.3 million, and by programme completion, approximately 23,000 workers had been supported.[^99]

- **Component 2: Systems Strengthening and Employability.** Following the initial emergency phase, a project restructuring repurposed remaining ISA funds to finance targeted employability programmes for approximately 2,800 Maldivians displaced by the pandemic. Three programme streams were offered: (i) a short vocational training programme; (ii) an apprenticeship programme; and (iii) a self-employment and microenterprise grant programme. This component also supported enhancements to the social protection delivery infrastructure, including beneficiary registration and grievance redress mechanisms.[^100]

**Sub-projects / Consultancies:**

The project included citizen engagement activities to improve targeting and grievance handling. A feedback mechanism was established through which workers could report issues with the ISA application and disbursement process. The World Bank subsequently published a case study documenting how citizen engagement improved the project's effectiveness in reaching vulnerable workers.[^101]

**Key Deliverables:**

1. Cash transfers delivered to approximately 23,000 COVID-affected workers
2. Employability programmes for 2,800 displaced workers (vocational training, apprenticeships, microenterprise grants)
3. Enhanced social protection registration and grievance systems
4. Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) developed

**Results / Impact:**

The project represented the first large-scale emergency cash transfer operation in the Maldives using existing social protection infrastructure. It demonstrated that the NSPA and Jobcentre systems could be repurposed for rapid crisis response, though implementation revealed significant gaps in the national social registry -- many informal and self-employed workers were not captured in existing databases. The project closing date extension from June to December 2022 reflected the need for additional time to deliver employability components. No publicly available Implementation Completion Report (ICR) was identified at time of writing.

##### Enhancing Employability and Resilience of Youth (MEERY) Project (P163818)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA
**Project ID:** P163818
**Period:** June 2019 -- December 2024 (five-year implementation)
**Budget:** US$20 million (IDA-64430 US$10 million + IDA-D4800 US$10 million)
**Status:** Active (implementation support mission conducted May--June 2025)
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Higher Education, Labour, and Skills Development; Ministry of Economic Development and Trade

**Objectives and Components:**

The Project Development Objective (PDO) is to improve the relevance of technical and vocational skills and foster entrepreneurship to promote youth employment in project priority sectors. Priority sectors are Tourism, Tourism-related Construction, Agriculture, and ICT.

- **Component 1: Fostering Skills Development in Priority Sectors.** This component finances skills training programmes aligned with labour market demand. More than 50 programmes in youth skills development have been supported, spanning ICT, tourism, and tourism-related construction. A Waste-to-Wealth Management course focusing on climate resilience was introduced and subsequently expanded into a broader Resource Management and Logistics Programme.[^102]

- **Component 2: Promoting Entrepreneurship and Employment.** This component supports entrepreneurship training, business incubation, and eLearning strategy development. A notable activity was the MEERY Pitch-Up Challenge, through which MVR 12.4 million was granted to 23 businesses.[^103] The component also supports the development of an eLearning infrastructure for remote island access to training.

- **Component 3: Project Coordination, Monitoring and Evaluation.** Standard project management component covering M&E systems, fiduciary management, and safeguards compliance.

**Sub-projects / Consultancies:**

The project has generated multiple procurement packages including consultancies for curriculum development, training provider accreditation, labour market information system development, and sector-specific skills needs assessments. The project website (meeryproject.mohe.gov.mv) lists ongoing TORs and procurement notices.

**Key Deliverables:**

1. 50+ youth skills development programmes delivered
2. Entrepreneurship grants to 23 businesses (MVR 12.4 million)
3. eLearning strategy and infrastructure for remote island training access
4. Climate-resilient Resource Management and Logistics Programme curriculum
5. Gender equality mainstreaming -- promoting women's economic participation as a core pillar

**Results / Impact:**

The project has been rated Satisfactory in recent Implementation Status Reports (ISRs). A 2023 Auditor General's report on the project's financial statements was published, indicating appropriate financial management. The project has been noted for its contribution to reducing the NEET (Not in Education, Employment, or Training) rate among Maldivian youth, though comprehensive outcome data on employment placement rates is pending final evaluation. An implementation support mission was conducted in May--June 2025, suggesting the project may have been extended beyond the original December 2024 closing date.[^104]

##### Sustainable and Integrated Labor Services (SAILS) Project (P177240)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA
**Project ID:** P177240
**Period:** Approved May 2022; Effective September 2022; Closing date TBD
**Budget:** US$24 million (IDA grant, 100% coverage)
**Status:** Active -- significant implementation delays
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Economic Development (subsequently transferred to Ministry of Higher Education, Labour, and Skills Development); Maldives Pension Administration Office (MPAO)

**Objectives and Components:**

The project aims to improve social protection coverage and employability of Maldivian workers by providing insurance against shocks and employment support services.

- **Component 1: Integrated Labour Market Services (US$8.70 million).** Expansion of the existing Job Centre with five regional hubs providing counselling, job search assistance, targeted training, and coaching. Includes targeted programmes for women jobseekers and promotion of employment in tourism and renewable energy sectors.[^105]

- **Component 2: Strengthening Social Insurance (US$14.30 million).** Design and implementation of an Unemployment Insurance (UI) scheme with joint employer-employee contributions, managed by the Maldives Pension Administration Office. Includes a savings mechanism allowing informal and self-employed workers to save and access funds during economic and climate-related shocks.

- **Component 3: Project Management (US$1.00 million).** Standard project coordination, fiduciary management, M&E.

- **Component 4: Contingent Emergency Response Component (CERC) (US$0.00 million allocated).** Standby component activatable during emergencies.

**Sub-projects / Consultancies:**

Key consultancies include the design of the Unemployment Insurance scheme, development of the Job Centre expansion model, IT systems for unemployment insurance management, and gender-responsive employment services design. Procurement plans have been updated multiple times.

**Key Deliverables (Planned):**

1. Operational Unemployment Insurance scheme
2. Five regional Job Centre hubs
3. Gender-responsive employment services
4. Savings mechanism for informal workers

**Results / Impact:**

As of the most recent ISR (early 2024), the project faces **significant implementation challenges**. The Unemployment Insurance Bill -- the centrepiece of the project -- has not been approved by Parliament, and all related activities under Component 2 are on hold. Recruitment of key personnel (CEO of Jobs Center and key PMU staff) was also delayed pending elections in April/May 2024. The mandate for labour shifted from the Ministry of Economic Development to the Ministry of Higher Education, Labour, and Skills Development (MOHELSD), creating additional institutional transition challenges. No restructuring has been formally processed, but the project is at risk of requiring one if the UI Bill continues to face legislative delays.[^106]

This project illustrates a recurrent challenge in World Bank operations in the Maldives: project designs that depend on legislative action face implementation risk when parliamentary timelines do not align with project implementation schedules.

##### Atoll Education Development Project (AEDP) (P177768) and Additional Financing (P181581)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA + Global Partnership for Education (GPE)
**Project ID:** P177768 (original); P181581 (Additional Financing)
**Period:** Approved September 2022; Effective December 2022; Closing June 2028
**Budget:** US$9.0 million original (IDA Grant US$4.5 million + IDA Credit US$4.5 million) + US$3.0 million Additional Financing (GPE Grant, approved October 2024) = US$12.0 million total
**Status:** Active -- rated Satisfactory
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Education

**Objectives and Components:**

The PDO is to enhance access to, and quality of, secondary education, with special focus on mathematics, science, and English language, and on improving education outcomes in the outer atolls.

- **Component 1: Enhancing Curriculum Delivery and Increasing Participation in Higher Secondary Education.** Supports curriculum reform and expanded access to higher secondary education in atoll schools.

- **Component 2: Continuing Teacher Development.** Professional development programmes for teachers, with emphasis on improving instruction in STEM subjects and English.

- **Component 3: Measuring and Enhancing School and System Performance.** Development of assessment frameworks and performance monitoring tools.

- **Component 4: Coordination, Monitoring, Capacity Building and Technical Assistance.** Project management and institutional capacity building.

- **Component 5: Contingency Emergency Response Component (CERC).** Standby emergency component.

**Additional Financing (P181581) Activities:**

The GPE-funded Additional Financing of US$3.0 million supports two specific activities:

1. **Human resources and capacity development** for education staff at national and atoll levels
2. **Education Management Information System (EMIS)** development with modern technology to cover all education agencies, including schools in outer atolls and islands

**Key Deliverables:**

1. Revised secondary education curricula for mathematics, science, and English
2. Teacher training programmes delivered across atoll schools
3. National learning assessment framework
4. EMIS covering all schools including outer atoll and island schools

**Results / Impact:**

As of the most recent ISR (2025), the project is performing well:
- Two out of four PDO indicators have achieved their targets; the remaining two are on track
- Nine out of 14 intermediate outcome indicators have achieved or exceeded targets; two have not been achieved; two lack adequate data; one is on track
- Overall performance rating: **Satisfactory**; risk level: **Moderate**
- Disbursement: US$2.42 million out of US$9.0 million (27% disbursement rate as of the ISR period)

The project represents the World Bank's primary engagement in the Maldivian education sector and addresses a critical structural challenge: the disparity in educational quality and access between Male and the outer atolls.

##### Digital Maldives for Adaptation, Decentralization and Diversification (P177040)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA
**Project ID:** P177040
**Period:** Approved June 2022
**Budget:** US$10 million (IDA Grant)
**Status:** Active
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Environment, Climate Change and Technology; Communication Authority of Maldives; National Centre for Information Technology; Department of National Registration

**Objectives and Components:**

While not a social sector project in the traditional sense, the Digital Maldives project has profound implications for social service delivery across the dispersed island geography.

- **Component 1: Broadband Market Competition.** Promotes competition in the broadband market through regulatory reform, with the goal of reducing connectivity costs and improving internet access across all inhabited islands. Digital connectivity is a prerequisite for remote social service delivery, telemedicine, and online education -- particularly critical for outer atoll communities.

- **Component 2: Digital Identification and Security.** Modernises the national identification system to facilitate online services and transactions. This has direct relevance to social protection delivery: the COVID-19 Income Support Project (P174014) revealed that many eligible workers were not captured in existing databases. A modern digital ID system would enable more effective targeting of social transfers, benefits verification, and reduction of inclusion/exclusion errors in social protection programmes.

- **Component 3: Climate Data and Analytics.** Improves climate-related data and analytics by tracking emissions, deploying modern technologies (including drones), and using existing data more efficiently for policymaking.

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

This project is integral to the digital transformation of social service delivery. The Maldives' geography -- approximately 200 inhabited islands spread across 900 km of ocean -- makes in-person service delivery extraordinarily costly. Digital infrastructure is the only feasible pathway to universal social service access. The digital ID component is particularly important for the social protection system, where inadequate beneficiary identification has been a persistent bottleneck. The project was approved alongside the SAILS project in a combined US$34 million package, signalling the World Bank's recognition that labour market and digital development are complementary investments.[^112]

##### Public Financial Management Systems Strengthening Project (P164322)

**Donor:** World Bank / IDA
**Project ID:** P164322
**Period:** Approved June 2018; multiple phases
**Budget:** US$12 million (additional financing provided subsequently)
**Status:** Active
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Finance

**Objectives and Components:**

The PDO is to enhance budget credibility, transparency, and financial reporting of central government finances. While not a social sector project per se, the PFM Strengthening Project has direct implications for social service delivery because it governs the fiscal infrastructure through which all social spending flows.

The project uses Disbursement-Linked Indicators (DLIs) rather than traditional components:

- **DLI #1:** Strengthen budget preparation and public investment management
- **DLI #2:** Strengthen control of the wage bill and staffing of core financial functions (US$1.5 million)
- **DLI #3:** Improve oversight and monitoring of State-Owned Enterprises (US$1.5 million)
- **DLI #4:** Increase transparency and external accountability (US$1.5 million)

Additional financing targets stronger preparation and prioritisation of high-impact and affordable public investments, optimised cost and risk management of public debt, and improved public procurement.

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

This project is relevant to social policy because: (i) wage bill control directly affects social service staffing levels across the atolls; (ii) SOE monitoring covers entities like the Housing Development Corporation and Aasandha Company that deliver social services; (iii) procurement reforms affect the efficiency of social programme implementation; and (iv) budget credibility is fundamental to whether budgeted social expenditure is actually released and spent.

### 2.2 Asian Development Bank (ADB)

#### Engagement History

As of 31 December 2024, ADB has committed 121 public sector loans, grants, and technical assistance totalling US$554 million to the Maldives.[^21] ADB's current sovereign portfolio includes five loans and ten grants worth US$253.26 million. While ADB's primary focus areas in the Maldives are energy, transport, public sector management, and climate resilience, several projects have significant social sector dimensions.

#### Narrative Assessment

ADB's most directly relevant social sector contribution was the 2012 Social Protection Index (SPI) study, which identified 20 distinct social protection programmes in the Maldives and calculated total social protection expenditure at MVR 782 million (3.5 percent of GDP) for the 2009 reference year. The study found that social assistance constituted 52 percent of total spending, social insurance 47 percent, and labour market programmes barely 1 percent.[^23] This analysis provided the empirical foundation for subsequent social protection reform efforts.

The 2025 GESI Diagnostic examined gender equality and social inclusion challenges across tourism, energy, and urban development, identifying overarching issues including low female labour force participation, geographic disparities between Male and atolls, and the persistence of gender-based violence.[^24] The 2024 Strengthening Gender Inclusive Initiatives Project, with a Project Administration Manual dated April 2024, focuses on addressing domestic violence, GBV, and advancing gender-responsive budgeting -- an area where ADB is one of the first partners to provide dedicated project-level support.[^25]

ADB's 2015 Country Diagnostic Study, *Overcoming the Challenges of a Small Island State*, provided a structural analysis of the Maldives' development constraints that has informed both ADB and World Bank programming.[^26]

#### Detailed Project Profiles

The ADB's engagement in the Maldives is governed by the Country Partnership Strategy (CPS) 2020--2024, which supports "a resilient and sustainable island life for all Maldivians" through three strategic objectives: (i) improving quality of life while ensuring environmental sustainability; (ii) strengthening competitiveness and diversifying the economic base; and (iii) enhancing public sector efficiency and fiscal sustainability. A new CPS for 2025--2029 is under preparation. The current sovereign portfolio comprises 5 loans and 10 grants worth US$253.26 million.[^107]

##### Enhancing Climate Resilience and Food Security Project (57002-001)

**Donor:** Asian Development Bank
**Project Number:** 57002-001
**Period:** Approved 2024; implementation ongoing
**Budget:** US$25.95 million total (US$4 million concessional loan + US$17.95 million ADF grant + US$3 million Japan Fund for Prosperous and Resilient Asia and the Pacific grant + US$1 million government co-financing)
**Status:** Active (most recently approved ADB project in Maldives)
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Tourism and Environment

**Objectives and Components:**

This is ADB's most recently approved sovereign project in the Maldives, addressing the intersection of climate vulnerability and food security -- two issues with direct social policy implications.

- **Climate and Disaster Resilience.** Upgrades early warning systems in Addu City and Male, enabling the Maldives Meteorological Service to establish real-time weather monitoring and early warning capabilities. Includes community training and awareness-raising on climate change adaptation and disaster preparedness and response. Early warning systems are critical social infrastructure: the 2004 tsunami demonstrated the catastrophic human cost of inadequate warning systems, and climate change is increasing the frequency of weather-related emergencies that affect vulnerable island communities.

- **Coastal Protection and Flood Mitigation (Kulhudhuffushi Island).** Constructs stormwater drainage and filtration systems, restores mangrove areas, and introduces nature-based solutions for coastal protection. Offshore and nearshore infrastructure (artificial reefs, berms, groynes) will be constructed for coastal protection and sand retention. Kulhudhuffushi is a regional hub in Haa Dhaalu Atoll and serves as a service delivery centre for surrounding islands; protecting it from flooding safeguards the social service infrastructure that serves the broader northern region.

- **Food Security and Urban Farming (Haa Dhaalu and Addu Atolls).** Introduces climate-smart urban farming technologies and practices. Constructs a multipurpose facility for the Hanimaadhoo Agriculture Center (HAC) to strengthen training and research services. Provides enhanced agricultural extension, training, and business advisory services. Beneficiaries receive matching grants to develop and expand agricultural businesses. This component directly addresses livelihood diversification in the outer atolls, where dependence on limited economic activities contributes to poverty and vulnerability.

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

The project demonstrates the growing recognition that climate resilience and social protection are inseparable in the Maldivian context. Food insecurity (the Maldives imports approximately 90% of its food), climate-induced displacement, and vulnerability to weather events are social policy challenges as much as environmental ones. The matching grant component for agricultural businesses represents an investment in livelihood resilience that complements the social protection transfers documented elsewhere in this encyclopedia.

##### The AIM Project – Accountability and Integration Matters

The AIM project – **Accountability and Integration Matters** – is a collaboration between the **European Union, UNDP Maldives, and the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD)**. Its objective is to create an **inclusive justice system for gender-based violence and domestic abuse survivors**. The project operates at the intersection of justice sector reform and GBV response, addressing the systemic barriers that prevent survivors from accessing effective legal remedies – barriers extensively documented throughout this encyclopedia, including the incomplete criminalisation of DV as a standalone offence, the rebuttable presumption of consent within marriage (Penal Code Section 130(b)), and the chronic shortage of shelter and legal aid services outside Male'.

The AIM project is notable for being a **joint EU-UNDP initiative** rather than sitting within any single partner's portfolio, which may explain its previous omission from standard project database searches. It represents one of the few development partner interventions directly targeting the justice system's capacity to serve GBV survivors – a critical gap identified in the UNFPA 2023 legal review and the CFW vulnerability mapping research.

#### Narrative Assessment

UNICEF's contribution to the Maldivian child protection system has been foundational. The 2006 Child Protection Procedure Manual, developed with UNICEF support and modelled on England's "Working Together to Safeguard Children" guidance, established the conceptual framework that continues to shape Maldivian child protection practice.[^30] While this transposition of a Western model into a small island developing state context has created significant operational tensions (documented extensively in Part IV of this encyclopedia), UNICEF's sustained technical presence has been essential in building whatever system exists.

The Maldives Child Protection Database (MCPD), launched in 2016 with UNICEF support, connected all 19 FCSCs to a centralised digital case management system for the first time, enabling national-level data on child protection referrals, case types, and outcomes.[^31] The IBAMA project extended the reach of formal child protection services into island communities through structured volunteer engagement, though the quality and activity levels of these groups vary enormously across islands.[^32]

UNICEF's Situation Analysis reports (2019, updated 2021 and 2025) have served as the primary evidence base for child-focused policy-making, providing disaggregated data on child wellbeing indicators across atolls and demographic groups. The 2025 "Building Futures" report represents the most recent comprehensive assessment of child rights and the adolescent challenge in the Maldives.[^33]

A critical gap in UNICEF's engagement is the persistent disconnect between the frameworks it promotes and the operational capacity of the Maldivian system to implement them. With 61 social workers serving 128,208 children across 189 inhabited islands, and average caseloads exceeding 160 cases per worker, the sophisticated case management models promoted by UNICEF require an implementation infrastructure that does not yet exist.[^34]

##### UNICEF Social Protection and Child Poverty Analytics

Beyond the eight flagship programmes, UNICEF Maldives plays a critical analytical role in generating evidence on child poverty and social protection system performance. This work, while not constituting a "project" with a discrete budget, has been among the most influential development partner contributions to Maldivian social policy.

**Social Sector Assessment (2022).** UNICEF produced a comprehensive Social Sector Assessment for the Maldives documenting the state of social services, coverage gaps, and system bottlenecks. This assessment is one of the most detailed publicly available analyses of social sector performance in the Maldives.[^113]

**Child Multidimensional Poverty Analysis.** UNICEF analysis found that **33% of children in the Maldives experience multidimensional poverty** -- higher than the 28% multidimensional poverty rate for Maldivians overall. The geographic dimension is stark: multidimensional poverty affects **40% of children in the atolls** compared to just **10% in Male**. In households with children, the nationwide rate is 34%. These figures have been instrumental in shifting policy discourse from income-based poverty measurement (which showed the Maldives as an upper-middle-income country with limited poverty) to a multidimensional understanding that reveals extensive deprivation in access to education, health, housing, water, and sanitation.

**Situation Analysis of Children and Youth.** UNICEF produced a comprehensive situation analysis presenting an analytical narrative of children's rights and well-being, examining progress, challenges, and opportunities for achieving child rights, patterns of deprivation, causes of these deprivations, and barriers preventing the fulfilment of child rights.

**Social Protection System Analysis.** UNICEF analysis has documented that social protection programmes benefiting children and adolescents are **fragmented**, with bottlenecks including:

- Poor data and inadequate systems to support effective social protection for the most vulnerable
- Poor targeting that has resulted in low coverage rates for child-focused benefits
- Disparities in access to critical services, including education and health, between Male and the remote islands
- Insufficient integration of disability-responsive social protection

These analytical products have been used extensively throughout this encyclopedia as primary evidence sources.

#### Narrative Assessment

UNFPA's most consequential contribution to Maldivian social policy has been the generation of evidence on gender-based violence. The 2007 Women's Health and Life Experiences Study, conducted by Emma Fulu with UNFPA support using the WHO multi-country methodology, remains the only methodologically rigorous national prevalence study on intimate partner violence in the Maldives.[^37] Despite its age, this study continues to serve as the primary reference point for GBV policy and programming. The commissioning of a new national DV baseline study by FPA in 2019 -- which has not been completed -- represents a significant gap that UNFPA has been unable to fill.

The COVID-19 GBV analysis, commissioned by UNFPA and conducted by the Institute of Research and Development, provided the most detailed examination of how pandemic restrictions compounded existing inequalities and increased women's vulnerabilities, analysing reported case data from FPA, the Maldives Police Service, and the Prosecutor General's Office.[^38]

The 2024 second edition of the National Guidelines for Healthcare Providers on GBV, developed with UNFPA support, represents an important step toward operationalising the health sector's role in GBV response -- though implementation across the dispersed island geography remains the fundamental challenge.[^39]

UNFPA's engagement with population data -- including the disability analysis from Census 2022 and demographic projections for the ageing population -- has been critical for evidence-based policy-making across multiple social sectors.[^40]

- **Output 2: Strengthened institutional capacity to support opportunities for adolescents and youth.** Youth-focused programming including adolescent health, comprehensive sexuality education, and youth participation.

   - Lack of coherence and consistency in interpretation and application of laws across sectors

#### Narrative Assessment

UNDP's most significant social-sector contribution has been the US$23.6 million GCF Water Security Project, which -- while primarily a climate adaptation intervention -- directly addressed social vulnerability by providing safe freshwater to approximately 105,000 people (30 percent of the population) across 49 islands in 13 atolls.[^43] The project established desalination facilities on four islands in the northern atolls as water production and distribution hubs, while 45 additional islands received improved rainwater collection infrastructure and groundwater protection. The social dimensions of this project are substantial: communities that previously faced periodic water shortages during dry seasons gained reliable access to safe drinking water, directly improving health outcomes and reducing the burden on women and girls who are disproportionately responsible for water collection.

UNDP's governance work has included support for the National Action Plan on Preventing and Countering Violent Extremism (2020--2024), which addressed the recruitment of Maldivian youth as foreign terrorist fighters and included a child rehabilitation component -- though only three children were successfully reintegrated through the National Reintegration Centre during the plan period.[^44]

The Rights Side of Life baseline human rights survey series (2005, 2011, 2019), conducted in partnership with HRCM and FPA, has provided the most systematic longitudinal data on public attitudes toward human rights in the Maldives.[^45]

##### Integrated National Financing Framework (INFF)

**Donor:** UNDP / Joint SDG Fund
**Period:** Development began June 2020; INFF endorsed March 2023; implementation ongoing
**Budget:** Joint SDG Fund contribution (amount not publicly specified)
**Status:** Active -- endorsed and in implementation phase
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Finance (lead); 13 government agencies on Steering Committee

**Objectives and Components:**

The INFF is a comprehensive financing framework for the Maldives' national development priorities and SDG achievement. The Maldives became the **first Small Island Developing State (SIDS) to formally develop and endorse an INFF**, out of 18 SIDS countries developing such frameworks.

The programme centres on two development priorities:

1. **Climate action** -- financing for climate adaptation and mitigation
2. **Reforms for quality, equitable, gender-responsive, and sustainable social service delivery** -- financing strategies to accelerate Agenda 2030 achievement

The INFF was developed through a UN Joint Programme led by the UN Resident Coordinator, with UNDP Maldives providing technical advisory to the Ministry of Finance. A high-level Steering Committee of 13 government agencies was co-chaired by the Minister of Finance and the UN Resident Coordinator.

2. **Maldives Climate Finance Hub (MCFH)** -- established within the Ministry of Finance as the primary mechanism to implement the financing strategy, with UNDP technical assistance
3. Action-oriented strategies to unlock domestic and external resources from private sector, international financing institutions, philanthropic, and impact/cause-driven sources

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

The INFF is critical to social policy because it determines how social sector financing gaps will be addressed. The framework provides strategies for augmenting public financing of social services with innovative financing instruments. The Maldives' fiscal constraints -- documented throughout this encyclopedia -- mean that social sector expansion depends on identifying new financing sources, exactly the purpose of the INFF.

##### GCF Water Security Project: Supporting Vulnerable Communities to Manage Climate Change-Induced Water Shortages

**Donor:** Green Climate Fund (GCF) via UNDP
**Period:** 2017--2023
**Budget:** US$28.2 million
**Status:** Completed (Final Evaluation available)
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Environment and Energy

**Objectives and Components:**

The project aimed to deliver safe and secure freshwater to 105,000 people across 49 islands in 13 atolls experiencing water shortages due to low rainfall and extended dry periods brought on by climate change.

**Key Interventions:**

1. **Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM) systems** installed on four main islands: Nolhivaranfaru, Foakaidhoo, Maduvvari, and Dharavandhoo
2. **Water desalination facilities** established on four islands across the most vulnerable northern regions, serving as production and distribution hubs for all seven northern atolls during the dry season
3. **Climate-resilient water infrastructure** designed to withstand projected climate impacts

**Results / Impact:**

The project delivered critical infrastructure for water security, with demonstrated social co-benefits:

- Reduced risk of waterborne disease
- Improved social security and reduced climate vulnerability
- Reduced outward migration from outer islands (a key driver of social dislocation documented in Part III of this encyclopedia)
- Flow-on benefits for local development, tourism, and livelihoods

A final evaluation was conducted, though detailed results are in the full evaluation report.

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

Water insecurity is a social determinant of health and a driver of inter-island migration. Communities without reliable water supply experience higher health costs, reduced productivity, and greater pressure to relocate to Male or regional centres. This project directly addresses the environmental determinants of social vulnerability that underpin many of the challenges documented in this encyclopedia.

#### Narrative Assessment

WHO's most innovative contribution has been the Faafu Atoll Primary Health Care Demonstration Site (FADS), which piloted the integration of NCD and mental health services into primary healthcare on a remote atoll. This demonstration project addressed the critical gap identified in Part XI of this encyclopedia: the health system's curative bias, where only US$11 per capita was spent on public health programmes compared to US$130 on inpatient curative services.[^48] By integrating targeted NCD screenings, lifestyle counselling, and identification and referral for common cancers and selected mental health conditions into the existing PHC framework, the FADS model offers a potential template for scaling across all 20 atolls -- though replication requires staffing and equipment investments that have not yet been committed.

The NDA/WHO Situational Analysis of Drugs (2021) provided the most comprehensive recent assessment of the drug situation in the Maldives, addressing a social issue that intersects deeply with child protection, domestic violence, and criminal justice systems.[^49]

WHO's consultancy for the National Mental Health Strategic Plan, initiated in 2024, addresses a gap flagged throughout this encyclopedia: the absence of a Mental Health Act and the lack of a comprehensive strategic framework for mental health services.[^50]

#### Detailed Programme Profiles

##### Faafu Atoll Primary Health Care Demonstration Site

**Donor:** WHO
**Period:** Launched December 2022; ongoing with planned expansion
**Budget:** Not publicly specified
**Status:** Active -- expansion underway
**Government Counterpart:** Ministry of Health

**Objectives and Components:**

The Faafu Atoll Demonstration Site (FADS) is a flagship WHO initiative to transform primary health care in the Maldives by integrating NCD diagnosis, screening, and treatment into the existing atoll-level PHC system. The project was jointly soft-launched by the WHO Regional Director for Southeast Asia and the Minister of Health in December 2022.

The integrated service delivery model includes:

- Population-level screening for non-communicable diseases (diabetes, hypertension, high cholesterol)
- NCD risk factor assessment
- Healthy lifestyle counselling
- Cancer screening
- Mental health condition identification and referral
- Treatment initiation and management at the primary care level

**Results / Impact:**

The demonstration site produced results that validated the integrated PHC model:

- **Over 90%** of the eligible population screened for diabetes, hypertension, high cholesterol, and NCD risk factors
- **9%** found to be living with diabetes; **88%** placed on treatment
- **14%** found to have hypertension; **95%** placed on treatment
- **22%** found to have high cholesterol; **73%** placed on treatment

Treatment rates improved substantially over time:

- Hypertension treatment: **92% to 95.5%** (March--November 2023)
- Diabetes treatment: **86% to 95.1%** (March--November 2023)
- High cholesterol treatment: **57% to 71.6%** (March--November 2023)

**Scale-Up Plans:**

Plans for expansion to **10 additional atolls** (out of the country's 20 administrative atolls) were scheduled for 2024. This expansion would represent a fundamental transformation of how NCDs are managed in the Maldives, shifting from a centralised, hospital-based model (where patients travel to Male for treatment) to a decentralised, community-based PHC model.

**Relevance to Social Policy:**

NCDs are the leading cause of morbidity and mortality in the Maldives. The cost of NCD treatment is a major driver of household out-of-pocket health expenditure and a cause of catastrophic health spending, particularly for outer island residents who must travel to Male for specialist care. The Faafu demonstration site addresses this by proving that effective NCD management can be delivered at the atoll level, potentially reducing health-related financial vulnerability.

### 2.11 Bilateral Partners

#### India

India is the Maldives' most significant bilateral development partner by volume. The partnership encompasses approximately US$500 million in lines of credit and grants across infrastructure, housing, health, and education. Key social sector contributions include:

- **Social Housing**: 3,300 social housing units delivered; 4,000 units under construction in Hulhumale under Buyers' Credit; agreement for an additional 2,000 units. This represents the largest external contribution to addressing the housing crisis documented in Part XII of this encyclopedia.[^67]
- **Health Cooperation**: Donation of two BHISHM (Aarogya Maitri Health Cubes) equipped for emergency medical aid; deepening of health cooperation for safe, quality, and affordable healthcare; promotion of hospital linkages between India and the Maldives.[^68]
- **Education**: Support for schools and educational infrastructure as part of broader development partnership.
- **MoU on Sustainable Urban Development**: Agreement on cooperation covering urban planning, smart cities, affordable housing, solid waste management, and urban green mobility.[^69]

India's social sector impact is primarily delivered through large-scale infrastructure projects rather than targeted social programme support. The social housing contribution is particularly significant given that 56.6 percent of Greater Male residents live in rented apartments and housing consumes up to 37 percent of household expenditure.[^70]

#### China

China has been a major infrastructure development partner, with approximately 70 percent of the Maldives' total external debt attributed to Chinese projects. The China-Maldives Friendship Bridge and social housing projects are the most visible contributions. The largest social housing project in the Maldives -- totalling 7,000 apartment units -- was constructed by Chinese firms.[^71] However, the debt sustainability implications of Chinese financing are a concern, with annual payments to China constituting approximately US$92 million (around 10 percent of the national budget).[^72]

#### Japan (JICA)

JICA's engagement in the Maldives spans four decades, with more than 360 Japan Overseas Cooperation Volunteers (JOCVs) having served in the country since the early 1980s. Current areas of cooperation include:

- **Human Resource Development Scholarships (JDS)**: Grant aid for Maldivian civil servants to pursue Master's and PhD degrees in Japan.[^73]
- **Education Volunteers**: JOCVs working in primary education and physical education across outer islands.
- **Knowledge Co-Creation Programmes (KCCP)**: Short-term training programmes for civil servants, with over 1,100 participants across different sectors.
- **Agriculture Survey (2023)**: Documentation of agricultural realities including saltwater intrusion and climate impacts on food production.
- **Renewable Energy**: Support for solar power development.[^74]

JICA's social sector contribution is primarily through human capital development rather than large-scale project financing, reflecting Japan's comparative advantage in capacity building and people-to-people exchange. The volunteer programme is distinctive in providing sustained, island-level presence that most other partners cannot match -- JOCVs live and work in atoll communities for extended periods, building relationships and local capacity in ways that short-term technical assistance missions cannot replicate. However, the volunteer programme operates independently of the broader social protection and child protection architecture, and integration with government social service delivery remains limited.

The 2023 JICA Agriculture Survey documented the practical realities facing Maldivian farmers, including saltwater intrusion into freshwater lenses and climate impacts on food production -- providing evidence that feeds into the food security-climate-social vulnerability nexus examined in Part XIV of this encyclopedia.

#### Australia (DFAT) and United Kingdom (FCDO)

Australian and UK social sector engagement in the Maldives has been limited and primarily channelled through multilateral mechanisms. UKAid funded a climate change and education report in 2010 that examined hazard profiles, vulnerability assessments, and climate change education strategies. The Commonwealth Equality Project (supported by Westminster Foundation for Democracy) produced a gender equality training manual in 2021 covering gender concepts, intersectionality, gender equality in Islam, and inclusive policymaking.[^75] The UK's Ocean Country Partnership Programme produced a GESI and SEAH Status Report for the Maldives in 2025. Neither country maintains a dedicated bilateral development programme for the Maldives social sector, and their contributions are primarily normative and knowledge-based rather than operational.

Australia's engagement has been through regional frameworks (DFAT's broader South Asia programme) and through the NORCAP expert deployments that it partially funds. The absence of a dedicated bilateral social sector programme from either Australia or the UK reflects the Maldives' upper-middle-income classification, which reduces its eligibility for some bilateral aid programmes despite its structural vulnerabilities as a SIDS.

### 3.2 Overlap and Complementarity

Several areas exhibit significant partner overlap, creating both opportunities for synergy and risks of coordination failure.

**GBV and gender**: UNFPA, ADB, UNDP, UN Women, EU, and UNICEF all claim engagement with gender equality and GBV prevention. This creates both complementarity (different agencies addressing different aspects -- UNFPA on evidence generation and health sector response, ADB on institutional reform and gender-responsive budgeting, UNICEF on child protection dimensions of GBV, UNDP on governance and local-level women's participation, UN Women on normative coordination, and the EU on governance and rule of law) and coordination challenges. The GBV sub-cluster activated during COVID-19 demonstrated that effective coordination is possible under crisis conditions: when the Health Emergency Operations Centre activated the sub-cluster comprising FPA, CFPS, police, and NGOs, each stakeholder understood their role and could convene to support survivors despite the operational constraints of lockdown.[^88] However, routine coordination mechanisms for gender programming remain less structured, and there is no standing inter-agency gender coordination group that meets regularly with the Ministry to align programming, share lessons, and avoid duplication. The consequence is that multiple partners produce separate analyses, guidelines, and capacity-building initiatives that may not build cumulatively toward systemic change.

**Social protection**: The World Bank and UNICEF jointly supported the National Social Protection Framework 2022--2026, while ADB's SPI analysis, ILO's social protection floor advisory work, and UNDP's climate-social protection integration all operate in the same policy space. The World Bank's SAILS project has the potential to serve as a coordination vehicle for social protection reform, but the multiplicity of advisory inputs risks overwhelming the limited institutional capacity of NSPA, which must manage Aasandha (with annual costs approaching MVR 2 billion), the disability allowance, the single parent allowance, the foster care allowance, and the food subsidy programme with a staff complement that is modest relative to these responsibilities. The risk is that partners generate competing reform agendas -- universal versus targeted approaches, insurance versus assistance models, digital-first versus in-person service delivery -- that NSPA lacks the analytical capacity to synthesise into a coherent strategy.

**Health**: WHO, World Bank, UNICEF, UNFPA, and IsDB all engage with the health sector, with overlapping mandates in RMNCAH, NCD prevention, and health systems strengthening. The RMNCAH Strategy 2020--2025 represents effective joint programming among three agencies (UNICEF, UNFPA, WHO), providing a model for how agencies can align behind a single government-led strategy with clear roles and responsibilities.[^89] The challenge is replicating this model in other health sub-sectors -- particularly mental health, where WHO is developing the strategic plan but no other partner is investing in service delivery capacity, and NCD prevention, where the Faafu Atoll demonstration project is a promising model but lacks the multi-partner investment needed for national scale-up.

**Climate and social resilience**: The climate-social nexus is an area of growing but still fragmented partner engagement. UNDP leads on GCF-funded water security; UNEP is developing the TRACT early warning systems project; the World Bank finances renewable energy with gender action plans; ADB addresses climate resilience through infrastructure; and GEF supports climate transparency reporting. The social dimensions of these climate projects -- their impacts on vulnerable populations, their implications for social protection systems, their interactions with food security, health, and displacement dynamics documented in Part XIV of this encyclopedia -- are often treated as cross-cutting concerns rather than core design parameters. A more integrated approach would embed social vulnerability analysis and social protection linkages into every climate adaptation project from design stage.

**Education**: The World Bank's Atoll Education Development Project and UNICEF's education quality work both address the same core challenge -- inadequate education outcomes in outer atolls -- but from different entry points (the World Bank through teacher effectiveness and secondary education, UNICEF through digital innovation and rights-based inclusion). JICA's education volunteers operate independently of both. IsDB has financed educational infrastructure. The Education Sector Analysis (2019) provided a comprehensive assessment that could serve as a coordination platform, but no regular education sector coordination meeting among partners appears to be operational.

### 3.3 Domains with Insufficient Partner Support

Several critical social policy domains receive inadequate development partner attention:

**Elderly care and ageing**: Despite the Maldives' rapidly ageing population (projected to reach 34.1 percent aged 60+ by 2054), no development partner maintains a dedicated project for elderly care. UNFPA and HelpAge International provide regional-level support for ageing data and policy, and ESCAP facilitated the MIPAA survey, but there is no equivalent to the UNICEF-supported child protection systems strengthening or the UNFPA-led GBV programming for the elderly population. This gap is particularly concerning given the government's own admission that "lack of overall political support" for ageing issues is "always" present.[^90]

**Substance abuse treatment and prevention**: Despite drug use affecting approximately 8 percent of users through injection, with half starting before age 17, and despite the deep intersection of substance abuse with child protection, domestic violence, and criminal justice systems, development partner engagement is limited to UNODC's law enforcement capacity building and a single World Bank-supported evaluation of drug treatment services in 2010. There is no comprehensive, sustained development partner programme for demand reduction, treatment, or rehabilitation.[^91]

**Mental health**: Despite 29.1 percent of the population reporting a mental health condition (2003 survey), no development partner has funded a dedicated mental health programme. WHO's consultancy for the National Mental Health Strategic Plan (2024) is a step forward, but it addresses planning rather than service delivery. The absence of a Mental Health Act and the lack of community-based mental health services across atolls represent gaps that no partner is currently addressing at scale.[^92]

**Juvenile justice and gang intervention**: The Juvenile Justice Act 2019 reformed the legal framework, raising the age of criminal responsibility to 15 and prohibiting the death penalty for juveniles, but implementation support from development partners has been minimal. The SAP light chapters note the need to address children recruited by gangs and to strengthen juvenile offender rehabilitation and reintegration, but no dedicated partner programme exists for this critical issue. Between 2013 and 2015, five juveniles were sentenced to death in the Maldives -- a situation that catalysed legislative reform but has not generated equivalent investment in the rehabilitative infrastructure that the new law requires.[^92a] The intersection of juvenile justice with drug trafficking (drug-related offences showed a 200 percent increase, primarily affecting males aged 16--30) and gang recruitment creates a complex operational challenge that no single partner is currently positioned to address.

**Housing affordability and homelessness prevention**: While India and China have invested heavily in social housing construction, and the World Bank has provided analytical support on housing markets and poverty, no development partner has directly addressed the housing affordability crisis that drives social vulnerability in the Greater Male Region. With 85 percent of household income reportedly consumed by housing costs in Male (HRCM rapid assessment, 2008) and 56.6 percent of residents living in rented apartments, the housing dimension of social protection remains an infrastructure problem treated separately from the social protection policy architecture that partners have helped design. The disconnect between the physical housing investments of bilateral partners and the social protection programming of multilateral partners represents a coordination gap with real consequences for vulnerable families.

**Data system integration**: Multiple partners have invested in separate data systems -- UNICEF in the MCPD for child protection, the World Bank in NSPA's social registry and SPIS, UNFPA in census disability analysis, UNDP in rights monitoring databases, and the government in the OneGov digital platform. Each investment makes sense in isolation, but the absence of a coordinated data integration strategy means that a social worker on an outer island cannot easily cross-reference whether a family receiving child protection intervention is also receiving the disability allowance, the single parent allowance, or Zakat distributions. The World Bank's SAILS project includes social registry development that could serve as an integration platform, but achieving interoperability across systems built by different partners with different technical standards will require dedicated coordination that is not currently in place.

## Section 4: Critical Assessment

### 4.1 What Has Worked

Several aspects of development partner engagement in the Maldivian social sector merit recognition as genuinely effective:

**Evidence generation**: Development partners have been the primary producers of evidence on which Maldivian social policy rests. The UNFPA-supported Fulu study (2007), the UNICEF Situation Analyses, the World Bank poverty assessments, the ADB SPI analysis, the HRCM/UNDP disability baseline, the Census disability and child equity analyses supported by UNFPA and UNICEF respectively, and the WHO STEPS and GYTS surveys have collectively built an evidence base that did not exist before partner engagement. Without this evidence, much of the legislative reform of the 2010s -- including the DVPA 2012, the Gender Equality Act 2016, the CRPA 2019, and the Juvenile Justice Act 2019 -- would have lacked the empirical foundation needed to sustain political support.[^93]

**Legal and policy framework development**: Partners have been instrumental in developing the legislative and policy architecture. UNICEF supported the CRPA 2019; UNFPA supported the GBV law review; WHO supported the Health Master Plan; the World Bank and UNICEF supported the NSPF 2022--2026; ADB is supporting gender-responsive budgeting. The result is a remarkably comprehensive policy framework for a country of half a million people.

**Crisis response**: The COVID-19 response demonstrated effective coordination. The World Bank's Income Support Allowance reached 23,000 workers within months; the GBV sub-cluster activated under the Health Emergency Operations Centre ensured 24/7 support for domestic violence victims during lockdown; WHO and UNICEF supported vaccine deployment; the EU and IsDB provided emergency funding.[^94] The crisis response model offers lessons for future shock-responsive social protection.

**Water security**: The GCF-UNDP water security project delivered tangible improvements to 105,000 people, demonstrating that climate adaptation projects can achieve measurable social outcomes when social dimensions are central to project design rather than afterthoughts.

### 4.2 What Has Not Worked

**Framework-implementation gap**: The most persistent failure of development partner engagement is the generation of frameworks, plans, and strategies that exceed the implementation capacity of Maldivian institutions. The NAPDI v6, NAPVAC, GEAP, NSPF, and other action plans examined in Part XIX of this encyclopedia are technically sophisticated documents produced with substantial partner investment, yet many lack costed budgets, numerical targets, or accountability mechanisms. The NAP-PRVAC 2017 was "never effectively implemented" despite its development with partner support, and the 2024 successor plan's own introduction acknowledges that "similar challenges were identified during the consultations conducted for the formulation of renewed NAP 2024-2028."[^95] Partners bear some responsibility for this pattern: the production of plans has become a deliverable in itself, detached from the operational reality of 61 social workers serving 128,208 children.

**Cultural contextualisation deficit**: The transposition of Western social work models -- particularly the English child protection framework -- into the Maldivian context has created operational tensions that persist decades later. The 2006 Child Protection Procedure Manual, modelled on England's "Working Together to Safeguard Children," assumed institutional infrastructure (inter-agency information-sharing agreements, statutory authority for social workers, supervised professional practice) that did not exist in the Maldives and largely still does not.[^96] Partners have been more effective at transferring frameworks than at adapting them to the realities of small island communities where social networks are dense, anonymity is impossible, and professional independence is compromised by social proximity.

**Workforce investment**: Despite universal recognition that the social service workforce is the binding constraint on social policy implementation, no development partner has invested at scale in social worker recruitment, training, retention, and supervision. The World Bank's MEERY project focuses on tourism, construction, ICT, and agriculture; ADB's gender project addresses budgeting and institutional reform; UNICEF provides training for existing workers but not the systemic workforce expansion that is needed. The gap between the 61 social workers currently serving the country and the workforce needed to implement the case management models promoted by partners remains unaddressed.[^97]

### 4.3 Donor Dependency Risks

The Maldives' reliance on development partners for social policy design and implementation creates several dependency risks:

**Technical dependency**: Critical social policy functions -- poverty measurement, GBV prevalence estimation, child welfare analysis, disability data collection, climate vulnerability assessment -- are substantially dependent on partner-funded technical assistance. If partner engagement were to contract (as has occurred during political crises and governance concerns), the government's capacity to generate the evidence needed for policy-making would be severely constrained.

**Financial dependency**: The fiscal sustainability of several social programmes depends implicitly on partner willingness to finance system development. The SAILS project's social insurance and active labour market components require sustained institutional investment beyond the project period. The MCPD case management system depends on technical infrastructure that UNICEF has supported. If partners were to withdraw, the operational capacity of these systems would be at risk.

**Model dependency**: The Maldives' social policy frameworks are substantially borrowed from international models promoted by development partners. This creates a form of intellectual dependency where policy-makers may lack the capacity or confidence to develop locally adapted solutions. The Gender Equality Act 2016 follows CEDAW frameworks; the CRPA 2019 follows CRC standards; the disability framework follows the CRPD; the child protection case management model follows England's "Working Together" guidance; the pension system's design draws on World Bank advisory frameworks; the NCD prevention approach follows WHO's building blocks model. While alignment with international standards is valuable -- and indeed, the treaty reporting process creates a useful accountability mechanism -- the risk is that implementation becomes an exercise in demonstrating compliance with external frameworks rather than solving locally defined problems. The vulnerability mapping research documented in Part V of this encyclopedia reveals forms of harm -- reproductive coercion by in-laws, technology-facilitated abuse in small island communities, the intersection of drug trafficking power with child protection decision-making -- that do not map easily onto the standardised risk assessment frameworks imported from Western jurisdictions. A genuinely effective response to these locally specific dynamics requires locally generated practice models, not further refinement of imported ones.

**Political leverage dependency**: Development partner engagement has at times served as a lever for governance reform -- the EU's conditionality around rule of law and human rights, the World Bank's emphasis on transparency and anti-corruption in health financing, and the UN human rights system's treaty reporting and shadow reporting processes all create external accountability pressures that complement domestic oversight. However, this external accountability function creates a dependency: when domestic accountability mechanisms are weak (as the encyclopedia documents across multiple sectors), the external pressure provided by development partners may be the primary driver of reform. If that pressure is removed -- through partner withdrawal, reduced political engagement, or shifts in global development priorities -- the reform momentum may dissipate.

### 4.4 Sustainability of Donor-Funded Programmes

The sustainability of partner-funded social programmes is a persistent concern. Several patterns emerge:

- **Project-to-system transition**: The most successful partner investments are those that build permanent institutional capacity rather than deliver time-limited services. The World Bank's SAILS project, if successful in establishing a social insurance system, would create a permanent programme that outlasts the project period. By contrast, many UNICEF and UNFPA programme activities (training workshops, technical assistance missions, awareness campaigns) leave limited institutional residue once funding ends.
- **Fiscal absorption**: The Maldives' fiscal position -- with government expenditure consistently exceeding revenue and public debt rising -- constrains the government's ability to absorb and sustain partner-funded programmes after project closure. The Aasandha universal health insurance scheme already consumes over MVR 1.9 billion annually; the Old-Age Basic Pension costs over MVR 1.5 billion; and social protection expenditure is growing. Adding new recurrent costs from partner-designed programmes places further pressure on already strained public finances.
- **Political cycle vulnerability**: The Maldives' political landscape is characterised by significant policy shifts across administrations. Programmes designed under one government may lose political support under the next. The Strategic Action Plan 2019--2023 assigned ambitious social policy targets, but the change of government in 2023 resulted in restructuring of the lead ministry (from MoGFSS to MoSFD), reassignment of priorities, and the need for successor planning documents (the SAP light chapters) that remain incomplete.

### 4.5 Coordination Failures and Successes

**Coordination successes**: The UNSDCF 2022--2026 provides a structured coordination framework. The joint RMNCAH Strategy (UNICEF, UNFPA, WHO) demonstrates effective multi-agency collaboration. The COVID-19 response activated coordination mechanisms (GBV sub-cluster, joint vaccine support) that functioned under pressure. The joint World Bank-UNICEF support for the NSPF demonstrates complementary engagement.

**Coordination failures**: Outside crisis periods, coordination is inconsistent. Multiple partners engage with gender/GBV without a clear division of labour. The absence of a dedicated elderly care partner means this population falls between the mandates of existing agencies. The drug and substance abuse response operates in silos from child protection and domestic violence programming despite deep operational interdependencies. The multiplicity of action plans produced with different partner support (NAPDI with UN agencies, NAPVAC with UNICEF, GEAP with ministry-led but partner-supported processes, NSPF with World Bank and UNICEF) creates a coordination burden that exceeds the capacity of MoSFD's policy department.

### 4.6 Recommendations for Future Partner Engagement

Based on the analysis in this appendix and the preceding chapters of this encyclopedia, the following recommendations are offered for the future of development partner engagement in Maldivian social policy:

1. **Invest in the social service workforce**: No amount of framework development, legislative reform, or digital system design will compensate for the absence of a functioning social service workforce across 189 inhabited islands. Partners should collectively prioritise a workforce development initiative that addresses recruitment, training (through MNU's Social Work degree programme), deployment, supervision, remuneration, and retention of social workers -- with a target of at least tripling the current complement of 61 workers within five years.

2. **Shift from plan production to implementation support**: Partners should reduce investment in producing new action plans, strategies, and frameworks -- the Maldives already has more plans than it can implement -- and redirect resources toward sustained implementation support, including embedded technical advisors in MoSFD, NSPA, and FCSCs, who can assist with the day-to-day operationalisation of existing commitments.

3. **Address neglected domains**: Dedicated partner programming is needed for elderly care, mental health, substance abuse treatment, and juvenile justice -- domains that currently fall between existing agency mandates.

4. **Adapt models to context**: Partners should invest in developing Maldivian-specific social work practice models that account for the realities of small island communities, rather than continuing to transpose frameworks designed for large, institutionally complex Western systems. This requires genuine co-design with Maldivian practitioners rather than technical assistance missions that deliver pre-packaged solutions.

5. **Strengthen sustainability mechanisms**: Partners should require sustainability plans for all project investments, including fiscal absorption analysis, government co-financing commitments, and transition plans for the post-project period.

6. **Consolidate coordination**: The UNCT should consider designating lead agencies for each social policy domain (not just outcome areas) to reduce overlap and ensure that coordination is operational rather than strategic. A quarterly social sector coordination meeting -- bringing together all partners with the MoSFD Policy Department -- would provide a structured mechanism for information sharing, gap identification, and joint problem-solving.

7. **Prioritise data system integration**: Multiple partners have invested in separate data systems (MCPD for child protection, SPIS for social protection, NDR for disability, OneGov for digital services). Partners should collectively support the integration of these systems into a unified social information platform, enabling cross-referencing of beneficiary data and reducing the fragmentation that currently characterises social service delivery.

### 4.7 Portfolio-Level Observations from Project Detail Review

#### 4.7.1 Portfolio Concentration and Gaps

Analysing the project-level detail reveals several patterns:

**Concentration in economic/labour market projects.** The World Bank's social sector portfolio in the Maldives is heavily oriented toward labour market and employability interventions (MEERY, SAILS, COVID-19 Income Support). While important, these projects address only one dimension of social policy. There is no major World Bank project specifically targeting child protection systems strengthening, disability services, aged care, or social work workforce development.

**ADB's gender project fills a critical gap.** The Strengthening Gender Inclusive Initiatives Project (54373-001) is notable for directly addressing the DV/GBV shelter gap, aged care, and early childcare -- areas that no other development partner project covers at scale. The US$10 million commitment, while modest, is the most significant external investment in the social service delivery infrastructure documented in this encyclopedia.

**UNICEF's flagship model is innovative but resource-constrained.** Eight flagship programmes on US$11.7 million over five years works out to approximately US$290,000 per flagship per year. This is sufficient for advocacy, technical assistance, and small-scale pilots, but not for systemic service delivery transformation. UNICEF's value in the Maldives is primarily as a technical partner and advocate rather than a major funder.

**UNFPA's programme is small but strategically important.** At US$3.5 million over five years, UNFPA's budget is modest, but the GBV law review and census support deliver disproportionate value relative to cost.

**WHO's PHC demonstration is the most compelling results story.** The Faafu Atoll results -- 90%+ screening coverage, dramatic treatment uptake improvements -- represent the clearest evidence of development partner impact on frontline service delivery documented anywhere in this encyclopedia.

#### 4.7.2 Coordination Architecture and the UNSDCF

All UN agency programmes in the Maldives operate under the United Nations Sustainable Development Cooperation Framework (UNSDCF), coordinated by the UN Resident Coordinator's Office. The UNSDCF for 2022--2026 aligns UN agency country programmes (UNICEF, UNFPA, UNDP, WHO, and others) with the Government's national development priorities and the 2030 Agenda. This coordination mechanism is important to understand because it shapes how individual agency programmes interact. For example:

- The INFF (UNDP-led) provides the financing architecture for the social service delivery improvements that UNICEF, UNFPA, and WHO programmes seek to achieve.
- The UNICEF-WHO Joint Programme on Mental Health demonstrates cross-agency collaboration within the UNSDCF framework.
- The UNFPA census support generates the population data that all agencies use for programme targeting and planning.
- UNDP's governance programming supports the institutional capacity of the ministries that implement UNICEF and UNFPA social sector programmes.

However, coordination between UN agencies and the Bretton Woods institutions (World Bank, ADB) is more limited. The World Bank and ADB operate under their own country partnership frameworks with separate results frameworks, reporting requirements, and implementation modalities. This creates a fragmented environment where, for instance, the World Bank's SAILS project is designing an unemployment insurance scheme while UNICEF is advocating for child-sensitive social protection, and UNFPA is generating population data that could inform targeting for both -- but there is no single coordination mechanism ensuring these parallel efforts are mutually reinforcing.

The Government of Maldives, particularly the Ministry of Finance (for development partner coordination) and the Ministry of Social and Family Development (for social sector coordination), must navigate these overlapping frameworks. The capacity constraints documented throughout this encyclopedia -- limited staff, high turnover, thin technical expertise -- mean that effective donor coordination itself requires investment in dedicated coordination capacity within government institutions.

#### 4.7.3 The AIM Project – A Joint EU-UNDP-MoSFD Initiative

The AIM (Accountability and Integration Matters) project, identified through MoSFD sources, is a joint EU-UNDP-MoSFD collaboration focused on creating an inclusive justice system for GBV and domestic abuse survivors. Its positioning across two development partners (EU financing, UNDP implementation) illustrates both the potential of joint programming and the coordination complexity it entails. The project directly addresses the justice access gaps documented in Part V and the legal framework weaknesses identified in the UNFPA 2023 GBV law review.

#### 4.7.4 Thematic Coverage Gaps in the Portfolio

A systematic review of the project portfolio reveals several social policy domains that receive minimal or no dedicated development partner project funding:

**Child protection systems.** Despite UNICEF's advocacy and analytical work on child protection, no development partner has a major funded project specifically targeting the child protection system. The system's foundational architecture -- case management protocols, foster care standards, institutional care quality, the Child and Family Protection Service -- has not been the subject of a dedicated investment project with discrete components and budget. UNICEF's work in this area operates through the country programme's regular resources, which are spread across eight flagship programmes. The absence of a dedicated child protection systems strengthening project is notable given the detailed assessment of system weaknesses in the Rogers, Ali & Naeem (2025) analysis documented elsewhere in this encyclopedia.

**Disability services.** No active development partner project focuses specifically on disability service delivery, despite the Disability Act (1/2010) and the ratification of the CRPD. The ADB Gender Inclusive Initiatives project touches on aged care (which overlaps with disability) but does not address the disability service system as such. The UNPRPD-supported disability situational analysis produced diagnostic findings but was not followed by an implementation-focused project with dedicated funding for system reform.

**Social work workforce development.** The Maldives has fewer than 50 professional social workers serving a population of over 500,000 across approximately 200 inhabited islands. No development partner project specifically targets social work education, professional development, or workforce expansion. The MEERY project addresses youth skills broadly but not social work as a profession. This gap is particularly significant because social worker capacity constrains the effectiveness of virtually every other social programme -- from child protection case management to GBV survivor support to aged care assessments.

**Housing and homelessness.** Despite housing being one of the main social issues in the Maldives (documented in Part XII of this encyclopedia), no development partner has a dedicated housing for vulnerable populations project. The Housing Development Corporation operates as an SOE, and World Bank SOE reform (P179286) may indirectly affect housing policy, but there is no project addressing social housing, transitional housing for GBV survivors (beyond ADB's three shelters), or homelessness prevention.

**Substance abuse treatment.** While UNICEF's Kihineh flagship includes mental health and substance abuse, no development partner has a dedicated project for substance abuse treatment infrastructure, residential rehabilitation, or harm reduction services -- despite substance abuse being identified as a critical social challenge across the atolls, particularly among young men in the outer islands.

#### 4.7.5 Legislative Dependencies as Implementation Risk

The SAILS project experience highlights a structural risk in development partner project design: where project components depend on legislative action (in this case, the Unemployment Insurance Bill), implementation can stall indefinitely if parliament does not act within the project timeline. This risk applies to multiple social policy reforms documented elsewhere in this encyclopedia, including amendments to the Child Rights Act, the Disability Act, and the Social Protection Act. Project designers at the World Bank and ADB must weigh whether to include legislative reform as a precondition (risking delay) or to proceed without it (risking that project investments lack the legal framework needed for sustainability). Neither approach has produced consistently satisfactory results in the Maldivian context.

#### 4.7.6 Data Gaps in Impact Assessment

A notable finding from this review is the limited availability of Implementation Completion Reports (ICRs) and rigorous impact evaluations for completed projects. The COVID-19 Emergency Income Support Project (P174014) -- one of the largest emergency interventions in Maldivian social policy -- does not have a publicly accessible ICR. The UNFPA Country Programme Evaluation noted that the organisation "does not have clear data" on how national frameworks have been implemented at the service delivery level. This pattern of weak impact documentation makes it difficult to assess what development partner engagement has actually achieved, as distinct from what it has funded.

The contrast with the WHO Faafu Atoll demonstration is instructive. WHO was able to document specific, measurable outcomes (screening coverage rates, treatment uptake percentages, treatment rate improvements over time) because the intervention was bounded, had a clear baseline, and used health system data that was already being collected. Most social sector projects in the Maldives lack these conditions. The social protection system does not generate the administrative data needed for rigorous impact evaluation. Child protection case management data is fragmented and unreliable. GBV reporting rates are known to vastly underrepresent actual prevalence. Without investment in the data infrastructure itself -- which only the ADB Gender Inclusive Initiatives project explicitly addresses through its GESI statistics component -- evaluating what development partner projects achieve will remain an exercise in documenting outputs (training sessions held, documents produced, systems designed) rather than outcomes (lives improved, vulnerability reduced, rights fulfilled).

#### 4.7.7 Sustainability and Government Ownership

A recurring concern across the portfolio is the sustainability of development partner interventions after project closure. Several patterns merit attention:

**Systems designed but not maintained.** Multiple projects have financed the design or development of information systems, registries, and management platforms (the MEERY eLearning infrastructure, the PFM project's financial systems, the proposed digital ID system). The operational question is whether the Government of Maldives has the budget, technical staff, and institutional commitment to maintain these systems after project funding ends. The Maldives' track record on this front is mixed -- the Pension Administration Office has successfully maintained its systems, but other digital initiatives have languished after donor funding ceased.

**Capacity built but not retained.** Training and capacity building are components of nearly every project documented in this appendix. However, the Maldivian public service experiences high turnover, particularly in the social sector and in atoll-level positions. Staff trained under one project cycle may have moved to other positions or left government service entirely by the time a subsequent project seeks to build on their skills. This creates a perpetual "training treadmill" where each new project must rebuild capacity that the previous project already developed.

**Policy frameworks produced but not implemented.** The UNFPA Country Programme Evaluation's finding that there is no clear data on how the Gender Equality Action Plan has been rolled out is emblematic of a broader pattern. Development partner projects frequently produce high-quality policy documents, action plans, and strategic frameworks. Implementation, however, requires sustained government budgetary commitment and institutional follow-through that extends far beyond the project period. The gap between framework production and implementation is a central challenge in the development partner-government relationship in the Maldivian social sector.

#### 4.7.8 The Scale Question

The total identified development partner commitment to social sector-relevant projects in the Maldives stands at approximately US$212 million. To put this in perspective, the Maldivian government's annual recurrent expenditure on the social sector (including health, education, social protection transfers, and social services) exceeds US$500 million per year. Development partner project financing thus represents a meaningful but not dominant share of total social sector resources. The real value of development partner engagement lies less in the absolute quantum of funding and more in the technical assistance, institutional design, and policy innovation that accompanies it. The unemployment insurance scheme being designed under SAILS, the PHC transformation model being piloted by WHO, the GESI data infrastructure being built by ADB, and the INFF financing architecture being operationalised by UNDP all have the potential to reshape how the Maldivian government allocates and manages its own much larger domestic social expenditure. Whether that potential is realised depends on the sustainability and government ownership factors discussed above -- and ultimately, on political will at the highest levels of government.

### 4.8 Areas for Further Investigation

Several project-level details could not be fully resolved through web searches and available documentation. The following items should be verified through direct consultation with development partners or government counterparts in a future revision:

1. **AIM project details:** The Accountability and Integration Matters (AIM) project has been identified as a joint EU-UNDP-MoSFD initiative for GBV justice system reform. Further details on budget, timeline, specific deliverables, and current implementation status should be obtained from UNDP Maldives or MoSFD.

2. **SAILS project current status (post-2024 elections):** The most recent ISR available was from early 2024 and noted that key appointments were on hold pending elections. Post-election developments -- including whether the Unemployment Insurance Bill has been reintroduced -- should be verified.

## Conclusion

The development partner landscape in the Maldivian social sector is characterised by a paradox: the country has received substantial and sustained international engagement -- from multilateral institutions, UN agencies, bilateral donors, and climate funds -- yet the fundamental operational challenges identified throughout this encyclopedia persist. The social service workforce remains critically understaffed. Case management systems exist in name but not in operational reality across most atolls. Action plans proliferate without implementation. Geographic disparities between Male and outer islands remain entrenched. Vulnerable populations -- the elderly, persons with mental health conditions, substance abuse sufferers, children recruited by gangs -- continue to fall between the mandates of existing programmes.

This is not primarily a failure of development partner intent or investment volume. The partners documented in this appendix have, in aggregate, invested hundreds of millions of dollars in the Maldivian social sector over the past two decades. They have built an evidence base, supported legislative reform, trained practitioners, funded emergency responses, and established institutional frameworks. What they have not achieved -- and what no external partner can achieve on behalf of a sovereign government -- is the sustained political prioritisation of social policy implementation, the allocation of domestic budgetary resources commensurate with legislative ambition, and the cultural and institutional adaptation that is required to make imported frameworks function in the specific social, geographic, and administrative context of the Maldives.

The challenge for the next decade is not to secure additional development partner engagement but to fundamentally shift the nature of that engagement: from framework production to implementation support, from evidence generation to evidence utilisation, from island-based pilot projects to systemic scale-up, and from parallel partner-led initiatives to genuinely government-owned programmes that partners support rather than drive. The Maldives' development trajectory -- from one of Asia's poorest nations to upper-middle-income status within a generation -- demonstrates that substantial change is possible. Achieving the same trajectory in social policy implementation will require development partners to subordinate their institutional interests to the operational realities of a system that serves half a million people across 35,000 square miles of ocean, one island at a time.
