# Part XV: Cross-Cutting Issues and Emerging Challenges

The preceding fourteen parts of this encyclopedia have examined specific domains of Maldivian social policy -- child protection, gender-based violence, disability, substance abuse, housing, health, and others -- as discrete systems with their own legal frameworks, institutional arrangements, and implementation challenges. Yet many of the problems facing the Maldives do not respect these sectoral boundaries. Violent extremism recruits from the same pool of disaffected youth that gangs target; divorce destabilises the same families that the child protection system struggles to support; the chronic shortage of professional social workers undermines every service delivery programme simultaneously; and the geographic fragmentation that hampers healthcare access equally impedes police response, court services, and welfare provision.

This final substantive chapter addresses nine cross-cutting issues that run through and beneath the policy domains already examined. These are not peripheral concerns or future hypotheticals. They are present-day realities that shape, constrain, and sometimes overwhelm the systems described elsewhere in this encyclopedia. Each section examines the issue's scale, the policy and legal responses that have been attempted, the frontline realities of implementation, and the structural gaps that remain.

## 15.1 Violent Extremism and Social Factors

### The Scale of the Threat

The Maldives occupies an uncomfortable distinction in global terrorism discourse: according to estimates cited by the U.S. Department of State, the country has produced one of the highest per capita numbers of foreign terrorist fighters (FTFs) who joined ISIS and al-Qaeda in Iraq and Syria in the wake of the Syrian Civil War.[^1] The National Counter Terrorism Centre (NCTC) reported that approximately 36 individuals constituted the core militant jihadist community in the Maldives, some of them returnees from the conflicts in Syria.[^2] These individuals have been linked to the 2007 Sultan Park bombing in Male', the 2021 attempted assassination of former President and then-Speaker of Parliament Mohamed Nasheed, and multiple other acts of arson, murder, and intimidation.[^3]

While the Maldives has not experienced the mass-casualty attacks that have struck neighbouring South Asian countries, the NAP on Preventing and Countering Violent Extremism (P/CVE) 2020-2024 makes clear that "there are real risks of occurrence and thus the threat of violence must be dealt with to prevent harm to people and property as well as the economy of the country."[^4] The threat is not confined to a handful of convicted terrorists. It extends into social media environments where hate speech and extremist content proliferate, into communities where separatist enclaves have formed, and critically, into the prison system where radicalisation operates as a structured recruitment enterprise.

### Push and Pull Factors

The NAP identifies a complex web of social, psychological, and structural factors that push individuals toward radicalisation or pull them into extremist movements. These are fundamentally social policy failures:

**Erosion of community identity.** Large-scale migration to Male' has produced a population living in short-term rentals, disconnected from the island-based community structures that historically provided social cohesion, mutual accountability, and informal governance. Parents working multiple jobs to afford Male's cost of living have less time for engagement with their children, particularly in a city where "urban, economic and social pressures are most intense."[^5]

**Disengagement and marginalisation.** Children and youth who fall out of the education system, or who are unable to find employment matching their aspirations, experience what the NAP describes as a loss of "dignity, agency and ambition." The demographic reality is stark: 40 percent of the resident population is aged between 15 and 34, and youth populations account for over 75 percent of the unemployed.[^6] The overall unemployment rate of 5.2 percent masks a youth unemployment crisis that is far more severe, particularly among males in Male'.

**The gang-extremism nexus.** The NAP explicitly notes that "domestic extremism has risen, often alongside the culture of gangs and substance abuse."[^7] This is not a casual observation. The ICSVE prison study found that prison extremist cells "work closely with gangs, organizing murders to silence their opponents as well as violent robberies to fund their activities." Many cell leaders are former gang leaders.[^8] The pathway from gang involvement to drug offending to imprisonment to extremist recruitment is a well-documented cycle in the Maldivian context.

**Failure of redemption pathways.** The NAP identifies "the immense difficulties of returning to a more socially acceptable pathway if a person somehow drops out of the mainstream" as a critical driver. Getting back on track after imprisonment, gang membership, drug treatment, or educational dropout "is a massive challenge that seems to have led some to try to find their idea of redemption in violent extremism."[^9]

### Prison Radicalisation

The 2022 ICSVE study on prison radicalisation, conducted by Dr. Anne Speckhard and published by Transparency Maldives and the Ministry of Home Affairs, provides the most detailed empirical account of how radicalisation operates within Maldivian correctional facilities. Based on interviews with 20 prisoners at Maafushi high-security prison (17 men and 3 women), one former prisoner, and extensive stakeholder consultations, the study reveals a structured extremist ecosystem operating within prison walls.

**Demographics of the radicalised.** The prisoner sample had a mean age at first arrest of 21.56 years, mean education of 8.2 years, and 64.7 percent were from poor or working-class backgrounds. 47.1 percent were divorced, and the mean prior prison time before their current sentence was 69.7 months -- indicating that these were individuals with extensive prior contact with the criminal justice system.[^10]

**The prison cell structure.** At Maafushi Prison, the extremist organisation operates with a hierarchical structure: one overarching emir leads all militant jihadists in the prison, each prison unit has its own emir, and the cells maintain a shura council (a panel of Islamic judges) that metes out punishments. The extremists "claim to be in a state of war and are waging jihad against the Maldivian government, which they believe to be taghut and not a legitimate Islamic government."[^11] They declare other prisoners who resist their ideology to be kafirs (unbelievers), and physical attacks on dissenters are carried out "ordered by their unit emirs."[^12]

**The drug-to-extremism pipeline.** A key finding is that many prisoners who become radicalised had been "previously imprisoned for drug and petty crimes" and were recruited into extremism as an alternative to their addictions. The militant jihadists effectively "treated" drug addictions while simultaneously indoctrinating new members -- filling the vacuum left by the absence of adequate rehabilitation programmes.[^13] As one interviewee, Faisal, described: "One group told me that if you are a drug user, you have to be executed... I said show me evidence from the Quran. They wouldn't show it but said this is the law of God."[^14]

**Material inducements.** The extremist cells are well-funded and offer new members religious education, mobile phones, money, and other material assets. For prisoners "struggling to find their place in prison," these inducements are powerful. Members with shorter sentences who agree to travel to Syria to join the jihad are offered financial support for their families.[^15]

**Staffing failures.** The study found that Maafushi Prison was 9.6 percent overcrowded with an inmate-to-staff ratio of 7.7 to 1. Stakeholders from the Ministry of Home Affairs, Ministry of Gender, and social services agencies "expressed concerns regarding understaffing, sharing that their case and social workers are overburdened and unable to devote a proper amount of time to each of the children and families whom they oversee."[^16] Staff were unable to "monitor radicalized prisoners and collect intelligence as much as they would like because they did not have the number of staff members required." Prison workers also lacked training in identifying and countering radicalisation, while state care facility staff "expressed concern that staff was not equipped to deal with radicalized children and youth."[^17]

### The Policy Response

The government's primary policy instrument is the National Action Plan on Preventing and Countering Violent Extremism 2020-2024, developed by the NCTC in partnership with UNDP Maldives. The NAP adopts a "whole of society" approach that combines security measures with social interventions organised around five strategic objectives:

1. Creating a more resilient, connected, and less vulnerable youth community through peer, psychosocial, and community support.
2. Improving care for young people so they can thrive in an environment where they feel comfortable and engaged.
3. Enhancing education to improve skills around social cohesion, critical thinking, communication, and online literacy.
4. Strengthening engagement with shared national and religious values to reduce the effect of malign external influences.
5. Ensuring that groups or individuals that isolate themselves from mainstream society are engaged through education and employment.[^18]

The NAP recognises that "poverty and the lack of education are not the sole or even key drivers of extremism -- many of those who have carried out violence around the world have come from privileged backgrounds."[^19] Instead, the focus is on fairness, inclusion, and the ability of young people to "find a means of redemption" when they are distanced from families, communities, or fail to meet expectations.

Key areas of the NAP include: policy-supported consensus dialogue on approaches to violent extremism, spearheaded by the Office of the President, MNDF, Ministry of Home Affairs, Ministry of Islamic Affairs, Fiq Academy, and Ministry of Education; development of a Communications Strategy addressing hate speech and radical social media content; reform of the judicial, policing, and correctional systems to reduce crime and gang activities; and addressing the role of women in radicalisation and deradicalisation.[^20]

### Implementation Gaps

Despite the comprehensive design of the NAP, implementation faces significant structural obstacles. The prison radicalisation study found "a lack of a prison-based rehabilitation programme in the area of countering radicalization and violent extremism in the Maldives."[^21] The Government's Strategic Action Plan aimed to reduce the incarcerated population by 20 percent by 2023 and increase prisoner participation in rehabilitation programmes by 50 percent by the same date -- targets whose achievement remains unverified. The NCTC hotline (1615) and the Counter Terrorism Centre at Bandaara Koshi on Ameeru Ahmed Magu in Male' provide institutional infrastructure, but their reach to outer atolls is limited.

The fundamental challenge is that P/CVE policy requires simultaneous action across justice, education, social services, religious affairs, and community development -- precisely the inter-agency coordination that the Maldivian bureaucracy has consistently struggled to achieve.

## 15.2 Human Trafficking -- Prevalence and Response

### The Scale of the Problem

The Maldives' human trafficking challenge is inseparable from its dependence on migrant labour. With a resident population of approximately 521,000, the country hosts an estimated 230,000 or more foreign workers, predominantly from Bangladesh, India, Sri Lanka, and Nepal.[^22] These workers form the backbone of the construction, tourism, fishing, and domestic service industries. The sheer ratio of foreign workers to citizens -- roughly one foreign worker for every two Maldivians -- creates a structural vulnerability that no amount of legislation can fully address without fundamental changes to the labour market model.

### Legal Framework

The Anti-Human Trafficking Act (Law No. 12/2013) criminalises all forms of trafficking in persons and establishes penalties of up to 15 years' imprisonment. The law covers forced labour, sexual exploitation, debt bondage, and the recruitment, transportation, transfer, harbouring, or receipt of persons through threat, force, or deception for the purpose of exploitation.[^23]

The Employment Act (Law No. 2/2008) provides additional protections, prohibiting forced labour (Article 3) and establishing the Labour Relations Authority and Employment Tribunal to adjudicate employment disputes. Article 25(a) of the Constitution abolishes slavery, servitude, and the performance of forced labour. The Maldives has ratified all eight fundamental ILO Conventions, including the Forced Labor Convention (No. 29), the Abolition of Forced Labor Convention (No. 105), the Minimum Age Convention (No. 138), and the Worst Forms of Child Labor Convention (No. 182).[^24]

### Frontline Realities

Despite this legal architecture, the U.S. Department of State has consistently identified significant implementation gaps in successive Trafficking in Persons Reports. The practical realities of migrant worker exploitation in the Maldives include:

**Recruitment debt bondage.** Workers from Bangladesh and other sending countries typically pay substantial recruitment fees -- sometimes equivalent to a year or more of expected earnings -- to labour agents. Upon arrival in the Maldives, they may find that the promised employment does not exist, that wages are lower than agreed, or that their employers confiscate their passports. This debt bondage effectively traps workers in exploitative conditions with no practical means of seeking redress.

**Wage theft.** Non-payment or chronic underpayment of wages is widespread among migrant workers in construction and domestic service. Workers who lack written contracts, whose contracts are in languages they cannot read, or whose employers simply refuse to pay have limited recourse. The Employment Tribunal in Male' is geographically inaccessible to workers on outer islands and resort islands, and the process of adjudication can take months during which the worker has no income.

**Resort island isolation.** Workers employed on resort islands face particular vulnerabilities. Physical isolation means they cannot easily access government services, legal assistance, or even other communities. Their continued presence on the island depends on the goodwill of the resort operator, creating a power imbalance that discourages complaints.

**Undocumented workers.** A significant population of migrant workers has fallen into undocumented status, either because their work visas were never properly processed by employers, because permits were allowed to expire, or because workers fled exploitative employers and lost their documented status in the process. These individuals are invisible to the regulatory system and acutely vulnerable to exploitation.

**Domestic workers.** Domestic workers, predominantly women from Sri Lanka and Bangladesh, work in private households where labour inspections are practically impossible. They may experience excessive working hours, confinement, non-payment of wages, and physical or sexual abuse with no witnesses and no practical means of complaint.

### Institutional Response and Gaps

The Anti-Human Trafficking Office, established under the law, is responsible for coordinating the national response. However, its effectiveness is constrained by the same capacity limitations that affect all Maldivian regulatory institutions. Labour inspections are infrequent, particularly outside Male'. Prosecutions under the trafficking act remain rare relative to the estimated scale of exploitation. Victim identification mechanisms are underdeveloped -- workers who escape exploitative conditions are often treated as immigration violators rather than trafficking victims.

The geographic dispersal of the workforce across hundreds of islands makes systematic monitoring effectively impossible with current resources. The regulatory architecture assumes a concentrated, accessible labour market. The reality is a workforce scattered across construction sites, resorts, fishing vessels, and private households on islands that may be hours from the nearest government office.

## 15.3 Gang Violence and Its Social Dimensions

### The Urban Phenomenon

Gang violence in the Maldives is overwhelmingly an urban phenomenon, concentrated in Greater Male' and to a lesser extent in Addu City. Male', with approximately 250,000 people compressed into 5.8 square kilometres, has one of the highest population densities in the world -- a physical environment that concentrates social pressures and provides the critical mass needed for organised criminal networks to form and sustain themselves.

### Structural Drivers

The gang phenomenon cannot be understood without reference to the social conditions that produce it:

**Housing crisis and overcrowding.** About 85 percent of Male's population lives in congested conditions, with multiple families sharing apartments designed for far fewer occupants.[^25] Young men in particular lack private space, pushing them into streets and public areas where gang recruitment occurs naturally. The World Bank (2014) found that 52 percent of young men and 46 percent of young women continue to live in their parents' homes after marriage, reflecting the impossibility of securing independent housing in Male'.

**Youth unemployment and underemployment.** While the overall unemployment rate is approximately 5.2 percent, youth populations account for over 75 percent of the unemployed.[^26] Many young people who have completed secondary education find that available jobs do not match their expectations or qualifications, while vocational training options are limited. The gap between aspirations and reality creates frustration that gangs exploit by offering income, status, and belonging.

**Family breakdown.** The Maldives' exceptionally high divorce rate (examined in detail in Section 15.4) means that a substantial proportion of young people grow up in reconstituted households or with reduced parental supervision. The ICSVE prison study found that among its sample of radicalised prisoners, 47.1 percent were divorced -- a rate that reflects broader patterns of family instability.[^27] Children who cycle between households of divorced and remarried parents may experience diminished attachment to family structures and increased vulnerability to peer recruitment by gangs.

**Drug market economics.** The gang ecosystem is sustained by the drug trade, particularly heroin and methamphetamine. Drug importation, distribution, and retail sales provide the economic foundation for gang operations. The NAP on P/CVE explicitly links gang culture and substance abuse to the growth of extremism, noting that "domestic extremism has risen, often alongside the culture of gangs and substance abuse."[^28]

### The Gang-Extremism Convergence

One of the most concerning developments documented in recent research is the convergence between gang networks and violent extremist organisations. The ICSVE prison radicalisation study found that prison extremist cells are "well funded and many being former gang leaders, work closely with gangs, organizing murders to silence their opponents as well as violent robberies to fund their activities."[^29] This convergence is not incidental. Both gangs and extremist cells operate through hierarchical command structures, enforce internal discipline through violence, derive income from illicit activities, and recruit from the same pool of marginalised, under-employed young men with grievances against the state.

In the Maldivian prison context, however, the relationship between gangs and extremists is complex. The ICSVE study notes that internationally, prison gangs can sometimes serve as a buffer against radicalisation because "prison gangs often maintain order and establish moral codes and behavior regulations to which prisoners must adhere; these codes and regulations are often incompatible with terrorist ideologies."[^30] In the Maldives, however, gangs "are often very supportive of militant jihadists and the two cooperate well together."[^31]

### Policy Responses

The Maldives Police Service Strategic Plan 2024-2028 addresses gang violence as part of a broader crime reduction strategy. The Plan's vision of "a safer community together" and its mission "to reduce crime and fear of crime by delivering trusted, human rights centred and collaborative policing services" reflect an awareness that enforcement alone is insufficient.[^32] The emphasis on community policing, crime prevention, and addressing "root causes of crime" acknowledges the social dimensions of gang violence.

However, effective gang intervention requires sustained investment in alternative pathways for young people -- employment programmes, vocational training, recreational facilities, mentoring, and family support services -- that are under-resourced across the board. The fundamental challenge is that gangs offer young men immediate material benefits, social belonging, status, and protection that the state has been unable to provide through legitimate channels.

## 15.4 Divorce -- Rates, Causes, and Socioeconomic Analysis

### Historical Context and Scale

The Maldives has historically had one of the highest divorce rates in the world, a distinction that has persisted despite decades of legal reform and social change. A commonly cited observation, reported by UNDP (2011), is that "a typical Maldivian woman is reported to marry three times by age 30."[^33] While this may overstate current patterns, it reflects a cultural reality in which divorce carries relatively little stigma and serial marriage is normalised.

Statistics from the Department of Judicial Administration (DJA, 2019) show that the highest divorce rates are among couples aged 26 to 39, with 58 percent of female and 59 percent of male divorcees in this age group. The next highest group is aged 18 to 25. The combined divorce rate for individuals aged 18 to 39 is 70 percent for both men and women.[^34]

### The Family Act (4/2000) and Legal Framework

The Family Act (Law No. 4/2000) was introduced specifically to address the high divorce rates of the late 1990s. It governs marriage, divorce, child custody, legitimacy, paternity, child support, and parental obligations. Key provisions include:

- **Marriage requirements:** Consent from both parties, witnesses, and adherence to Shariah principles. The minimum marriage age is 18, with exceptions.
- **Dower payments (mahar):** Mandatory payment from groom to bride as part of the marriage contract.
- **Divorce procedures:** Judicial intervention is required, prohibiting the informal extrajudicial divorces that were previously common. Husbands initiate divorce through the court, with mandatory reconciliation efforts. Women can seek divorce on grounds of indignity, violence, sinful compulsion, or prolonged abstinence.
- **Polygamy:** Men permitted up to four wives if financially capable.
- **Marriage Awareness Programme:** Article 85(h) of the Family Court Regulation mandates a programme on legal, religious, social, and economic aspects of marriage.[^35]

Before the Family Act, divorces could be performed through unilateral verbal statements by the husband -- a common practice in many Islamic communities that allowed impulsive dissolutions without judicial oversight or consideration of economic consequences.[^36] The Act's requirement for court-based processes initially reduced divorce rates, but they have subsequently remained high.

### Causes of Divorce: Research Findings

A 2024 qualitative study by Ahmed and Riyaz, published in the Maldives National Journal of Research, conducted in-depth interviews with eight divorced individuals (six women and two men) from the Greater Male' area, collectively representing 15 divorce cases. The study identified nine primary factors contributing to divorce:

**Immaturity and early marriage.** Most participants highlighted immaturity and lack of responsibility as key factors, noting that early marriages exacerbated these issues. Societal pressure pushes young people into marriage to escape crowded living conditions or to seek privacy from extended family cohabitation. As one participant described: "I imagined marriage was like a fairy tale, living in a beautiful house and having beautiful crockery.... Our first divorce happened because of my husband's immaturity."[^37]

**Infidelity.** The second most commonly cited factor. Participants described discovering affairs through phone evidence, confessions from other women, and observed behaviour.

**Incompatibility and poor communication.** Participants described fundamental differences in values, lifestyle expectations, and communication failures, often exacerbated by controlling behaviour from spouses.

**Financial instability.** High living costs in Greater Male' require both spouses to work, reducing time for intimacy and increasing family tensions. Financial burdens create stress between spouses and with extended families.

**Inadequate housing and in-law conflicts.** The housing crisis directly contributes to marital breakdown. One male participant described being "financially dependent and had nowhere to go after marriage. We initially stayed at a friend's place, then moved to my father's house but had to stay in the sitting room with no privacy."[^38] Extended family cohabitation in cramped conditions amplifies interpersonal conflicts.

**Domestic violence.** A 2004 survey found one in three women aged 15 to 49 had experienced physical or sexual abuse.[^39] Cultural norms viewing husbands as dominant contribute to domestic violence in some situations, and DV is frequently linked to divorce.

**Substance abuse.** Drug and alcohol use featured in multiple divorce accounts, consistent with the broader substance abuse crisis examined in Part VIII of this encyclopedia.

**Marrying for the wrong reasons.** Participants described marriages entered into primarily to escape family situations, achieve social expectations, or escape crowded living conditions, rather than genuine compatibility.

**Women's empowerment.** As women gain economic independence and education, they are increasingly unwilling to remain in unsatisfying or abusive marriages and are more likely to initiate divorce.[^40]

### Socioeconomic Consequences

The consequences of high divorce rates ripple across multiple social policy domains:

**Child welfare.** Children of divorced parents frequently face challenges in emotional, psychological, cognitive, and physical development. They may lose faith in marriage and perceive divorce as a normative solution to conflict rather than as a last resort. Communities with higher divorce rates experience elevated crime rates, "particularly among youth from broken families, who exhibit significantly higher rates of delinquency compared to those from intact families."[^41]

**Women's economic vulnerability.** Despite trends toward women's empowerment, divorce frequently leaves women in precarious economic situations, particularly when child custody responsibilities limit employment options. The lack of comprehensive childcare support systems and the inadequacy of child maintenance enforcement compound these vulnerabilities.

**Housing instability.** In Male's already desperate housing market, divorce creates immediate housing crises for one or both former spouses. The circular relationship between housing and divorce is evident: congested housing contributes to marital breakdown, while marital breakdown creates additional housing demand.

**Intergenerational transmission.** Research consistently shows that parental divorce increases the likelihood of divorce in children's own marriages, creating a self-reinforcing cycle across generations.

### Policy Gaps

Despite the scale and consequences of divorce in the Maldives, Ahmed and Riyaz (2024) note that "systematic studies on the high divorce rate in the Maldives are scarce," and "none of these studies addresses the root cause of the high divorce rate."[^42] The mandatory Marriage Awareness Programme under the Family Court Regulation exists in law but its effectiveness has not been systematically evaluated. Relationship counselling is not available as a public service in most islands. Premarital education programmes are absent or minimal. The policy response to divorce in the Maldives remains reactive rather than preventive, focused on managing the consequences of marital breakdown rather than addressing its structural causes -- congested housing, youth unemployment, inadequate family support systems, and the normalisation of serial marriage.

## 15.5 Human Rights Awareness and Education

### The "Rights Side of Life" Survey Series

The most comprehensive longitudinal data on human rights awareness in the Maldives comes from "The Rights Side of Life" (RSL) survey series, conducted jointly by the Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM) and UNDP. Three waves have been completed: RSL.1 in 2005, RSL.2 in 2011, and RSL.3 in 2019 (published 2020). The 2019 survey involved 1,160 respondents from 20 locations across seven regions, including Male', Addu, 13 outer islands, 4 resorts, and Thilafushi industrial island.[^43]

### Key Findings: Progress and Regression

The survey series reveals a mixed picture of progress and regression across different dimensions of human rights awareness:

**Awareness of HRCM.** Public awareness of the Human Rights Commission has increased significantly: from 40.8 percent in 2005 to 72.8 percent in 2011 to 84.8 percent in 2019. Migrant worker awareness of HRCM showed particularly notable growth, from 9.0 percent in 2011 to 41.2 percent in 2019. Women's awareness remained consistently high (84.9 percent in 2011, 87.0 percent in 2019), while men's awareness increased substantially from 63.7 percent to 80.7 percent over the same period.[^44]

**Declining HRCM approval.** Despite increased awareness, public satisfaction with HRCM has declined. The proportion of respondents rating HRCM as doing an "excellent or good job" fell from 39.7 percent in 2005 to 27.0 percent in 2019, with no significant difference between the 2011 (26.7 percent) and 2019 ratings. Meanwhile, 31.1 percent were dissatisfied and thought HRCM was "not doing a good job."[^45]

**Understanding of HRCM's functions.** Over a quarter (27 percent) of respondents were unable to define the role of HRCM. In 2011, 70.0 percent stated that HRCM's role was to protect human rights; by 2019, this had declined to 42.8 percent. Understanding of HRCM's monitoring role suffered a significant decline from 18.0 percent to 4.1 percent. Understanding of its investigative role dropped from 31.0 percent to 12.9 percent.[^46]

**Community awareness of human rights.** Only 13.4 percent of respondents reported high awareness of human rights, while 23.2 percent reported poor understanding. Women displayed greater confidence in their human rights awareness (60.5 percent reporting moderate awareness) compared to men (46.6 percent). Only 36.5 percent could name five human rights, a decline from 45.1 percent in 2011. However, the number of people unable to name any human rights decreased significantly from 44.5 percent in 2011 to 15.4 percent in 2019.[^47]

**Prioritisation of rights.** Respondents identified rights of the child as the most important human right from a list of twenty-one -- a considerable departure from 2011, when the right to access education was cited as paramount. Women's right to equality was identified as the second most important right (10.9 percent), followed by the right to education and adequate healthcare (10.5 and 10.1 percent respectively).[^48]

**Constitutional awareness.** 85.7 percent of respondents asserted the importance of knowing about constitutional rights and freedoms, with no significant variation across gender, geography, or type of local administration. A marked improvement was observed in people reporting having read the constitutional chapter on Fundamental Rights and Freedoms -- from 11.2 percent in 2011 to 20.2 percent in 2019. However, men (23 percent) were more likely to have read it than women (18 percent). 77.6 percent supported an initiative to provide copies of the Constitution to every household.[^49]

**Perceptions of crime.** 40.7 percent of people believed there had been a significant increase in crime, while the number believing crime had increased rose from 52.6 percent in 2011 to 60.0 percent in 2019. Urban residents (55.2 percent) reported higher perceptions of crime increase than outer island residents (31.7 percent). Most victims of crime identified by respondents were between 19 and 34 years old, and 49.6 percent of respondents reporting crime victimisation were from urban areas.[^50]

**Democratic reform attitudes.** Views on democratic success were evenly divided: 31.0 percent thought the democratic process was successful, 30.0 percent thought it was not, and 18.0 percent thought it had been "successful to some extent." Corruption (21.0 percent) and lack of trust in political parties (20.0 percent) were identified as the leading factors undermining democratic success.[^51]

### Human Rights Education Gaps

The survey data reveals a paradox: Maldivians are increasingly aware that human rights exist and that institutions like HRCM are mandated to protect them, but understanding of what specific rights are, how they function, and how to access redress mechanisms has not kept pace. The decline in understanding of HRCM's specific functions -- monitoring, investigation, complaint handling -- suggests that awareness has remained superficial. People know the institution exists but do not understand what it does.

Human rights education in schools remains limited and uneven. While the national curriculum includes elements of civic education, systematic human rights education that would equip young Maldivians to understand their constitutional rights, access complaint mechanisms, and participate meaningfully in democratic governance is not yet embedded across the education system. The geographic challenge is acute: while urban populations have greater access to civil society organisations, media, and public discourse on rights, outer island communities depend almost entirely on school-based education for rights literacy.

The 89.2 percent of respondents who called for HRCM to strengthen "creating awareness on human rights" and the 89.0 percent who called for "strengthening protection of the rights of vulnerable groups" indicate clear public demand for expanded rights education and institutional effectiveness.[^52]

## 15.6 Digital Governance and Social Service Delivery

### The Promise and Challenge of Digital Transformation

The Maldives' extreme geographic fragmentation -- approximately 187 inhabited islands scattered across 26 atolls spanning 90,000 square kilometres of ocean -- makes digital service delivery not merely convenient but essential. Physical access to government services requires sea or air travel that may be expensive, weather-dependent, and time-consuming. For populations on remote outer islands, a trip to Male' for a government service can consume days and cost a significant portion of monthly income.

### Progress in Digital Infrastructure

The Maldives has achieved relatively high internet penetration for a small island developing state. Mobile phone coverage extends to the vast majority of inhabited islands, and submarine cable connections have progressively improved bandwidth to the atolls. The National Social Protection Agency (NSPA) has developed digital systems for administering welfare transfers, including the Aasandha universal health insurance scheme, elderly care allowances, disability allowances, and single-parent benefits. These systems have reduced the need for in-person verification visits for some routine transactions.

Government online portals allow citizens to access certain services remotely, including application forms, status inquiries, and document submissions. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital adoption across government agencies, forcing the development of remote service delivery mechanisms that would otherwise have taken years to implement.

### Implementation Gaps

Despite progress, the digital transformation of social service delivery faces significant structural challenges:

**Digital literacy disparities.** Access to technology does not automatically translate into effective use. Older populations, less educated communities, and residents of remote islands may lack the digital literacy needed to navigate online service portals. Social protection beneficiaries -- who tend to be among the most vulnerable populations -- are precisely those least likely to possess the digital skills required to access online services.

**Last-mile connectivity.** While internet coverage is broad, the quality and reliability of connections on outer islands remains inconsistent. Service portals that function adequately on Male's broadband connections may be unusable on the intermittent, low-bandwidth connections available on some outer islands.

**Interoperability failures.** Government data systems were largely developed in silos, with different ministries and agencies using incompatible databases. The social protection system managed by NSPA, the child protection case management system, the health information system, and the police records system do not seamlessly share data. This means that a family receiving social welfare transfers, a child in the protection system, and a domestic violence case may exist in three separate databases with no automatic cross-referencing, resulting in fragmented service delivery and gaps in identification of vulnerable households.

**The continued necessity of in-person services.** Many social services inherently require human interaction. Child welfare assessments, domestic violence interventions, disability evaluations, and mental health support cannot be delivered through online portals. Digital systems can enhance administrative efficiency, but they cannot replace the social workers, counsellors, and case managers who must physically visit homes, assess situations, and provide support. The risk of over-investing in digital infrastructure while under-investing in human service capacity is real.

**Data protection concerns.** As government agencies digitise sensitive personal information -- health records, welfare status, protection orders, disability assessments -- the absence of comprehensive data protection legislation becomes a significant vulnerability. The consolidation of personal data across multiple government systems without adequate privacy safeguards raises concerns about misuse, unauthorised access, and discrimination.

### The Digital Divide as a Service Delivery Gap

The digital divide in the Maldives maps directly onto the island-urban divide examined in Section 15.8. Populations in Male' and major urban centres have access to both digital and in-person services. Populations on remote islands increasingly have access to digital systems but with lower quality connections and less digital literacy, while simultaneously experiencing reduced access to in-person services due to the withdrawal of government offices and staff from smaller islands. The result is that digital transformation, unless carefully managed, can widen rather than narrow the service delivery gap between urban and rural populations.

## 15.7 Social Work Professionalisation

### Workforce Shortages

Every section of this encyclopedia has identified the shortage of qualified social workers as a fundamental constraint on social service delivery. Whether the issue is child protection, domestic violence response, disability services, elder care, substance abuse treatment, or P/CVE interventions, the frontline reality is the same: too few workers, inadequately trained, poorly compensated, spread across too many islands, carrying caseloads that make meaningful engagement with vulnerable individuals and families impossible.

### The Pay Classification Framework

The National Pay Commission's 2023 classification of social service workers provides the most authoritative picture of how the profession is structured and compensated in the Maldivian public service. The classification falls within MISSCO Code 2635, Skill Level 4, and the Legal, Cultural & Social Service job family, Institutional Category 3. Critically, the document notes that "there is no mandatory license or training" required for any level of social service work.[^53]

The career ladder comprises the following tiers:

**Chief Social Services Officer / Chief Social Service Worker** (Public Service Rank 10):
- Basic salary: MVR 16,969 per month
- Job allowance: MVR 9,719 (Step 1) to MVR 12,635 (Step 3)
- Attendance benefit: MVR 263 daily
- Education: MNQF Level 10 in a related field with 4 years' experience, or MNQF Level 9 with 8 years' experience
- Core duties: Formulate national frameworks, oversee social protection implementation, identify emerging issues, coordinate with international organisations, promote treaty compliance[^54]

**Lead Social Service Worker / Lead Family Intervention Officer / Lead Children's Service Worker / Lead Foster Care Officer** (Public Service Rank 9):
- Basic salary: MVR 16,319 per month
- Job allowance: MVR 7,615 (Step 1) to MVR 9,899 (Step 3)
- Attendance benefit: MVR 206 daily
- Education: MNQF Level 10, or MNQF Level 9 with 4 years' experience[^55]

**Social Services Coordinator / Social Care Coordinator / Social Service Work Supervisor / Social Service Analyst** (Public Service Rank 8):
- Basic salary: MVR 14,126 per month
- Job allowance: MVR 6,592 (Step 1) to MVR 8,570 (Step 3)
- Attendance benefit: MVR 178 daily
- Education: MNQF Level 9, or MNQF Level 7/8 with 4 years' experience[^56]

The fields of study accepted for recruitment are broad, encompassing Social Services, Social Work, Psychology, Counselling, Sociology, Child Protection & Child Welfare, Child Youth & Family Practice, Marriage & Family Studies, Family Therapy, Behavioural Health Science, Juvenile Justice, and related disciplines.[^57]

### Structural Problems

**Absence of professional licensing.** The absence of mandatory licensing means there is no regulatory mechanism to ensure minimum competency standards, ongoing professional development, ethical compliance, or accountability for malpractice. Anyone with the requisite academic qualification can practise social work regardless of practical competency, supervised placement experience, or demonstrated ability to manage complex cases involving vulnerable populations.

**Compensation inadequacy.** While the salary figures appear reasonable in absolute terms, they must be assessed against the cost of living in Male' -- where most social workers are based -- and the stress, emotional burden, and personal risk associated with the work. Social workers handling child abuse cases, domestic violence interventions, and interactions with radicalized individuals or gang-affiliated families face occupational hazards that the compensation does not reflect. The result is chronic difficulty in recruitment and high turnover.

**Training pipeline.** The Maldives National University and a limited number of private institutions offer programmes in social work and related fields, but the output of qualified graduates is insufficient to meet demand. The requirement for MNQF Level 9 or 10 qualifications for senior positions means that many positions cannot be filled domestically, while international recruitment faces its own barriers of language, cultural competency, and willingness to work in remote island settings.

**Caseload pressure.** The ICSVE prison radicalisation study documented that social workers and case workers from the Ministry of Gender, Family, and Social Services "are overburdened and unable to devote a proper amount of time to each of the children and families whom they oversee."[^58] This is not a new finding. Every assessment of social service delivery in the Maldives over the past decade has identified the same problem: caseloads that make meaningful engagement impossible, leading to superficial check-ins, missed warning signs, and reactive rather than preventive intervention.

**Geographic distribution.** Social workers are concentrated in Male' and a handful of larger islands. Outer islands that host Family and Children's Service Centres (FCSCs) may have one or two social workers responsible for populations spread across multiple islands in an atoll, requiring sea travel for home visits and making emergency response impractical. Islands without FCSCs depend on council staff with no social work training.

### The Professionalisation Agenda

The path toward a professional social work system requires several concurrent reforms:

1. **Statutory regulation:** Establishment of a Social Work Registration Board or equivalent body with authority to set professional standards, maintain a register of qualified practitioners, require supervised practice placements as a condition of registration, and enforce ethical standards.

2. **Mandatory licensing:** Introduction of licensing requirements that go beyond academic qualifications to include supervised practice hours, continuing professional development, and periodic re-registration.

3. **Salary reform:** Adjustment of compensation to reflect the complexity, risk, and emotional burden of social work practice, particularly for frontline workers handling child protection, domestic violence, and other high-risk cases.

4. **Training expansion:** Significant investment in social work education programmes, including scholarships for students from outer atolls, practical placement arrangements across geographic areas, and specialised training modules in emerging areas such as P/CVE, trafficking, and digital safety.

5. **Workload management:** Establishment of maximum caseload standards based on case complexity, with staffing levels adjusted to meet these standards rather than staffing levels being used to determine what services can be provided.

## 15.8 Island-Urban Disparities in Service Access

### Geographic Barriers to Service Delivery

The Maldives' geography imposes a service delivery challenge that is unique in its scale and complexity. Of approximately 1,190 islands, 187 are inhabited, spread across 26 natural atolls (organised into 20 administrative atolls). The population ranges from Male' with approximately 250,000 people to islands with fewer than 200 residents. This extreme variation in population size, combined with geographic isolation, means that the per-capita cost of service delivery on small outer islands is many multiples of what it costs in Male' -- and yet the populations on those islands have the same constitutional entitlements to healthcare, education, justice, and social protection.

### Disparities Across Sectors

**Healthcare.** Male' concentrates the vast majority of specialist medical facilities, including Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) and private hospitals. Outer islands are served by health centres of varying quality, many of which lack even basic diagnostic equipment, medications, or qualified staff. Patients requiring specialist care must travel to Male' -- a journey that can take hours by speedboat or a day by ferry, depending on weather conditions. Emergency medical evacuations are available but weather-dependent and expensive.

**Education.** While primary education is available on most inhabited islands, secondary and higher secondary education is concentrated in larger islands and urban centres. Students from small islands must board in larger islands or Male' to continue their education beyond primary level, imposing financial and social costs on families. The quality of teaching varies dramatically between Male' schools and outer island schools, contributing to disparities in educational outcomes.

**Justice.** The Magistrate Court system provides basic judicial services in the atolls, but serious criminal cases, family law disputes, and appeals must be heard in Male'. Access to lawyers is extremely limited outside Male' and Addu. Victims of domestic violence, child abuse, or trafficking on outer islands face practical impossibility in accessing justice -- the physical distance, cost of travel, and absence of legal aid combining to make court-based remedies theoretical rather than practical for many.

**Policing.** The Maldives Police Service maintains stations across the atolls, but staffing levels on outer islands are minimal. The police response to serious incidents on small islands may involve officers travelling from another island, creating response delays that would be unacceptable in an urban context. Community policing -- identified as a priority in the MPS Strategic Plan 2024-2028 -- requires sustained police presence and community relationships that are difficult to maintain with minimal outer-island staffing.

**Social services.** The Family and Children's Service Centres (FCSCs) provide the primary social service delivery mechanism in the atolls, but their coverage is incomplete and their staffing is chronically inadequate. An FCSC social worker responsible for multiple islands in an atoll must travel by sea to conduct home visits, respond to emergencies, and follow up on cases -- a logistical challenge that limits the frequency and quality of contact with vulnerable families.

### The Male'-Centric Policy Trap

A persistent structural problem is that policy is designed in Male' by officials who work in Male' and whose daily experience is of Male's relatively well-resourced service environment. Policies that assume the availability of specialist staff, referral pathways, institutional coordination, and physical infrastructure that exist in Male' are systematically unrealistic when applied to an island with 500 residents, no resident social worker, no court, and a health centre staffed by a single community health worker. The "policy gap" between intention and implementation is thus partly a "geography gap" -- the distance between the Male'-based policymaker's assumptions and the outer-island frontline worker's reality.

### Population Consolidation and Its Discontents

Successive governments have pursued population consolidation as a response to the impossibility of providing full-service government on every inhabited island. The logic is straightforward: if it is not economically feasible to place a hospital, school, court, and police station on an island with 200 residents, then those residents should be encouraged or assisted to relocate to larger islands where services can be provided efficiently.

The reality is more complex. Island communities have deep historical, cultural, and economic attachments to their home islands. Fishing livelihoods depend on proximity to specific fishing grounds. Agricultural land is island-specific. Community identity, social networks, and kinship structures are place-based. Forced or incentivised relocation imposes significant social costs that are difficult to quantify but deeply felt by affected communities.

The alternative -- accepting that outer-island populations will receive lower-quality services -- conflicts with constitutional guarantees of equal rights and creates a two-tier citizenship that is politically unsustainable and ethically problematic. The Maldives has not yet found a satisfactory resolution to this fundamental tension.

## 15.9 Decentralisation and Its Impact on Social Services

### The Legal Framework

The Decentralisation Act (Law No. 7/2010), formally titled the Act on Decentralization of the Administrative Divisions of the Maldives, was enacted to give effect to the constitutional commitment to local self-governance. The Act establishes Atoll Councils and Island Councils as elected local government authorities (LGAs) with defined mandates for service delivery, development planning, and local governance. City Councils govern Male', Addu, and Fuvahmulah. Women's Development Committees (WDCs) were established through a 2010 amendment to ensure women's participation in local governance.[^59]

The Act was the product of the democratic reforms that followed the 2008 Constitution, reflecting a recognition that the highly centralised governance system inherited from decades of autocratic rule was incompatible with effective service delivery across a geographically dispersed nation. Decentralisation was intended to bring decision-making closer to communities, improve responsiveness to local needs, and create accountability mechanisms that the centralised system lacked.

### Council Mandates and Service Delivery

Island Councils are mandated to deliver a range of services at the local level, including:

- Preparation and implementation of island development plans
- Management of local infrastructure and public spaces
- Facilitation of social service delivery in coordination with central government
- Registration and civil documentation services
- Coordination with central government agencies including FCSCs
- Local dispute resolution and community safety

In practice, councils have become the primary point of contact between citizens and the state on many outer islands, particularly for social services. When a child protection concern arises, when a family needs welfare assistance, when domestic violence occurs -- it is often the island council that is first contacted, regardless of whether the council has the mandate, training, or resources to respond.

### The WDC Experience

The Assessment of Women's Development Committees (2015), conducted by Transparency Maldives, provides detailed evidence of the challenges facing local governance structures. WDCs were established to ensure women's participation in local decision-making and to address gender-specific community needs. However, the assessment revealed significant constraints:

**Relationship with Island Councils.** WDCs frequently reported difficult relationships with Island Councils, including exclusion from council meetings, lack of information sharing, and dismissal of WDC concerns. The structural arrangement -- in which WDCs are advisory rather than executive bodies -- limits their ability to influence resource allocation or service delivery decisions.[^60]

**Lack of budgetary authority.** WDCs have no independent budget and depend on Island Councils for operational funding. This financial dependence undermines their autonomy and makes their activities contingent on the goodwill of council members who may not prioritise gender issues.

**Training needs.** WDC members reported significant training needs across governance, project management, advocacy, and substantive policy areas. Many members lacked understanding of their legal mandate, the decentralisation framework, or the mechanisms available to influence local decision-making.[^61]

**Public engagement.** The level of community awareness of and engagement with WDCs varied significantly across islands, with some communities actively supporting WDC activities and others viewing them as peripheral or unnecessary.

### Fiscal Decentralisation Failures

The most fundamental constraint on decentralised service delivery is fiscal. While the Decentralisation Act transferred service delivery mandates to councils, the transfer of corresponding financial resources has been incomplete and inconsistent. Councils depend on central government block grants that are often insufficient to cover mandated services, arrive late, and are subject to political considerations. Councils have limited revenue-raising authority and the tax base on small islands is negligible.

The result is what might be termed "unfunded mandates" -- councils are legally responsible for services they cannot afford to deliver. This fiscal gap is most acute in social services, where the costs of qualified staff, travel between islands, and emergency response are high relative to available budgets. Councils faced with impossible choices between maintaining basic infrastructure (harbours, roads, water systems) and investing in social service capacity predictably prioritise the visible physical infrastructure.

### The Capacity Gap

Beyond fiscal constraints, councils face severe capacity limitations. Island Council members are elected, not appointed on the basis of technical qualifications. Council secretariats are small and staff turnover is high. The technical knowledge required to manage social service delivery -- understanding child protection protocols, domestic violence response procedures, disability assessment processes, or welfare eligibility criteria -- is specialised and cannot reasonably be expected of generalist council staff.

The gap between the decentralisation Act's aspiration and the reality of council capacity creates a paradox: the communities most in need of effective social services -- small, remote islands with limited economic opportunities and significant social challenges -- are precisely those whose councils have the least capacity to deliver them.

### Coordination Across Sectors

Decentralisation in the Maldives has produced a complex governance structure in which central government ministries, independent commissions (HRCM, Anti-Corruption Commission), autonomous agencies (NSPA, NCTC), atoll councils, island councils, city councils, and WDCs all have overlapping responsibilities for aspects of social service delivery. The coordination mechanisms between these layers are underdeveloped, leading to confusion about responsibilities, duplication of some functions, and gaps in others.

A domestic violence case on an outer island, for example, may involve the island council (first responder), the police (investigation), the FCSC (social worker assessment), the Ministry of Gender (policy oversight), the Family Court (protection orders), the NSPA (welfare support), and HRCM (rights monitoring) -- each operating with different protocols, reporting lines, and geographic constraints. The absence of integrated case management systems and clear inter-agency protocols means that the victim's experience of the system is fragmented, with each agency addressing a narrow slice of the situation and no single point of accountability for the overall response.

### Reforms Needed

Effective decentralisation of social services requires:

1. **Fiscal matching:** Ensuring that financial transfers to councils are sufficient to cover the actual costs of mandated social services, with formula-based allocations that account for geographic isolation, population dispersion, and social vulnerability indicators.

2. **Capacity building:** Sustained investment in council staff training, particularly in social service delivery, child protection, domestic violence response, and welfare administration.

3. **Minimum service standards:** Establishment of nationally mandated minimum standards for social service delivery that councils must meet, with central government support to enable compliance and accountability mechanisms for failure.

4. **Integration frameworks:** Development of clear inter-agency protocols and integrated case management systems that define responsibilities, establish referral pathways, and create accountability for outcomes across government layers.

5. **WDC strengthening:** Reform of the WDC structure to provide independent budgetary authority, clearer mandates, and stronger formal linkages to council decision-making processes.

## Conclusion

The nine issues examined in this chapter are not separate problems requiring separate solutions. They are manifestations of a common set of structural challenges: a small, geographically fragmented nation attempting to build comprehensive social service systems with limited fiscal resources, a thin human capital base, and governance structures that are still maturing after the democratic transition of 2008.

The young man in Male' who drops out of school, cannot find employment, joins a gang, develops a drug habit, is imprisoned, and encounters extremist recruitment in prison is not moving between separate policy domains. He is travelling a single pathway of cumulative disadvantage that the existing sectoral approach to social policy has failed to intercept at any point. The woman on an outer island who experiences domestic violence, cannot access police or courts, receives no social worker visit, and has no economic alternative to remaining in the abusive household is not failed by one system but by the simultaneous inadequacy of multiple systems that were never designed to work together.

The challenge for Maldivian social policy in the coming decade is not merely to strengthen individual sectoral systems -- though that is necessary -- but to build the connective tissue between them: integrated data systems, inter-agency protocols, cross-trained workforces, and governance structures that treat the vulnerable individual or family as a whole rather than as a collection of separate cases in separate systems. The structural reforms required -- professionalisation of social work, genuine fiscal decentralisation, digital integration of service delivery, and sustained investment in outer-island capacity -- are known. What remains is the political will and institutional commitment to implement them at the scale the challenge demands.
