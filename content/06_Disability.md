# Part 1: Historical Evolution and Policy Development


## 6.1 Historical Evolution of Disability Policy: From Confinement to Rights


### §6.13.1 The Pre-Legislative Era: Charity, Confinement, and Community Silence

The history of disability policy in the Maldives before the enactment of the Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010) reflects a pattern common across small island developing states: an absence of formal state policy, reliance on family and community systems of care, and where institutional provision did exist, a charitable and custodial orientation rather than a rights-based one.

For most of Maldivian history, persons with disabilities lived within extended family structures, relying on kin networks for care and support. In a society structured around close-knit island communities where extended families occupied collective living arrangements, this informal care model had genuine protective elements – persons with disabilities were embedded in social structures that provided daily care and social inclusion at the household level.[^121] However, this inclusion was conditional, partial, and invisible to formal systems. It conferred no rights, set no standards for care quality, and provided no protection when family circumstances changed – through death of caregivers, migration to Malé for employment, or family breakdown.

For persons with severe physical, intellectual, or psychosocial disabilities whose needs exceeded what informal family care could provide, the response was largely one of social confinement. Such persons were kept within household boundaries, often not attending school or participating in community life, with their condition understood through religious and folk frameworks rather than medical or rights-based ones.[^122] The concept of disability as a social category warranting state policy attention did not exist in formal terms until the late twentieth century.

The Islamic context of Maldivian society shaped attitudes toward disability in complex ways. On one hand, Islamic teaching emphasises care for the vulnerable and the religious obligation of family members to support those unable to care for themselves – providing a normative framework that made informal family care a moral duty rather than an optional arrangement.[^123] On the other hand, disability was sometimes understood through frameworks of divine will or spiritual causation that, while not necessarily stigmatising in intent, could discourage engagement with medical or social interventions. The intersection of Islamic values and disability attitudes in the Maldives remains understudied, though the UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis notes that community and religious leaders represent an underutilised resource for disability inclusion advocacy.[^124]

### §6.13.2 Reehendi: The Institutional Care Model and Its Legacy

The most significant pre-2010 institutional response to disability was the Reehendi residential shelter, which represented the primary formal state provision for persons with severe disabilities for decades. Reehendi functioned as a congregate care facility for persons with physical and intellectual disabilities who could not be supported in family settings, providing residential accommodation, basic care, and limited skills activities.[^125]

Reehendi embodied the charitable-custodial model of disability provision: persons with disabilities were recipients of care rather than rights-holders, and the institution's role was to manage and contain rather than to support community inclusion or independent living. Reehendi's legacy is ambivalent – it represented the state's willingness to take responsibility for persons with severe disabilities who might otherwise have been entirely without support, but it also represented the institutionalisation of disability as a category of persons to be housed separately from mainstream society rather than included within it.[^126]

The establishment of ADDA (Agency for Disability and Different Ability) and its subsequent reforms have not fully replaced the institutional care model. As of 2025, elements of congregate care continue within ADDA's service architecture, and the transitional care functions that Reehendi historically performed have been distributed but not transformed.[^127] The 2025 Situational Analysis found that no strategic plan exists for transitioning from institutionally-oriented disability provision to a community-based independent living model – a gap that represents a fundamental departure from Article 19 of the CRPD.

### §6.13.3 The Road to the Disability Act (2010): Advocacy, International Norms, and Political Moment

The Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010) emerged from a combination of disability advocacy by emerging civil society organisations, increasing engagement with international disability rights norms following Maldives' ratification of the CRPD in 2009, and a broader democratic opening in which rights-based legislation across multiple social domains was being developed.[^128]

Maldives ratified the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities on 5 April 2009 – an early ratification that was at least partially symbolic, reflecting the government's stated commitment to international human rights standards during the post-2008 democratic transition period, without the administrative infrastructure necessary for implementation.[^129] The Optional Protocol to the CRPD, which enables individual communications and inquiries to the CRPD Committee, has not been ratified by Maldives as of 2025, limiting the international accountability mechanism available to persons with disabilities who have exhausted domestic remedies.

The Disability Act (2010) represented a genuine legislative advance. It established: a broad definition of disability; the right to equality and non-discrimination; the right to education, healthcare, employment, and social protection on an equal basis with non-disabled persons; obligations for physical accessibility of public buildings; and the establishment of the Disability Council as a coordinating body. However, from its inception, the Act had structural limitations – it contained programmatic language without enforcement mechanisms, timelines, or dedicated budgetary allocations. It established rights without remedies, and coordination structures without operational capacity.[^130]

The Act's definition of disability drew from a mixed model – combining medical diagnostic categories with a recognition of barriers-based impairment – but did not adopt the purely social model articulated in the CRPD's preamble, which recognises disability as arising from the interaction between persons with impairments and attitudinal and environmental barriers.[^131] This definitional tension has persisted through subsequent policy instruments, contributing to a disability system that responds primarily to diagnosed medical conditions rather than to the full spectrum of functional limitations recognised by the Washington Group methodology.

### §6.13.4 The 2010–2019 Implementation Decade: Institutional Growth Without System Change

The decade between the Disability Act and the major policy reforms of 2019 was one of gradual institutional development without fundamental change in disability policy architecture. The key developments of this period include:

**Disability Allowance Establishment**: The Disability Allowance (Regulation 2011/R-3) was established as a monthly cash transfer of MVR 2,000 for persons with severe disabilities and MVR 1,000 for those with moderate disabilities. Administration was through the National Social Protection Agency (NSPA) and later the Social Protection Administration (SPA). The tiered structure created administrative complexity and perverse incentives, as persons with disabilities had an interest in receiving a severe disability classification to access the higher allowance rate.[^132]

**ADCC to ADDA Transition**: The Adult Disability Care Centre (ADCC) was restructured as the Agency for Disability and Different Ability (ADDA), with an expanded mandate that included awareness-raising, assistive device provision, vocational training, and limited community outreach in addition to residential care functions. This restructuring was significant in naming terms – reflecting a shift from a care orientation to an inclusion orientation – but did not fundamentally transform the operational model.[^133]

**NAPDI v1–v5**: Successive iterations of the National Action Plan for Disability Inclusion (NAPDI) were developed during this period, establishing the structure of disability policy planning in Maldives. These plans articulated goals and strategies across education, employment, health, and social protection, but were consistently criticised for lack of measurable targets, inadequate budgetary commitments, and weak coordination across implementing ministries.[^134]

**Inclusive Education Policy (2013)**: The Ministry of Education introduced an Inclusive Education Policy in 2013 that set out a framework for integrating children with disabilities into mainstream schools, providing the basis for SEN teacher recruitment and training. Implementation was uneven, particularly in outer atolls, but the policy established an institutional infrastructure for inclusive education that would be codified in the Education Act (2021).[^135]

**NDR Development**: Work on the National Disability Register (NDR) began during this decade, though the registry was not formally launched until January 2023. The extended development period reflected the governance complexity of creating a cross-ministry data system and establishing the disability assessment and classification protocols that would determine eligibility.[^136]

### §6.13.5 The 2019 Reforms: Employment Disincentive Removal and Allowance Standardisation

The year 2019 brought two policy reforms that, while individually specific, had significant structural implications. First, the Disability Allowance was standardised to MVR 2,000 per month for all registered persons with disabilities regardless of severity classification – simplifying the system, increasing support for those previously in the moderate category, and removing the incentive to seek severe disability classification.[^137]

More fundamentally, the government confirmed in 2019 that the Disability Allowance would not be reduced or withdrawn when a person with disabilities entered formal employment. This removed a poverty trap that had been identified by disability advocates and researchers as a primary structural barrier to labour market participation. The previous structure – in which gaining employment risked reducing or eliminating the allowance – created a rational incentive to remain outside formal employment, particularly when available jobs paid wages insufficient to fully replace the lost allowance income.[^138]

These 2019 reforms illustrated a pattern that characterises disability policy evolution in Maldives: meaningful change is achievable when the policy problem is clearly defined, the reform is administratively manageable, and there is aligned political will. The broader challenge is extending this pattern of targeted improvement to the more complex structural reforms required by CRPD compliance – in legal capacity, community living, accessibility standards, and systematic data collection – where the administrative and political obstacles are substantially greater.

## 6.2 The 2010 Baseline: From Where the System Started


### §6.57.1 Historical Data Trajectory

The HRCM/UNDP 2010 Baseline Assessment on Activities Addressing Rights of Persons with Disabilities provides the most comprehensive picture of what disability provision looked like at the time of CRPD ratification. Comparing the 2010 baseline with the 2025 situation reveals what has changed – and what has not.

**Historical disability prevalence data** from the Baseline:
| Year | Survey | Count | % of population |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1981 | First disability screening | 1,390 | 0.9% |
| 2000 | Population and Housing Census | 4,728 | 1.75% |
| 2001–2002 | Velezine/MGFDSS survey | 9,216 | 3.4% |
| 2009 | HI disability screening survey | – | 8.1% (all disabilities) / 4.7% (severe permanent) |
| 2022 | Census WG-SS | 24,401 | 6.9% |

The trajectory reveals a consistent picture of previous undercounting: as measurement methods improved – from simple screening to survey-based assessment to standardised WG-SS methodology – disability prevalence estimates rose from 0.9% to 6.9%. This is not a real increase in disability; it is an improvement in measurement.[^556]

### §6.57.2 The 2010 Service Baseline: How Far the System Has Come

The 2010 Baseline documented a disability service system that was far more rudimentary than today's:

**Education**: Only 230 children with disabilities were enrolled in schools in 2009, across 11 special schools – 127 in Malé and 103 in atolls. The estimated number of children with disabilities nationally was 2,250 (EDC 2009) – meaning approximately 10% enrollment and 90% exclusion. This compares with the current SEN-teacher-in-every-school framework and 91-97% overall school attendance among children with disabilities (though with high domain-specific out-of-school rates as documented in §6.54).[^557]

**Healthcare**: In 2010, there were only 2 psychiatrists in Maldives – both working at IGMH's outpatient facility. There were no psychiatrists in regional hospitals and no mental health clinic. The Home for People with Special Needs (Guraidhoo) had 173 beds (119 psychiatric, 46 geriatric) with 120 staff – of whom only USS 21 per staff per year was invested in training.[^558]

**Financial assistance**: The blind persons allowance – the only disability-specific financial assistance in 2010 – had just been raised to MVR 1,500 (by 2009 HRCM advocacy), after being MVR 500 for years. Only 220 persons registered as totally or legally blind were receiving this. No general disability allowance existed.[^559]

**Assistive devices** (2008–2009): 90 wheelchairs provided; 45 hearing aids distributed (dropping to 23 by end of 2009); 488 spectacle pairs provided. These quantities, for a disability population estimated at 8.1%, represent severe under-provision.[^560]

**Sign language**: The first Maldivian Sign Language Dictionary (containing signs for approximately 650 words) was produced on 28 October 2009 – just 15 years ago. The Maldives Deaf Association was established in 2008 and officially launched on 27 October 2009 with 58+ members.[^561]

### §6.57.3 What the 2010 Baseline Got Right: The Unresolved Gaps

The HRCM/UNDP 2010 Baseline identified 13 service gaps that remain substantially unresolved in 2025:

**Early identification and intervention**: The 2010 Baseline found no evidence of early detection initiatives and identified the absence of early intervention services as a critical gap, noting that maximum developmental benefit occurs in the first three years. In 2025, early intervention services remain largely absent outside Malé – with only MIA providing structured early intervention for autism in the capital. The first 1000 days are still largely unserved for children with disabilities in outer atolls.[^562]

**Transition planning**: The 2010 Baseline noted the absence of transition planning from school to secondary, vocational, or tertiary education. In 2025, transition planning remains absent from the SEN framework, and the higher education gap (tertiary education without disability support at MNU and MIT) persists.[^563]

**Disadvantages for outer atoll residents**: The 2010 Baseline specifically identified that all procedures required Malé travel, all certifications required Malé doctors, and no psychiatrists or physiotherapy services existed in regional hospitals. The geographic equity gap identified in 2010 remains the defining structural failure of the disability system in 2025.[^564]

**Coverage of disability benefits**: The 2010 Baseline identified the inequity of disability financial assistance – only the legally blind received support – and called for standardised criteria across disability types. The 2019 standardisation of the disability allowance to MVR 2,000 for all registered persons partially addressed this, but the 25.6% coverage rate (Hameed et al., 2022) means that the fundamental access gap persists.[^565]

**Workplace**: The 2010 survey of Haa Alifu and Haa Dhaalu atolls found that 71% of working-age men with disabilities and 93% of working-age women with disabilities were not in work – a near-total employment exclusion in these atoll communities. The national Census 2022 employment-to-population ratio of 31% for persons with disabilities, while an improvement, shows that employment exclusion remains severe.[^566]

**Assistive device access** in 2008: 72% of persons with hearing difficulties had no hearing aids; 54% of males and 89% of females with vision difficulty had no spectacles; 76% of men and 81% of women with walking difficulties had no wheelchair or tricycle; 47% of persons with vision difficulties had no sight tests. These figures from 15 years ago establish a baseline of near-total unmet assistive technology need. No equivalent national assistive technology access survey has been conducted since to measure progress.[^567]

# Part 2: Legal Framework and the Disability Act


## 6.3 Disability Act 2010 and Implementing Regulations


### The Protection of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Provision of Financial Assistance Act (Act 8/2010)

The Disability Act was enacted in 2010, the same year the Maldives ratified the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD). It defines disability as "persons who have long-term physical, mental, intellectual or sensory impairments which in interaction with various barriers may hinder their full and effective participation in society on an equal basis with others" – a framing broadly aligned with the CRPD's social model.[^17]

The Act's provisions are extensive. It covers:

- **Legal rights**: Assistance to ensure legal rights, access to public infrastructure and transportation, health services and transportation
- **Equal opportunities**: Participation in employment and education, protection from violence and exploitation
- **Social and cultural participation**: Access to social and cultural activities, participation during disasters and emergencies
- **Media representation**: Representation on media
- **Gender-specific provisions**: Specific articles related to women and children with disabilities
- **Financial provisions**: Allowance and equipment for persons with disabilities
- **Registry**: Establishment of a National Disability Registry to facilitate services and special assistance[^18]

The Act also mandated the formation of a **Disability Council** – a monitoring body situated under the Ministry responsible for disability affairs, tasked with monitoring state institutions, proposing recommendations, and resolving issues. However, the Council's current composition includes only **one member with a disability**, elected to represent the entire disability community – a provision widely criticised as inadequate.[^19]

### Implementing Regulations

Several key regulations have been formulated under the Act:

1. **Regulation on the Protection of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Providing Financial Assistance (2011/R-3)**: Defines the parameters of individuals who can be registered and the categories of disabilities, including blind or visually impaired persons, deaf persons or persons with speech impairment, persons with physical disabilities, persons with intellectual disabilities or brain injury, persons with psychosocial disabilities, persons with autism, persons with learning disabilities, and persons with multiple disabilities.[^20]

2. **Regulation on Determination and Registry of Persons with Disabilities (2021/R-54)**: Updated criteria for registration in the NDR.[^21]

3. **Minimum Standards for Accessibility (2013/R-557)**: Covers accessibility to infrastructures of education institutions, health facilities, therapy centres, and government buildings housing people under state care. Notably, this regulation has a narrow scope – it does not cover all public buildings, private establishments, or transportation infrastructure.[^22]

4. **Regulation on Providing Employment Opportunities to Persons with Disabilities (2024/R-9)**: The most recent regulation, establishing a separate registry of persons with disabilities seeking job opportunities and mandating the development of competency profiles. It requires employers to assess needs and make reasonable accommodations. A technical committee under MSFD monitors implementation, but the regulation does not mandate the inclusion of persons with disabilities on this committee.[^23]

### Policy on Allowance for Persons with Disabilities (2023)

A subsequent policy document further guides the allowance structure, detailing the tiered assistance system:

| Allowance | Amount (per month) |
|---|---|
| Basic allowance | MVR 2,000 |
| General additional allowance | MVR 1,000 |
| Carer allowance | MVR 2,000 |
| Self-care allowance | MVR 1,000 |
| Allowance for households with 3+ persons with disability (per additional person) | MVR 1,000 |

*Source: Policy for Allowance for Persons with Disabilities, 2023.*[^24]

All persons in the NDR receive the basic and general additional allowance, totalling MVR 3,000 (USD 194.55) per month. Additional allowances are provided based on the person's ability to perform tasks independently. The maximum monthly allowance is **MVR 7,000 (USD 453.96)**, equivalent to the minimum wage for public sector employees.[^25]

### Gaps Between Law and Implementation

Despite the comprehensive legal framework, implementation gaps are severe. The HRCM's 2023 CRPD Shadow Report documented significant issues in enforcing the Act's provisions, noting "incongruence between key legislations, and the regulations, policies or guidelines formulated to aid implementation" as "a critical barrier predominant in many areas."[^26] For example, despite being accorded equal marriage rights in law, there are no regulations or policies to ensure accessibility of the marriage process for persons with disabilities.[^27]

The Mental Health Policy (2021-2025), while formulated to address psychosocial disabilities, has not been backed by a Mental Health Act – leaving a critical legislative gap. Mental health professionals consistently highlight the urgent need for such legislation to provide the necessary structure and framework for quality service delivery.[^28]

## 6.4 The Disability Act Review (2025): Stakes, Process, and Reform Priorities


### §6.28.1 The Review Announcement and Its Significance

The announcement at the CRPD Committee's 33rd Session in August 2025 that Maldives is undertaking a comprehensive participatory review of the Disability Act (2010) represents the most significant disability policy development of the reporting period. The review provides an opportunity to address, in a single legislative reform, the most significant structural deficiencies that have accumulated since the Act's enactment fifteen years ago.[^276]

The review was explicitly described as "participatory" – suggesting a process that will involve OPDs, persons with disabilities, and civil society, not merely government actors. The HRCM, which has monitoring responsibility under Article 33(2) and which produced the Shadow Report that contributed to the CRPD Committee review, has a critical role in ensuring that the review process meets participatory standards.[^277]

### §6.28.2 Priority Reform Areas

Based on the evidence assembled across this chapter – from the CRPD Periodic Report, the UNPRPD Situational Analysis, the HRCM Shadow Report, the NAPDI evidence cross-reference, and the statistical and qualitative research record – the following represent the most critical areas for legislative reform in the Disability Act review:

**Supported Decision-Making**: Replace guardianship-based substituted decision-making with a supported decision-making framework consistent with CRPD Article 12 and CRPD General Comment No. 1. This is the most fundamental legal reform required – it is not possible to comply with the Convention while maintaining a legal capacity framework that extinguishes the right of persons with disabilities to make their own decisions.[^278]

**Accessibility Standards and Enforcement**: Establish binding, time-bound, enforceable accessibility standards for public buildings, transport infrastructure, and information and communication technology. The existing Act's aspirational accessibility language, after fifteen years without implementation, must be replaced by standards with compliance timelines, an audit mechanism, and a designated enforcement authority.[^279]

**Anti-Discrimination Enforcement**: Establish an effective disability discrimination complaints mechanism – with investigation authority, remedies, and deterrent penalties – applicable to both public sector and private sector actors. The current framework's non-enforcement of discrimination prohibitions makes them effectively unenforceable symbolic statements.[^280]

**Community Living**: Establish a legal framework for personal assistance services and community-based disability support, consistent with Article 19's requirements. This framework should specify funding obligations, service standards, and an implementation timeline for extending personal assistance beyond Malé.[^281]

**OPD Participation**: Embed mandatory OPD participation in policy development, implementation monitoring, and legislative review processes. This should include structural mechanisms – OPD representation on the Disability Council, OPD participation in NAPDI development and monitoring, and OPD inclusion in budget planning – not merely consultative invitations.[^282]

**Data Rights**: Establish a legal obligation for disability-disaggregated outcome data collection across education, health, employment, and social protection systems. This should include a requirement for the NDR to collect and publish outcome data, and for the Statistics Compendium to include disability outcome indicators in its annual reporting.[^283]

### §6.28.3 Risks to the Review Process

The review's potential carries corresponding risks. Previous policy reform processes in Maldives – including the several iterations of NAPDI – have produced detailed frameworks without the enforcement mechanisms and budgetary commitments needed for implementation. There is a risk that the Disability Act review produces an updated text that is technically more comprehensive than the 2010 Act but is not accompanied by the regulatory development, budgetary allocation, and institutional capacity required for implementation.[^284]

The political economy of disability policy reform in Maldives – as in many small states – involves limited fiscal space, competing policy priorities, and the vulnerability of disability policy to deprioritisation when economic or security pressures intensify. The success of the review will depend not just on the quality of the legislative text but on the political commitment to the sustained implementation effort that comprehensive reform requires.[^285]

The engagement of the CRPD Committee – which will receive Maldives' response to its Concluding Observations in the next reporting cycle – provides a continuing external accountability mechanism. Civil society's capacity to use this mechanism – submitting shadow reports, engaging with treaty body processes, and translating CRPD Concluding Observations into domestic advocacy – will be a critical factor in determining whether the review's outcomes are implemented or remain on paper.[^286]

## 6.5 Legal Capacity, Guardianship, and Access to Justice for Persons with Disabilities


### §6.19.1 Legal Capacity Gaps

The Maldivian legal capacity framework for persons with disabilities is incompatible with CRPD Article 12. Under existing civil law, persons with intellectual disabilities or severe psychosocial disabilities may be placed under guardianship arrangements that extinguish their right to make independent decisions regarding property, marriage, contracts, medical treatment, and movement. These arrangements are family-controlled, lack systematic judicial oversight, are not subject to periodic review, and provide no effective right of appeal.[^200]

The CRPD Committee's General Comment No. 1 (2014) on Equal Recognition Before the Law is unambiguous: all persons with disabilities enjoy legal capacity on an equal basis with others in all aspects of life. The Convention requires that states replace substituted decision-making systems (guardianship) with supported decision-making frameworks – arrangements that respect the person's autonomy, will, and preferences while providing support for decision-making where needed.[^201]

No legislative reform of the guardianship framework has been undertaken in Maldives during the 2019–2025 reporting period. The State Party's CRPD Periodic Report acknowledges this incompatibility and commits to engaging with the Law Reform Commission and Attorney General's Office to develop a supported decision-making framework as a legislative priority for 2026–2028. For persons with disabilities currently under guardianship arrangements, this timeline means a further period of legally-enforced incapacity without the rights protections the Convention requires.[^202]

The ongoing review of the Disability Act (2010) – announced at the CRPD Committee's 33rd Session in August 2025 as a comprehensive participatory review – provides a vehicle for introducing supported decision-making provisions within the disability law framework. However, the more fundamental reform of civil law guardianship provisions requires amendment across multiple legislative instruments and engagement with the judiciary, creating a more complex reform pathway.[^203]

### §6.19.2 Access to Justice: Courts, Procedures, and the Reporting Gap

Access to justice for persons with disabilities in Maldives is constrained by physical, procedural, and informational barriers at every stage of the justice process. Court buildings are not systematically accessible to persons with physical disabilities. MSL interpretation is not routinely available at court proceedings for deaf parties or witnesses. Braille or accessible-format court documents are not produced. Legal aid under the Legal Aid Act (2016) does not include disability-specific provisions or outreach.[^204]

The geographic concentration of courts – in Malé and atoll capital islands – creates a compounding barrier for persons with disabilities in small outer islands. A person with a disability in a small outer island who has experienced a crime, contract dispute, or family matter requiring court proceedings must travel to the nearest court (often the atoll capital), navigate an inaccessible court building, participate in proceedings without sign language interpretation or other procedural accommodation, and either stay in the atoll capital or return and make repeated journeys.[^205]

The most troubling justice gap is the zero-prosecution record for disability-based discrimination or abuse of persons with disabilities. The State Party's CRPD Periodic Report notes that no conviction records exist for disability-based discrimination, which may reflect the systemic under-reporting of violence and abuse against persons with disabilities, rather than an absence of such abuse. The UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis and the HRCM Shadow Report 2023 both document elevated risks of exploitation and abuse for persons with disabilities – particularly women and children with disabilities – without these cases reaching the formal justice system at any significant rate.[^206]

### §6.19.3 Liberty and the Involuntary Hospitalisation Question

The Maldivian legal framework permits involuntary hospitalisation of persons with "mental illness" under health and public order frameworks. The CRPD Committee has clarified through General Comment No. 1 and its 2015 Guidelines on Article 14 that disability – including psychosocial disability – may never serve as a justification for deprivation of liberty. The State Party's CRPD Periodic Report acknowledges that these provisions have not been reviewed for compatibility with Article 14 and commits to review as part of the mental health legislative reform process.[^207]

The mental health legislative reform process referenced in this commitment is ongoing as of 2025 but has not produced enacted legislation. The Mental Health Act has not been passed in a form that addresses the CRPD's supported decision-making and anti-coercion requirements. Until this reform is completed, persons with psychosocial disabilities in Maldives remain potentially subject to liberty deprivation on disability grounds – in violation of their Convention rights.[^208]

# Part 3: Disability Prevalence and Data


## 6.6 Disability Prevalence in the Maldives: Census and Survey Data


Understanding disability prevalence in the Maldives requires navigating multiple data sources that use different definitions, methodologies, and coverage criteria. The result is a fragmented statistical picture, though the broad contours are becoming clearer with each successive data exercise.

### Census 2022: The Most Comprehensive Count

The 2022 Population and Housing Census represents the most complete attempt to measure disability in the Maldives. For the first time, the Census adopted the Washington Group Short Set on Functioning (WG-SS) – an internationally standardised instrument that measures functional difficulty across six domains (seeing, hearing, walking, cognition, self-care, and communication) rather than relying on medical diagnosis.[^1] The Census revealed that a total of **24,401 persons with disabilities** resided in the Maldives, constituting approximately **7% of the population**.[^2]

Disaggregation of this figure shows important patterns:

| Category | Number | Share |
|---|---|---|
| Women | 13,456 | 55% |
| Men | 10,945 | 45% |
| Children (5–17 years) | 3,401 | 14% |
| Youth (15–24 years) | 1,695 | 7% |
| Residing in Male' | 8,453 | 35% |
| Residing in atolls | 15,948 | 65% |

*Source: Maldives Bureau of Statistics, Census 2022 Infographic.*[^3]

A greater number of women were found to be living with a disability, yet they were more likely to have a "mild" or "moderate" disability, whereas men were more likely to have multiple disabilities.[^4] The geographic split is significant: nearly two-thirds of persons with disabilities live in the atolls, far from the concentration of specialist services in Male'.

### Limitations of the Census Data

The WG-SS has a well-known limitation: it was not designed to capture intellectual and psychosocial disabilities adequately, as the enhanced version of the tool (WG-ES) would be required for that purpose.[^5] A nationwide survey conducted by the Ministry of Health in 2003 had found that 29.1% of respondents reported living with a mental health condition (including both neurological and psychosocial conditions), but no updated formal data is available.[^6] The National Drug Use Survey 2011/2012 found that one-third of people living with drug addiction reported a mental health condition.[^7] These figures suggest the Census significantly undercounts psychosocial and intellectual disabilities.

### The National Disability Registry vs. Census

Despite the Census counting 24,401 persons with disabilities, only **13,656 persons** were registered in the National Disability Registry (NDR) as of December 2024.[^8] This discrepancy – the registry captures barely 56% of the Census count – is explained by multiple factors: lack of awareness about the registration process, barriers in the application system, stigma, and the exclusion of "high functioning" individuals from the registry because it is tied to the disbursement of financial assistance.[^9]

The NDR, maintained by NSPA and overseen by the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MSFD), breaks down registered persons by disability type:

| Disability Type | Women | Men | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Autism | 122 | 462 | 584 |
| Hearing and speech disability | 907 | 813 | 1,720 |
| Intellectual disability | 852 | 1,102 | 1,954 |
| Learning disability | 18 | 34 | 52 |
| Multiple disabilities | 1,045 | 1,916 | 2,961 |
| Physical disability | 1,723 | 2,189 | 3,912 |
| Psychological disability | 603 | 896 | 1,499 |
| Visual disability | 460 | 514 | 974 |
| **Grand Total** | **5,730** | **7,926** | **13,656** |

*Source: Ministry of Social and Family Development, December 2024.*[^10]

The gender ratio in the registry differs markedly from the Census: the NDR shows more men (58%) than women (42%), whereas the Census found more women (55%). This suggests women face greater barriers to registration – a finding corroborated by the Hameed et al. (2022) study discussed below.

### The 2019 HIES Disability Module

The 2019 Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES) included disability questions using the WG-SS methodology. It found an overall disability prevalence of **9% nationally**, with higher rates among women and in the atolls compared to Male'.[^11] Using the HIES data, 8,948 persons were identified in administrative records at the time.[^12] The HIES also established baseline data for comparing disability prevalence across geographic areas and socioeconomic strata.

### The DHS 2009 and 2016-17

The 2009 Maldives Demographic and Health Survey – the first nationally representative DHS – included disability questions, though using older methodological approaches.[^13] The 2016-17 DHS provided updated data on demographic and health indicators but disability measurement remained limited.[^14] Neither survey achieved the coverage or methodological rigour of the later Census and HIES exercises using the WG-SS.

### Disability Allowance Coverage Study (Hameed et al., 2022)

A landmark mixed-methods study by Hameed, Banks, Usman, and Kuper (2022) found that only **25.6% of people with disabilities** receive the Disability Allowance, with lowest coverage among women, older adults, those in Male', and those with sensory impairments.[^15] Barriers to uptake included lack of information about the allowance, perceptions of disability that excluded milder conditions, eligibility criteria that were poorly understood, geographic factors requiring travel to Male' for specialist assessments, and stigma. This study, published in *Global Social Policy*, remains the most rigorous assessment of disability allowance coverage in the Maldives.

### The Incheon Strategy Indicators

The Maldives reported on Incheon Strategy Goal 8 indicators in 2015, focused on improving the reliability and comparability of disability data, including targets on disability prevalence statistics, baseline data tracking, and disaggregated data availability for women and girls with disabilities.[^16] Progress on these indicators has been uneven: the Census 2022 was a major step forward, but administrative data systems remain fragmented.

## 6.7 Census 2022 Disability Analysis: Deep Statistical Profile


### §6.53.1 Methodology and Publication

The authoritative disability statistical reference for Maldives is the Census 2022 disability analysis produced by the Maldives Bureau of Statistics (MBS) – written by Fathimath Riyaza, checked by Aishath Shahuda, and proof-read by Professor Lakshman Dissanayake. Preliminary results were published 30 March 2023; final results launched 11 July 2023. The analysis applies Washington Group Short Set on Functioning (WG-SS) methodology across six functional domains: seeing, hearing, walking, cognition (remembering), self-care, and communication.[^517]

The WG-SS approach measures functional difficulty rather than medical diagnosis, capturing persons who experience "a lot of difficulty" or "cannot do at all" across these six domains. This methodology produces a 6.9% national disability prevalence (24,401 persons in the resident Maldivian population aged 5 and above) – approximately 1 in every 10 individuals – making the 2022 Census the most comprehensive disability measurement Maldives has undertaken.[^518]

The analysis was supported by UNFPA, UNICEF, UNDP, ADB, World Bank, PARIS21, and IOM – a broad international partnership that reflects the methodological significance of the exercise and provides a benchmark for subsequent data collection investments.[^519]

### §6.53.2 Prevalence by Gender, Location, and Age

**Gender distribution**: Women have slightly higher disability prevalence overall (7.8% vs. 6.1% for men), accounting for 55% of the disability population (13,456 women, 10,945 men). However, this female numerical dominance reflects age-structure effects – women live longer and are therefore overrepresented in older age groups where disability rates are highest – rather than any inherent disability vulnerability differentials between sexes at younger ages.[^520]

**Geographic distribution**: Atoll-based populations show substantially higher disability rates than Male'. In the atolls, female disability prevalence is 8.8% and male is 6.9%; in Malé, female prevalence is 6.3% and male is 5.0%. This urban-atoll differential persists when age-adjusted, suggesting that atoll populations face compounding risk factors for disability acquisition including higher NCD burden, poorer nutrition, reduced preventive healthcare access, and occupational hazards (particularly in fishing communities).[^521]

**Atoll variation**: GA Atoll and M Atoll show the highest concentration of disability nationally; Dh Atoll shows the lowest prevalence at 2%. These atoll-level variations reflect different demographic profiles (older age structures in some atolls where younger residents have migrated to Male'), different occupational hazard profiles, and different NCD prevalence patterns.[^522]

**Age profile** – the most dramatic dimension of the data:

| Age Group | Disability Prevalence |
|---|---|
| 5–9 years | 4% |
| 10–14 years | 4% |
| 15–19 years | 3% |
| 20–39 years | 3% |
| 40–44 years | 5% |
| 50–54 years | 9% |
| 55–59 years | 13% |
| 60–64 years | 17% |
| 65–69 years | 22% |
| 70–74 years | 30% |
| 75+ years | 55% |

The elderly population (65+ years) constitutes only 5% of the resident Maldivian population but accounts for 30% of all persons with disabilities. Among those aged 75 and above, 55% experience functional difficulty – meaning more than half of the oldest Maldivians live with disability. This age-disability profile has profound implications for disability policy planning: as the Maldivian population ages over the coming decades, the absolute number of persons with disabilities will increase substantially even if age-specific prevalence rates remain constant.[^523]

### §6.53.3 Disability Type and Functional Domain Analysis

The six WG-SS functional domains reveal the following disability prevalence:

| Functional Domain | National Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Walking | 3.2% |
| Seeing | 2.7% |
| Remembering | 2.1% |
| Self-care | 1.2% |
| Communicating | 1.2% |
| Hearing | 1.2% |

Walking and seeing are the most prevalent functional limitations nationally – reflecting the combined contribution of age-related mobility limitations (musculoskeletal degeneration, arthritis, stroke sequelae) and vision loss (cataracts, diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration). The cognitive domain (remembering) ranks third, capturing both dementia in older persons and intellectual/developmental disabilities in children and working-age adults.[^524]

**Gender patterns within domains**: Women experience greater difficulty in walking and vision-related disabilities; men show higher likelihood of multiple disabilities and difficulties in self-care and communication. In GA Atoll – which has the highest overall disability prevalence – vision difficulties reach 5.6%, approximately twice the national average for this domain, likely reflecting inadequate access to ophthalmology services in this geographic location.[^525]

**Age patterns within domains**:
- Children (5–17): Predominantly seeing (1.5%), remembering (1.4%), and communication (1.4%) – reflecting developmental disability and congenital conditions
- Youth (18–35): Seeing and remembering
- Adults (36–64): Walking and seeing – mid-life onset of musculoskeletal conditions and vision degradation
- Elderly (65+): Mobility is the most common domain (higher among women), with 50% of elderly persons with disabilities experiencing difficulties in multiple domains[^526]

### §6.53.4 Multiple Disability – Severity and Clustering

**Multiple disability prevalence** is a critical dimension of support needs:
- Over one-third (33%) of persons with disabilities have two or more disability types
- Nearly one-fifth (20%) report two distinct disabilities simultaneously
- 17% report three or more disabilities

Men show higher likelihood of multiple disabilities than women. Among the elderly (65+), one in two persons with disability experiences multiple disabilities – compounding the care and support needs of the most rapidly growing age segment of the disability population.[^527]

**Severity distribution** across the WG-SS four-point scale:
- None: 75% of all persons
- Mild: 15%
- Moderate: 6%
- Severe: 1%

Urban/rural variation in severity is notable: mild severity is slightly more common in urban areas (17% in Malé vs. 15% in atolls), while severe disability rates are comparable (2% in both). Women are more likely to have mild/moderate disabilities; men are more likely to have severe disability or multiple disabilities.[^528]

### §6.53.5 Disability and Migration

The Census 2022 migration findings present a striking pattern: only 6.2% of migrants (persons who have moved between islands or from atolls to Malé) have disabilities, compared to 93.8% who do not. The median age of migrants with disabilities (53 years) is substantially older than migrants without disabilities (34 years), suggesting that migration among persons with disabilities is primarily return migration (older persons returning to family origins when care needs increase) rather than the economic migration and education migration that drives younger non-disabled migration flows.[^529]

Geographic patterns in migrant disability rates reflect this dynamic: non-administrative islands show only 1.5% migrant disability prevalence, while atoll administrative centres show 7.9%. Medical purpose migration is cited by 8% of persons with disabilities as a reason for migration – reflecting the geographic concentration of specialist healthcare in Malé and the compulsion to relocate for healthcare access.[^530]

These findings have important policy implications: the conventional understanding of urbanisation as drawing younger working-age populations toward Male' for employment and education applies to the non-disabled population, while persons with disabilities are more likely to migrate for medical reasons and at older ages – reversing the direction of movement (to rather than from atoll communities) that characterises family-based return migration.[^531]

### §6.53.6 Disability and Marriage

The marital status data from Census 2022 reveals important vulnerability patterns for women with disabilities:

| Status | Women without disability | Women with disability |
|---|---|---|
| Married | 68% | 54% |
| Never married | 19% | 11% |
| Divorced | 9% | 13% |
| Widowed | 4% | 22% |

Women with disabilities show much higher widowhood (22% vs. 4% without disability) – reflecting the age-skewed disability distribution (more older women with disabilities who are more likely to have been widowed) and the vulnerability of elderly women who acquired disability after their spouse's death. Higher divorce rates among women with disabilities (13% vs. 9%) may reflect the relationship strain associated with disability onset, or social patterns in which disability reduces women's social desirability as marriage partners.[^532]

**Median age at first marriage** is lower for persons with disabilities than without:
- Persons with disability: Both sexes 20, female 18, male 22
- Persons without disability: Both sexes 22, female 20, male 24

By severity, the pattern intensifies: persons with severe disabilities (both sexes) first marry at a median age of 18 – two to four years younger than those without disability. This earlier marriage among persons with severe disabilities, particularly women (median 18 for severe vs. 21 for no disability), may reflect family strategies to establish care arrangements, social pressure on families of women with disabilities to secure their future, or the lower social bargaining position of women with disabilities in marriage markets.[^533]

## 6.8 Disability Prevalence Trend: From 2017 to 2025


### §6.61.1 The Increasing Prevalence Trend

The Moosa et al. (2024) study notes that disability prevalence in Maldives increased from 6.8% in 2017 to 9.2% in 2019 – and projects further increases due to the aging population. This increase is partly real (driven by the ageing population and NCD burden) and partly methodological (improved identification and registration).[^592]

The implication of an increasing prevalence trend is that disability planning in Maldives must be forward-looking – designed for a disability population that is growing, not stable. Policies sized for the current 24,401 census estimate will be insufficient for the 28,000–32,000 persons with disabilities that population projections suggest by 2035 as the over-60 population expands.[^593]

### §6.61.2 NCD-Driven Disability Increase

The Maldivian NCD epidemic – particularly the high prevalence of type 2 diabetes – is a primary driver of acquired disability growth. As the diabetes-affected population ages, diabetic complications (retinopathy causing blindness, peripheral neuropathy causing amputation, cardiovascular disease causing stroke) will generate increasing disability incidence. Without aggressive NCD prevention and early complication management, this acquired disability wave will continue to grow.[^594]

The NCD-disability intersection requires explicit policy integration: NCD prevention programmes should be understood as disability prevention programmes; and the disability system should actively reach out to persons who acquire disabilities through NCD complications, supporting their NDR registration, disability allowance access, rehabilitation linkage, and vocational reorientation where employment is affected by acquired disability.[^595]

## 6.9 Disability Data Sources: Washington Group Methodology, NDR Limitations, and the Measurement Gap


### §6.25.1 Census 2022 and Washington Group Methodology

The 2022 Population and Housing Census application of the Washington Group Short Set on Functioning (WG-SS) represents the methodological gold standard of disability measurement available to Maldives. The WG-SS asks about functional difficulty across six domains – seeing, hearing, walking, cognition, self-care, and communication – using a four-point response scale (no difficulty, some difficulty, a lot of difficulty, cannot do at all). It produces a prevalence estimate focused on functional limitation rather than diagnostic category, enabling cross-country comparison and aligning with the CRPD's social model orientation.[^249]

The 2022 Census found 24,401 persons with disabilities (7% of population) using WG-SS criteria. This figure is substantially higher than previous administrative data estimates – reflecting both genuine undercounting in earlier measures and the WG-SS instrument's sensitivity to functional difficulty that falls below the threshold of severe disability often captured in administrative data.[^250]

However, the WG-SS has documented limitations for some disability categories. It is less sensitive to psychosocial and intellectual disabilities than to physical and sensory disabilities – the six functional domains do not directly capture the cognitive, emotional, or social functioning limitations associated with mental health conditions and intellectual disabilities. As a result, the 24,401 census figure is likely an undercount for these groups.[^251]

### §6.25.2 NDR-Census Coverage Gap and Its Implications

The gap between NDR registration (14,566 as of July 2025) and Census-estimated disability prevalence (24,401) – approximately 9,835 persons unregistered – has direct implications for policy design and budgetary planning. The disability allowance system, vocational training services, assistive technology provision, and any future personal assistance or community support programmes are all designed around the registered population. If 40% of persons with disabilities are not registered, programmes sized for the registered population systematically underserve the actual disability population.[^252]

Planning future disability service expansion without resolving the NDR coverage gap risks perpetuating the current structural exclusion. A programme that increases disability vocational training capacity by 50% from current levels still serves only a small fraction of the actual disability population if the registration system captures only 60% of that population.[^253]

### §6.25.3 Psychosocial Disability: The Data Void

The most significant data gap in Maldivian disability data concerns psychosocial disability – the category of disability arising from mental health conditions. The 2022 Census WG-SS instrument's limited sensitivity to psychosocial disability, combined with the NDR's historical focus on physical and sensory disabilities, means that the scale of psychosocial disability as a functional category remains unmeasured in Maldives.[^254]

Mental health prevalence data – drawn from WHO estimates and Maldivian mental health sector administrative data – suggests that depression, anxiety disorders, and severe mental health conditions affect a significant proportion of the population. However, no systematic methodology exists for converting mental health prevalence data into disability prevalence data – that is, for estimating what proportion of persons with mental health conditions experience functional limitations that rise to the level of disability as defined by CRPD and WG-SS criteria.[^255]

This data void has practical consequences: persons with psychosocial disabilities are underrepresented in the NDR, excluded from or underserved by vocational training programmes designed for persons with physical disabilities, and invisible in disability policy monitoring and evaluation. The CRPD Committee has consistently called on states to collect disability-disaggregated data that includes psychosocial disability – a call that Maldives has not yet been able to respond to with comprehensive measurement.[^256]

### §6.25.4 Disability Outcome Data: The Fundamental Absence

The most consequential gap in Maldivian disability data is not in prevalence measurement but in outcome measurement. Neither the NDR, the Census, nor any other data system routinely tracks the educational attainment, employment participation, income level, health status, or social participation of persons with disabilities over time.[^257]

Without outcome data, it is impossible to answer the fundamental questions that disability policy should be designed to answer: Are the education, employment, and social protection programmes for persons with disabilities making a measurable difference to their lives? Are persons with disabilities doing better over time – more educated, more employed, healthier, more socially included? Where interventions are working, what is causing them to work? Where outcomes are poor, what are the barriers?

The State Party's CRPD Periodic Report commitment to developing a National Disability Data Strategy – integrating NDR data with education, health, and employment administrative systems – represents the correct policy direction. The implementation challenge involves establishing data governance agreements across multiple ministries, developing standardised data exchange protocols, and embedding disability-disaggregated reporting requirements into the regular statistical output of education, health, and labour ministries.[^258]

# Part 4: The National Disability Register and Allowance


## 6.11 National Registry of Persons with Disabilities


### Registration Process

The registration process begins with the submission of an application form via the NDR Portal, an online system that is itself not in an accessible format for persons with disabilities. Applicants typically rely on their families to navigate and complete the form.[^29] In some islands, councils have assisted with submissions, but this support is inconsistent – some councils state that the application must be completed by the person or their family alone.[^30]

Medical documentation is required as evidence of disability. The approval of medical documentation is inconsistent: for some applicants, a general practitioner's certificate sufficed; for others, specialist documentation was demanded. Specialist doctors are scarce in the atolls, requiring expensive travel to Male' or regional hospitals. For persons with mobility restrictions, acquiring these documents is a significant barrier. Mental health assessments for psychosocial disabilities are especially difficult to obtain, as psychiatrists and psychologists are concentrated in Male' with long waiting lists.[^31]

### Delays and Procedural Barriers

Once submitted, applications have faced delays of **up to 12 months** before receiving approval and the subsequent monthly allowance.[^32] Applicants and their families must constantly follow up on application status. The committee that evaluates applications lacks adequate representation from the disability community, and some participants in Focus Group Discussions (FGDs) conducted for the 2024 UNPRPD situational analysis claimed that the process was facilitated through political connections for some individuals.[^33]

Status updates are communicated via text messages – inaccessible to deaf persons, persons with visual impairments, and others depending on alternative communication modes. There is no sign language interpretation or multiple language option available for these communications.[^34]

### Temporary vs. Permanent Registration

For persons with certain types of disabilities, particularly psychosocial disabilities, registration is temporary and must be renewed. This requires re-undertaking the registration process and submitting updated medical records every two years – a recurring burden that disrupts continuity of benefits.[^35]

### Exclusionary Effects

Being registered as a person with a disability has paradoxically excluded some individuals from other social protection programmes. Some participants reported being denied *Zakat* (Islamic charitable fund) distributions because they were already registered for disability allowance, even though their families lived in poverty and the allowance was insufficient to cover daily and medical expenses.[^36]

The registration system also creates a gap between the 24,401 persons identified by the Census and the 13,656 actually registered – meaning approximately **10,745 persons with disabilities** (44% of the total) are not receiving any disability-specific support from the state.

## 6.12 The National Disability Register: Architecture, Coverage Gap, and the 25.6% Problem


### §6.15.1 NDR Design and Formal Launch

The National Disability Register (NDR) was formally launched on 5 January 2023, following years of development under the joint administration of ADDA (Agency for Disability and Different Ability) and the National Social Protection Agency (NSPA). The NDR represents the most significant administrative data system specifically targeting persons with disabilities in Maldivian history – a centralised registry intended to link disability identification, needs assessment, and service entitlement administration in a single system.[^157]

The NDR's formal architecture includes: an application and registration process involving medical assessment by authorised assessors; classification of disability by type (physical, sensory-visual, sensory-hearing, intellectual, multiple) and severity (mild, moderate, severe, profound); geographic tagging by island and atoll; and linkage to the Disability Allowance administration system through NSPA. The design envisions the NDR as the operational backbone of disability policy – the system through which the government knows who its disability constituency is, where they live, what they need, and what they are receiving.[^158]

### §6.15.2 NDR Growth Trajectory and Coverage Gap

From its formal launch in January 2023, the NDR has grown rapidly in absolute terms:

| Date | NDR Registrations |
|---|---|
| December 2022 (pre-launch) | 10,701 |
| December 2024 | 13,656 |
| July 2025 | 14,566 |

This growth trajectory reflects genuine outreach efforts – public awareness campaigns, island council engagement, and the removal of some administrative barriers to registration. However, the critical benchmark is not the absolute registration figure but the coverage ratio against the 2022 Census disability estimate of 24,401 persons.[^159]

As of July 2025, the NDR covers approximately 60% of the Census-estimated disability population. The implications are significant: 40% of persons with disabilities in Maldives – approximately 9,800 persons – are not registered and therefore cannot access disability-specific services, including the Disability Allowance. This is not a marginal technical shortfall but a structural failure of the disability system's foundational infrastructure.[^160]

### §6.15.3 Causes of the Coverage Gap

Multiple intersecting factors explain the NDR's coverage gap:

**Geographic access to assessment**: NDR registration requires a medical assessment by an authorised assessor – a doctor or specialist designated for this purpose by ADDA/NSPA. Authorised assessors are concentrated in Malé and atoll capital hospitals. For persons with disabilities living in small outer islands, accessing an assessment requires inter-island travel, accommodation, and time – costs that may be prohibitive, particularly for persons with severe physical disabilities for whom travel itself is the barrier.[^161]

**Documentation requirements**: The registration process requires identity documentation and medical records that some persons with disabilities in outer islands may not have in accessible form. For persons with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities, the bureaucratic complexity of the application process may be a barrier even when geographic access is theoretically available.[^162]

**Awareness deficit**: Community-level awareness of the NDR and its associated benefits is uneven. In communities with active disability-focused civil society presence or strong island council engagement, registration rates are higher. In communities lacking these connective structures – which includes many small outer islands – awareness remains low and the NDR registration rate correspondingly limited.[^163]

**Disability type exclusions**: The NDR's classification categories and assessment protocols are better calibrated to physical and sensory disabilities than to psychosocial or intellectual disabilities. The 2025 Situational Analysis found that persons with psychosocial disabilities – experiencing mental health conditions that rise to the level of functional impairment – are substantially underrepresented in the NDR relative to their estimated prevalence. Persons with mild cognitive disabilities face similar registration barriers. This exclusion is not merely a data problem; it means that these populations receive no disability-specific state support while experiencing significant functional limitations.[^164]

**Stigma barriers**: For some disability categories – particularly psychosocial and intellectual disabilities – stigma may discourage self-identification and registration. Disability registration requires acknowledging and formally documenting a disability category, which in some communities and families carries negative social connotations that can outweigh the financial benefit of the disability allowance.[^165]

### §6.15.4 NDR and Emergency Preparedness: A Critical Integration Gap

The NDR's isolation from other administrative data systems creates a specific operational risk in the context of Maldivian climate vulnerability. The National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA), responsible for emergency preparedness and response, does not have systematic access to NDR data. In a disaster scenario – coastal flooding, severe storm, freshwater crisis – first responders at the island level do not know from official systems how many persons with disabilities live on their island, where they are located, or what their specific evacuation and support needs are.[^166]

This integration gap has not been resolved in the five years since the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic revealed its practical consequences. During COVID-19, persons with disabilities – particularly those dependent on ADDA respite services and those with communication disabilities requiring accessible health information – were not prioritised in emergency response planning at the national level. The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015–2030), to which Maldives is a signatory, calls specifically for disability-inclusive disaster risk reduction; the operational gap between the NDR and the NDMA's emergency planning systems means this commitment remains aspirational.[^167]

### §6.15.5 NDR Data Quality and the Outcome Measurement Gap

A further limitation of the NDR in its current form is its focus on input data – who is registered, where they live, what disability category they carry – rather than outcome data. The NDR does not systematically track the educational attainment, employment status, health outcomes, or social participation of registered persons with disabilities. This means that the NDR, despite being the most comprehensive disability data system Maldives has produced, cannot answer the fundamental policy evaluation questions: Are persons with disabilities doing better? Are the programmes designed to support them making a measurable difference?[^168]

The State Party's CRPD Periodic Report commitment to developing a National Disability Data Strategy – integrating NDR data with education, health, and employment administrative systems – represents the correct policy direction. Implementation will require sustained investment in inter-ministry data governance, standardised data exchange protocols, and the political will to prioritise disability-disaggregated outcome tracking within broader statistical development plans.[^169]

## 6.13 Disability Allowance and Benefits


### Historical Evolution

The Disability Allowance was created by the Disability Act 2010 and further administered under the Social Protection Act 2014. From 2010 until mid-2023, all eligible persons received a flat rate of **MVR 2,000 per month** (approximately USD 130) – an unconditional, non-means-tested cash transfer.[^37]

### 2023 Overhaul

In June 2023, the government overhauled the benefit structure, raising the base allowance to MVR 3,000 and introducing the tiered supplement system described in Section 6.2. Under the new scheme:

- Persons fully dependent on others for daily care receive an additional MVR 2,000 (total MVR 5,000)
- Those needing assistance with basic tasks receive an extra MVR 1,000 (total MVR 4,000)
- Households with multiple disabled members may receive an extra MVR 1,000 per additional person[^38]

According to officials, after these changes: approximately 15% of beneficiaries receive MVR 6,000, another 25% receive MVR 5,000, 15% receive MVR 4,000, and the remaining ~42% receive the base MVR 3,000.[^39]

### Coverage and Expenditure

As of mid-2023, **11,371 people** were receiving disability benefits nationwide, up from around 8,500 in 2019.[^40] Annual expenditure on disability allowances exceeded **MVR 111 million** in the first half of 2023 alone.[^41] In addition to cash allowances, registered persons receive support for assistive devices and therapy.

### Disability Therapy Assistance Program

Since 2019, NSPA (with the Ministry of Health) has run a Disability Therapy Assistance Program, which by 2022 was supporting over **500 individuals** with physiotherapy, speech therapy, or occupational therapy costs.[^42] Therapeutic services covered include speech therapy, behavioural therapy, psychotherapy, occupational therapy, physiotherapy, early intervention, and other services as needed. However, accessing therapy coverage remains a challenge: applicants face a lack of information about the process, complicated procedures, and partial cost coverage that leaves out-of-pocket expenses for families.[^43]

### The Disability Identification Card

A disability identification card was introduced to ease access to public services such as receiving priority healthcare and free public transportation. However, the card does not provide consistent benefits. Some hospitals prioritise persons with disabilities for consultations upon presenting the card; others only exempt them from queuing for memos or provide no priority at all. Persons with invisible disabilities are usually not given any priority in receiving healthcare services.[^44]

On public transport, the card is more consistently applied – buses and RTL (Raajje Transport Link) ferries generally provide free transportation upon presentation. However, the majority of FGD participants in the 2024 UNPRPD assessment were **unaware of the card's existence**, the application process, and its benefits.[^45]

### Adequacy Assessment

FGD participants unanimously agreed that the allowance was insufficient to cover daily and medical expenses. They argued that the person's living conditions and medical needs should be factored into the amount. The Hameed et al. (2022) study found that even after the 2023 increases, the allowance "still may not cover all needs," leading to continued calls for further increases or better targeting.[^46]

## 6.14 Disability Allowance: Design, Administration, and the Coverage Gap in Detail


### §6.16.1 Allowance Design and Administration

The Disability Allowance (established under Regulation 2011/R-3, amended to its current form in 2019) is the primary disability-specific social protection measure in Maldives. Administered by the Social Protection Administration (under NSPA/MoSFD), the allowance provides MVR 2,000 per month to all persons registered in the NDR, regardless of disability type or severity classification following the 2019 standardisation.[^170]

For the Maldivian disability system, the allowance serves multiple functions simultaneously: it is an income supplement; a de facto recognition of disability status; a proxy for service entitlement; and a primary mechanism through which the state's disability spending reaches individuals. The allowance's centrality to the disability system reflects both the relative underdevelopment of non-cash disability services and the genuine income inadequacy that characterises the economic position of many persons with disabilities.

From January to June 2023, total disability allowance expenditure was MVR 111 million – reflecting the scale of the programme and its central role in social protection for persons with disabilities who are registered.[^171] By mid-2023, approximately 11,371 persons were receiving the allowance – a figure that, cross-referenced against the NDR registration of approximately 12,000 at the same period, suggests relatively high uptake among registered persons. The crisis is not that registered persons fail to receive the allowance, but that so many eligible persons are not registered.

### §6.16.2 The Adequacy Question: MVR 2,000 in the Maldivian Cost of Living Context

MVR 2,000 per month (approximately USD 130 at 2025 exchange rates) must be assessed against the Maldivian cost of living to understand its adequacy as a social protection measure. In Malé – where 35% of persons with disabilities live and where most disability services are concentrated – rent for a basic room in a shared apartment can cost MVR 3,000–5,000 per month. Food, transport, and utilities represent additional substantial costs.[^172]

The allowance is therefore insufficient to support independent living in the urban context where services are located, without additional income sources. For persons with disabilities whose disability prevents them from working or limits their earning capacity – which describes a significant proportion of severely disabled persons – the allowance alone does not provide an adequate standard of living. This creates a structural dependency on family support that is incompatible with the CRPD's vision of independent living and community inclusion.[^173]

In outer atoll contexts, the cost of living is lower, and the allowance represents a more meaningful contribution to household income. However, the paradox is that service provision in outer atolls is minimal, meaning that persons with disabilities in these communities receive cash support but not the services – rehabilitation, assistive technology, personal assistance, accessible transport – that would enable meaningful social participation.

### §6.16.3 The 2019 Employment Disincentive Reform: Impact and Limitations

The 2019 confirmation that the Disability Allowance would not be reduced upon gaining formal employment removed the structural poverty trap that disability advocates had identified as a primary barrier to labour market participation. This was genuinely significant policy reform – it aligned the allowance's design with international best practice on benefit portability and employment transition support.[^174]

However, the reform's impact on employment participation has been modest in isolation, because the allowance trap was not the only barrier to employment for persons with disabilities. Workplace inaccessibility, attitudinal discrimination by employers, absence of supported employment services, skills and qualification gaps resulting from educational under-provision, and geographic concentration of employment opportunities in Malé while the majority of persons with disabilities live in atolls – these barriers remain substantially unaddressed. Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 and the 350 civil service/SOE placements by August 2025 represent important additional progress, but the employment participation gap documented in LFPR data (41% for men, 28% for women with disabilities) remains large.[^175]

## 6.15 The Hameed et al. (2022) Study: A Deep Analysis of Allowance Exclusion


### §6.62.1 Research Design and Significance

The Hameed et al. (2022) study – *Access to the Disability Allowance in the Maldives: National coverage and factors affecting uptake* – published in *Global Social Policy* (authored by Shafa Hameed and Sofoora Kawsar Usman from LSHTM with Hannah Kuper and Lena Morgon Banks) – is the only nationally representative mixed-methods study of disability allowance coverage in Maldives. Its combination of a population-based household survey (N=6,500 persons, July–December 2017, nationally representative two-stage stratified sample) with qualitative in-depth interviews (23 participants with disabilities and caregivers, covering both Male' and outer atolls, enrolled and non-enrolled) makes it the most methodologically rigorous source on disability social protection in the country.[^596]

The study's primary finding – that only 25.6% of persons with disabilities receive the disability allowance – establishes the foundational evidence of the coverage crisis. But its qualitative depth goes further, identifying the five distinct mechanisms through which eligible persons are excluded from a programme to which they are legally entitled. These mechanisms – information gaps, identity barriers, geographic/logistical barriers, stigma, and procedural complexity – operate simultaneously and cumulatively for many persons with disabilities, creating compounded exclusion.[^597]

### §6.62.2 The Five Exclusion Mechanisms – In Detail

**Mechanism 1: Lack of Information and Awareness**

The primary barrier to allowance uptake is simple: many eligible persons with disabilities do not know the programme exists. Information dissemination for the disability allowance relies primarily on word-of-mouth rather than systematic public campaigns, official community outreach, or dissemination through the health or education systems where persons with disabilities most commonly come into contact with the state.[^598]

Outer atoll residents face particular information access limitations – where civil society organisations (which serve as informal information channels in Male') are absent and community-level government communication relies on island councils that may not systematically share welfare programme information. The result is a pattern in which the most isolated, most vulnerable, and most geographically excluded persons with disabilities are precisely those least likely to hear about the programme through informal channels.[^599]

**Mechanism 2: Identity and Eligibility Perception Barriers**

The second mechanism operates through disability identity and self-classification. Many persons with functional limitations do not perceive themselves as "disabled" in ways that would make them eligible for the allowance – either because they reject the disability label due to stigma, because they do not understand that functional limitations (rather than medical diagnoses) are the qualifying criterion, or because they compare their situation to others with more visible or severe impairments and conclude their condition does not qualify.[^600]

Persons with sensory impairments – deaf, hard-of-hearing, blind, or low-vision individuals – are the lowest-coverage group. This is partly because their disability may not involve contact with health or social service systems that would identify them as potentially eligible, and partly because MSL-using deaf persons, in particular, may have a strong positive cultural identity as Deaf (rather than "disabled") that makes the disability label and the associated welfare benefit less salient.[^601]

Persons with psychosocial (mental health) disabilities face a related but distinct barrier: mental health stigma means that many do not disclose mental health conditions to assessors or family members, do not seek the diagnosis that would support an allowance application, and may actively avoid being identified as having a "mental" condition even when aware that the allowance covers psychosocial disability.[^602]

A specific misconception documented by Hameed et al. is the belief that the allowance is means-tested – that it is available only to the very poorest households. Since the allowance is not formally means-tested (it is available to all registered persons with disabilities regardless of income), this misconception creates exclusion among lower-middle and middle-income households with persons with disabilities who incorrectly believe they are ineligible. This perception barrier is partly driven by the structural positioning of the allowance within the social protection system, adjacent to means-tested programmes, and by the absence of explicit public communication that the allowance is universal rather than targeted.[^603]

**Mechanism 3: Geographic and Logistical Barriers**

The most structurally significant exclusion mechanism for the majority of persons with disabilities (65% in atolls) is the geographic barrier to assessment. The NDR assessment process requires attendance at an authorised assessment facility – primarily IGMH in Male'. For an outer atoll resident, completing this assessment requires:

1. Arranging inter-island ferry transport to Male' (or to an atoll capital with a regional hospital, if assessment is available there)
2. Paying ferry fares (potentially substantial relative to household income)
3. Taking time off work or other responsibilities
4. Finding accommodation in Male' if multiple visits are required
5. Potentially arranging a companion or support person for the journey

The Hameed et al. study's cost analysis found that for outer atoll residents, the total cost of the application and assessment process – including ferry fares, accommodation, and time costs – can exceed the monthly value of the allowance itself (MVR 2,000). For a family considering whether to invest this cost in the hope of successfully obtaining the allowance, the rational calculation may be negative if there is uncertainty about assessment outcome or if the household cannot afford the upfront costs.[^604]

This cost-barrier mechanism has a perverse equity effect: the poorest households in the most remote locations – those with the greatest need for the allowance – face the highest transaction costs to access it. The allowance's implicit first cost is highest for those it is most designed to help.[^605]

**Mechanism 4: Stigma and the Charity Framing**

Stigma operates as both an identity barrier (as described above) and an access barrier at the household and community level. The perception that disability allowance receipt marks a person or family as disabled, dependent, and in receipt of government charity creates social pressure against application – particularly in the small, tight-knit island communities of the outer atolls where privacy is limited and social reputation is a critical resource.[^606]

Women are particularly subject to this mechanism. Married women whose husbands or families disapprove of the disability identification – seeing it as an admission of weakness or a basis for reduced social status – may be unable to apply without family support. The finding that married women with disabilities have lower allowance uptake than single women or men is consistent with this dynamic: in household decision-making structures where male family members control financial decisions and social reputation management, a woman's disability allowance application is not solely her own decision.[^607]

The qualitative interviews document the "charity framing" of the allowance as a specific cultural obstacle: the perception that government assistance for disability is a form of begging (saailu) rather than a rights-based entitlement. This framing – which reflects the broader cultural tension between Maldivian Islamic values of self-sufficiency and the emerging rights-based welfare state – positions the disability allowance in opposition to social dignity rather than as an expression of it.[^608]

**Mechanism 5: Assessment and Application Procedural Complexity**

Even persons who are aware of the programme, identify as eligible, and have overcome geographic and stigma barriers face a procedural barrier in the application and assessment process itself. The assessment – conducted by health professionals at authorised sites, including a physical examination and functional assessment – may:

- Be physically inaccessible (assessment tables not height-adjustable, facilities without ramp access, examination rooms without sign language accommodation)
- Involve communication barriers for deaf applicants who have no sign language interpreter
- Apply restrictive assessment criteria that do not adequately capture functional limitations
- Require documentation that some applicants do not possess (identity documents, medical records)
- Have extended processing timelines that create uncertainty and administrative burden

Literacy barriers affect the application form completion process – persons with visual disabilities or low education attainment may be unable to complete written forms independently, requiring family or intermediary assistance that introduces privacy and autonomy concerns.[^609]

The extended timeline from application to decision – and the absence of clear communication about application status – creates additional deterrent effects, particularly for persons who have made the substantial investment of traveling to Male' for assessment and face an uncertain waiting period before knowing whether they will receive the benefit.[^610]

### §6.62.3 Policy Recommendations from Hameed et al.

The Hameed et al. study's policy recommendations are directly actionable and have not been fully implemented in the seven years since the study's data collection:

1. **Systematic public awareness campaigns** targeted at high-barrier groups (women, older adults, sensory impairments, outer atoll communities), in accessible formats including sign language, audio, and Easy Read
2. **Decentralisation of assessment services** – establishing assessment capacity in outer atoll health centres, training regional health workers in disability assessment, and considering mobile assessment teams
3. **Cost reduction** – eliminating assessment fees, providing transport subsidies for outer atoll applicants, and offering travel voucher or reimbursement schemes
4. **Procedural simplification** – streamlining documentation requirements, clarifying eligibility criteria publicly, and providing accessible application support
5. **Clear communication that the allowance is not means-tested** – addressing the specific misconception that limits uptake among non-poorest eligible households
6. **Integration with community health worker networks** – using the existing community health infrastructure to identify persons with disabilities and support their allowance applications[^611]

## 6.16 The Disability Allowance in Comparative Perspective: Regional Benchmarking


### §6.43.1 South Asian Cash Transfer Comparisons

The Maldivian Disability Allowance of MVR 2,000 per month (approximately USD 130) can be contextualised against regional disability cash transfer programmes, though the significant differences in cost of living across South Asia make direct comparison challenging.

In the regional context, the Maldivian allowance compares favourably in absolute terms with disability cash transfers in Bangladesh, Nepal, and parts of India – where disability allowances are often equivalent to USD 5–20 per month. However, the Maldivian cost of living – particularly housing costs in Malé – is substantially higher than in other South Asian countries, meaning that the purchasing power of MVR 2,000 in Maldives is considerably lower than comparative monetary values suggest.[^439]

Sri Lanka's disability allowance programme, the Samurdhi and disability pension schemes, and the Indian state-level disability pension schemes offer different models of disability income support. The Sri Lankan model provides categorical pensions for specific disability types with means-testing, while the Maldivian model provides a universal flat-rate payment to all registered persons with disabilities regardless of income. The Maldivian universal approach is simpler and avoids the exclusion of persons who have some income, but the flat rate fails to address the differential income needs of persons with different disability types and severities.[^440]

### §6.43.2 The Pathway to Graduated Support

A reform pathway for the Disability Allowance that would address its current design limitations – without simply increasing the flat rate universally – would involve a tiered support model that accounts for disability severity and extra cost burden. Such a model could include:

- A base universal payment for all registered persons with disabilities (equivalent to current MVR 2,000)
- An additional severe disability supplement for persons with the highest support needs (assessed through a standardised functional assessment)
- A carer's supplement for persons with disabilities who have a designated informal carer – recognising the caregiver's contribution and providing partial income compensation
- Disability-related extra cost payments for specific high-cost items (assistive technology, medication, specialised transport) that are not covered by Aasandha[^441]

This graduated model would require more sophisticated assessment and administration than the current flat-rate system, but would more accurately target support to those with the greatest need and highest costs. The ongoing NDR development provides a data infrastructure on which graduated assessment could be built, if the assessment protocols are extended to capture functional impairment severity and individual support need.[^442]

# Part 5: ADDA and Governance Architecture


## 6.17 ADDA: Mandate, Architecture, and Operational Realities


### §6.17.1 Institutional History and Restructuring

The Agency for Disability and Different Ability (ADDA) was established through a restructuring of the Adult Disability Care Centre (ADCC), the operational disability services agency that preceded it. The restructuring reflected a conceptual shift – from a care centre for adults with disabilities to an agency promoting disability inclusion – but the extent to which the name change has been accompanied by operational transformation remains contested by disability advocates.[^176]

ADDA operates under MoSFD (Ministry of Social and Family Development) and holds the primary operational mandate for disability services in Maldives. Its formal functions include: residential and respite care; vocational training and employment placement; assistive device provision; disability awareness and community education; support for OPD development; coordination of the NDR (jointly with NSPA); and technical support to other ministries on disability inclusion.[^177]

### §6.17.2 Operational Capacity and Geographic Concentration

ADDA's operational capacity is heavily concentrated in Malé. The agency's physical facilities – its vocational training workshops, respite care facilities, and administrative offices – are located in the capital. Outer atoll ADDA presence is limited to periodic outreach visits and coordination with island councils, rather than any permanent decentralised service infrastructure.[^178]

This geographic concentration has profound implications for the 65% of persons with disabilities who live outside Malé. For this majority, ADDA's services are theoretically available but practically inaccessible. The gap between ADDA's policy mandate (to serve all persons with disabilities in Maldives) and its operational infrastructure (Malé-centric facilities and staffing) represents one of the most significant structural challenges in the disability system.[^179]

### §6.17.3 Vocational Training: Model and Limitations

ADDA's vocational training programme provides skills development and some employment placement support for persons with disabilities. Programmes include arts and crafts production (particularly traditional Maldivian crafts), basic computing, and other vocational skills assessed as matching participants' capabilities. The programme has been running for several years and represents ADDA's most visible service delivery activity.[^180]

However, the programme's design and participant profile reveal significant limitations. Participation is dominated by persons with physical disabilities; persons with intellectual and psychosocial disabilities are substantially underrepresented, in part because the training model does not systematically accommodate the support needs of these groups. The vocational skills offered – arts and crafts, basic computing – may not align with labour market demand in the Maldivian context, where employment in tourism, government service, and the retail sector is dominant.[^181]

No systematic outcome tracking exists for ADDA's vocational training graduates – no data is published on what proportion of training completers gain employment, at what wages, in what sectors, and whether employment is sustained over time. This absence of outcome data makes it impossible to assess the programme's effectiveness or to compare it against alternative uses of the same resources.[^182]

### §6.17.4 Respite Care and Family Caregiver Support

ADDA provides limited respite care services in Malé that enable family caregivers – predominantly women – to take temporary breaks from caring responsibilities. This respite function recognises that in the absence of personal assistance services or supported living options, family caregivers carry an intense and continuous care burden that has physical, psychological, and economic consequences.[^183]

The respite services are available only in Malé. For the majority of family caregivers in outer atolls, no equivalent formal support exists. The absence of respite care for outer atoll caregivers – who may be providing intensive physical care for family members with severe disabilities, without break and without formal support – represents a hidden welfare crisis that is invisible in current monitoring data.[^184]

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022) revealed the fragility of this arrangement: when ADDA facilities closed during lockdown periods, caregivers who had depended on respite services found themselves without any formal support. This experience highlighted the urgency of developing caregiver support models that are resilient to service disruption and geographically distributed beyond Malé.[^185]

### §6.17.5 Assistive Technology: Provision, Gaps, and Import Costs

ADDA coordinates assistive technology provision – wheelchairs, crutches, white canes, hearing aids, and other mobility and communication devices – primarily in Malé. The volume of assistive technology distributed is not publicly reported in a systematic way, and demand significantly exceeds supply for most device categories.[^186]

The geographic and logistical challenges of assistive technology provision in the Maldives are severe. Importing assistive technologies is costly – standard international procurement through humanitarian channels is complicated by the lack of large-scale international NGO presence and the difficulty of shipping to outer island communities. Customs duties on some assistive device categories add to cost. Maintenance and repair of complex assistive technologies (power wheelchairs, hearing aid programming, communication devices) requires technical capacity that does not exist in outer atoll settings.[^187]

Aasandha health insurance provides limited coverage for some assistive devices through the health system, but coverage is inconsistent and does not extend to the full range of assistive technologies needed for daily living. The result is a system in which persons with disabilities who need assistive technologies face a combination of availability gaps, cost barriers, and geographic inaccessibility that leaves many without the devices that would enable meaningful social participation.[^188]

## 6.18 Governance and Accountability: The Disability System's Structural Weaknesses


### §6.65.1 Inter-Ministerial Coordination Failure

The UNPRPD Situational Analysis identifies inter-ministerial coordination as among the most critical systemic gaps in the disability governance architecture. Disability is a cross-sector issue – its implementation requires coordinated action from the Ministries of Social and Family Development, Health, Education, Housing, Finance, Justice, and Transportation, as well as the NDMA, NSPA, HRCM, and the local government system. However, these actors operate in largely independent silos, each managing their own disability-related mandates without systematic coordination.[^621]

The Disability Council is the designated coordination mechanism, but its operational effectiveness has been inconsistent. Information sharing across ministries on disability-related activities is limited. Budget allocations for disability in sector ministries are not transparent or coordinated through any central disability financing mechanism. Disability-related decisions by one ministry (for example, the introduction of digital-only service delivery platforms by the Ministry of Finance) are made without disability impact assessment by disability-focused agencies (ADDA).[^622]

This coordination failure has concrete consequences: the NDR is not integrated with NDMA emergency planning (§6.15.4); NCD prevention does not connect to disability allowance registration for newly acquired disabilities (§6.61.2); transport infrastructure investments do not incorporate disability accessibility requirements (§6.18.3); and public housing developments do not include accessible unit requirements (§6.21.3).[^623]

### §6.65.2 Budget Transparency and Disability Financing

The fiscal architecture of disability in Maldives lacks transparency. Total government spending on disability – across ADDA operations, NSPA disability allowance payments, Department of Inclusive Education, and disability-related health services – is not consolidated in a disability budget statement. Individual programme expenditures are reported (the MVR 111 million H1 2023 allowance figure; the ADDA operational budget; the SEN teacher salary budget) but the aggregate disability financing picture is not publicly reported.[^624]

The absence of a consolidated disability budget prevents accountability assessment: it is not possible to determine whether the government's total disability spending is adequate, efficiently allocated, or equitably distributed between Malé and atoll populations. International organisations working on disability financing – the Global Disability Fund, UNPRPD – have recommended that states develop disability-inclusive budget analysis methodologies that track disability-related expenditures across all ministry budgets rather than just disability-specific line items.[^625]

### §6.65.3 The Disability Council – Reform Blueprint

Based on the evidence across the Situational Analysis, Shadow Report, and NAPDI assessment, the following elements constitute the minimum reform package for the Disability Council to fulfil its coordination mandate:

**Statutory mandate**: The Disability Council's mandate, composition, and meeting frequency must be established in the revised Disability Act – not through ministerial regulation that can be changed without legislative process. The Act should specify: composition (minister-level representation from specified ministries); minimum meeting frequency (quarterly); quorum requirements; and decision-making procedures.[^626]

**OPD representation with substantive rights**: OPD representatives must have voting rights on the Disability Council, not merely observer or consultative status. The principle of "nothing about us without us" requires that persons with disabilities have decision-making authority in the body that sets disability policy direction, not merely advisory access to it.[^627]

**Secretariat**: The Council requires a functioning secretariat – dedicated staff within MoSFD responsible for agenda preparation, minute-taking, inter-ministerial communication, and follow-up on Council decisions. Without a secretariat, the Council's coordination function remains nominal.[^628]

**Publication and accountability**: Council minutes, decisions, and implementation reports should be publicly published – enabling civil society, OPDs, the HRCM, and the CRPD Committee to assess whether the Council's coordination function is being exercised and whether its decisions are being implemented by member ministries.[^629]

# Part 6: National Action Plan on Disability (NAPDI)


## 6.19 NAPDI Version 6: The Current National Action Plan Framework


### §6.14.1 NAPDI as Policy Instrument: Structure and Ambition

The National Action Plan for Disability Inclusion Version 6 (NAPDI v6) is the current governing framework for disability policy implementation in Maldives, representing the latest iteration of a planning process that has produced successive action plans since the enactment of the Disability Act in 2010. NAPDI v6 sets out five policy goals, twenty-five strategic objectives, and a range of planned activities across education, employment, health, social protection, and governance.[^139]

The plan's formal architecture reflects learning from previous iterations. NAPDI v6 includes more specific activity descriptions than its predecessors and acknowledges more explicitly the evidence gaps that constrain implementation planning. However, analysis of NAPDI v6 in the context of the encyclopedia's cross-referencing exercise (summarised in §19 of this encyclopedia) reveals persistent gaps between the plan's stated ambitions and the evidence base needed to drive implementation.[^140]

### §6.14.2 Policy Goal 1: Inclusive and Equitable Quality Education for All Children with Disabilities

NAPDI v6's first goal addresses educational inclusion – the domain where policy development has been most sustained and where institutional infrastructure (SEN teachers, the Department of Inclusive Education, the Maldives Institute for Autism) has developed most substantially.

**Strategies under Goal 1** include: universal SEN teacher placement in all schools; development of an individual learning plan system; accessible school building standards; outreach to children with disabilities not currently in school; and tertiary education disability support services.

**Evidence cross-reference**: The UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis confirmed that every mainstream school in Maldives has at least one SEN-trained teacher as of 2024 – a genuine achievement attributable to sustained investment in SEN teacher training through NIE. However, the SEN teacher-to-student ratio is not standardised, and the capacity of individual SEN teachers varies substantially by school and atoll.[^141] The GPE THAIBA grant (USD 9.5 million, 2024) addresses some of these capacity gaps, including funding for accessible learning materials, SEN teacher professional development, and outreach to out-of-school children with disabilities, but the geographic scale of the challenge – 187 inhabited islands – means that resource concentration in Malé will persist even with enhanced outer atoll investment.

**Key evidence gap**: National data on the education status of children with disabilities is not disaggregated in a way that enables tracking of out-of-school children by disability type and island. NAPDI v6's education targets cannot be meaningfully measured without this data infrastructure. The encyclopedia's statistics compendium (§17.4) confirms the absence of this data point.[^142]

**Critical analytical finding**: The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA, established 2018) represents the most specialised disability education resource in the country, but serves children with autism spectrum conditions in Malé only. Children with autism in outer atolls have no equivalent specialised resource. NAPDI v6 does not contain a specific strategy for replicating or extending autism-spectrum support services beyond Malé – a gap that appears more acute given the generally recognised trend of increasing autism identification globally, including in island contexts where early identification has been limited.[^143]

### §6.14.3 Policy Goal 2: Employment and Economic Participation for Persons with Disabilities

NAPDI v6's employment goal addresses a domain characterised by the most significant recent policy development – Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 – but also by some of the most intractable structural barriers.

**Strategies under Goal 2** include: civil service and SOE disability employment targets; vocational training expansion; private sector disability hiring incentives; and the elimination of discrimination in recruitment processes.

**Evidence cross-reference**: The 2024/R-9 regulation establishing civil service and SOE employment frameworks represents the most operationally specific policy development in the disability employment domain. Over 350 persons with disabilities had been placed through this framework by August 2025 – a measurable output. The MVR 100 million women's entrepreneurship fund with 25% reserved for women with disabilities (2024) adds an important gender-responsive dimension.[^144]

However, the National Social Protection Agency's income data (referenced in §17.4 of the Statistics Compendium) shows that the Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR) for persons with disabilities is 41% for men and 28% for women – substantially below the national average. The 350 civil service/SOE placements, while meaningful, represent a tiny fraction of the estimated 24,401 persons with disabilities nationally, and an even smaller fraction of those of working age who are not in formal employment.[^145]

**Key evidence gap**: No disability-disaggregated unemployment rate is collected through official labour force surveys. The National Disability Register does not capture employment status of registrants. Without this data, NAPDI v6's employment targets cannot be tracked, and the true scale of the employment participation gap for persons with disabilities remains unmeasured.[^146]

**Critical structural issue**: The private sector – which employs approximately 60% of the Maldivian formal workforce – is addressed in NAPDI v6 through aspirational strategies but not through binding obligations or incentive mechanisms. Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 applies only to civil service and state-owned enterprises. No private sector disability quota, hiring subsidy, reasonable accommodation mandate, or anti-discrimination enforcement mechanism has been implemented. Given the dominance of the tourism sector (resorts, guesthouses, water sports operators) in private sector employment, and the physical nature of many tourism-adjacent jobs, disability-inclusive private sector employment requires specific enabling policy – not generic anti-discrimination language.[^147]

### §6.14.4 Policy Goal 3: Healthcare and Habilitation Access for Persons with Disabilities

NAPDI v6's health goal addresses the intersection of disability and healthcare access, including both primary care access and specialist habilitation and rehabilitation services.

**Strategies under Goal 3** include: disability-inclusive primary care protocols; outer island health centre accessibility; rehabilitation service expansion; mental health access for persons with psychosocial disabilities; and NCD-disability integration.

**Evidence cross-reference**: Aasandha universal health insurance provides financial access to healthcare for all registered persons with disabilities, including covering specialist consultations and hospital admission. This is a foundational enabler. However, the 2025 Situational Analysis found that physical accessibility of health centres – ramps, accessible bathrooms, examination table adaptation – remains the exception rather than the norm in outer island health facilities.[^148]

The rehabilitation service gap is acute. IGMH in Malé provides the primary physiotherapy and occupational therapy services. No community rehabilitation programme exists nationally. For a person who acquires a disability – through accident, stroke, or progressive condition – in a small outer island, the pathway to rehabilitation involves travel to Malé, extended stays in often expensive urban accommodation, and separation from family and community. The financial and social costs of rehabilitation access disproportionately burden persons with disabilities from lower-income atoll households.[^149]

The intersection of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) and disability is particularly significant in the Maldivian context. The high national prevalence of type 2 diabetes – driven by dietary change, reduced physical activity, and urbanisation – generates substantial acquired disability through diabetic retinopathy (vision loss), peripheral neuropathy (sensation and mobility impairment), and limb amputation. NCD policy and disability policy are managed through separate government structures with limited coordination, meaning that the disability implications of NCD progression are not systematically addressed at either the prevention or consequence management stage.[^150]

### §6.14.5 Policy Goal 4: Social Protection and Standard of Living

NAPDI v6's social protection goal addresses the adequacy of cash transfers and services for persons with disabilities, including the disability allowance, housing, and poverty reduction.

**Evidence cross-reference**: The most significant finding in this domain – drawn from Hameed et al. (2022), the NSPA administrative data, and the 2025 Situational Analysis – is that only 25.6% of persons with disabilities identified in the 2022 Census receive the Disability Allowance. This constitutes a coverage crisis: three-quarters of persons with disabilities in the Maldives receive no formal disability-specific income support despite statutory entitlement.[^151]

The causes of this coverage gap are systemic and mutually reinforcing: NDR registration is a prerequisite for allowance receipt; NDR registration requires a medical assessment by authorised assessors concentrated in Malé; persons in outer atolls face transport costs and procedural complexity in accessing assessment; awareness of the entitlement is limited in communities without significant civil society presence; and the NDR's disability categories historically underrepresented psychosocial and mild cognitive disabilities.[^152]

Housing represents a further critical gap in social protection. No accessible housing standard exists in Maldivian building regulations. No priority allocation of housing to persons with disabilities exists within public housing programmes. No home modification grant scheme exists to enable persons with newly acquired disabilities to remain in their homes. The 2025 Situational Analysis found that many persons with disabilities – particularly those with physical disabilities requiring wheelchair access – live in homes that are effectively inaccessible, requiring modifications that fall entirely on families to fund.[^153]

### §6.14.6 Policy Goal 5: Governance, Participation, and Accountability

NAPDI v6's fifth goal addresses the structural governance of disability policy – the coordination mechanisms, data systems, participation of OPDs, and monitoring framework through which all other goals are to be implemented and held accountable.

**Evidence cross-reference**: The Disability Council, established as the central coordinating body under the Disability Act, has experienced inconsistent operation across successive administrations. Its mandate, composition, meeting frequency, and decision-making authority have not been consistently defined or enforced. The 2025 Situational Analysis found that inter-ministerial coordination on disability issues remains weak, with individual ministries implementing disability-related activities in isolation rather than within a coordinated strategic framework.[^154]

The HRCM's role as the Article 33(2) independent monitoring mechanism is formally established but practically constrained. HRCM's disability monitoring activities have produced important reports – including the Shadow Report to the CRPD Committee in 2023 – but the Commission lacks dedicated disability monitoring staff, a systematic outer island monitoring methodology, and the budgetary resources to conduct the frequency of monitoring visits that the geographic challenge of the Maldives requires.[^155]

The participation of organisations of persons with disabilities (OPDs) in policy development is formally acknowledged in NAPDI v6 but practically limited. Key OPDs – the Maldives Association of Persons with Disabilities (MAPD), the Blind and Visually Impaired Society of Maldives (BVISM), the Maldives Deaf Association, Care Society, and the Maldives Autism Association – have engaged in consultative processes, but their participation in ongoing implementation monitoring, budget planning, and policy revision is not systematically structured.[^156]

## 6.20 NAPDI Version History: From V1 to V6 -- A Policy Planning Genealogy


### §6.50.1 NAPDI v1 Through v3: Establishing the Framework

The National Action Plan for Disability Inclusion (NAPDI) has gone through six iterations since the Disability Act's enactment in 2010, each building on (and usually acknowledging the implementation gaps of) its predecessor. This genealogy of action plans reveals both the increasing sophistication of disability policy planning in Maldives and the persistent gap between planning ambition and implementation reality.[^487]

NAPDI v1 (approximately 2011–2013) was the foundational document, establishing the basic framework of disability policy priorities following the Disability Act's passage. It identified education, employment, health, and social protection as priority domains and established the Disability Council as the coordination mechanism. Implementation was limited by the absence of funded activities and the nascent state of the institutional infrastructure.[^488]

NAPDI v2 and v3 (approximately 2014–2018) refined the framework, adding more specific activities and beginning to integrate the CRPD's rights-based language into action plan framing. These iterations coincided with the development of the NDR (in progress), the establishment of ADDA as a restructured agency, and the early phases of the inclusive education policy implementation. However, the fundamental implementation challenges – coordination gaps, budgetary limitations, data absence – persisted.[^489]

### §6.50.2 NAPDI v4 and v5: Increasing Specificity

NAPDI v4 and v5 (approximately 2019–2022 and 2022–2024) reflected the policy developments of those periods – the 2019 allowance reform, the NDR formal launch, Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 preparations, and the Education Act 2021. These plans were more specific in their activity descriptions and more explicit in acknowledging the evidence gaps that constrained implementation monitoring.[^490]

The CRPD's influence on NAPDI language became more explicit through these iterations – the rights-based framing, the CRPD article references, and the acknowledgement of international monitoring obligations became standard features of action plan text. However, the fundamental structural weakness of successive NAPDIs – aspirational targets without enforcement mechanisms, budgetary commitments, or accountability structures – was not resolved.[^491]

### §6.50.3 NAPDI v6: Current Framework and Assessment

NAPDI v6 – the current plan – represents the most sophisticated iteration yet. Its five policy goals, twenty-five strategies, and cross-referenced evidence table (analysed in §6.14 of this chapter) provide a more rigorous framework than previous iterations. The plan's explicit acknowledgement of evidence gaps and the cross-referencing of strategies against research evidence represents methodological maturity.[^492]

The critical assessment of NAPDI v6 in §6.14 identified persistent gaps: unmeasurable targets due to data absence, private sector employment gap, no personal assistance or community living strategy, OPD participation structure inadequate for genuine inclusion. These gaps are not failings of the planning document alone – they reflect the structural limitations of a disability system that has not yet been transformed from a services-based to a rights-based model.[^493]

The next NAPDI iteration – which will follow the Disability Act review – provides an opportunity to design a plan that addresses these structural limitations: measurable and measured targets, funded activities, accountability mechanisms, and structural OPD participation. The quality of this next plan will be a critical indicator of whether the Disability Act review has produced genuine policy transformation or merely updated the text without transforming the system.[^494]

# Part 7: Inclusive Education


## 6.21 Inclusive Education Policies and Challenges


### Policy Framework

The Inclusive Education Policy was first formulated in 2013 and updated in 2021 to align more closely with the CRPD, marking a shift from "special needs" language to an inclusive education framework – at least on paper.[^47] The Education Act 2020 (Act 24/2020) further reinforced the right to education for all children, including those with disabilities.

### Special Education Needs Units

"Special Education Needs" (SEN) units – comprising a SEN teacher and classroom space – have been established in government schools. As of 2017, **87% of government schools** across the country had SEN units.[^48] However, their functioning has been limited by lack of funding and qualified teachers. More recently, SEN units have been removed in some schools in an effort to practice "inclusion" by placing children with disabilities in mainstream classes. However, these children are **not provided with any reasonable accommodations** to fulfil their learning needs: deaf students lack sign language interpreters, students with intellectual disabilities are allocated to higher grades based on age rather than learning needs, and students with visual impairments do not receive educational materials in accessible formats.[^49]

### The Sign Language Gap

A recent government decision to switch from teaching Dhivehi sign language to American Sign Language (ASL) in Male' schools has caused major disruption within the deaf community. The majority of deaf persons and existing interpreters use Dhivehi sign language. There is no standardised, formal sign language taught consistently across the country, which deepens the communication barrier.[^50]

### Individualised Education Plans

The Individualised Education Plan (IEP), mandated for students with complex learning profiles, is not adequately implemented. There is a lack of harmonisation between students' therapy and the school curriculum, meaning children with disabilities do not receive individualised learning opportunities that meet their support needs.[^51]

### Higher Education

Higher education institutions remain largely inaccessible. While some have formulated internal inclusion policies, these are not effectively implemented. Courses are designed for persons without disabilities, and lecturers do not make adequate accommodations. Students with disabilities have been asked to find their own helpers during examinations.[^52] Student loan schemes now include reserved slots and needs assessments for students with disabilities, but significant barriers persist in the atolls where higher education opportunities are especially limited.[^53]

### Bullying, Harassment, and Exclusion

Students with disabilities face bullying and harassment from peers and even teachers, with some schools taking no action when incidents are reported. Children with disabilities are excluded from extracurricular activities including Quran competitions and sports tournaments. Parents have had to contest with the Ministry of Education to ensure their children could sit for O-Level examinations.[^54]

## 6.22 Inclusive Education in Depth: From Policy Commitment to Classroom Reality


### §6.29.1 The Inclusive Education Policy Architecture

Inclusive education for children with disabilities in Maldives has a more developed institutional infrastructure than any other disability domain – the product of sustained investment over more than a decade and substantial international technical and financial support. Understanding the architecture of this infrastructure, and its operational gaps, is essential for assessing both what has been achieved and what remains to be accomplished.

The foundational policy document is the 2013 Inclusive Education Policy, developed by the Ministry of Education in collaboration with UNICEF and the National Institute of Education (NIE). The policy established inclusive education as the guiding principle – not integration into segregated special units, but genuine inclusion in mainstream classroom settings with appropriate support. It provided the basis for SEN teacher training through NIE and created an institutional home for inclusive education within the Ministry of Education through the Department of Inclusive Education.[^287]

The 2021 Education Act codified inclusive education as a statutory obligation, elevating it from policy to law. The Act's inclusive education provisions establish the right of every child – including every child with a disability – to quality education in an inclusive setting, with reasonable accommodations and appropriate support. The Department of Inclusive Education (ied.gov.mv) is designated as the lead implementing body.[^288]

This legislative architecture is genuine progress. A child in Maldives has a statutory right to inclusive education – a right established in law, administered through a dedicated department, and supported by an international funding partnership. This formal framework compares favourably with many countries at similar development levels and reflects the political prioritisation that education has received in the Maldivian governance agenda.[^289]

### §6.29.2 SEN Teacher Infrastructure: Coverage and Capacity

The SEN teacher deployment framework – requiring at least one SEN-trained teacher in every mainstream school – has been operationalised with notable success in coverage terms. As of 2024, the CRPD Periodic Report confirms that every mainstream school in Maldives has at least one SEN-trained teacher. With approximately 213 schools across the country, this represents a substantial deployment achievement.[^290]

SEN teachers are trained through NIE's Special Education Needs programme – a qualification pathway that equips teachers with disability awareness, assessment techniques, learning support strategies, and individual learning plan development skills. The NIE programme has produced graduates serving across all atolls, providing a geographically distributed specialist capacity that is unusual in Maldivian service provision (where most specialised human resources concentrate in Malé).[^291]

However, the "one SEN teacher per school" standard – while a meaningful baseline – is insufficient where children with multiple disabilities or high support needs require intensive individualised support. A single SEN teacher in a school with multiple children with disabilities, including children with complex needs, is stretched beyond the capacity for effective individual support. The SEN teacher-to-student ratio has not been standardised nationally, meaning that schools with high concentrations of children with disabilities are not provided with additional SEN teacher allocation based on need.[^292]

The capacity variation among SEN teachers is also a concern. NIE training provides a generalist SEN foundation, but children with specific disabilities – autism spectrum conditions, hearing impairment requiring sign language instruction, visual impairment requiring Braille teaching, intellectual disability requiring adapted curriculum – need specialist support that generalist SEN training does not fully provide. The expansion of specialised teacher competencies beyond the generalist SEN framework has not been systematically resourced.[^293]

### §6.29.3 Individual Learning Plans: Policy Requirement and Implementation Gap

The SEN policy framework requires SEN teachers to develop individual learning plans (ILPs) for children with disabilities – personalised educational plans that set learning objectives, specify teaching adaptations, and plan support arrangements for each child. ILPs are the operational tool through which inclusive education's commitment to personalised support is supposed to be implemented.[^294]

The development and quality of ILPs in practice is highly variable. In schools with experienced and well-supported SEN teachers, ILPs are developed collaboratively with parents, reviewed regularly, and genuinely guide classroom practice. In schools where SEN teachers are newly trained, work in isolation without professional support, or face class sizes and administrative loads that limit planning time, ILPs may be produced as administrative compliance documents rather than genuine tools of educational planning.[^295]

No national ILP quality monitoring system exists. The Department of Inclusive Education conducts school visits but does not routinely assess ILP quality or track whether ILP objectives translate into learning outcomes for children with disabilities. This monitoring gap means that the system cannot distinguish between schools where inclusion is genuine and effective and schools where it is formal but hollow.[^296]

### §6.29.4 Out-of-School Children with Disabilities: The Hidden Exclusion

The most significant gap in the inclusive education picture is the unknown and estimated-to-be-high number of children with disabilities who are not in school. National data on out-of-school children is not disaggregated by disability type – a fundamental data gap that prevents the systematic identification and outreach to children with disabilities who remain outside the education system.[^297]

The structural barriers that keep children with disabilities out of school in Maldives include: physical inaccessibility of school buildings; absence of transport support for children with mobility impairments; parents' lack of awareness that their child with a disability is entitled to inclusive education; teacher and school community attitudinal barriers that discourage enrolment; and the absence of specialised early intervention services that would prepare children with developmental disabilities for school entry.[^298]

In outer atoll communities, these barriers are compounded by geographic isolation, weaker civil society presence, and lower SEN teacher capacity. A child with a significant intellectual disability in a small outer island community may face a situation where the local school's SEN teacher lacks the training and resources to support effective inclusion, alternative specialist services do not exist, and the family has no awareness that a rights-based legal framework exists to support their child's education.[^299]

The GPE THAIBA grants' explicit commitment to outreach to out-of-school children with disabilities provides a funding vehicle for systematic identification and re-enrolment efforts. The challenge is translating this commitment into an operational outreach mechanism that reaches the most remote islands – which is exactly where the highest concentrations of unidentified out-of-school children with disabilities are likely to be.[^300]

### §6.29.5 Maldives Institute for Autism: Model and Geographic Limitation

The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA), established in 2018 in Malé, provides the most specialised disability-specific education resource in the country. MIA offers centre-based early intervention and schooling for children with autism spectrum conditions, including a structured teaching programme, therapist support, parent involvement, and outreach advice to mainstream schools.[^301]

MIA represents a genuinely valuable resource – the clinical and educational expertise concentrated in the Institute has produced meaningful outcomes for enrolled children, and the Institute's outreach function provides guidance to mainland SEN teachers supporting children with autism in mainstream settings. The Maldives Autism Association's partnership with MIA has extended family support through community networks.[^302]

However, MIA's geographic limitation is stark: it serves children in Malé only. A child with autism spectrum conditions in an outer atoll has no equivalent specialised resource. The nearest service may be a SEN teacher in the local school who has received generalist training not specifically calibrated to autism support, with no specialist consultation pathway available. Telehealth and distance learning technologies could theoretically bridge this gap – MIA conducting remote assessment and coaching of outer atoll SEN teachers working with children with autism – but no systematic remote support programme has been established.[^303]

NAPDI v6 does not contain a specific strategy for extending autism-specialist services beyond Malé. Given the global trend of increasing autism identification rates as awareness and diagnostic capacity expand, this gap is likely to become more acute as identification of autism in outer atoll communities increases without corresponding service development.[^304]

### §6.29.6 Higher Education: The Invisible Ceiling

The inclusive education framework's attention effectively ends at secondary school. Maldives National University (MNU), Maldives Institute of Technology (MIT), and other tertiary education institutions do not have systematic disability support services, accessible facilities, or reasonable accommodation policies for students with disabilities who successfully complete secondary school.[^305]

This creates a structural ceiling: a student with a disability who, with SEN teacher support, navigates primary and secondary education successfully, encounters a tertiary education system without equivalent support at the critical transition to higher education. The absence of disability support at MNU and MIT functions as a disincentive to higher education aspiration for students with disabilities and as a mechanism of educational exclusion at the tertiary level.[^306]

The CRPD Periodic Report identifies tertiary education disability support as a gap without specifying a remediation plan. The ongoing Disability Act review and the next iteration of NAPDI provide vehicles for establishing tertiary disability support obligations, but implementation requires both institutional policy development within MNU and MIT and budgetary allocation for specialist staffing and accessible infrastructure.[^307]

## 6.23 Education and Disability: The Full Statistical Picture


### §6.54.1 Literacy Rates: The English Language Gap

The literacy data from Census 2022 reveals a profound educational disadvantage for persons with disabilities:

| Literacy | Persons with disability | Persons without disability |
|---|---|---|
| Dhivehi literacy | 89% | 99% |
| English literacy | 41% | 86% |

The Dhivehi literacy gap (89% vs. 99%) is significant but the English literacy gap is enormous (41% vs. 86%). In Maldives, English is the medium of instruction at secondary level and the dominant language of employment in the formal sector – particularly in tourism (resort communication), healthcare (doctor-patient interaction at IGMH), and government (policy documentation). English literacy at 41% for persons with disabilities means that only 2 in 5 can engage with the dominant language of formal employment and education – a critical labour market barrier.[^534]

Women with disabilities show slightly lower English literacy (38%) than men (46%), compounding the gendered employment disadvantage. Age-group analysis shows the sharpest English literacy gap among children (5–17 years) – where persons without disability achieve near-universal English literacy through school, while children with disabilities who are out of school or in under-resourced inclusive settings fall far behind.[^535]

### §6.54.2 School Attendance: The Surface vs. the Substance

School attendance data presents an apparent paradox: 91–97% of children with disabilities (by age bracket) attend school – seemingly close to the 98–100% rate for children without disability. However, this aggregate figure masks two critical sub-patterns:

**Out-of-school rates by disability type**: Children with certain disability types face dramatically higher out-of-school rates:

| Disability Domain | Girls not in school | Boys not in school |
|---|---|---|
| Communication | 34.5% | 50.5% |
| Self-care | 32.8% | 47.3% |
| Walking | 25.9% | 40.7% |
| Remembering | 29.3% | 38.5% |
| Seeing | 19.0% | 19.8% |
| Hearing | 10.3% | 11.0% |

Children with communication disabilities show the highest out-of-school rates – with half of boys (50.5%) and more than one-third of girls (34.5%) with communication difficulties not in school. Children with self-care difficulties (severe physical or intellectual disability) show similarly high exclusion rates. These are precisely the children with the most complex educational needs, who are most excluded from a system that does not have the infrastructure to serve them.[^536]

**Gender gap in out-of-school rates**: Boys with disabilities are consistently more likely to be out of school than girls with disabilities across all disability domains. This counterintuitive pattern – which reverses the gender parity improvement seen in non-disabled populations where girls have reached and exceeded boys in school attendance – may reflect family decisions to prioritise girls' schooling, cultural expectations about boys' engagement in economic activity, or the interaction between disability type and gender in shaping family educational strategies.[^537]

### §6.54.3 Educational Attainment: The Structural Exclusion

The educational attainment data for persons with disabilities (15+ years) reveals systemic underachievement:

| Educational Level | Persons with disability | Persons without disability |
|---|---|---|
| Never attended school | 19% | Minimal |
| Primary education only | 40% | 3% |
| Secondary education | 19% | 41% |
| Degree and above | 3% | 11% |

Nearly one-fifth (19%) of persons with disabilities have never attended school – compared to a negligible proportion of non-disabled persons. Primary education only is the peak educational attainment for 40% of persons with disabilities – proportions that reflect both decades of educational exclusion and the current incomplete implementation of inclusive education. At the top end, only 3% of persons with disabilities complete degree-level education (vs. 11% without disability) – a severe gap that limits access to the professional and managerial employment sectors.[^538]

**Educational completion rates by level** reveal where the system loses students with disabilities:

| Level | Completion (with disability) | Completion (without disability) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary (15–17 years) | 82% | 100% |
| Lower secondary (18–20 years) | 76% | 98% |
| Upper secondary (21–23 years) | 21% | 31% |

The gap widens at each successive level – from an 18-percentage-point gap at primary to a 10-point gap at upper secondary (21% vs. 31%), but from an already low base. The 21% upper secondary completion rate for persons with disabilities signals that even those who persist through lower secondary education face severe barriers to upper secondary completion – in accessible examination formats, available support at this level, and the transition to post-primary schooling.[^539]

### §6.54.4 NEET – Not in Education, Employment, or Training

NEET rates among youth with disabilities reveal the cumulative failure of education and employment systems:

| NEET Category | Persons with disability | Persons without disability |
|---|---|---|
| International youth (15–24 years) | 38% | 19% |
| Local youth (18–35 years) | 48% | 22% |
| Women (international, 15–24) | 41% | – |
| Men (international, 15–24) | 36% | – |
| Women (local, 18–35) | 56% | – |
| Men (local, 18–35) | 39% | – |

Among local youth (18–35), nearly half (48%) of persons with disabilities are in NEET status – disengaged from both education and the formal labour market. For young women with disabilities, the NEET rate among local youth reaches 56% – more than half of young disabled women are neither studying, working, nor in training.[^540]

Atoll-based youth with disabilities show higher NEET rates (41%) than their Male' counterparts (34%) – reflecting fewer education and employment options in outer atoll settings. The NEET gap between persons with and without disabilities (approximately 26 percentage points for local youth) represents the policy failure to provide either inclusive education that successfully transitions students with disabilities into employment, or employment support programmes that provide pathways from school to work.[^541]

# Part 8: Employment and Economic Participation


## 6.24 Employment and Economic Participation


### Labour Force Data

Census 2022 data shows stark disparities: the labour force participation rate stands at **41% for men with disabilities and 28% for women with disabilities**, compared to much higher rates among the general population.[^55] Economic opportunities allocated for persons with disabilities have largely benefited men, with no quotas for women in place.

### Government Employment Initiatives

The state has rolled out several interventions:

- Designating positions for persons with disabilities in State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs)
- Enrolling persons with disabilities in a National Apprenticeship programme
- Establishing a separate registry of persons with disabilities seeking job opportunities
- Developing competency profiles for job seekers with disabilities[^56]

The 2024 Regulation on Providing Employment Opportunities requires employers to assess the needs of employees with disabilities and make reasonable accommodations. However, the regulation does not mandate inclusion of persons with disabilities on the monitoring technical committee.[^57]

### Barriers to Employment

Access to jobs is greatly limited by reduced access to education. Persons with disabilities enter a competitive recruitment process at a significant disadvantage. Corruption and nepotism compound the problem: "people with either political or family connections usually become hired for jobs."[^58]

Work environments are inaccessible: employers fail to provide accessible forms of punch-in/out systems, flexible sick leave for mental health conditions, or workspace modifications. Persons with disabilities are typically hired for stereotypical support positions with limited pathways for career progression and salary increases. Job opportunities created specifically for persons with disabilities tend to be "tokenistic and lack concrete job descriptions."[^59]

Teachers with disabilities or those who developed disabilities have been **discouraged or forced to resign** – for example, by having their classrooms moved to ground floors without other accommodations, or by schools refusing to make workplace adjustments.[^60]

### Women with Disabilities and Employment

Women with disabilities were not encouraged to live independently, citing safety concerns, and typically relied on their families. This lack of financial independence has left women with disabilities more vulnerable to violence (including sexual violence) and exploitation. The prevalence of sexual violence against children with disabilities was found to be significantly high at **60%**, with a large majority of survivors being girls with disabilities.[^61]

Family members have obstructed women with disabilities from becoming employed, especially for jobs outside the island. Some FGD participants reported resigning from jobs because their husbands were unhappy with them working following pregnancy.[^62]

## 6.25 Employment and Economic Participation: From Policy to Labour Market Reality


### §6.20.1 The Employment Participation Gap – Evidence and Scale

The employment situation of persons with disabilities in Maldives represents one of the most documented gaps in the disability system. The Labour Force Participation Rate for persons with disabilities – 41% for men and 28% for women, compared to national averages substantially higher – reflects both supply-side barriers (skills gaps, disability impairment, health conditions) and demand-side discrimination (employer attitudes, physical barriers, reasonable accommodation absence).[^209]

The gender dimension is particularly marked: women with disabilities have an LFPR eleven percentage points below men with disabilities, reflecting the compounded barriers facing women with disabilities in a labour market where gender-based occupational segregation and discrimination already disadvantage women without disabilities.[^210] The MVR 100 million women's entrepreneurship fund with 25% reserved for women with disabilities represents an attempt to address this intersectional disadvantage through targeted economic inclusion – but the scale of the fund relative to the size of the employment gap among women with disabilities requires realistic assessment.

### §6.20.2 Employment Regulation 2024/R-9: Architecture and Early Implementation

Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 represents the most operationally specific disability employment policy instrument in Maldivian history. The regulation establishes:

- A registry of job-seeking persons with disabilities, enabling employers to identify candidates
- A framework for providing employment opportunities for persons with disabilities in civil service positions and state-owned enterprises
- A technical advisory structure to support implementation
- Reporting requirements for participating agencies[^211]

By August 2025, over 350 persons with disabilities had been placed in employment through government-facilitated programmes under this framework. This is a concrete outcome – names, positions, salaries, employers – that represents genuine progress beyond previous disability employment strategies that remained at the level of aspiration without placement mechanisms.[^212]

However, critical questions about the quality and sustainability of these placements have been raised. Are the placements matched to persons' qualifications, interests, and disability-related needs? Are reasonable accommodations being provided in the workplace? Are employers receiving adequate guidance and support? Are the placements in substantive roles with career development potential, or in marginal positions created to fulfil a policy metric? No published evaluation of placement quality, workplace accommodation, or sustainability exists as of 2025.[^213]

### §6.20.3 Private Sector: The Missing Dimension

The most significant structural gap in disability employment policy is the absence of any binding framework for the private sector – which employs the majority of the Maldivian formal workforce. Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 applies only to civil service and state-owned enterprises. No private sector disability quota, hiring subsidy, tax incentive for disability employment, reasonable accommodation mandate, or anti-discrimination enforcement mechanism has been enacted.[^214]

The Employment Act (2/2008) contains a general prohibition on employment discrimination on grounds of disability and a requirement for employers to provide suitable working conditions, but these provisions are effectively unenforced. No inspection regime targets disability discrimination. No disability-focused complaints mechanism exists within the labour administration. No case of private sector disability discrimination has been prosecuted.

The tourism sector – which employs a large proportion of the private sector workforce – presents particular dynamics. Resort employment in the Maldives involves physical environments (jetties, boats, beach settings) and job types (marine sports, hospitality, maintenance) that may require physical adaptations for employees with some disabilities, while offering other roles (administration, communications, services) that are potentially well-suited to persons with disabilities if physical barriers are removed. No sector-specific disability employment strategy for tourism has been developed.[^215]

## 6.26 Employment and Disability: Granular Evidence


### §6.55.1 Sectoral and Occupational Analysis

The Census 2022 employment data provides granular sector and occupation analysis that has not been previously incorporated into disability policy analysis:

**Top employment sectors for women with disabilities**:
1. Education: 17% (reflecting SEN teacher roles and general teaching positions among less severely disabled women)
2. Manufacturing of food products: 17% (home-based food production – informal economy)
3. Public administration and defence: 12%
4. Manufacturing of dress: 10% (home-based garment making)
5. Human health activities: 9%

**Top employment sectors for men with disabilities**:
1. Public administration and defence: 14%
2. Fishing and aquaculture: 10% (traditional fishing sector)
3. Accommodation: 9% (tourism sector)
4. Warehousing and support activities: 9%
5. Electricity, gas, steam and air conditioning: 7%

**Top occupations for women with disabilities**:
1. Food processing workers: 20% (predominantly informal home-based)
2. Cleaners and helpers: 14%
3. Teaching professionals: 11%
4. Handicraft and printing workers: 10%

**Top occupations for men with disabilities**:
1. Market-oriented skilled forestry/agricultural workers: 9%
2. Science and engineering professionals: 9%
3. Cleaners and helpers: 8%
4. Protective services workers: 8%

This occupational profile reveals a dual employment pattern: on one hand, persons with disabilities are represented in lower-skilled, lower-wage occupations (food processing, cleaning, handicrafts) that reflect the economy's demand for less formally credentialed labour; on the other hand, some persons with disabilities have reached professional occupations (teaching, science and engineering). The distribution reflects the wide heterogeneity of the disability population – the employment trajectories of a person with mild sensory disability and high educational attainment differ fundamentally from those of a person with severe physical disability and limited education.[^542]

### §6.55.2 Informal Employment: The Hidden Precarity

Informal employment – work outside the formal employment protection system, without contracts, benefits, or labour law coverage – is substantially more prevalent among workers with disabilities than among those without:

| Category | Informal employment rate |
|---|---|
| Women with disability | 50% |
| Men with disability | 35% |
| Women without disability | 31% |
| Men without disability | 23% |

One in two women with disabilities who are employed work in informal conditions – predominantly in home-based food production, domestic service, handicraft making, and informal care work. This 50% informal employment rate (vs. 31% for women without disability) reflects the intersection of gender-based occupational segregation and disability-related exclusion from formal employment.[^543]

Geographic analysis confirms the expected pattern: atoll-based workers with disabilities show higher informal employment rates (46%) than their Male' counterparts (34%), as formal sector employment is concentrated in the capital.[^544]

Average working hours are lower for women with disabilities (6.3 hours/week) than for women without disability (7.0 hours/week), reflecting either the shorter working hours of informal and part-time employment, or the reduced employment intensity resulting from disability-related health management needs. Atoll-based women with disabilities work the fewest hours (5.7 hours/week).[^545]

### §6.55.3 Reasons for Non-Employment

The Census data on reasons for non-employment reveals the dramatically different reality facing persons with and without disabilities:

**Persons without disability (main reasons for not seeking work)**:
- Household chores: 52%
- Engaged in studies/training: 24%
- Family/household responsibilities: 4%

**Persons with disability (main reasons)**:
- Own disability/injury: 45%
- Family/household responsibilities: 20%
- Medical purpose: 7%
- Retired/pensioner: 6%

For nearly half of non-employed persons with disabilities, their own disability or injury is the direct reason for non-employment – distinguishing them from non-disabled non-employed persons who are predominantly engaged in household and caring responsibilities. The 20% citing family/household responsibilities among non-employed persons with disabilities may reflect both disability-related isolation in the household and the gendered expectation that women with disabilities perform domestic care work in the absence of formal employment opportunities.[^546]

### §6.55.4 Employment-to-Population Ratio: The Participation Gap in Full

The employment-to-population ratio – the share of the working-age population actually in employment – most clearly quantifies the labour market participation gap:

| Category | Employment-to-population ratio |
|---|---|
| Persons with disability | 31% |
| Persons without disability | 63% |
| Women with disability | 26% |
| Men with disability | 39% |
| Women without disability | 47% |
| Men without disability | 78% |

The ratio peaks at ages 40–44 for persons with disabilities (54%), still substantially below the non-disabled peak at the same age group (73%). Women with disabilities have an employment-to-population ratio of 26% – barely above one-quarter – compared to 47% for women without disability.[^547]

The 66% of persons with disabilities who are outside the labour force entirely – neither employed nor actively seeking work – includes those whose disability precludes employment, those who have withdrawn from job search after repeated failures, and those who have never entered the labour market due to educational exclusion and skill deficits.[^548]

## 6.27 The Disability-Poverty Nexus: Evidence and Policy Implications


### §6.21.1 Disability and Poverty: Bidirectional Causation

The relationship between disability and poverty in Maldives reflects the global pattern of bidirectional causation: poverty increases the risk of acquiring disability (through malnutrition, inadequate healthcare, occupational injury, and limited access to preventive services), while disability increases the risk and depth of poverty (through reduced employment capacity, additional care costs, and social exclusion).[^216]

The poverty rate among households headed by a person with a disability is 9.6% – compared to the national poverty rate, this represents a significant differential that reflects both the direct income effects of disability (reduced labour force participation) and the indirect costs of disability (transport to access services, cost of assistive technology, medication costs, care arrangements).[^217]

This 9.6% figure is drawn from administrative data and Census cross-tabulation, but likely understates the true poverty exposure among persons with disabilities because it captures household poverty at a single point in time and does not measure the financial vulnerability (near-poverty) that disability households experience through dependence on cash transfers insufficient to cover the full cost of disability-related needs.

### §6.21.2 The Disability Extra Cost Burden

Research on disability and poverty consistently finds that persons with disabilities face a higher effective cost of living than non-disabled persons at the same income level – the "disability extra cost" – because of expenditure on assistive technologies, healthcare, personal assistance, accessible transport, and home modifications.[^218] In the Maldivian context, these extra costs are amplified by the geographic dispersion that requires inter-island travel for service access, the import cost premium for assistive technologies, and the lack of government subsidies for disability-related expenditures beyond the flat allowance.

The MVR 2,000 monthly allowance does not incorporate any assessment of individual disability-related costs. A person with a mild physical disability who is employed and faces minimal disability-related costs receives the same allowance as a person with a severe spinal cord injury who requires an imported power wheelchair, intensive personal care, specialised medical equipment, and regular specialist consultations in Malé. The flat-rate design is administratively simple but fails to address the differential extra cost burden across disability types and severity levels.[^219]

### §6.21.3 Housing Poverty and Physical Confinement

For persons with physical disabilities – particularly wheelchair users and those with severe mobility impairments – housing is a critical determinant of participation. Homes built without ramps, accessible bathrooms, or appropriate room widths effectively confine residents with disabilities within the building, preventing participation in community life.[^220]

No accessible housing standard exists in Maldivian building regulations. No priority housing allocation for persons with disabilities exists in public housing programmes. The Flat scheme – public housing provided primarily in Hulhumale as part of the government's urbanisation and housing policy – has not incorporated mandatory accessible design standards for a proportion of units. Home modification grants for persons who acquire disabilities are not systematically available.[^221]

The result is that many persons with physical disabilities in Maldives live in a state of architectural confinement – trapped in inaccessible homes in communities with inaccessible public spaces, dependent on family members for even the most basic physical mobility. This architectural poverty is invisible in standard poverty measurement, which counts household income but not the functional exclusion that comes from inaccessible environments.

## 6.28 Disability in the Maldivian Economy: Tourism, Fisheries, and Structural Exclusion


### §6.34.1 Tourism Sector and Disability Employment

Tourism dominates the Maldivian economy, generating approximately 30% of GDP directly and a larger proportion indirectly through associated retail, transport, and services. The tourism sector employs a significant proportion of the formal workforce in resort operations, guesthouses, water sports, dive schools, and associated hospitality services.[^360]

The tourism sector's relationship with disability employment is characterised by both potential and significant barriers. The potential lies in the diversity of roles within the tourism sector – many administrative, communications, customer service, and hospitality roles are compatible with a wide range of disabilities with appropriate accommodation. The barriers lie in the physical environment of tourism operations (resorts built on small islands accessible by boat, with extensive walking required), the absence of any sector-specific disability employment strategy, and the dominance of the sector by foreign-managed resort companies that do not face domestic disability employment obligations.[^361]

Tourism development has not incorporated disability accessibility as a design requirement. Maldivian resorts – marketed to global visitors as premium experiences – are designed for able-bodied guests and employees. The absence of universal design standards in resort construction means that even resorts built after the Disability Act's enactment are not accessible to guests or staff with mobility impairments. This is both a domestic disability employment barrier and a limitation on disability tourism – a global market segment of persons with disabilities and their families who seek accessible holiday destinations.[^362]

### §6.34.2 Fisheries Sector and Acquired Disability

The fisheries sector, while declining in relative economic importance, remains culturally significant and provides livelihoods for a substantial proportion of outer atoll communities. Fishing – particularly traditional pole-and-line tuna fishing – is physically demanding and carries significant occupational injury risk.[^363]

Fishing-related injuries – from boat accidents, equipment injuries, rough-water incidents, and the physical strain of manual fishing work – are a source of acquired disability among working-age men in fishing communities. No occupational injury compensation scheme specifically addressing fisheries workers with disability exists. Workers who acquire disabilities through fishing-related accidents enter the general disability system – which, as documented throughout this chapter, provides inadequate support for the working-age acquired disability population.[^364]

The occupational health infrastructure for the fisheries sector is limited, and the intersection of fisheries occupational health and disability prevention has not been addressed in disability policy planning. NAPDI v6 does not contain a fisheries-disability intersection strategy, despite the sector's relevance for outer atoll disability prevalence.[^365]

### §6.34.3 The Geography of Disability Poverty: Atolls and Economic Marginalisation

The geographic distribution of poverty in Maldives – with higher poverty rates in outer atolls than in the Malé urban agglomeration – intersects with the geographic distribution of disability (65% of persons with disabilities live outside Malé) to produce a compounded rural-disability poverty profile.[^366]

Persons with disabilities in outer atolls face poverty risks that differ from those in Malé in character as well as severity. In outer atolls, the cost-of-living is lower, but the availability of disability-specific income (the allowance) depends on registration in the NDR, which requires travel to an assessment location. Employment opportunities compatible with disability are fewer – the formal sector is smaller, most available employment is physically demanding, and the absence of ADDA vocational training means skills development pathways are unavailable.[^367]

The combination of lower average incomes, limited formal employment, minimal disability service access, and geographic isolation from support networks creates a poverty trap for persons with disabilities in outer atoll communities that is qualitatively different from the urban poverty trap – and that requires specifically designed interventions rather than urban-focused programmes extended to atoll settings with insufficient adaptation.[^368]

# Part 9: Healthcare, Rehabilitation, and Assistive Technology


## 6.29 Access to Healthcare and Rehabilitation Services


### Healthcare System Barriers

Despite universal health insurance through Aasandha, persons with disabilities face numerous barriers to healthcare. At the early stages, there is a lack of proper assessment and early identification interventions.[^63] The healthcare sector does not routinely prioritise persons with disabilities: they experience long waitlists, difficulty making appointments, and challenges securing controlled medications. Despite the disability identification card, many still wait in long queues – especially problematic for children with autism, developmental disabilities, or psychosocial disabilities who cannot tolerate extended waiting periods.[^64]

### Mental Health Services

Mental health services for persons with disabilities are severely limited. Emergency mental health care is available only at the Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) and Hulhumale' Hospital. The ER services at IGMH are ill-equipped for emergency mental health care, leading to traumatic experiences and overall hesitancy among persons with psychosocial disabilities in seeking help during emergencies.[^65]

Wait times for counselling and psychotherapy are "considerably long at public and private facilities," and persons with disabilities are "usually provided only medication with no adequate follow-ups for therapy." There is limited coverage for mental health care services by the government health insurance scheme or other insurance companies.[^66]

### Atoll-Level Healthcare Gaps

The situation is particularly acute outside Male'. There is a significant lack of specialist doctors, laboratory services, and therapeutic services in atoll and regional hospitals. Availability of psychotherapy, speech therapy, physiotherapy, and cognitive therapies is "extremely limited in the atolls and even the regional hospitals lack the human resources and infrastructure to provide these services."[^67] Most therapies cannot be conducted online. Severe cases such as suicidal attempts do not receive critical attention in the atolls due to the lack of mental health professionals, forcing families to make the difficult decision to migrate to Male'.[^68]

### Healthcare Provider Attitudes

Healthcare providers are not sufficiently trained to work with persons with disabilities. There is a "commonly held belief among healthcare professionals that medical conditions experienced by persons with disabilities are a direct result of their disability," affecting proper investigations and accurate diagnoses. Persons with invisible disabilities and neurodiverse people (especially women) face challenges in being taken seriously and tend to be misdiagnosed. Healthcare staff also "infantilise adults with disabilities," limiting their ability to make independent decisions about their care.[^69]

### Home Visit Programme

The government runs a home visit programme for bedridden patients. A 2023 study by Taj and Suzana found **high overall satisfaction** with drug prescriptions and consultations but **very low satisfaction** with procedures conducted during visits and caretaker training programmes, based on a survey of 123 caretakers in Greater Male'.[^70]

## 6.30 Healthcare for Persons with Disabilities: Access, Quality, and the NCD-Disability Interface


### §6.30.1 Aasandha as Foundation and Its Limits

Aasandha, the universal health insurance scheme introduced in 2012, provides all Maldivian residents – including persons with disabilities – with financial coverage for healthcare at the point of use. The scheme covers primary care at health centres, specialist consultations, hospital admission, and emergency care. For persons with disabilities, Aasandha removes the cost barrier that historically prevented many from accessing specialist and hospital services.[^308]

However, Aasandha's design as a universal scheme – covering everyone on equal terms – means it does not address the additional healthcare needs specifically associated with disability. It does not provide enhanced coverage for disability-related assistive technologies beyond the standard formulary. It does not fund personal assistance or home-based care. It does not fund rehabilitation services beyond the clinical setting. It does not prioritise disability access to services in the geographic allocation of healthcare resources.[^309]

The consequence is a healthcare system in which persons with disabilities theoretically have equal financial access to services but face substantially unequal practical access due to physical accessibility barriers, geographic concentration of specialist services, and the absence of disability-specific clinical protocols in primary care settings.[^310]

### §6.30.2 Physical Healthcare Accessibility

The physical accessibility of healthcare facilities is a critical barrier for persons with physical disabilities. Health centres in outer atolls – the primary care facilities for the majority of the population outside Malé – are typically built without ramps, accessible bathrooms, or adapted examination equipment. A wheelchair user attending a health centre on a small island may be unable to enter the building, access a consultation room, or use the bathroom facilities independently.[^311]

IGMH – the tertiary care hospital in Malé and the primary specialist resource – has undergone partial accessibility improvements but is not fully accessible throughout. The CRPD Periodic Report notes that healthcare facilities in outer islands remain largely inaccessible for persons with physical disabilities as a systemic pattern, not as isolated exceptions.[^312]

Regional hospitals in atoll capitals – the intermediate care tier between health centres and IGMH – vary in accessibility, with some newer facilities incorporating ramp access and other basic accessibility features, while older buildings have not been retrofitted. No national healthcare facility accessibility audit has been conducted, and no mandatory accessibility standard for new health facility construction has been established.[^313]

### §6.30.3 Rehabilitation: The Service Desert

Rehabilitation services – physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy, and prosthetics – are concentrated in IGMH in Malé. No systematic community rehabilitation programme exists in Maldives at the national level. For persons who acquire disabilities – through accidents, strokes, spinal cord injuries, or progressive neurological conditions – outside Malé, the pathway to rehabilitation is either extended travel to Malé (with all associated costs and family disruption) or no rehabilitation at all.[^314]

The consequences of rehabilitation inaccessibility are significant and compound over time. A person who acquires a disability in an outer atoll and does not receive timely rehabilitation may experience avoidable secondary health consequences – contractures, pressure injuries, depression – that a rehabilitation programme would have prevented. The window for effective rehabilitation after acute disability onset is often narrow: delayed or absent rehabilitation can mean the difference between partial or full functional recovery and permanent severe disability.[^315]

Paediatric habilitation – the early intervention and developmental support for children with disabilities – faces similar geographic inequity. While ADDA and MIA provide early intervention services for children in Malé, children with developmental disabilities in outer atolls typically have no access to habilitation services during the critical early childhood years when intervention is most effective.[^316]

### §6.30.4 Mental Health Services for Persons with Psychosocial Disabilities

The mental health service infrastructure in Maldives is reviewed in detail in Part IX of this encyclopedia (§9: Mental Health). In the disability context, the critical concern is the adequacy of mental health services for persons with psychosocial disabilities – those experiencing mental health conditions that rise to the level of functional disability – and the coordination between the mental health and disability service systems.[^317]

Persons with severe psychosocial disabilities in Maldives typically rely on IGMH's Department of Mental Health and the Mahchangolhi Mental Health Centre for clinical psychiatric treatment. These services are Malé-concentrated, understaffed relative to need, and focused on clinical treatment rather than on the social inclusion and community participation dimensions of psychosocial disability.[^318]

Community-based mental health services – which would provide ongoing support to persons with psychosocial disabilities in their communities, supporting daily functioning, social participation, and crisis prevention – do not exist at scale outside Malé. The Mental Health Act reform process, ongoing as of 2025, provides a legislative opportunity to establish community mental health services as a statutory obligation, but implementation requires sustained investment in a community mental health workforce that does not currently exist.[^319]

### §6.30.5 NCDs, Diabetes, and Acquired Disability

The intersection of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) and disability is particularly significant in the Maldivian healthcare context. Maldives has one of the highest rates of type 2 diabetes in the South Asian region – a chronic disease epidemic driven by rapid dietary and lifestyle change – and diabetes generates substantial acquired disability through three primary pathways.[^320]

**Diabetic Retinopathy**: Progressive damage to retinal blood vessels caused by poor blood glucose control leads to visual impairment and blindness. Diabetic retinopathy is the leading preventable cause of blindness in many countries with high diabetes prevalence. In Maldives, ophthalmology services – including the regular diabetic eye screening that is the primary prevention strategy – are concentrated in Malé, meaning that persons with diabetes in outer atolls do not receive the annual eye examinations that would identify and treat retinopathy before vision loss becomes severe.[^321]

**Peripheral Neuropathy and Foot Complications**: Diabetes-related nerve damage (peripheral neuropathy) leads to loss of sensation in the feet, creating risk of undetected injury, infection, and ultimately limb amputation. Diabetes foot care – regular professional foot examination, patient self-care education, and prompt treatment of infection – is not systematically available in outer island health centres. The number of diabetes-related amputations in Maldives is not systematically reported but is known to be a significant contributor to acquired physical disability.[^322]

**Stroke and Cardiovascular Disability**: Diabetes and associated cardiovascular disease are major risk factors for stroke. Stroke is a leading cause of acquired physical and cognitive disability. Stroke rehabilitation services – physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy – are concentrated in IGMH, creating the same geographic access barriers for stroke survivors in outer atolls as for persons with other acquired disabilities.[^323]

The absence of systematic coordination between the NCD prevention and management programme and the disability service system means that the disability implications of NCD progression are not being addressed at either the prevention or consequence management stage. A person who acquires diabetes-related visual impairment or amputation does not automatically receive disability registration support, assistive technology, vocational rehabilitation, or disability allowance information – these connections must be navigated individually through a system that does not systematically link NCD and disability case management.[^324]

## 6.31 Disability and Healthcare: From Aasandha Coverage to Rehabilitation Access


### §6.64.1 Reproductive and Maternal Health for Women with Disabilities

The HRCM Shadow Report and UNPRPD Situational Analysis both identify reproductive and maternal health as a specific, underaddressed gap for women with disabilities. This gap encompasses:

**Family planning access**: Women with disabilities – including those with physical disabilities, intellectual disabilities, and sensory disabilities – do not receive systematically accessible family planning information and contraceptive services. Healthcare providers may not initiate conversations about family planning with women with disabilities, reflecting assumptions about their sexual activity, reproductive desires, or capacity to parent. The HRCM FGD findings document healthcare provider bias affecting reproductive decision-making for women with disabilities.[^616]

**Antenatal and postnatal care**: Maternity services at health centres and IGMH are not systematically designed for women with physical, sensory, or intellectual disabilities. Examination tables are not height-adjustable for wheelchair transfer; MSL interpretation is not available for deaf women during antenatal consultations; written birth plans and discharge information are not provided in accessible formats; and breastfeeding support is not adapted for mothers with physical disabilities affecting hand/arm function.[^617]

**Obstetric risk management**: Women with physical disabilities – particularly spinal cord injuries, lower limb paralysis, and severe musculoskeletal conditions – face specific obstetric risks (autonomic dysreflexia during labour in spinal injury, cardiac stress in some conditions) that require obstetrician-specialist coordination. The concentration of obstetric care at IGMH in Malé, while necessary for complex cases, creates geographic access barriers for women with disabilities in outer atolls who are at risk and cannot access timely specialist care.[^618]

### §6.64.2 Mental Health Crisis Response

The HRCM Shadow Report documents that mental health crisis response – emergency psychiatric care – is not accessible in a disability-inclusive manner. Crisis response services concentrate in Malé; outer atoll residents experiencing acute psychiatric crises have access only to primary care health workers without specialist training. The intersection of the Mental Health Act's involuntary hospitalisation provisions and the geographic concentration of psychiatric care means that the first response to acute psychiatric crisis in an outer atoll community may be police involvement, involuntary transport to Malé, and hospitalisation – a cascade that violates both the CRPD's liberty principles and the wellbeing of the person in crisis.[^619]

For persons with psychosocial disabilities who are stable but need ongoing community support – counselling, medication management, peer support – community mental health services are essentially absent in outer atoll settings. Telepsychiatry has been piloted during COVID-19 but has not been established as a funded ongoing programme. The result is that most persons with psychosocial disabilities outside Malé manage their conditions without professional support, relying on family, religious resources, and traditional coping mechanisms.[^620]

## 6.32 Early Intervention and the First Five Years


### §6.44.1 The Critical Window of Early Childhood

The developmental research evidence is clear: early intervention for children with disabilities and developmental delays in the first five years of life – when brain plasticity is at its peak – produces the greatest long-term impact on functional outcomes, educational achievement, and adult independence. Early identification and intervention can significantly reduce the long-term support needs of children with many disability types, including autism, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, and developmental language disorders.[^443]

In Maldives, early intervention services are minimal and geographically concentrated. The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA) provides early intervention for children with autism in Malé. IGMH's paediatric service provides some early intervention for children with developmental disabilities identified through clinical pathways. Beyond these two resources – both Malé-based – early intervention for children with disabilities in the Maldives does not systematically exist.[^444]

The absence of early intervention in outer atolls means that children with developmental disabilities in these communities reach school age without the foundational skills development that early intervention would have provided. The SEN teacher who receives such a child in the school system must then address, in a mainstream classroom, the developmental gaps that might have been largely resolved through two to three years of early intervention – a less effective and more resource-intensive pathway than timely early intervention would have been.[^445]

### §6.44.2 Newborn Hearing Screening and Early Identification

Universal newborn hearing screening – the practice of testing all newborns for hearing loss before hospital discharge – is the global standard for early identification of Deaf and hard-of-hearing children. Early identification of hearing loss enables earlier fitting of hearing aids or cochlear implants (where appropriate and desired), earlier access to sign language input for Deaf children, and earlier development of communication skills during the critical early language acquisition window.[^446]

Maldives does not have a national universal newborn hearing screening programme. The absence of such a programme means that Deaf children are typically not identified until they fail to meet developmental communication milestones – often between 18 months and 3 years of age – resulting in a significant delay in access to language and communication support during the most critical developmental period.[^447]

The infrastructure for universal newborn hearing screening is not complex – the primary technology is a portable otoacoustic emissions (OAE) device that can be operated by trained health workers. A national programme could be implemented through the existing IGMH maternity unit in Malé and extended to regional hospitals in atoll capitals. The question is not technical feasibility but policy prioritisation and budgetary commitment within the maternal and child health system.[^448]

### §6.44.3 Autism Identification: Expanding Awareness and Assessment

The identification of autism spectrum conditions in Maldives has improved substantially since the establishment of MIA in 2018, which created a clinical referral pathway and a growing community of practice among SEN teachers familiar with autism indicators. However, autism identification in outer atolls remains limited – children showing early autism indicators (limited eye contact, absence of pointing, repetitive behaviours, social interaction difficulties) may not be identified by community health workers or health centre staff who lack autism-specific screening training.[^449]

The implementation of standardised autism screening tools (such as the M-CHAT for toddlers) in routine developmental checks at child health clinics would create an identification pathway that does not require specialist assessment. Children identified through screening could then be referred to MIA or regional health professionals for formal diagnosis and intervention planning. This cascade model – community screening, targeted referral, specialist assessment – is the standard approach in health systems with limited specialist capacity and is applicable to the Maldivian context.[^450]

## 6.33 Technology, Assistive Technology Innovation, and Digital Accessibility


### §6.45.1 Assistive Technology as a Rights Issue

Assistive technology – the full range of devices, equipment, and software that enable persons with disabilities to function with greater independence, participate in education and employment, and engage in daily life – is explicitly addressed in CRPD Article 20 (Personal Mobility) and Article 26 (Habilitation and Rehabilitation). Access to appropriate assistive technology is not a luxury but a rights issue: without a wheelchair, a blind person without their cane, a deaf person without a hearing aid or signed communication, many disability-related functional limitations cannot be addressed.[^451]

Assistive technology provision in the Maldives is severely inadequate in both scale and quality. ADDA coordinates distribution of basic assistive devices (wheelchairs, crutches, white canes) primarily in Malé. The range of devices available is limited. Provision is demand-driven rather than needs-assessed – persons who know about the service and travel to access it receive devices; persons in outer atolls who do not know about the service or cannot travel do not.[^452]

No national assistive technology needs assessment has been conducted. No systematic data exists on the unmet assistive technology need in the population – the number of persons with physical disabilities who need a wheelchair but do not have one; the number of persons with visual impairments who need a white cane; the number of hearing-impaired persons who could benefit from a hearing aid. This data absence makes it impossible to quantify the problem or to plan systematic provision.[^453]

### §6.45.2 Digital Assistive Technologies and Accessibility

The rapid development of digital assistive technologies – screen readers, text-to-speech software, speech recognition, communication augmentative devices, smart home adaptations – offers significant potential for persons with disabilities in Maldives. Smartphone-based assistive technologies, many available through free or low-cost applications, have democratised access to tools previously available only to residents of well-resourced countries.[^454]

Persons with visual impairments in Maldives use smartphone screen readers (VoiceOver on iOS, TalkBack on Android) to navigate digital information, communicate, and access services. Persons who are non-verbal may use AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication) applications on tablets as communication aids. These technologies work in Dhivehi (to varying degrees depending on language support) and provide functionality that is affordable and portable even in outer atoll settings.[^455]

However, the effective use of digital assistive technologies requires digital literacy – the skills to navigate devices and applications that many persons with disabilities, particularly older persons and those in outer atoll communities with lower digital exposure, may not possess. No national digital literacy programme for persons with disabilities exists. The ADDA vocational training includes basic computing but does not systematically incorporate digital assistive technology training.[^456]

### §6.45.3 Telehealth and Remote Service Delivery

Telehealth – the delivery of healthcare and rehabilitation services through video and digital platforms – offers significant potential for reducing the geographic access barriers that prevent persons with disabilities in outer atolls from accessing specialist services. A physiotherapist at IGMH conducting a telehealth session with a post-stroke patient in an outer atoll, guided by the local health centre worker, extends rehabilitation access without requiring the patient to travel to Malé.[^457]

COVID-19 accelerated telehealth adoption across the health system – teleconsultations were implemented at scale as an infection control measure. However, this acceleration was not accompanied by systematic disability accessibility assessment. Video teleconsultations require visual and auditory participation that may not be accessible to Deaf patients without sign language interpretation, or to patients with cognitive disabilities who struggle with screen-mediated communication.[^458]

The potential of telehealth for disability service delivery has not been systematically explored in the Maldivian policy context. ADDA has not developed a telehealth component for vocational guidance, assistive technology assessment, or psychosocial support. The CRPD's Article 26 requirement for habilitation and rehabilitation services to be as close as possible to the person's own community suggests telehealth as a legitimate complement to in-person services in the Maldivian archipelagic context.[^459]

# Part 10: Accessibility of Infrastructure and Public Services


## 6.34 Accessibility of Infrastructure and Public Services


### Built Environment

Despite the Disability Act's provisions on accessibility, compliance is poor. The framework lacks compliance documents, and inspections are insufficient. At the atoll level, the responsibility to ensure adherence to the Construction Act (4/2017) falls to local councils, but when central state institutions do not comply, local councils lack the authority to halt construction.[^71]

Key findings from the 2024 UNPRPD assessment on physical accessibility:

- **Public buildings**: Most lack ramps, large doorways, or lifts to accommodate wheelchairs. When ramps are present, they are usually at the back of buildings. Local council premises and police buildings are frequently inaccessible.[^72]
- **Mosques**: Structures are usually not wheelchair accessible and lack tactile features to guide blind persons toward the *Qibla* and worshippers' rows.[^73]
- **Roads**: Limited wheelchair accessibility, lack of streetlights for persons with night blindness, and vehicles frequently parked on ramps or accessible pavement points. *Joalifathi* (woven chairs) and trees planted in the middle of pavements create obstacles. There is no adequate policy on designated parking for persons with disabilities.[^74]
- **Public spaces**: Parks, beaches, and stadiums are physically inaccessible, preventing children with disabilities from attending school activities. Parks lack accessible toilets, walkways, and seating.[^75]

### Housing

Social housing and other government housing schemes generally do not include considerations for persons with disabilities. Buildings lack accessible toilets, have thresholds in hallways, ramps that are absent or too narrow, and no tactile identification of building floors. Where considerations exist, housing points are allocated to the parent/guardian rather than the person with the disability. There are **no supported living housing options** to promote independent living, reflecting the expectation that all persons with disabilities will live with parents or family members.[^76]

Persons with disabilities who need to rent apartments face active discrimination from landlowners who believe they will not be able to pay rent money.[^77]

### Transportation

All forms of transportation pose challenges. Buses in Male' and Hulhumale' are available free of charge upon presenting the disability card, but many buses are not accessible – long waiting times for accessible buses, damaged ramps, and lack of accessible route displays are common problems. There are **no accessible taxis** available, aside from vehicles operated by MAPD (Maldives Association of Persons with Disabilities).[^78]

The Raajje Transport Link (RTL) speedboat ferry system is cheaper but not fully accessible: harbours and jetties are not accessible, wheelchairs cannot move independently on speedboats, and the online ticketing app is inaccessible. Priority seating enforcement is inadequate.[^79]

Air transport, perceived as the most accessible mode, still presents challenges: the deaf community faces barriers accessing flight information without sign language interpretation, airport terminals are not fully accessible, there are no accessible private toilets, and policies on automatic wheelchairs (containing lithium batteries) are inconsistently applied.[^80]

### Digital Accessibility

Most websites and online applications of state institutions do not include accessibility features. The *eFaas* digital identity platform, while promising, is currently inaccessible to persons with disabilities. Laws and regulations do not exist in multiple languages and easy-to-read formats. The Thaana script presents additional challenges for blind and visually impaired persons accessing online content – special software is needed but there is limited availability of and awareness about such tools.[^81]

### Banking Services

Banking is a considerable challenge. Persons with disabilities living on remote islands where the bank branch is on another island, or who cannot independently sign documents, are excluded from receiving bank cards. Online banking applications are inaccessible. The lack of sign language interpreters across all sectors remains "a substantial barrier for deaf persons to access services."[^82]

## 6.35 Accessibility: Physical, Transport, Information, and Communication Dimensions


### §6.18.1 The Structural Accessibility Deficit

Accessibility – the physical, transport, and information infrastructure that enables persons with disabilities to participate in public life – represents one of the most pervasive and visible failures of disability policy implementation in Maldives. Despite being required under the Disability Act (2010) and mandated by CRPD Article 9, binding accessibility standards have not been enacted as enforceable legislation during the fifteen years since the Act's passage.[^189]

The 2025 Situational Analysis confirmed that public buildings – including schools, health centres, government offices, courts, and community halls – remain largely inaccessible to persons with physical, visual, or hearing disabilities across the country. The pattern is better in Malé, where some recent infrastructure development has incorporated ramps and accessible features, but even in the capital, compliance is inconsistent and not subject to systematic audit or enforcement.[^190]

### §6.18.2 Physical Infrastructure Accessibility

The primary physical accessibility barriers for persons with mobility impairments are: steps at building entrances without ramp alternatives; narrow doorways that prevent wheelchair passage; inaccessible toilets and bathrooms in public buildings; inadequate pathway surfacing in outer island communities; and building designs that require users to traverse distances or terrains that are impassable for wheelchair users or persons with limited mobility.[^191]

Local councils are responsible for public infrastructure in their jurisdictions, and the 2025 Situational Analysis found that council accessibility investment is highly variable. Some councils – particularly in Malé and the larger atoll capital islands – have made partial accessibility improvements in response to ADDA guidance or civil society advocacy. The majority, particularly in smaller and more remote islands, have made no systematic accessibility investment. No national accessibility audit has been conducted, and no standardised accessibility compliance framework for local government has been established.[^192]

The Sinamale Bridge (2018), connecting Malé and Hulhule, was a landmark infrastructure investment but was designed without comprehensive accessibility standards throughout. Its footpaths are navigable by wheelchair users in some sections but not others, and the bridge lacks tactile guidance strips for visually impaired pedestrians along its full length. This represents a missed opportunity: a high-profile infrastructure project constructed without applying the accessibility standards that the Disability Act had mandated for eight years.[^193]

### §6.18.3 Inter-Island Transport: The Mobility Barrier for 65%

The most structurally distinctive accessibility challenge in Maldives is inter-island transport. The country's geographic character – 187 inhabited islands across 26 atolls, connected primarily by inter-island ferry services and seaplanes – means that for most persons with disabilities, the journey to access any specialist service (ADDA vocational training, IGMH rehabilitation, NDR registration assessment, specialist healthcare, court proceedings, higher education) requires inter-island travel.[^194]

Ferry services – the primary transport mode for the majority of the population – are not wheelchair accessible. Ferry landings typically require boarding small boats and navigating uneven surfaces, presenting serious challenges for wheelchair users and persons with mobility impairments. No ferry accessibility standard or retrofitting programme exists. Seaplane services – used for faster journeys to remote atolls – are physically inaccessible to wheelchair users. For the 65% of persons with disabilities living outside Malé, transport inaccessibility constitutes a compounded barrier to virtually every formal service the state provides.[^195]

The development of fast ferry routes and inter-atoll transport infrastructure – part of the government's connectivity agenda – has not been accompanied by disability accessibility requirements. This represents a systematic missed opportunity: as new transport infrastructure is built, the default design does not incorporate disability accessibility, and retrofitting after construction is invariably more costly and less effective.[^196]

### §6.18.4 Information and Communication Accessibility

Information accessibility for persons with visual, hearing, and cognitive disabilities is severely limited in Maldives across all communication channels:

**Sign Language**: Maldives Sign Language (MSL) is used by the deaf community but has not been accorded legal recognition as an official language. Government press conferences, parliamentary proceedings, public emergency broadcasts, and health campaigns are not routinely interpreted into MSL. The number of trained and certified sign language interpreters in Maldives is insufficient to meet demand – the country lacks a formal interpreter certification pathway and national sign language curriculum. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the absence of MSL interpretation in public health communications left the deaf community without timely access to critical health information.[^197]

**Braille and Alternative Formats**: Braille resources and audio-description services for public information are absent or negligible. Government publications, legal documents, school textbooks, and health information materials are not routinely available in Braille. Easy Read materials – simplified, visual versions of complex documents accessible to persons with intellectual disabilities – are not produced. The 2023 presidential election breakthrough (visually impaired voters using a tactile template to cast ballots independently) demonstrates that accessible solutions are achievable when political commitment exists, but this remains an isolated example rather than a systemic approach.[^198]

**Digital Accessibility**: Government websites have not been systematically assessed against WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) standards. OneGov.mv – the central government services portal – has not published an accessibility statement. Mobile applications used for government service delivery have not been tested for screen reader compatibility. As government services increasingly migrate to digital platforms, the absence of digital accessibility standards risks creating a new layer of exclusion for persons with visual, cognitive, and hearing disabilities.[^199]

# Part 11: Disability by Type and Population Group


## 6.36 Psychosocial Disability


### §6.27.1 Defining the Population

Psychosocial disability – disability arising from mental health conditions that result in functional impairment in daily life – is among the least visible and most underserved categories in the Maldivian disability system. Persons with psychosocial disabilities include those with severe depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, and other conditions that chronically limit cognitive, social, and occupational functioning.[^268]

The invisibility of psychosocial disability in Maldives has multiple causes. Stigma associated with mental health conditions – stronger in many communities than stigma associated with physical disability – discourages self-identification and service seeking. The NDR's classification framework and assessment protocols are calibrated to physical and sensory disabilities, meaning that persons with psychosocial disabilities face additional barriers to registration beyond those experienced by other disability groups. The concept of "disability" in public discourse is most readily associated with physical impairment – wheelchairs, blindness, deafness – rather than with the functional limitations of mental health conditions.[^269]

### §6.27.2 Service Intersection: Mental Health and Disability Systems

In Maldives, mental health services (concentrated in IGMH's psychiatry unit and the Mahchangolhi Mental Health Centre) and disability services (concentrated in ADDA) operate in largely separate systems with limited coordination. A person with severe psychosocial disability may be known to the mental health system (as a patient receiving psychiatric treatment) while remaining unregistered in the NDR and unknown to ADDA.[^270]

The consequence is a service gap: persons with psychosocial disabilities may receive psychiatric treatment but not disability-specific support – vocational training, assistive technology, respite care, or disability allowance. The mental health system's focus on clinical treatment leaves unaddressed the social and economic consequences of psychosocial disability that are the domain of the disability system.[^271]

The ongoing mental health legislative reform process – referenced in the State Party's CRPD Periodic Report – presents an opportunity to establish explicit linkages between the mental health and disability systems. Legislative reform that ensures persons with psychosocial disabilities are recognised as disability rights-holders, not merely mental health patients, would be a significant step toward addressing this coordination gap.[^272]

### §6.27.3 The Moosa et al. (2024) Phenomenological Evidence

The Moosa et al. (2024) phenomenological study – drawn from 15 interviews with persons with disabilities in Maldives – provides qualitative evidence of lived experience that statistical data cannot capture. The study's central finding is captured in the phrase "environment is physically disabling" – the recognition that it is not intrinsic impairment but environmental and social barriers that generate the most significant limitations on participation.[^273]

This finding aligns with the CRPD's social model orientation and has direct implications for policy design. If the primary driver of disability-related disadvantage is environmental and social barriers – inaccessible buildings, transport exclusion, attitudinal discrimination, information inaccessibility – then disability policy should focus primarily on barrier removal rather than impairment management.[^274]

The phenomenological evidence also illuminates the social experience of disability in Maldivian communities: the shame associated with being identified as disabled; the isolation experienced by persons confined to inaccessible homes; the dependency on family members that constrains personal autonomy; and the contrast between the formal rights framework (Disability Act, CRPD ratification) and the daily lived reality of exclusion. This qualitative dimension is essential for understanding why policy progress does not automatically translate into improved lived experience, and what kinds of social change – attitudinal, community-level, interpersonal – must accompany formal policy reform.[^275]

## 6.37 Intellectual Disability: The Most Underserved Category


### §6.37.1 Defining and Identifying Intellectual Disability in the Maldivian Context

Intellectual disability – characterised by significant limitations in intellectual functioning (reasoning, learning, problem-solving) and adaptive behaviour, originating before age 18 – is among the most underserved and least visible disability categories in Maldivian policy and services. In the absence of dedicated epidemiological surveys, the prevalence of intellectual disability in Maldives is estimated through international benchmarks and cross-atoll comparison with Census data, but no robust national prevalence figure exists.[^398]

The 2022 Census WG-SS methodology captures cognitive difficulty (the "cognition" domain) but is not designed to identify intellectual disability specifically – it captures functional difficulties in cognitive tasks without applying the diagnostic criteria (standardised IQ testing, adaptive behaviour assessment) used in clinical identification. As a result, intellectual disability is substantially undercounted in Census data, and persons with intellectual disabilities are underrepresented in the NDR.[^399]

Clinical identification of intellectual disability in Maldives is concentrated in Malé – through the IGMH paediatric service and, for children, through SEN teacher assessment processes within schools. For children in outer atolls, clinical intellectual disability assessment may never occur – the child may be known to the school's SEN teacher as having significant learning difficulties, but without formal psychological assessment. This means that many adults with intellectual disabilities in outer atoll communities have never received a formal diagnosis, are not registered in the NDR, and receive neither the disability allowance nor any disability-specific support.[^400]

### §6.37.2 Institutional Provision and Its Gaps

Children and adults with intellectual disabilities in Maldives have minimal dedicated service provision. The Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA) serves children with autism spectrum conditions – a subset of intellectual and developmental disabilities – but provides no direct services for children with non-autism intellectual disabilities. No dedicated day programme, residential support, or skills development resource for adults with intellectual disabilities exists in the formal disability service system.[^401]

ADDA's vocational training programme is predominantly oriented toward persons with physical disabilities and does not systematically accommodate the support needs of persons with intellectual disabilities – who require structured, supported, and repetitive learning approaches, reduced work pace, job coaching, and ongoing supervision that the current vocational training model does not provide.[^402]

In many families, adults with intellectual disabilities remain permanently in family care, with no prospect of increasing independence, skills development, or community participation. The expectation that intellectual disability means lifelong total dependence on family – rather than supported semi-independence – is pervasive and is not actively challenged by the disability system, which provides no pathway for persons with intellectual disabilities toward greater autonomy.[^403]

### §6.37.3 Inclusion in Education: Achievements and Realistic Limits

The Education Act (2021) and the Inclusive Education Policy (2013) apply to children with intellectual disabilities as to all children with disabilities. SEN teachers are trained to develop individual learning plans for children with intellectual disabilities, to adapt curriculum expectations, and to support differentiated learning within mainstream classrooms.[^404]

In practice, inclusion of children with significant intellectual disabilities in mainstream classrooms requires a level of SEN teacher capacity, classroom adaptation, and school community readiness that is not consistently available – particularly in small outer island schools. A child with moderate to severe intellectual disability in a mainstream classroom without an assigned support worker, in a class of 30 students with a single teacher and one SEN teacher managing the whole school, is not meaningfully included even if formally enrolled.[^405]

The Maldivian inclusive education framework does not have a systematic strategy for children with the most complex and high-support intellectual disabilities. The absence of a continuum of educational provision – from supported mainstream inclusion, through enhanced resource provision, to specialist settings for the most complex needs – means that the choice is between under-resourced mainstream inclusion and total exclusion from education. Neither option delivers the quality education that inclusive education policy aspires to.[^406]

### §6.37.4 Guardianship and Legal Capacity for Persons with Intellectual Disabilities

Persons with intellectual disabilities are among those most directly affected by the guardianship framework discussed in §6.19. They are the population most at risk of having their legal capacity permanently extinguished through family-controlled guardianship arrangements without judicial oversight or periodic review.[^407]

The development of supported decision-making frameworks – the reform priority identified in the CRPD Periodic Report for 2026–2028 – is most urgently needed for persons with intellectual disabilities, for whom the right to make decisions about daily life, relationships, healthcare, and living arrangements is most consistently denied by the current legal framework. A supported decision-making system for persons with intellectual disabilities must include capacity assessment protocols, support provision mechanisms, safeguards against exploitation by support providers, and a review mechanism to ensure that support arrangements remain appropriate as the person's circumstances change.[^408]

## 6.38 Deaf Community and Sign Language Rights


### §6.38.1 Maldives Sign Language: Status and Community

The Deaf community in Maldives constitutes a distinct linguistic and cultural community with Maldives Sign Language (MSL) as its primary language. MSL has developed organically within the Deaf community over generations, incorporating elements of community signing, international sign language influences, and the visual-gestural communication adaptations specific to the Maldivian cultural context.[^409]

The size of the Deaf community in Maldives is not precisely known. Census 2022 WG-SS data captures persons with hearing difficulty but does not distinguish between Deaf persons who use MSL as their primary language and persons with acquired or late-onset hearing loss who communicate primarily in spoken Dhivehi. Community estimates suggest several hundred to over a thousand Deaf MSL users, constituting a small but linguistically distinct community.[^410]

The CRPD (Article 21) specifically recognises sign languages as languages, requiring states to recognise and promote the use of sign languages. Maldives Sign Language is not accorded legal recognition as an official language in Maldives as of 2025, and MSL is not included in the Constitution's language provisions. This absence of legal recognition has practical consequences: MSL cannot formally claim the same communication rights as spoken Dhivehi in government proceedings, education, or public services.[^411]

### §6.38.2 Sign Language Interpretation: The Critical Infrastructure Gap

The practical manifestation of MSL's marginalisation is the shortage of trained sign language interpreters. No government-funded sign language interpretation service exists for general public access. Interpretation services are provided informally through Deaf community networks and, in limited contexts, by individual interpreters contracted for specific events.[^412]

The consequence of this shortage is that Deaf persons face systematic exclusion from public participation in virtually every domain where communication is the medium of access:
- Government press conferences and public information are not interpreted into MSL
- Court proceedings do not routinely provide MSL interpretation
- Medical consultations, hospital admissions, and health centre visits are conducted without systematic sign language accommodation
- Parliamentary debates and committee hearings are not interpreted into MSL
- Emergency broadcasts – including COVID-19 health communications – are delivered without MSL interpretation[^413]

The absence of a formal interpreter training and certification programme in Maldives is the structural root of the shortage. Without a curriculum, a qualification framework, and a career pathway, the supply of trained interpreters cannot keep pace with demand. The Maldives Deaf Association has advocated for formal sign language interpreter training through NIE or a designated institution, but this has not been operationalised.[^414]

### §6.38.3 Deaf Education: Sign-Bilingual Approaches vs. Oral Method

Children who are Deaf from birth or early childhood require educational provision that addresses their language needs – specifically, access to instruction in a visual language (MSL) that is accessible without hearing. The Maldivian inclusive education framework does not specify a Deaf education methodology, leaving schools to accommodate Deaf children through the same SEN teacher framework used for other disabilities.[^415]

In the absence of sign-bilingual education – which would provide instruction through MSL as the primary language of instruction, with Dhivehi (written) as a second language – many Deaf children are educated through an oral method that assumes residual hearing or lip-reading ability. For children with profound hearing loss for whom oral communication is inaccessible, this approach results in educational deprivation despite formal school enrolment.[^416]

The Maldives Deaf Association and BVISM have advocated for MSL-medium education as a right, not as a remedial intervention – recognising MSL as a full language capable of medium of instruction in any subject, not as a communication accommodation for a deficit. This advocacy position aligns with CRPD Article 24, which recognises the right of Deaf children to instruction in sign languages.[^417]

## 6.39 Older Persons with Disabilities: The Intersection of Ageing and Impairment


### §6.39.1 Ageing, Disability, and Increasing Prevalence

The intersection of ageing and disability in Maldives reflects a global pattern: disability prevalence increases sharply with age, with the over-60 population experiencing substantially higher rates of functional limitation than younger cohorts. Census 2022 data confirms this pattern – a disproportionate share of persons with disabilities are in older age categories, with conditions including age-related visual and hearing loss, mobility limitations from musculoskeletal deterioration, and cognitive impairment from dementia and Alzheimer's disease.[^418]

Maldives is experiencing a demographic transition – the proportion of the population over 60 is increasing as life expectancy rises and fertility rates decline. This demographic shift will increase the absolute and relative prevalence of disability in the population over the coming decades, creating growing demand for disability services at a time when the disability system is already significantly under-resourced.[^419]

The disability system in Maldives is primarily oriented toward working-age persons with disabilities – its employment, vocational training, and economic inclusion programmes target persons of working age. Older persons with age-related disabilities are served by the disability allowance (which has no age ceiling) but not by the employment and inclusion programmes, and may fall into a gap between the disability system and the elderly care system that the Elderly and Ageing chapter of this encyclopedia (§7) documents separately.[^420]

### §6.39.2 Dementia and Cognitive Disability: The Coming Challenge

Dementia – including Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia – represents a growing and largely unaddressed disability category in Maldives. No national dementia strategy exists. No systematic dementia identification or care pathway has been developed. The memory care residential provisions that higher-income countries provide for persons with advanced dementia do not exist in Maldives.[^421]

The primary care model for persons with dementia in Maldives is family-based – spouses and adult children providing the intensive daily care that severe dementia requires. As described in §6.22, this family care burden falls disproportionately on women. The specific psychological and physical demands of dementia caregiving – the behavioural challenges of moderate-to-severe dementia, the 24-hour supervision requirement, the grief of watching a family member lose cognitive capacity – are not recognised or supported by the formal disability or elderly care system.[^422]

The intersection of dementia and legal capacity is particularly complex. As persons with dementia lose cognitive function, the question of supported decision-making and legal capacity becomes acute – a person with moderate dementia may retain capacity for some decisions while requiring support for others. The Maldivian guardianship framework, with its all-or-nothing approach to legal capacity, is particularly ill-suited to the graduated and fluctuating capacity characteristics of dementia.[^423]

## 6.40 Youth with Disabilities: Transition Planning and the School-to-Life Gap


### §6.40.1 The Transition Gap

The transition from education to adult life is a critical and poorly supported period for young persons with disabilities in Maldives. Young people completing secondary education with disabilities face a transition gap – the absence of systematic planning, support, and pathways between school and adult life – that can result in a sharp regression in independence and participation after leaving the supported environment of school.[^424]

Transition planning – the development of individual plans for post-school education, employment, living arrangements, and social participation for young people with disabilities – is not a systematic feature of the Maldivian SEN framework. SEN teachers may informally support students approaching school-leaving age with transition considerations, but no formal transition planning process, inter-agency coordination mechanism, or dedicated transition support service exists.[^425]

The absence of transition planning means that young people with disabilities leave school without a plan for what comes next. ADDA's vocational training is available for some – but capacity is limited, the programme is Malé-concentrated, and it does not serve persons with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities effectively. Further education at MNU or MIT is theoretically available but lacks disability support. Employment is possible for some but faces structural barriers as described in §6.20. The result, for many young people with disabilities, is a post-school period of reduced activity, withdrawal from community participation, and increased dependence on family.[^426]

### §6.40.2 Young Persons with Disabilities and Digital Inclusion

Young persons with disabilities in Maldives – like their peers without disabilities – are digital natives for whom smartphone and internet access is the primary medium of social connection, information access, and entertainment. Digital inclusion represents a significant opportunity for disability inclusion: digital platforms can provide information in accessible formats, enable remote participation in education and employment, and connect isolated outer island community members to peer networks and support.[^427]

However, digital inclusion for persons with disabilities requires accessible design – websites and apps that work with screen readers for visually impaired users, captioned video content for deaf users, simplified interfaces for users with cognitive disabilities. The absence of digital accessibility standards in Maldives means that many of the most important digital services – OneGov.mv, education platforms, health information portals – are not accessible to persons with relevant disabilities.[^428]

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital service delivery across multiple sectors – healthcare (telehealth), education (remote learning), and government services (online applications). These digital channels were implemented without systematic disability accessibility assessment, creating a new layer of inaccessibility that compounds existing barriers. The shift toward digital government services has particular consequences for older persons with disabilities and persons with intellectual disabilities who may face additional barriers to digital engagement.[^429]

## 6.41 Migrant Workers with Disabilities in Maldives


### §6.41.1 An Invisible Population

Maldives hosts a large population of migrant workers – primarily from Bangladesh, India, Sri Lanka, and Nepal – who provide labour across the construction, hospitality, domestic service, and fisheries sectors. The NDR and disability allowance system does not cover non-Maldivian residents, meaning that migrant workers who acquire disabilities while working in Maldives fall outside the disability support system entirely.[^430]

The intersection of migrant worker vulnerability and disability is particularly acute in the construction sector, where occupational injury risk is high and labour protections are weakly enforced. A migrant construction worker who sustains a disabling injury – spinal cord damage, traumatic brain injury, amputation – in Maldives faces a situation in which: employer liability may be denied or contested; no state disability support is available; repatriation to the home country may be complicated by the severity of injury; and the home country's disability system may itself be inadequate for the level of need.[^431]

The CRPD applies to all persons in a state's jurisdiction regardless of nationality or immigration status. Maldives' CRPD obligations therefore extend to migrant workers with disabilities within its territory. The absence of any policy, programme, or data collection addressing this population represents a gap in CRPD compliance that has not been raised in domestic disability policy discourse.[^432]

## 6.42 Children with Disabilities in Alternative Care and the Child Protection Interface


### §6.49.1 Intersection of Disability and Child Protection Systems

Children with disabilities in Maldives exist at the intersection of two separate but interacting systems: the disability system (ADDA, NDR, disability allowance, inclusive education) and the child protection system (CFPS under MoSFD, Child Protection Act 2019, child protection case management). When a child with a disability comes into contact with the child protection system – because of abuse, neglect, parental inability to care, or parental illness – neither system is adequately prepared to serve them.[^478]

The child protection framework under the Child Protection Act (2019) establishes case management processes, investigation protocols, and alternative care options for children at risk. These processes are designed primarily for children without disabilities – the case management forms, investigation interviews, therapeutic services, and alternative care placements are not adapted to serve children with diverse disability-related needs. A child who is deaf, non-verbal, or has significant cognitive limitations cannot participate meaningfully in standard investigation interviews without communication accommodation.[^479]

CFPS case workers – who manage child protection cases across the country – do not receive systematic disability awareness or disability-adapted interview training. The Child Protection Act's provisions on therapeutic support and recovery services (Articles addressing Article 39 of the CRC) do not specify disability-adapted therapeutic interventions. This gap between child protection system design and disability needs is documented in the CRPD Periodic Report (Article 16) and the UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis.[^480]

### §6.49.2 Children with Disabilities in Residential Care

The 91% institutional care rate for children in formal alternative care documented in the child protection chapter of this encyclopedia (§4) is particularly concerning when applied to children with disabilities. Children with severe or complex disabilities whose families cannot provide care – whether due to poverty, caregiver incapacity, or the severity of the child's support needs – are at high risk of placement in residential facilities rather than family-based alternatives.[^481]

The Maldivian residential care system for children does not have disability-specific units equipped with the specialist staff, medical equipment, therapy resources, and adapted environments that children with complex disabilities need. A child with a severe physical disability placed in a general children's residential facility may be physically cared for but cannot access the developmental, therapeutic, and educational support that their disability requires.[^482]

The CRPD's Article 19 (living independently in the community) applies to children as well as adults – it requires that children with disabilities have access to family-based care or family-substitute care within the community, not institutional placement. The development of disability-inclusive foster care and community-based family support for children with disabilities is a priority that bridges the child protection and disability systems without currently being owned by either.[^483]

### §6.49.3 Parental Disability and Child Protection Risks

A further disability-child protection interface concerns children whose parents have disabilities – particularly cognitive or psychosocial disabilities that affect parenting capacity. Parents with intellectual disabilities, severe mental health conditions, or psychosocial disabilities may face child protection system involvement because of concerns about their parenting capacity, without receiving the disability-specific parenting support that would enable them to parent safely.[^484]

The rights of parents with disabilities to maintain their family relationships – and the obligation of the state to provide appropriate support to enable this – is addressed in CRPD Article 23 (Respect for Home and the Family). The Maldivian child protection system does not have a framework for disability-adapted parenting support – programmes that teach and support parenting skills in ways accessible to parents with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities.[^485]

Without disability-adapted parenting support, child protection system responses to families with disabled parents risk defaulting to removal – which violates both the parental rights of the parent with disability and the family unity rights of the child. The development of supported parenting frameworks is a reform priority for the disability-child protection interface.[^486]

# Part 12: Violence, Abuse, and the Criminal Justice System


## 6.43 Violence, Exploitation, and Abuse: Persons with Disabilities as a High-Risk Population


### §6.32.1 Elevated Vulnerability: Evidence and Causes

Persons with disabilities in Maldives face significantly elevated risks of violence, exploitation, and abuse. The UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis, the HRCM Shadow Report, and the CRPD Periodic Report all acknowledge this risk, though the evidentiary base for quantifying it – in a context where reporting is severely limited – is incomplete.[^337]

The structural drivers of elevated violence risk for persons with disabilities include:

**Dependency and Power Imbalance**: Many persons with disabilities are dependent on family members or care providers for daily living support, creating power differentials that can be exploited. The absence of personal assistance services means that this dependence is often on informal caregivers without formal oversight or professional accountability.[^338]

**Confinement and Isolation**: Physical inaccessibility of homes and communities, combined with transport barriers, means that many persons with disabilities spend their lives within limited physical and social boundaries. Isolation from community networks reduces the likelihood of abuse being witnessed, reported, or acted upon.[^339]

**Communication Barriers**: Persons with communication disabilities – deaf persons, persons with intellectual disabilities, persons with severe autism – may face particular difficulties in reporting abuse, communicating with justice system actors, and accessing support services that are not adapted to their communication needs.[^340]

**Absence of Formal Safeguarding**: No formal adult safeguarding framework exists in Maldives for persons with disabilities who experience abuse by family members, care providers, or community members. ADDA has limited case management capacity but no legal mandate or operational infrastructure for systematic adult safeguarding.[^341]

### §6.32.2 Children with Disabilities: The 60% Sexual Violence Statistic

The most concerning violence risk data point for persons with disabilities in Maldives concerns children. The UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis (drawing on the UNPRPD SitAn 2024 document, produced by Faisal) reports that children with disabilities account for 60% of child sexual abuse cases in the context of care or institutional settings – a figure that, if accurate, indicates a catastrophic failure of child protection for the most vulnerable children.[^342]

This 60% statistic must be contextualised carefully. The figure likely reflects the particular vulnerability of children in residential or institutional care settings – where oversight is limited, power differentials between children and care providers are acute, and disclosure mechanisms are absent or ineffective – rather than all children with disabilities in all settings. However, even with this contextualisation, the finding demands urgent attention.[^343]

The UNPRPD Situational Analysis found that children with disabilities face specific barriers to child protection:
- Child protection workers (CFPS case managers) lack systematic disability-awareness training, limiting their capacity to identify abuse in non-verbal children, conduct disability-appropriate investigations, and provide disability-sensitive therapeutic support
- Communication barriers mean that children with communication disabilities cannot participate meaningfully in abuse investigations or court proceedings
- Residential care settings for children with disabilities are not subject to sufficiently frequent and thorough independent monitoring
- Parents and community members may not recognise abuse when the child has a communication disability and cannot disclose verbally[^344]

The Child Protection Act (2019) provides a rights-based framework for child protection but does not contain specific provisions on safeguarding children with disabilities. The NAPVAC 2024–2028 national action plan to combat violence against children does not include specific strategies addressing the heightened risk for children with disabilities. This absence of disability-specific child protection provisions constitutes a systemic failure to protect the most vulnerable children in the child protection population.[^345]

### §6.32.3 Women with Disabilities: Intersectional Violence Risk

Women with disabilities face compounded violence risks arising from the intersection of disability-related vulnerability and gender-based violence risk. The Sexual Offences Act (17/2014) and the Prevention of Domestic Violence Act (3/2012) provide general protections but do not include disability-specific provisions that account for the particular vulnerabilities of women with disabilities in accessing protection and justice.[^346]

The Family Protection Authority (FPA) – the primary domestic violence support agency – provides shelter and case management for survivors of domestic violence, including survivors with disabilities. However, FPA shelter facilities are not systematically accessible to persons with physical disabilities, and sign language services for deaf survivors of domestic violence are not routinely available. A deaf woman surviving domestic violence who contacts the FPA may find that the communication infrastructure to support her case management is absent.[^347]

Women with disabilities in outer atolls face the most acute protection gap: geographic isolation from FPA services (concentrated in Malé and atoll capitals), inaccessible transport for evacuation from abusive situations, and community-level reporting barriers in small island communities where privacy is limited and social pressure to maintain family stability is strong.[^348]

### §6.32.4 Institutional Monitoring: The Oversight Gap

The CRPD Periodic Report acknowledges concern that residential care settings for persons with disabilities have not been subject to systematic independent monitoring. ADDA manages some residential care functions; civil society organisations manage others; and informal family-based residential arrangements (persons with severe disabilities living in family compounds without formal care arrangements) account for a further portion of the residential disability population.[^349]

HRCM has monitoring authority over residential care settings and has conducted visits to disability-related facilities. However, the frequency of monitoring visits, the depth of monitoring methodology, and the follow-up mechanisms when concerns are identified are not publicly documented in a form that enables assessment of monitoring effectiveness.[^350]

The State Party's commitment in the CRPD Periodic Report to establishing a systematic monitoring mechanism – consistent with the OPCAT framework – for all residential settings accommodating persons with disabilities represents a clear standard. Implementation requires a designated monitoring body, a methodology, a funding allocation, and a legal mandate that extends to privately managed and civil society-managed residential disability facilities – not only state-managed facilities.[^351]

## 6.44 Disability and the Criminal Justice System


### §6.42.1 Persons with Disabilities in the Justice System as Accused, Witness, and Victim

Persons with disabilities interact with the criminal justice system in three primary roles – as accused persons, as victims and witnesses, and occasionally as justice actors (lawyers, judges, court staff). In each of these roles, the justice system's failure to accommodate disability creates distinct rights violations.[^433]

As accused persons, persons with intellectual and psychosocial disabilities face particular risks. Police interrogation procedures are not adapted for persons with intellectual disabilities who may not understand their rights, may comply with interrogation pressure without genuine understanding of the consequences, and may give unreliable accounts of their own actions and intentions. The right to legal assistance is formally provided under the Legal Aid Act, but legal aid lawyers are not systematically trained in intellectual disability communication accommodation.[^434]

The Penal Code (9/2014) provides that criminal responsibility may be modified where a person lacked the capacity to understand or control their conduct due to mental condition – a provision that could protect persons with severe psychosocial or intellectual disabilities from criminal conviction in situations where their disability was the proximate cause of the offending behaviour. However, the application of these provisions requires psychiatric assessment capacity and legal expertise that is not systematically available in courts outside Malé.[^435]

Persons with disabilities detained in the Maldives Correctional Service do not receive disability-specific accommodation in practice. Prison facilities lack accessibility features for persons with physical disabilities. Disability identification at admission is not systematic – prisoners with intellectual disabilities may be unidentified throughout their period of detention. No equivalent of the disability register or reasonable accommodation framework applies within correctional settings.[^436]

### §6.42.2 Disability as a Risk Factor in Juvenile Justice

The intersection of disability and youth in the justice system is particularly concerning. The overrepresentation of young people with intellectual and learning disabilities in youth justice systems is a documented global pattern – driven by educational exclusion, social disadvantage, limited self-regulation capacity, and vulnerability to peer influence that characterise these disability groups.[^437]

Maldives' youth justice system (addressed in §10 of this encyclopedia) does not have systematic protocols for disability screening, supported communication, or disability-adapted diversion programmes. A young person with an undiagnosed intellectual disability who commits a minor offence may enter the formal youth justice system rather than being diverted to a disability-appropriate support service – an outcome that benefits neither the young person nor the justice system.[^438]

# Part 13: Community Inclusion, Independent Living, and Social Dimensions


## 6.45 Community Inclusion, Independent Living, and the Shift Away From Institutionalisation


### §6.22.1 Article 19 and the Maldivian Reality

CRPD Article 19 – the right to live independently and be included in the community – establishes three core entitlements: the right to choose where and with whom one lives; access to personal assistance; and access to community services. The 2025 Situational Analysis found that none of these three entitlements is systematically fulfilled in Maldives as of 2025.[^222]

The right to choose where and with whom one lives is practically unavailable for most persons with severe disabilities, who must live either with family caregivers (the dominant model) or in institutional settings (the ADDA-administered Reehendi-successor facilities). Independent housing for persons with disabilities – supported by personal assistance services – does not exist as a funded option. There is no strategic plan for transitioning from family care and institutional models toward community-based independent living alternatives.[^223]

Personal assistance – the human support service that enables persons with high support needs to live independently and participate in community life – does not exist as a funded programme in Maldives. This is not simply an absence of a particular service type; it is the absence of the enabling infrastructure that makes Article 19's vision achievable in practice. Without personal assistance funding, persons with severe disabilities who lack family support have no option but institutionalisation.[^224]

### §6.22.2 Community Services and the Geographic Void

Community services for persons with disabilities in Maldives – occupational therapy, social work, day activities, peer support, rehabilitation – are concentrated in Malé and absent or minimal in outer atolls. The concept of community-based rehabilitation (CBR) – the WHO-recommended approach of bringing rehabilitation support to persons in their own communities through multi-sector coordination – has not been adopted as national policy.[^225]

The geographic reality of the Maldives makes community service delivery inherently challenging. A CBR programme that works through island council infrastructure, training community health workers in basic disability assessment and support, and providing telehealth links to specialist services in Malé could adapt the CBR model to the atoll context. Such a programme has been discussed in policy circles but not implemented.[^226]

### §6.22.3 The Family Caregiver – The Invisible Pillar of the Disability System

The Maldivian disability system functions, in operational reality, on the unpaid labour of family caregivers – predominantly women – who provide the personal care, transport assistance, communication support, and daily living assistance that formal state services do not. This caregiver workforce is invisible in official statistics, receives minimal formal support (limited to ADDA respite care in Malé), and is subject to the same geographic access limitations as the persons they support.[^227]

The gendered nature of caregiving is documented but not comprehensively measured. In the Maldivian cultural context, care work within the family is understood as a female responsibility – mothers, sisters, daughters, and wives assume primary caregiving roles. The economic costs of caregiving – lost employment opportunity, reduced income, career interruption – fall disproportionately on women. This gendered cost is not captured in disability expenditure data, which measures state spending on formal services rather than the opportunity cost of family caregiving.[^228]

The 2025 Situational Analysis recommends the development of a caregiver support framework – including formal recognition of caregiver status, basic income support, respite care expansion beyond Malé, peer support networks, and access to skills training for family-based care provision. NAPDI v6 acknowledges caregivers as a priority group but does not specify a funded programme of support.[^229]

## 6.46 Disability Disclosure, Stigma, and Changing Narratives


### §6.47.1 Stigma as a Systemic Barrier

Stigma – the social devaluation of persons associated with perceived differences from social norms – operates as a systemic barrier to disability inclusion in Maldives that cuts across all service domains. Stigma affects disability disclosure (discouraging registration and service use), educational participation (parental reluctance to identify children as disabled), employment (employer discrimination), community participation (social exclusion and self-withdrawal), and access to justice (non-reporting of violence and abuse).[^468]

Disability stigma in the Maldivian context takes multiple forms. The stigma associated with visible physical impairment – wheelchair use, facial disfigurement, limb amputation – tends to be more social (exclusion from community activities, pity rather than rights-based interaction) than hidden. The stigma associated with intellectual disability, psychosocial disability, and autism is often more severe – including misconceptions about dangerousness, contagion, and the ability to participate in family and community life.[^469]

The Moosa et al. (2024) phenomenological study documents the lived experience of stigma: the shame of being identified as disabled within close-knit island communities where privacy is limited; the pressure on families to conceal a disabled family member from community visibility; the internalised stigma that leads persons with disabilities to underestimate their own capacity and withdraw from activities they could participate in with appropriate support.[^470]

### §6.47.2 Changing Narratives: Media, Advocacy, and Identity

The counterweight to stigma is a positive disability identity and narrative – the recognition of disability as a dimension of human diversity, the celebration of disability culture and achievement, and the representation of persons with disabilities as rights-holders and community contributors rather than objects of charity or pity.[^471]

Maldivian media has not yet systematically adopted disability inclusion as a representation priority. Persons with disabilities rarely appear as news sources, TV presenters, or prominent public figures in mainstream media. Disability coverage tends to be episodic – fundraising stories, disability-day features – rather than integrated into the full range of human stories that media covers.[^472]

Social media – particularly Facebook and Instagram – has created alternative representation spaces where persons with disabilities can share their experiences, build communities, and challenge mainstream narratives on their own terms. Maldivian disability advocates and OPD members have used social media effectively to document accessibility failures (photographing inaccessible buildings, filming inaccessible ferry landings), celebrate disability achievements (the 2023 election milestone), and build public awareness of disability rights.[^473]

ADDA's awareness mandate includes disability public education – campaigns around the International Day of Persons with Disabilities (3 December), accessibility awareness, and OPD promotion. These campaigns reach Malé-based audiences with variable reach to outer atolls. A more strategic disability narrative programme – engaging schools, mosques, media, and community organisations with disability inclusion messaging – could contribute to the attitudinal change that no amount of formal policy can substitute for.[^474]

### §6.47.3 The Role of Schools in Changing Attitudes

Schools are among the most important sites for attitudinal change regarding disability. When children grow up learning alongside peers with disabilities – seeing them as classmates, friends, and community members rather than strangers to be feared or pitied – the foundation is laid for more inclusive adult communities.[^475]

The inclusive education framework, in addition to its direct educational benefits for children with disabilities, has an indirect community benefit: it creates intergenerational attitudinal change by normalising the presence of children with disabilities in mainstream school settings. The quality of inclusive education matters for this attitudinal function as well as the individual benefit – tokenistic inclusion that segregates children with disabilities within nominally mainstream settings does not produce the same attitudinal benefit as genuine, respectful, peer-integrated inclusion.[^476]

Teacher attitudes toward disability inclusion are a particularly important target for change. SEN teacher training addresses disability knowledge but not always the deeper attitudinal frameworks – about disability, capability, and inclusion – that influence how SEN teachers interact with their students and model disability interaction for the wider school community. NIE's SEN programme curriculum should incorporate explicit reflection on disability attitudes and the social model of disability as foundational content.[^477]

## 6.47 Disability and Religion: Islamic Values as a Resource


### §6.46.1 The Convergence of CRPD and Islamic Ethics

The analysis of Islamic values and disability inclusion in §6.35.3 established the broad convergence between CRPD rights obligations and Islamic ethical frameworks relevant to disability. This section elaborates on specific dimensions of this convergence and its practical policy implications.

Islam's foundational commitment to human dignity (karama) establishes that every person – regardless of physical or mental capacity – possesses inherent worth and deserves respectful treatment. This principle directly supports the CRPD's non-derogation basis: disability cannot reduce a person's fundamental dignity or justify their exclusion from community life.[^460]

The concept of la darar (no harm) in Islamic jurisprudence provides a specific basis for addressing environmental barriers that cause disability-related harm. An inaccessible building that prevents a wheelchair user from accessing a mosque, government office, or school causes concrete harm to that person's religious and civic participation. Under la darar analysis, the obligation to prevent harm would support accessibility requirements as a religious obligation – not merely a civil rights requirement.[^461]

### §6.46.2 Zakat, Sadaqah, and Disability Support

Islamic charitable obligations – zakat (obligatory almsgiving) and sadaqah (voluntary charity) – provide established mechanisms for community support of persons in need, including persons with disabilities. In the Maldivian context, these mechanisms operate through mosque-based collections, family networks, and increasingly through digital platforms that enable targeted charitable giving.[^462]

The Islamic Finance Act and the Zakat administration in Maldives provide an opportunity to channel religious charitable resources toward disability-specific purposes – assistive technology provision, caregiver support, accessible housing modifications, or vocational training. The integration of Islamic charitable mechanisms into disability policy has not been systematically explored in the Maldivian context, though some OPDs receive informal community donations channelled through religious giving.[^463]

The explicit recognition of disability-directed giving as a legitimate zakat category – by religious authorities and the official Zakat administration – could mobilise community resources for disability inclusion that supplement government spending. This approach would align with Islamic charitable tradition, reach communities that are not well served by formal government programmes, and build a cultural narrative that disability support is a community obligation – not merely a government function.[^464]

### §6.46.3 Accessible Mosques and Religious Participation

Mosques are the central community gathering spaces in Maldivian society – sites of daily prayer, Friday congregational prayer, community events, and social support networks. The inaccessibility of mosques to persons with physical disabilities constitutes exclusion from the most fundamental arena of community religious and social life.[^465]

Most mosques in Maldives – including the national mosque in Malé (Islamic Centre) – do not meet accessibility standards for wheelchair users or persons with mobility impairments. Steps at entrances, narrow doorways, and inaccessible ablution (wudu) facilities prevent participation by many persons with physical disabilities. The requirement for ablution before prayer presents specific challenges for persons with mobility impairments, cognitive disabilities, and chronic health conditions, which Islamic jurisprudence has addressed through provisions for dry ablution (tayammum) but which physical accessibility barriers compound.[^466]

ADDA's disability awareness work has not systematically addressed mosque accessibility – an omission that reflects the separation of disability policy from religious institution management and the absence of inter-agency coordination between ADDA and the Ministry of Islamic Affairs. Including mosque accessibility as a component of the Disability Act review, or establishing a Ministry of Islamic Affairs disability inclusion policy, would address one of the most socially significant accessibility gaps in Maldivian community life.[^467]

## 6.48 Disability and Political Participation: From Exclusion to Representation


### §6.33.1 The 2023 Election Milestone and Its Limitations

The 2023 presidential election represented a historic moment for disability inclusion in Maldivian democracy: for the first time, visually impaired voters were able to cast their ballots independently and confidentially using a specially designed tactile template. This innovation – achieved through advocacy by BVISM and collaboration with the Elections Commission – enabled blind and visually impaired voters to participate in a secret ballot without assistance from a sighted person, preserving both the right to vote and the right to a private, unmonitored vote.[^352]

This achievement matters both substantively and symbolically. Substantively, it enabled a group of citizens who had previously been dependent on assistance (and therefore potentially monitored or influenced) to exercise their franchise independently. Symbolically, it demonstrated that accessible election design is achievable with political commitment, and provided a precedent and model for further election accessibility improvements.[^353]

However, the 2023 milestone was a point solution to a single barrier rather than the product of a comprehensive election accessibility policy. Voting station physical accessibility – ramps, accessible booths, accessible parking – was not comprehensively addressed. Election information in Braille and MSL was not systematically provided. The tactile template was developed for one election rather than embedded in standing electoral regulations. And the achievement, while real, was limited to one disability category (visual impairment) among many.[^354]

### §6.33.2 Underrepresentation in Political Life

Persons with disabilities are substantially underrepresented in elected office at all levels of Maldivian governance – the People's Majlis (Parliament), Atoll Councils, City Councils, and Island Councils. No disability quota or reserved seat provision exists in electoral law. No positive action measure to support candidates with disabilities has been established by political parties or the Elections Commission.[^355]

The underrepresentation of persons with disabilities in elected office is not merely a statistical pattern – it has practical policy consequences. Policy priorities, resource allocation, and legislative design are influenced by the life experiences and priorities of elected representatives. The absence of representatives with lived disability experience in legislative and governance bodies reduces the probability that disability perspectives will be embedded in mainstream policy decisions, rather than addressed only through disability-specific siloed measures.[^356]

The CRPD Periodic Report notes that no disability quota or reserved seat provision exists in electoral law, and that the technical advisory committee established under Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 was criticised for initially lacking meaningful representation of persons with disabilities. This pattern – formal structures for disability policy that do not include persons with disabilities in decision-making positions – is a recurring governance failure that undermines both the quality of policy outcomes and the Convention's "nothing about us without us" principle.[^357]

### §6.33.3 OPD Governance and Self-Representation

The principle of self-representation – that OPDs should be led and governed by persons with disabilities themselves, not by non-disabled allies or family members acting on behalf of disabled persons – is a defining characteristic of organisations that qualify as OPDs under CRPD standards. In Maldives, the OPD ecosystem, while still developing, is generally constituted on this self-representation principle.[^358]

The practical challenge is ensuring that OPD governance structures are accessible to persons with the full range of disabilities relevant to each organisation's mandate. A deaf association that conducts all its meetings in spoken Dhivehi without MSL interpretation excludes deaf members from meaningful participation in their own organisation. An organisation of persons with disabilities that holds its meetings in inaccessible venues excludes members with physical disabilities. These accessibility obligations apply within civil society organisations as well as in government institutions.[^359]

## 6.49 Housing and Disability: Overcrowding, Ownership, and Urban Vulnerability


### §6.56.1 Household Composition and Size

The Census 2022 data shows that 22% of all Maldivian households – approximately 1 in 4–5 – contain at least one person with a disability. Atoll households are more likely to contain persons with disabilities (25%) than Male' households (18%), reflecting the higher disability prevalence in atoll populations.[^549]

Households containing persons with disabilities are substantially larger than those without:
- With disability: Average 6 persons
- Without disability: Average 4.9 persons

This size difference (1.3 additional members) reflects multiple factors: extended family structures in disability households, where multiple generations live together to share caregiving responsibilities; larger family size in atoll communities; and the presence of multiple persons with disabilities in the same household (17% of disability households have three or more members with disabilities).[^550]

Female household heads predominate in disability households: 55% of households with persons with disabilities are headed by women (vs. 46% of households without). This feminisation of household headship in disability contexts reflects widowhood patterns (22% of women with disabilities are widowed) and the expectation that women will assume the household management role when a male household member has a severe disability.[^551]

### §6.56.2 Overcrowding

Households with persons with disabilities are more likely to be overcrowded:
- With disability: 8.7% overcrowded (at household level); 14% at population level
- Without disability: 6.2% overcrowded (at household level); 11% at population level

In Malé specifically, overcrowding affects 22% of the population in disability households vs. 17% in non-disability households – a pattern that reflects the acute housing shortage in the capital, the larger household size characteristic of disability households, and the financial constraints that prevent disability households from accessing larger or more affordable accommodation.[^552]

The physical consequences of overcrowding for persons with disabilities are significant: crowded living conditions in Malé apartments (typically multi-room apartments in multi-storey buildings without lifts) may make wheelchair access within the home impossible, confine severely disabled household members to a single room, and create hazardous environments for persons with mobility, cognitive, or psychiatric disabilities.[^553]

### §6.56.3 Mobile Phone Ownership: The Digital Access Gap

Mobile phone ownership data reveals a digital access gap that compounds information inaccessibility for persons with disabilities:
- Persons with disability: 81% own mobile phones
- Persons without disability: 97% own mobile phones

This 16-percentage-point gap represents approximately 4,600 persons with disabilities without mobile phone access – persons who are therefore excluded from the digital communication and service access that smartphones increasingly provide. The gap is wider in atolls (76% for disability vs. 96% without) than in Malé (87% vs. 98%).[^554]

Counterintuitively, women with disabilities show slightly higher mobile phone ownership (82%) than men with disabilities (79%) – a reversal of patterns typically seen in lower-income contexts. This finding may reflect the importance of mobile communication for women who are homebound due to disability, for whom phones provide the primary access to social networks and information that mobility barriers otherwise prevent.[^555]

# Part 14: Civil Society and Organisations of Persons with Disabilities


## 6.50 Organisations of Persons with Disabilities: The Civil Society Sector


### §6.23.1 The OPD Ecosystem

The CRPD principle of "nothing about us without us" – the requirement that persons with disabilities be actively involved in all decisions affecting them – is operationalised through organisations of persons with disabilities (OPDs). In Maldives, a small but active OPD ecosystem has developed since the 2000s, providing advocacy, peer support, community services, and policy engagement that partially fills gaps in state provision.[^230]

The key OPDs in the Maldivian disability sector include:

**Maldives Association of Persons with Disabilities (MAPD)**: The national umbrella OPD, providing cross-disability advocacy and representation at the national policy level. MAPD has engaged with NAPDI consultations, CRPD reporting processes, and domestic policy advocacy. Its capacity is constrained by limited funding and the challenge of representing a diverse disability community spread across a geographically dispersed country.[^231]

**Blind and Visually Impaired Society of Maldives (BVISM)**: A single-impairment OPD focused on the needs of persons with visual impairments. BVISM has advocated for accessible information formats, Braille resource development, and the successful 2023 election accessibility initiative – the tactile ballot template that enabled visually impaired voters to cast ballots independently. BVISM represents an important model of disability-specific advocacy producing concrete policy outcomes.[^232]

**Maldives Deaf Association**: Representing the deaf community, the Maldives Deaf Association advocates for Maldives Sign Language recognition, sign language interpreter training, and accessible public information. The Association operates with limited resources and faces the particular challenge of serving a community whose communication difference makes information access fundamentally different from other disability groups.[^233]

**Care Society**: Provides direct services to persons with disabilities and their families, including day activities, respite care support, and community outreach in Malé. Care Society bridges the OPD and NGO models – providing both advocacy and service delivery functions.[^234]

**Maldives Autism Association**: Established to represent and support the autism community, the Maldives Autism Association works alongside the Maldives Institute for Autism (MIA) to support families and advocate for autism-specific educational and social support. The Association has been active in early identification campaigns and parent support.[^235]

### §6.23.2 OPD Capacity Constraints and Funding Vulnerability

The OPD ecosystem in Maldives faces systemic capacity constraints. Most OPDs operate with minimal paid staff, relying heavily on volunteer time and member contributions. Funding is precarious, combining small government grants, international donor support (primarily UNICEF and UNPRPD-aligned funding), and event-based fundraising. No systematic government framework for sustainable OPD funding exists.[^236]

The geographic concentration of OPDs in Malé means that persons with disabilities in outer atolls have limited access to OPD support and representation. OPD members on outer islands are connected primarily through digital communication – Facebook groups, WhatsApp – rather than in-person services and activities. This creates a representation gap: outer atoll disability experiences and needs are less visible in national policy processes than those of Malé-based members.[^237]

The HRCM Shadow Report 2023 noted that OPD representatives had been involved in the Disability Act review consultation process, but questioned whether the consultation mechanisms were designed to enable meaningful influence over the review's direction and outcome, or whether OPD participation was tokenistic – providing the formal appearance of "nothing about us without us" without the substantive policy influence the CRPD requires.[^238]

### §6.23.3 OPDs and the Disability Act Review Process

The announcement at the CRPD Committee's 33rd Session in August 2025 that the Disability Act (2010) is under comprehensive participatory review is a significant development. The review provides an opportunity to address the Act's most significant structural deficiencies – its lack of enforcement mechanisms, its definitional limitations, its silence on supported decision-making, and its failure to establish an accessible infrastructure standards framework.[^239]

The quality of OPD participation in this review will be a critical determinant of its outcome. If OPDs are engaged only at the consultation stage – invited to submit written comments or attend meetings, without structural influence over the review's agenda, terms of reference, or draft text – the review risks producing an amended Act that addresses peripheral issues while leaving the core structural deficiencies intact. CRPD Article 4(3) requires the active involvement of persons with disabilities, including through their representative organisations, in the development and implementation of legislation and policies.[^240]

## 6.51 Evidence from Focus Groups: Voices of Persons with Disabilities


### §6.58.1 Community Participation: The 5% Finding

The Moosa et al. (2024) study – which conducted focus groups and in-depth interviews with 15 participants across six island locations (Addu/Seenu Atoll, Kalhuduffushi/Haa Dhaalu, Gan/Laamu Atoll, Male'/Kaafu Atoll, Madifushi/Thaa Atoll, Rasgatheem/Raa Atoll) – provides the most geographically diverse qualitative evidence of disability experience in Maldives. The study's central quantitative finding is that only 5% of persons with disabilities engage in community activities – a figure that documents the depth of social exclusion.[^568]

This 5% community participation figure should be understood not as a preference for isolation but as the measurable outcome of physical barriers, attitudinal barriers, transport barriers, and the cumulative effect of a built environment and social system that has not designed for disability inclusion. Focus group participants articulated this with specificity:

*"Although some government places that provide services do (have access), but not social spaces like park, places of entertainment, and in some island community centres, there are no opportunities for people like us or children with disabilities."*[^569]

*"Even though we are blind, we still have physical strength, we also like to lift, for example, heavy things or change arrangements of the house or even go for picnics... they will tell me why you don't wait a bit at home... they don't make me part of the fun."*[^570]

These testimonies capture the two dimensions of social exclusion: inaccessible physical environments, and the attitudinal exclusion that tells persons with disabilities that they should remain within the household rather than participate in community life.

### §6.58.2 Violence Against Women with Disabilities: The 40–60% Finding

The Moosa et al. (2024) study reports that an estimated 40–60% of women with disabilities are subjected to sexual abuse – a finding drawn from focus group discussions and consistent with the UNPRPD Situational Analysis figure that children with disabilities account for 60% of child sexual violence cases in care settings. This range (40–60%) spans different measurement methodologies and population definitions, but its consistent direction and magnitude demands urgent policy attention.[^571]

The structural causes of this severe violence prevalence are clearly identified in the qualitative evidence: dependency on carers for physical needs creates acute power differentials; physical and communicative limitations make reporting difficult; the social isolation of persons with disabilities means that abuse occurs in private settings without witnesses; and accessible reporting mechanisms and disability-aware investigation processes do not exist.[^572]

Focus group participants described the political instrumentalisation of disability as an additional vulnerability dimension: *"Politicians use the CWDs as a tool for their campaigns. We believe this is wrong. Every time they say, in our government, these children will not be neglected, that these services will be provided, but there's no mechanism to provide services from one place for PWDs."* This finding – that political actors exploit children with disabilities as campaign props without providing substantive services – represents a specific form of institutional exploitation that disability advocates have documented but policy processes have not addressed.[^573]

### §6.58.3 Disability Allowance Coverage: The 23.3% Historical Figure

The Moosa et al. (2024) study cites 23.3% disability allowance coverage based on 2017–2019 data – slightly below the Hameed et al. (2022) figure of 25.6% drawn from 2022 Census data. The convergence of these two estimates from different methodologies and time periods strengthens the finding that approximately one-quarter of persons with disabilities receive the allowance – and that this coverage rate has improved only marginally between 2017 and 2022 despite the NDR formal launch and outreach activities.[^574]

The allowance's adequacy is directly challenged in focus group testimony: *"MVR 2,000 = US$129.32"* is cited as grossly inadequate for disability-related needs. Participants identified the complications of the assessment and eligibility process as barriers – consistent with the HRCM Shadow Report's finding that procedural complexity is among the primary factors explaining the large coverage gap.[^575]

### §6.58.4 Education Barriers – The Teacher, Parent, and System Perspective

The qualitative evidence on education barriers is particularly rich, capturing the perspective of disabled children, their parents, and teachers simultaneously:

**Parental experience of segregation and exclusion**:
*"I did not get any support from the school management, there were complaints from parents of other children in the class, that their children were not getting the attention because my child is with special needs."*[^576]

**Braille inaccessibility outside Male'**:
*"If we talk about blind children, (school name) school in Maldives only provides education using braille, this service is not there for any blind children anywhere else in the country. Even when they do use braille, it is also not done appropriately."*[^577]

**Teacher training gap**:
*"What happens is when teachers finish their teacher training, their attitudes and they not being aware of how to attend to children with disabilities. Teachers are not comfortable to face children with disabilities... if they conduct teacher education, their curriculum should include (how to attend to children with disabilities) for a reasonable time."*[^578]

**Long-term dependency concern**:
*"Always the child (with disabilities) is viewed as someone they have to take care of their whole life and they are obliged to look after the disabled child forever. This is the thinking of parents of a disabled child, but they don't think about how this child will survive once they pass away."*[^579]

These testimonies capture the cumulative effect of educational exclusion: teacher discomfort, parental isolation, peer family resistance, geographic resource limitation, and the absence of any transition planning that prepares children with disabilities for adult independence.

### §6.58.5 Employment – Capability Evidence and the English Barrier

A striking theme in the qualitative employment evidence is the assertion – by persons with disabilities themselves – that their capabilities exceed the opportunities offered to them:

*"Everything a normal child can do in front of a computer; a blind child can also do in 90% capacity. So, these are also jobs (they can do as well), so we need to create more job opportunities, strengthen education, strengthen the existing laws, and things would work out for better then."*[^580]

The English language barrier emerges as a specific, nameable obstacle – both to employment and to healthcare access:

*"English has become important, for example, in a resort reception, they can work or if we look at the situation of doctors who is speaking in English, we are not able to go and talk to doctor we have to talk to someone else to convey or communicate with the doctor on our behalf because we don't know English so we can't communicate our problems to doctors the best way."*[^581]

This English literacy barrier connects the Census 2022 finding (41% English literacy for persons with disabilities vs. 86% without) with lived employment and healthcare access consequences – demonstrating that the statistical gap has direct daily life implications for persons with disabilities navigating a formal sector that operates primarily in English.

## 6.52 CRPD Compliance and Shadow Reports


### Ratification and Reporting

The Maldives ratified the CRPD in 2010 and submitted its initial report under Article 35 of the Convention in 2018 (originally due in 2012).[^83] The report outlined the legal framework established by the Disability Act and described the institutional architecture for disability rights.

### HRCM CRPD Shadow Report (2023)

The Human Rights Commission of the Maldives submitted a comprehensive 57-page shadow report to the UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in 2023. The report addressed priority concerns across multiple areas:

- **Inclusive education**: Significant gaps between policy and practice, with inadequate implementation of the Inclusive Education Policy
- **Accessibility**: Poor enforcement of minimum accessibility standards
- **Employment**: Limited employment opportunities and workplace discrimination
- **Healthcare**: Barriers to accessing adequate healthcare services
- **Domestic violence**: Persons with disabilities facing elevated vulnerability to GBV
- **Implementation gaps**: The extensive legislative framework required to fulfil disability rights "is indicative of the level of involvement needed by both state and non-state actors"[^84]

### International Commitments

Beyond the CRPD, the Maldives has endorsed:

- The **Jakarta Declaration on the Asian and Pacific Decade of Persons with Disabilities (2023-2030)**, reaffirming commitment to accelerate disability rights through the SDGs, CRPD, and Incheon Strategy[^85]
- The **Universal Declaration of Balanced and Inclusive Education** (SDG 4)[^86]
- The **Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction**, highlighting the importance of including persons with disabilities as agents of change in DRR[^87]

The CEDAW Committee's general recommendations also urge the Maldives to submit information on women with disabilities and ensure their equal access to opportunities in all facets of life, though CEDAW does not specifically mention women with disabilities in its text.[^88]

### The UNPRPD Situational Analysis (2024)

The most comprehensive assessment to date is the UNPRPD Situational Analysis conducted in December 2024, authored by Ayesha Junaina Faisal. Based on Focus Group Discussions with 245 participants (134 persons with disabilities and 111 parents/support persons) across four locations (Haa Dhaalu, Dhaalu, Gaafu Alifu atolls, and Male' City), plus stakeholder interviews with state agencies, OPDs, CSOs, service providers, and UN agencies, the report provides the most detailed picture available of the lived experience of disability in the Maldives.[^89]

## 6.53 The CRPD Monitoring Cycle: Obligations, Reporting, and Accountability Mechanisms


### §6.31.1 Maldives' CRPD Obligations and Reporting Record

Maldives ratified the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities on 5 April 2009, becoming a State Party with obligations under all 50 substantive articles of the Convention. The CRPD Committee – the treaty body established under Article 34 of the Convention – monitors implementation through a combination of State Party periodic reports, civil society and NHRI shadow reports, and constructive dialogue.[^325]

The State Party has been slow in fulfilling its reporting obligations. The CRPD Periodic Report covering the period 2019–2025 was the subject of the Committee's 33rd Session review in August 2025 – a significant delay relative to the standard CRPD reporting cycle. The delay reflects both the limited administrative capacity for treaty body reporting in Maldives and the genuine governance challenges of managing a complex multi-ministry reporting process across a geographically dispersed state.[^326]

### §6.31.2 The 33rd Session Dialogue: Key Lines of Inquiry

The CRPD Committee's review of Maldives at its 33rd Session in August 2025 produced a constructive dialogue in which Committee members raised specific concerns across multiple articles. Key lines of inquiry included:

**Legal Capacity**: Committee members pressed on the timeline for guardianship reform and the development of supported decision-making legislation, noting that the fifteen-year gap since CRPD ratification without legislative action on this most fundamental right represents a significant failure of implementation.[^327]

**Accessibility**: The absence of binding accessibility standards after fifteen years of CRPD obligation was noted as a systemic failure. Committee members asked about the timeline for enacting and enforcing accessibility legislation, and about accountability mechanisms for the inaccessibility of newly constructed public infrastructure.[^328]

**Employment**: The Committee welcomed Employment Regulation 2024/R-9 and the 350 civil service/SOE placements but asked about private sector obligations, reasonable accommodation frameworks, and the plan for closing the large LFPR gap between persons with and without disabilities.[^329]

**Data**: The NDR coverage gap (60% of census-estimated disability population) and the absence of disability-disaggregated outcome data were raised as fundamental monitoring obstacles. The Committee noted that without adequate data, the State Party cannot demonstrate progress on most CRPD article obligations.[^330]

**OPD Participation**: The quality and depth of OPD involvement in policy processes – including the Disability Act review – was questioned. Committee members emphasised the requirement for structural OPD participation, not merely consultative engagement.[^331]

### §6.31.3 HRCM as Article 33(2) Mechanism

The Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM) serves as Maldives' designated independent monitoring mechanism under CRPD Article 33(2) – the "A-status" NHRI responsible for monitoring, promoting, and protecting the rights of persons with disabilities. The HRCM's production of the 2023 Shadow Report – submitted to the CRPD Committee in advance of the 33rd Session review – demonstrates the Commission's active engagement with the CRPD monitoring process.[^332]

The HRCM's disability monitoring capacity is constrained by the same factors that limit all Maldivian institutions: limited budget, insufficient specialist staff, and geographic reach challenges across 187 inhabited islands. The Commission conducts periodic monitoring visits and receives complaints from persons with disabilities, but the frequency and geographic coverage of its disability-specific monitoring activities falls short of what systematic CRPD compliance assessment requires.[^333]

The recommendation from the CRPD Committee for Maldives to strengthen HRCM's disability monitoring capacity – through dedicated staffing, methodology development, and budgetary allocation – is a standing recommendation that requires action at the executive and parliamentary level, not merely at the Commission itself.[^334]

### §6.31.4 The Optional Protocol Gap

Maldives has not ratified the Optional Protocol to the CRPD, which would enable individual communications to the CRPD Committee from persons in Maldives who claim violations of their Convention rights, and inquiry procedures allowing the Committee to investigate situations of grave or systematic violation. The Optional Protocol's non-ratification means that international accountability for CRPD violations in Maldives operates only through the periodic reporting cycle – without the individual petition mechanism that provides the most direct accountability for specific rights violations.[^335]

The ratification of the Optional Protocol has been recommended by the CRPD Committee and by civil society, including in the HRCM Shadow Report. The State Party's continued non-ratification reflects either a deliberate choice to limit international accountability or a failure to prioritise this step in the treaty ratification agenda. Given Maldives' active engagement with the CRPD reporting process, ratification of the Optional Protocol would signal genuine commitment to comprehensive accountability and would provide an additional channel for individual rights protection without the political risks often associated with complaint mechanisms.[^336]

### §6.63.2 The Deaf Community – Dhivehi Sign Language Recognition

Both the HRCM Shadow Report and the UNPRPD Situational Analysis identify DSL (Dhivehi Sign Language) non-recognition as a specific, named policy gap that is achievable without extraordinary resource investment. The absence of DSL legal recognition – despite the existence of an active deaf community, the Maldives Deaf Association (established 2008), and a growing MSL vocabulary (the 2009 dictionary provided approximately 650 signs, with continued community development since) – means that:

- Government press conferences and public announcements are not legally required to include DSL interpretation
- Courts are not legally required to provide DSL interpretation in proceedings involving deaf parties
- Health consultations have no legal framework for MSL accommodation
- Emergency alerts have no accessibility requirement for the deaf community
- Deaf children have no legal right to education in their language[^613]

The HRCM Shadow Report recommends DSL recognition as an official language – a step that would require parliamentary action (constitutional amendment or language law reform) but would provide the legal foundation for the cascade of accessibility obligations that recognition entails. Sri Lanka's recognition of Sri Lanka Sign Language under its Sign Language Act (2021) provides a regional model; New Zealand's recognition of New Zealand Sign Language as an official language (2006) provides a high-income country benchmark.[^614]

### §6.63.3 Deinstitutionalisation: The Policy-Implementation Gap

A specific CRPD compliance issue documented across the Situational Analysis and Shadow Report is the gap between deinstitutionalisation policy statements and continuing institutional care reality. Maldives' disability policy documents reference commitment to community-based living and deinstitutionalisation, but:

- No timeline for reducing institutional care placements has been established
- No budget allocation for developing community-based alternatives has been made
- No personal assistance scheme exists to enable community living for persons with high support needs
- Reehendi's successor facilities continue to provide congregate care as the primary formal alternative to family care

The CRPD Committee has consistently found that deinstitutionalisation commitments without implementation plans, budgets, and timelines do not constitute CRPD compliance with Article 19. The Maldivian situation – policy statements without implementation mechanisms – is precisely the pattern the Committee identifies as inadequate.[^615]

## 6.55 The HRCM Shadow Report 2023: Independent Monitoring Findings


### §6.60.1 Cross-Article Implementation Assessment

The HRCM Shadow Report to the CRPD Committee (57 pages, 2023) provides the most comprehensive independent monitoring assessment of CRPD implementation in Maldives. Its article-by-article analysis – drawing on focus group discussions and stakeholder consultations – identifies implementation gaps that complement and reinforce the findings from the UNPRPD Situational Analysis and the Census 2022 disability report.

The Shadow Report documents, through focus group discussions, the lived experience of discrimination across six domains:
- **Employment**: Discrimination in hiring, retention, and promotion
- **Education**: School placement barriers and curriculum access denial
- **Healthcare**: Service denial and provider judgement
- **Public services**: Accessibility denial and service refusal
- **Housing**: Rental discrimination
- **Social interactions**: Community stigma[^586]

The finding that persons with disabilities report fear of retaliation affecting their willingness to lodge discrimination complaints – even when they know about complaint mechanisms – is particularly significant. Effective anti-discrimination law requires accessible, safe, and trustworthy complaint mechanisms. The current complaint architecture in Maldives – which requires formal complaints to the HRCM or courts, both located primarily in Malé – does not provide this for persons with disabilities in outer atolls.[^587]

### §6.60.2 Focus Group Findings on Violence and Abuse

The HRCM Shadow Report's FGD findings on violence against women with disabilities document:
- Higher rates of intimate partner violence among women with disabilities
- Fear of separating from abusive partners due to economic dependence – a pattern that directly links inadequate disability income support (the allowance insufficiency) with violence exposure: women who cannot financially sustain themselves outside an abusive relationship are trapped in that relationship by economic necessity
- Limited shelter access (FPA shelters not wheelchair accessible)
- Inadequate police response to reports of domestic violence involving women with disabilities[^588]

The economic dependence trap is particularly important policy evidence. The 28% female disability LFPR (vs. 50% without disability) and the 50% informal employment rate among employed women with disabilities create a situation in which many women with disabilities are either entirely economically dependent on a partner or earning income insufficient to support independent housing. An abusive partner represents a critical economic resource that these women cannot readily sacrifice – a situation that no amount of shelter provision or police training can resolve without also addressing the economic independence gap.[^589]

### §6.60.3 Accessibility Quantified: The FGD Evidence

The HRCM Shadow Report documents that 77% of reported accessibility barriers affect job access – the single largest domain consequence of inaccessibility. This figure – derived from FGD discussions with persons with disabilities – captures the chain reaction of inaccessibility: inaccessible transport → cannot reach workplace → cannot maintain employment → economic dependence → poverty vulnerability.[^590]

The report's findings on women-specific accessibility challenges extend the standard accessibility analysis: women with disabilities identify bathroom accessibility in healthcare settings (culturally appropriate facilities accessible to wheelchair users), clothing modifications in culturally conservative contexts (modest dress while also being accessible for catheter or incontinence management), and the specific communication accessibility needs of deaf and hard-of-hearing women in obstetric and gynaecological care settings.[^591]

## 6.56 International Cooperation and Global Frameworks in Maldivian Disability Policy


### §6.26.1 UNDP/UNPRPD: The Situational Analysis and Its Significance

The UNDP/UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in Maldives – published January 2025 – represents the most comprehensive independent assessment of the Maldivian disability situation conducted to date. Produced through the UNPRPD's Maldives programme (formally part of the Global Disability Fund programme inception activities), the analysis examined compliance with CRPD articles, identified structural barriers across service domains, and produced recommendations aligned with the Convention's requirements.[^259]

The Situational Analysis's significance for Maldivian disability policy is threefold. First, it provides an independent evidence base – not filtered through government reporting – that identifies implementation gaps more candidly than official documentation typically permits. Second, it serves as the primary reference for the CRPD Committee's constructive dialogue with Maldives, shaping the lines of questioning and the Committee's emerging Concluding Observations. Third, it provides civil society – including OPDs and the HRCM – with evidence to cite in advocacy and monitoring work.[^260]

### §6.26.2 GPE THAIBA: Education Financing and Disability Inclusion

The Global Partnership for Education (GPE) grant portfolio for Maldives – the THAIBA Agenda – represents the most significant international education financing commitment specifically addressing disability inclusion. The USD 9.5 million grant (2024) includes a USD 3 million Multiplier grant with the World Bank and is explicitly designed to prioritise children with disabilities as among the most at-risk learners in the Maldivian education system.[^261]

The THAIBA grants will fund inclusive education system transformation through 2028, including: accessible learning material development; SEN teacher professional development; outreach to out-of-school children with disabilities; and school accessibility improvements in selected atolls. The geographic selectivity of the grants – which will focus on specific atolls and islands – means that the system transformation will not reach all schools simultaneously.[^262]

The GPE framework's emphasis on disability as a priority cross-cutting theme is notable – it positions inclusive education not as a supplementary activity but as a central goal of education system improvement. This creates an accountability mechanism: GPE grant disbursements are linked to progress indicators, including disability-inclusive education metrics.[^263]

### §6.26.3 Global Disability Fund and HRCM Shadow Report

The Global Disability Fund's Maldives Country Brief (2025) provides a concise external assessment of disability rights progress and gaps, primarily drawing on the UNPRPD Situational Analysis and community consultation with OPDs. As a programmatic document supporting the UNPRPD's work, the Brief identifies strategic priorities for international cooperation support in the next programme period.[^264]

The HRCM Shadow Report to the CRPD Committee (2023) – a 57-page independent monitoring report produced by the Human Rights Commission of Maldives – documented "incongruence between key legislations" in the disability legal framework and highlighted implementation gaps across CRPD articles. The Shadow Report's submission to the CRPD Committee contributed to the Committee's decision to schedule Maldives for review at its 33rd Session (August 2025) – creating the accountability moment that prompted the Disability Act review announcement and the government's engagement at Geneva.[^265]

### §6.26.4 SAARC Regional Frameworks

Maldives participates in SAARC (South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation) frameworks on disability, including the SAARC Social Charter which contains disability inclusion commitments. However, regional coordination on disability policy implementation within SAARC remains nascent – the Association's disability frameworks produce policy statements rather than operational cooperation mechanisms.[^266]

The South Asian comparative context is relevant for Maldivian disability policy in several ways. Sri Lanka's disability registry and allowance system provides a nearby model for NDR development. Bangladesh's community-based rehabilitation programme demonstrates the potential of CBR in resource-constrained contexts. India's Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (2016) – which introduced a quota system for government employment and education – provides a legislative model for stronger disability inclusion mandates. However, the Maldivian context – small island state, dispersed geography, very small population – requires adaptation of these larger-country models rather than direct adoption.[^267]

## 6.57 International Comparison: Small Island State Context


### §6.59.1 Maldives Within the Small Island Disability Context

Disability policy in small island developing states (SIDS) shares structural challenges – geographic dispersion, small populations, limited institutional capacity, high dependence on single-sector economies – that differentiate SIDS from both large developing countries and small high-income states. The Moosa et al. (2024) study provides comparative disability prevalence data:

| Country | Disability prevalence |
|---|---|
| Maldives (2019 estimate) | 9.2% |
| Fiji | ~13.7% |
| Mauritius | 3.75% |
| Seychelles | 4.24% |
| India (Census 2011) | ~2.2% |
| Indonesia | ~9% |
| Australia | 17.7% |

The Maldives' 9.2% estimate (drawn from an earlier period than the Census 2022 WG-SS figure of 6.9%) places it within the middle range of the SIDS disability prevalence spectrum – lower than Fiji, higher than Mauritius and Seychelles. The variation reflects measurement methodology differences as much as real prevalence differences. The Maldives' geography – dispersed archipelago – creates service delivery challenges more acute than those facing Mauritius or Seychelles, which are single-island states.[^582]

### §6.59.2 Applying SIDS-Specific Policy Models

The SIDS context suggests policy solutions specifically calibrated to small, dispersed populations rather than adapted from large-country frameworks:

**Telehealth and distance service delivery**: SIDS contexts have pioneered telehealth models precisely because specialist-population ratios and geographic distribution make in-person specialist services infeasible at scale. Pacific SIDS (Fiji, Tonga, Vanuatu) have developed telehealth platforms connecting outer island health workers with specialists in capital cities. These models are directly applicable to Maldivian disability – telepsychiatry, telephysiotherapy, and teleASD (autism remote support) have demonstrated effectiveness in comparable geographic contexts.[^583]

**Community health worker disability training**: Community-based rehabilitation (CBR) models that train community health workers in basic disability assessment, assistive technology provision, and referral have been implemented successfully in Pacific SIDS and South Asian contexts. The Maldivian community health worker network – through which the Ministry of Health maintains basic health outreach on inhabited islands – provides a platform for CBR-style disability support that has not yet been utilised.[^584]

**Regional hub service concentration**: Fiji's divisional hospital structure provides a useful comparative model: specialist services concentrated in four divisional hospitals (Suva, Lautoka, Labasa, and Nausori) providing hub-and-spoke service access for island communities within each division. A similar five-region hub structure for Maldives, as proposed in §6.35.5, aligns with the best practice from comparable SIDS service delivery challenges.[^585]

# Part 16: Climate, COVID-19, and Cross-Cutting Issues


## 6.58 Disability and Climate Change: Vulnerability, Intersectionality, and Policy Gap


### §6.24.1 Maldives' Climate Vulnerability and Its Disability Dimension

Maldives is among the world's most climate-vulnerable nations – 80% of its land area is less than one metre above sea level, and the country faces compound climate risks including sea-level rise, coral bleaching and fisheries collapse, freshwater lens salinisation, and increased intensity of tropical weather events. The intersection of climate vulnerability and disability creates a risk profile that is particularly severe for persons with disabilities in the Maldivian context.[^241]

Persons with disabilities face heightened climate vulnerability through multiple pathways: reduced capacity for self-evacuation in emergency events; greater dependence on infrastructure (health facilities, assistive technology, power supply for medical equipment) that is vulnerable to climate disruption; higher health risks from heat stress and air quality degradation for persons with respiratory conditions and intellectual disabilities; and reduced ability to access adaptation information and warning systems due to communication accessibility barriers.[^242]

### §6.24.2 Disaster Risk Reduction and the NDR Integration Gap

The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015–2030), to which Maldives is a signatory, specifically calls for disability-inclusive disaster risk reduction at all levels of governance. The National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) is responsible for emergency preparedness and response, but has not integrated NDR data into its emergency planning systems.[^243]

The operational consequence of this gap is concrete: in a disaster scenario requiring evacuation of an island community, NDMA and island council emergency responders do not know from official systems how many persons with disabilities live on their island, where they are located, whether they require evacuation assistance, or what their specific support needs are. A database exists – the NDR – that contains precisely this information, but it is not accessible to those responsible for emergency response.[^244]

The 2020 COVID-19 pandemic illustrated the consequences of this planning gap. Persons with disabilities were not identified and prioritised in the initial emergency response planning. Vaccination rollout in some atolls did not account for the mobility constraints of persons with disabilities in accessing vaccination sites. ADDA respite facilities closed without alternative support arrangements for dependent family caregivers. Emergency health information was not provided in accessible formats.[^245]

### §6.24.3 Climate Adaptation, Relocation, and Disability

The Maldivian government's population consolidation policy – the strategic relocation of small island populations to larger hub islands in response to climate vulnerability – has profound disability implications. Relocation from small islands to hub communities involves disrupting the social networks, family support systems, and community relationships on which most persons with disabilities depend for daily living support.[^246]

Hub island communities in which relocated populations are settled do not systematically have the accessible infrastructure, ADDA services, SEN school resources, or community disability support systems that persons with disabilities need. A family with a severely disabled member relocating from a small island to a hub community may find that their destination has better physical infrastructure and stronger service access, or may find that the move has disrupted informal support networks without providing any formal replacement.[^247]

No disability impact assessment framework for climate relocation decisions has been developed. NAPDI v6 does not contain a climate-disability intersection strategy. The 2025 Situational Analysis identifies this gap as a priority area for policy development, given the accelerating pace of climate-driven vulnerability and the likelihood that population relocation pressures will intensify in the coming decade.[^248]

## 6.59 COVID-19 Impact on Persons with Disabilities


The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the depth of exclusion faced by persons with disabilities. When the Public Health Emergency was declared in March 2020, there was **insufficient planning to assist persons with disabilities**. OPDs were not represented in the national task force, limiting their ability to advocate for the disability community's needs.[^90]

### Key Impacts

- **Isolation facilities**: Persons with disabilities who tested positive were taken to isolation facilities but faced significant challenges due to lack of accessible information about the facilities and their procedures. Cases of negligence were reported – persons with disabilities had limited access to support during isolation.[^91]
- **Lockdown barriers**: Helpers were disallowed from assisting persons with disabilities – they could not enter hospitals to provide sign language interpretation or assist in seeking banking and other services.[^92]
- **Mental health deterioration**: Persons with psychosocial disabilities, especially those outside Male', had limited access to mental health professionals and could not receive timely treatment during lockdowns.[^93]
- **Face mask barriers**: The enforcement of face masks posed additional communication barriers for deaf persons who relied on lip reading.[^94]
- **Vehicle ban**: The vehicle ban forced all vehicles to be parked, with many parked on ramps and near pavement access points, making roads inaccessible to persons with disabilities.[^95]
- **Home delivery services**: No special considerations were made for persons with disabilities in ordering groceries, medication, or other necessities through home delivery or online services, most of which were inaccessible.[^96]
- **Education disruption**: Children with disabilities found it challenging to understand the COVID-19 situation and had limited resources to navigate the difficulties. The shift to online learning exacerbated exclusion for students without accessible devices or internet connectivity.[^97]

While accessibility to news updates regarding COVID-19 increased (including some sign language interpretation during press conferences), information and guidelines related to COVID-19 were still largely presented in inaccessible formats.[^98]

## 6.60 Gender and Disability Intersectionality


The intersection of gender and disability produces compounded disadvantage that is poorly understood and inadequately addressed in Maldivian policy.

### Economic Marginalisation of Women with Disabilities

Census data shows that fewer women with disabilities participated in economic activities: the labour force participation rate is **28% for women with disabilities** compared to 41% for men with disabilities.[^99] Economic opportunities have largely benefited men in the absence of gender quotas. Women with disabilities are not encouraged to live independently, typically relying on families, which has left them more vulnerable to violence and exploitation.[^100]

### Violence Against Women and Girls with Disabilities

The HRCM CRPD Shadow Report (2023) found that the prevalence of sexual violence against children with disabilities was **significantly high at 60%**, with a large majority of survivors being girls with disabilities.[^101] Women with disabilities face domestic violence, physical abuse, threats, and harassment from family members, in workplaces, and from members of the public. The inaccessibility of reporting mechanisms – voice calls for police, lack of sign language interpretation at FCSCs – means many incidents go unreported.[^102]

### Caregiving Burden

The 2024 UNPRPD assessment observed that **primary support persons were mostly women**, highlighting the gendered nature of caregiving. Support persons require flexible workplace policies, respite services, and psychosocial support – but no formal support systems exist. The burden placed on women (managing both household and income-generating activities) is significant and unaddressed by current policy.[^103]

### Exclusion from Feminist and Women's Rights Movements

Critically, "feminist and women's rights discourses in Maldives do not adopt an intersectional lens and thus exclude women with disabilities in their advocacy." Even among state institutions, the disability community is perceived as homogenous in terms of gender, resulting in weak data collection on the gendered implications of disability and a lack of targeted interventions.[^104]

### Reproductive Health and Autonomy

Women with disabilities face restricted autonomy in healthcare decisions. Families commonly decide on behalf of their deaf children to undergo cochlear implantation, and families make healthcare decisions for children of deaf parents without parental involvement. Women with psychosocial disabilities face greater acceptance from families than men with psychosocial disabilities, but this acceptance often translates into confinement and reduced independence rather than genuine empowerment.[^105]

## 6.61 Cross-Sectoral Disability Inclusion: Mainstreaming Across Ministry Mandates


### §6.51.1 Ministry of Education and Disability

The Ministry of Education's disability inclusion responsibilities – implemented through the Department of Inclusive Education – are the most developed of any sector ministry's disability mandate. The Education Act (2021) establishes the legal basis; the Department of Inclusive Education provides operational guidance; NIE provides SEN teacher training; and the GPE THAIBA grants provide international financing support.[^495]

Areas requiring further development include: disability-disaggregated education statistics to track out-of-school children; standardised ILP quality monitoring; tertiary education disability support at MNU and MIT; and autism-specialist service extension beyond Malé. The Ministry's engagement with ADDA on the intersection of education and broader disability system concerns is limited – a coordination gap that the Disability Council should bridge.[^496]

### §6.51.2 Ministry of Health and Disability

The Ministry of Health's disability inclusion responsibilities span: physical accessibility of health facilities; disability-competent primary care; rehabilitation service availability; NCD-disability intersection management; maternal health for women with disabilities; and mental health services for persons with psychosocial disabilities.[^497]

No national health system disability inclusion policy exists – disability in health settings is addressed through Aasandha's universal coverage (removing cost barriers) and through the CRPD Periodic Report commitments, but not through a dedicated Ministry of Health disability strategy. The NCD programme does not systematically integrate disability prevention and management. Rehabilitation services remain IGMH-concentrated with no community rehabilitation programme.[^498]

The intersection of maternal health and disability is insufficiently addressed. Women with disabilities – particularly those with physical disabilities, intellectual disabilities, and sensory impairments – face specific barriers and risks in the maternity care system. Accessible antenatal care, disability-appropriate delivery options, and disability-sensitive postnatal support are not systematically provided.[^499]

### §6.51.3 Ministry of Finance and Disability Budget

The Ministry of Finance's role in disability policy is primarily budgetary – allocating resources to MoSFD for ADDA operations and the disability allowance, and determining the overall fiscal space for disability spending. The Ministry of Finance has not been engaged as a stakeholder in disability policy development beyond its budget allocation function.[^500]

The development of a fiscal case for disability inclusion – demonstrating the cost-benefit rationale for increased disability spending through employment participation gains, reduced long-term dependence, and economic productivity contributions – is a missing element of disability policy advocacy in Maldives. The Ministry of Finance's engagement with disability policy would benefit from evidence that disability spending produces economic returns, not merely welfare obligations.[^501]

### §6.51.4 Ministry of Housing and Disability

The Ministry of Housing's disability inclusion responsibilities – accessible housing standards, priority allocation for persons with disabilities, home modification programmes – are among the least developed of any sector ministry's disability mandate. No accessible housing policy exists; no disability housing allocation exists; no home modification grant programme exists.[^502]

The Flat scheme – the government's major public housing programme providing apartments primarily in Hulhumale – has not incorporated universal design standards as a mandatory requirement for a proportion of units. As Hulhumale is developed as a planned urban expansion of the capital, the opportunity to embed universal design from the outset – rather than retrofitting accessibility into an inaccessible built environment – is being missed.[^503]

### §6.51.5 Attorney General's Office, Law Reform Commission, and Legal Reforms

The most urgent legal reforms required for CRPD compliance – supported decision-making legislation replacing guardianship, binding accessibility standards, disability discrimination enforcement mechanisms – require engagement from the Attorney General's Office (drafting legislation), the Law Reform Commission (legal framework review), and the Parliament (enactment). These legal reform actors have not been systematically engaged as disability policy stakeholders.[^504]

The CRPD Periodic Report's commitment to Law Reform Commission engagement on supported decision-making legislation (2026–2028 timeline) requires immediate follow-through. Given the typical timeframes for legislative drafting, consultation, and parliamentary passage in Maldives, a 2026–2028 commitment for supported decision-making legislation requires that the drafting process begin in 2026 at the latest – meaning that engagement with the Attorney General's Office and Law Reform Commission should begin in 2025 as a priority preparation step.[^505]

# Part 17: Reform Agenda, Evidence, and Conclusion


## 6.62 Toward a Transformed Disability System: Reform Architecture and Political Economy


### §6.35.1 The System Transformation Challenge

The disability system in Maldives in 2025 stands at a critical juncture. The formal policy architecture – the Disability Act, NAPDI v6, the NDR, the CRPD Periodic Report commitments – articulates an ambitious rights-based vision. The operational reality – 40% NDR coverage, 25.6% allowance uptake, no personal assistance services, no binding accessibility standards, no supported decision-making framework – documents the persistent gap between vision and implementation.[^369]

Bridging this gap requires not a single policy intervention but a transformation of how the disability system is designed, financed, and governed. Several design principles, drawn from the evidence reviewed throughout this chapter, can guide this transformation:

**Rights as the Baseline**: Policy design should proceed from the recognition that CRPD rights are legal obligations, not aspirational goals. This shifts the question from "what services can we afford?" to "what rights must we fulfil?" – and then to "how do we build the capacity to fulfil them?" The rights-as-baseline approach constrains the use of resource limitation as a permanent justification for rights violations, while acknowledging that implementation timelines must reflect capacity realities.[^370]

**Disability as Mainstreamed, Not Siloed**: The disability system cannot deliver rights solely through ADDA, NSPA, and the disability-specific mechanisms. Education, health, transport, housing, employment, and justice systems must all incorporate disability inclusion as a core operational obligation – not as an optional add-on managed by disability specialists on the margins. Mainstreaming disability inclusion means embedding it in the mandates, standards, monitoring systems, and budgets of all relevant ministries.[^371]

**Evidence-Driven Planning**: NAPDI v6's planning cannot be meaningfully monitored or adjusted without disability outcome data. The National Disability Data Strategy commitment in the CRPD Periodic Report must be treated as a prerequisite for the next NAPDI iteration – without outcome data, the next plan will repeat the pattern of unmeasurable targets and unverifiable progress claims.[^372]

**Geographic Equity as Non-Negotiable**: Policy design that concentrates disability services in Malé while 65% of persons with disabilities live in atolls is not merely operationally inefficient – it is rights-violating. Geographic equity in disability service access requires either decentralisation of services or systematic support for atoll residents to access Malé services (transport subsidies, temporary accommodation, remote service delivery via telehealth and digital platforms).[^373]

**OPD Partnership as Structural**: The next NAPDI must embed OPD participation not as a consultative afterthought but as a structural feature of design, implementation, and monitoring. This includes: OPD representation on the Disability Council with voting rights; OPD participation in NAPDI monitoring committees; OPD involvement in budget review processes; and OPD-led community monitoring of service delivery quality in outer atolls.[^374]

### §6.35.2 The Fiscal Case for Disability Inclusion

The fiscal analysis of disability inclusion in Maldives is underdeveloped – there has been no comprehensive costing of the gap between current disability service provision and CRPD-compliant provision, and no cost-benefit analysis of disability inclusion investments that might make the fiscal case to a Ministry of Finance focused on budget sustainability.[^375]

The global evidence on the economics of disability inclusion suggests a positive investment case across multiple dimensions. Educational inclusion of children with disabilities reduces long-term dependency and increases lifetime employment and tax contributions. Employment support for working-age persons with disabilities reduces long-term allowance dependence and increases economic productivity. Accessible built environments and transport infrastructure generates benefits beyond disability users – older persons, pregnant women, persons with temporary injuries – that justify investment as universal design rather than disability-specific expenditure.[^376]

In the Maldivian context, the most compelling near-term fiscal case relates to the NDR coverage gap and the allowance uptake crisis. The current situation – where 74% of eligible persons with disabilities do not receive their statutory allowance entitlement – is not primarily a government expenditure saving but a failure of rights delivery that simultaneously maintains an under-resourced disability system. Closing the NDR coverage gap to achieve 90% uptake would require increased spending (approximately 3.5x current allowance expenditure at full uptake), representing a genuine fiscal commitment that requires government budget decision-making – not simply administrative improvement.[^377]

### §6.35.3 Islamic Values and Disability Inclusion: An Underutilised Convergence

Islamic values and CRPD rights obligations are not in tension in the Maldivian disability context – they are in significant convergence. Islamic teaching on the obligation to care for and include the vulnerable, on the prohibition of harm (la darar), on human dignity (karama), and on social solidarity (zakat and sadaqah as mechanisms of community support for those in need) provides a normative framework that supports disability inclusion as a religious as well as rights-based obligation.[^378]

This convergence has been underutilised in disability policy and community advocacy in Maldives. Religious scholars and imams – influential voices in Maldivian communities – have rarely been engaged as advocates for disability inclusion. Friday sermons (khutbas) do not routinely address disability as a social inclusion issue. The UNPRPD 2025 Situational Analysis identifies community and religious leaders as an underutilised advocacy resource, suggesting that strategic engagement with religious leadership on disability inclusion framing could shift community attitudes and social norms in ways that formal policy alone cannot achieve.[^379]

The Islamic prohibition on harm (la darar) provides a particularly direct framing for accessibility advocacy: inaccessible buildings, transport, and information systems cause concrete harm to persons with disabilities by excluding them from participation in community life. This framing grounds accessibility obligation in Islamic jurisprudential concepts familiar to Maldivian communities, providing a complementary normative basis alongside the CRPD's human rights framework.[^380]

### §6.35.4 Disability System Governance: The Disability Council and Coordination Architecture

The Disability Council – established under the Disability Act as the central coordinating body for disability policy – represents the institutional mechanism for whole-of-government disability governance. Its mandate encompasses inter-ministerial coordination, NAPDI oversight, OPD engagement, and strategic disability policy direction.[^381]

In practice, the Disability Council's effectiveness has been limited by inconsistent meeting schedules, variable engagement from member ministries, unclear decision-making authority, and limited secretariat capacity within MoSFD to support its functions. The Council has not functioned as the authoritative inter-ministerial coordination mechanism that effective disability system governance requires.[^382]

Strengthening the Disability Council requires: constitutional establishment within revised disability legislation with a clear mandate, composition, and meeting frequency; executive-level membership (minister or deputy minister level) from key ministries (Education, Health, Finance, Housing, Justice) to ensure policy authority; secretariat capacity funded within MoSFD's budget; formal OPD representation with substantive participation rights; and publication of minutes and decisions to ensure accountability.[^383]

### §6.35.5 Regional Hub Service Delivery: An Atoll Architecture for Disability Services

The most operationally innovative possibility for extending disability services to outer atolls within Maldives' geographic and fiscal constraints is the development of an atoll-hub service delivery model. Under this model, each of the five administrative regions of Maldives (North, North Central, Central, South Central, South) would host a regional disability service hub – a facility providing specialist assessment, vocational training, assistive technology provision, rehabilitation services, and OPD support – within the existing regional hospital or atoll capital infrastructure.[^384]

A regional hub model would:
- Reduce the travel requirement for NDR assessment from inter-atoll to intra-region, significantly lowering barriers to registration for persons in outer islands
- Enable vocational training programmes to serve persons with disabilities in regional populations without requiring relocation to Malé
- Provide a physical base for regional OPD chapter activities
- Create a platform for outreach to the most remote islands through hub-based visiting teams
- Enable telehealth connections from outer island health centres to regional hub specialists[^385]

This model requires investment in regional capacity – physical facilities, specialist staffing, operational budgets – and cannot be implemented without sustained budgetary commitment from MoSFD and the Ministry of Finance. However, it represents a more feasible pathway to geographic equity in disability services than attempting to replicate Malé-standard services on every inhabited island – an approach that is neither fiscally realistic nor operationally sustainable at Maldives' scale.[^386]

## 6.63 Research Evidence and Recommendations


### Key Gaps Identified

The 2024 UNPRPD Situational Analysis identified the following overarching gaps:

1. **Medical vs. social model**: A continued reliance on the medical and charity model to guide interactions with persons with disabilities, rather than the human rights-based social model mandated by the CRPD[^106]
2. **Coordination failure**: Severe lack of multi-sectoral collaboration; other state institutions defer all disability matters to MSFD rather than mainstreaming disability rights within their own mandates[^107]
3. **Data fragmentation**: No central database, no harmonised disability measurement across agencies, and limited data collection on disability in education, employment, and other sectors[^108]
4. **Psychosocial disability gap**: Persons with psychosocial and intellectual disabilities are the most marginalised even within the disability community, with limited representation in OPDs and CSOs[^109]
5. **Geographic disparity**: OPDs primarily operate from Male', with no island or atoll-level OPDs. The needs of persons with disabilities in the atolls "have not been amplified to the same extent as those in Male'"[^110]
6. **Accountability deficit**: The Disability Council lacks independence and power; all stakeholder organisations agreed that the current accountability mechanism "was not adequate or effective"[^111]
7. **Budget inadequacy**: Most state institutions do not have specific budget allocations for disability inclusion, and budgetary constraints are forcing institutions to "prioritise financing basic functions within their mandate and disregard inclusivity and accessibility measures"[^112]

### The Qualitative Picture: Voices from Focus Groups

The UNPRPD assessment's FGDs revealed lived experiences that statistics alone cannot capture:

- Families continue to **confine persons with disabilities** to their homes as a "safety precaution," particularly those with psychosocial or intellectual disabilities[^113]
- People with invisible disabilities face additional stigma as they are believed to be **feigning their disability for benefits**[^114]
- Persons with disabilities are actively **discriminated against during custody proceedings**, as courts view disability as evidence of incompetence[^115]
- Digital tools that could assist persons with disabilities are **blocked by the Bank of Maldives** "as the risk for potential fraud and scams by the general public is perceived to be greater than the benefits that persons with disabilities may gain from them"[^116]
- Vote buying targeting persons with disabilities is "rampant among candidates, with the promise of providing financial aid to persons with disabilities to procure assistive devices or obtain medical treatment"[^117]

### The Moosa, Musthafa, and Hassan (2024) Study

A qualitative phenomenological study by Moosa, Musthafa, and Hassan (2024) explored the social grievances of 15 persons with disabilities in the Maldives using the Agenda 2030 framework. The study found that "the environment is physically disabling, access to quality education and social protection is limited," and concluded that "a human rights-based approach to social protection is needed to advance the Leave No One Behind agenda."[^118]

### The Intersectionality Study (McNamara and Clissold, 2019)

Research by McNamara and Clissold (2019) applied an intersectionality framework to Laamu Atoll, exploring how overlapping identity categories – location, youth and gender, disability, marital status – shape vulnerability. The study found that disability combined with other marginalised identities (such as being female, elderly, or living on a remote island) created layered disadvantages that simple categorical approaches could not capture.[^119]

### Baseline Assessment (HRCM/UNDP, 2010)

The earliest systematic assessment of disability activities in the Maldives was conducted by HRCM with UNDP support in 2010, providing a foundational inventory of disability-related programmes, services, and legal provisions that existed prior to the Disability Act's enactment.[^120]

### Priority Recommendations

Drawing from the evidence base, the most pressing priorities for disability policy reform in the Maldives include:

1. **Legislative reform**: Enact a Mental Health Act to provide a legal framework for psychosocial disability services; amend the Disability Act to ensure greater representation of persons with disabilities on the Disability Council; extend accessibility standards beyond the current narrow scope
2. **Registry reform**: Simplify and make accessible the NDR registration process; ensure consistency in medical documentation requirements; reduce delays to under 30 days; provide multiple formats for communication
3. **Service decentralisation**: Extend therapeutic services (speech, occupational, physio-) to the atolls through telehealth and circuit therapist models; establish community-based support and respite services for caregivers
4. **Data harmonisation**: Establish a central disability database accessible across government agencies; adopt WG-ES (enhanced version) in future censuses and surveys to capture psychosocial and intellectual disabilities
5. **Disability-inclusive budgeting**: Require all state institutions to include disability budget lines; ensure the Disability Council has an independent budget
6. **Employment quotas and accommodations**: Implement enforceable employment quotas with monitoring mechanisms; mandate reasonable accommodations with compliance auditing
7. **Accessibility enforcement**: Create accessibility compliance certificates for all new construction; extend minimum standards beyond education and health facilities to all public buildings, transport, and digital platforms
8. **Gender and disability mainstreaming**: Integrate disability into gender equality frameworks and vice versa; collect and publish gender-disaggregated disability data across all sectors
9. **Accountability**: Make the Disability Council independent of the Ministry, with its own budget and enforcement powers; strengthen HRCM's capacity to monitor CRPD implementation

## 6.64 Conclusion: From Rights on Paper to Rights in Life


### §6.52.1 What the Evidence Shows: A Systems Assessment

Disability policy in the Maldives in 2025 can be assessed across four dimensions:

**Legislative Architecture**: Strong. The Disability Act (2010), CRPD ratification (2009), Education Act (2021), Employment Regulation (2024/R-9), and the ongoing Disability Act review create a rights-based legal framework that provides the formal basis for comprehensive disability inclusion. The critical gaps – supported decision-making, binding accessibility standards, OPD participation structures – are identified and targeted for reform.[^506]

**Institutional Infrastructure**: Developing. ADDA, the Disability Council, the NDR, the Department of Inclusive Education, and the HRCM monitoring mechanism provide an institutional architecture that, while under-resourced and Malé-concentrated, represents a genuine disability service and governance infrastructure. The NDR is growing; ADDA's mandate is expanding; the HRCM's monitoring engagement with CRPD processes is real.[^507]

**Service Delivery**: Weak and geographically inequitable. The gap between the 35% of persons with disabilities in Malé who have access to ADDA services, inclusive schools, IGMH rehabilitation, and OPD networks – and the 65% in atolls who have access to almost none of these – is the most critical operational failure of the Maldivian disability system. Closing this gap is the most impactful disability policy priority.[^508]

**Rights Culture and Inclusion**: Emergent. The attitudinal change required for genuine disability inclusion – in communities, employers, healthcare providers, justice actors, and government officials – is a long-term project that is at an early stage. The 2023 election milestone, the growth of OPD networks, and the increasing engagement of international human rights mechanisms are building blocks of a rights culture, but the daily lived experience of most persons with disabilities in Maldives reflects exclusion rather than inclusion.[^509]

### §6.52.2 The Three Non-Negotiable Reforms

Based on the comprehensive evidence review in this chapter, three reforms are identified as non-negotiable – reforms without which fundamental CRPD compliance is structurally impossible regardless of other progress:

**1. Supported Decision-Making Legislation**: The guardianship framework that extinguishes legal capacity for persons with intellectual, cognitive, and psychosocial disabilities must be replaced with a supported decision-making system. This is the most fundamental rights reform required, affecting the right of persons with disabilities to direct their own lives. Without this reform, all other disability rights are conditional on the goodwill of family guardians rather than secured in law.[^510]

**2. Binding Accessibility Standards with Enforcement**: The fifteen-year absence of enforceable accessibility standards – fifteen years since the Disability Act mandated accessibility, fifteen years of public buildings, transport infrastructure, and information systems built without disability access – must end with the Disability Act review. Binding standards, a compliance timeline, an audit mechanism, and an enforcement authority are essential. New public infrastructure constructed without meeting accessibility standards after the review must carry legal liability.[^511]

**3. National Disability Data Strategy**: Without disability-disaggregated outcome data, disability policy is planning in the dark. The National Disability Data Strategy committed to in the CRPD Periodic Report must be implemented as a priority, integrating NDR data with education, health, and employment administrative systems, and establishing regular disability outcome reporting as a statutory obligation across relevant ministries.[^512]

### §6.52.4 The Voice of Persons with Disabilities

The Moosa et al. (2024) phenomenological study – drawing on the lived experiences of fifteen persons with disabilities in Maldives – captures the voice that this chapter's data and analysis must ultimately serve. The central finding that "the environment is physically disabling" – that it is barriers, not impairment, that produce the most significant limitations on participation – contains an implicit demand: remove the barriers, and participation becomes possible.[^513]

This demand is not abstract. It is the demand of the wheelchair user who cannot enter the government office to register in the NDR. The Deaf woman who cannot communicate with the health centre nurse. The parent of a child with autism in an outer atoll who has never had access to early intervention. The young man with an intellectual disability who will spend his adult life in family confinement because no supported independent living option exists. The elderly woman with dementia whose care falls entirely on her daughter because no respite service is available.[^514]

The Disability Act review, the NAPDI revision, the supported decision-making legislation, the accessibility standards, the NDR coverage expansion, the regional hub service model – these are not bureaucratic exercises. They are the mechanisms through which the Maldivian state can fulfil its fifteen-year-old promise, made when it ratified the CRPD in 2009, that all persons with disabilities in Maldives will enjoy the full range of human rights and fundamental freedoms.[^515]

The evidence in this chapter shows what is required. The political will to act on that evidence, sustained across electoral cycles and fiscal pressures, is the final and most uncertain determinant of whether Maldivian disability policy moves from rights on paper to rights in life.[^516]

### §6.66.1 Hameed, S., Banks, L.M., Usman, S.K. and Kuper, H. (2022)

**Full citation**: Hameed, S., Banks, L.M., Usman, S.K. and Kuper, H. (2022). "Access to the Disability Allowance in the Maldives: National coverage and factors affecting uptake." *Global Social Policy* (Special Issue – Disability and Social Policy). London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine. 21 pages.

**Research design**: Mixed methods – nationally representative population-based survey (N=6,500, two-stage stratified sample, July–December 2017) using WG-SS Enhanced (adults) and UNICEF Child Functioning Module (children); qualitative in-depth interviews (23 participants – 18 direct, 5 proxy – purposive sample across Male' and outer atolls, enrolled and non-enrolled).[^630]

**Key findings**:
- 25.6% national disability allowance coverage – 74.4% of eligible persons not receiving allowance
- Lowest coverage: sensory impairments; women; older adults; wealthier households (means-testing misperception)
- Five exclusion mechanisms: information gaps; disability identity/eligibility perception barriers; geographic/logistical barriers; stigma/charity framing; procedural complexity
- Outer atoll transaction costs for application and assessment can exceed one month's allowance value
- Application costs create perverse exclusion of poorest, most remote eligible persons
- Sensory impairment low coverage attributable to non-identification through health/social systems and positive Deaf identity not mapping onto "disability" label
- Means-testing misconception specifically documented as access barrier for non-poorest eligible households

**Recommendations**: Systematic public awareness campaigns; assessment decentralisation to outer atolls; cost reduction (fee elimination, transport subsidies); procedural simplification; clear non-means-tested eligibility communication; community health worker integration.[^631]

**Context**: LSHTM disability and international development research partnership; EU/UNDP-Maldives supported research. Published in a peer-reviewed international journal providing methodological credibility and international comparative context.

- Mobile phone: 81% vs. 97%[^632]

### §6.66.3 Moosa, S., Musthafa, H.S. and Hassan, A. (2024)

**Full citation**: Moosa, S., Musthafa, H.S. and Hassan, A. (2024). "Challenges and approaches for Agenda 2030: Perspectives of persons with disabilities in a small island context." *The International Education Journal: Comparative Perspectives*, Vol. 23, No. 1. Te Manawa Teki Trauma Research Centre (New Zealand), University Canada West, Maldives National University. Funded by European Union through UNDP Maldives.

**Research design**: Phenomenological qualitative inquiry – focus groups and in-depth interviews with 15 participants across six island locations (Addu/Seenu Atoll, Kalhuduffushi/Haa Dhaalu, Gan/Laamu Atoll, Male', Madifushi/Thaa Atoll, Rasgatheem/Raa Atoll).

**Key findings**:
- Only 5% of persons with disabilities engage in community activities
- 40–60% of women with disabilities subjected to sexual abuse
- 23.3% disability allowance coverage (2017–2019 data)
- Disability prevalence increased from 6.8% (2017) to 9.2% (2019)
- Four main themes: disabling physical environment; exclusion and limited social participation; limited opportunities for education and skills development; inadequate social protection and safety
- International comparison: Fiji 13.7%, Mauritius 3.75%, Seychelles 4.24%
- Political exploitation of children with disabilities in campaigns documented
- "Environment is physically disabling" – central analytical finding
- Verbatim evidence on Braille inaccessibility, teacher attitude gaps, employment capability, English language barriers[^633]

### §6.66.4 HRCM/UNDP (2010) – Baseline Assessment

**Full citation**: Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM) and UNDP (2010). *Activities Addressing Rights of Persons with Disabilities: A Baseline Assessment*. CDE Consulting. April 2010. Foreword by Ahmed Saleem, President of HRCM.

**Historical significance**: The foundational audit of disability services at the time of CRPD ratification (April 2010). Provides the 2010 baseline against which 15 years of implementation can be measured.

**Key statistics**:
- Historical prevalence: 1981 (0.9%), 2000 (1.75%), 2002 (3.4%), 2009 HI survey (8.1% all disabilities; 4.7% severe permanent)
- Education 2009: Only 230 children with disabilities in school (of estimated 2,250 = 10% enrollment)
- Healthcare: 2 psychiatrists nationwide; 120-bed psychiatric facility at Guraidhoo; USD 21 per staff per year in training
- Blind allowance: MVR 1,500 by 2009 (after HRCM advocacy); 220 recipients
- Assistive devices (2008): 90 wheelchairs; 45 hearing aids; 488 spectacle pairs
- First MSL Dictionary: 28 October 2009 (~650 words); Maldives Deaf Association launched 27 October 2009
- Employment: Haa Alifu/Haa Dhaalu survey – 71% working-age men with disabilities and 93% of women not in work
- Assistive device unmet need: 72% with hearing difficulties had no hearing aids; 76–89% with mobility difficulties had no wheelchair

**29 recommendations** spanning: CRPD implementation; early intervention; education; mental health; residential care; employment; information access; building accessibility; staff training; and an "Office for Disability" governance structure.[^634]
