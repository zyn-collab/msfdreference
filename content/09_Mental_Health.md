# Part IX: Mental Health and Psychosocial Support

## Introduction

Mental health has been a historically neglected dimension of social policy in the Republic of Maldives. For decades, the nation's health system prioritised communicable disease control, maternal and child health, and the epidemiological transition toward non-communicable diseases, while mental health remained largely invisible in policy discourse, budgetary allocations, and service design. The consequences of this neglect are now visible: rising rates of depression and anxiety among young people, high levels of suicidal ideation among adolescents, a substance abuse problem deeply intertwined with untreated psychological distress, and a mental health workforce so thin that, until recently, patients in Male' waited over eighteen months to see a psychiatrist.

The Constitution of the Republic of Maldives (2008), in Article 23(c), guarantees every citizen "good standards of health care, physical and mental," placing mental health on equal constitutional footing with physical health.[^1] Yet the gap between this constitutional promise and the operational reality of mental health services remains wide. The Maldives' unique geography -- 1,192 islands scattered across 26 natural atolls, with approximately 187 inhabited islands -- creates service delivery challenges unlike those faced by any other country in South Asia. A person experiencing a psychiatric crisis on a remote island may be days of boat travel away from the nearest psychiatrist. Stigma, deeply rooted in cultural and religious interpretations that attribute mental illness to spiritual causes (*jinni* possession, *sihr* or sorcery), compounds access barriers by discouraging help-seeking behaviour.

This chapter provides a comprehensive analysis of the Maldivian mental health and psychosocial support landscape, tracing the evolution of policy frameworks, mapping the current service delivery architecture, examining prevalence data with particular attention to youth and adolescent populations, and assessing the intersecting crises of substance abuse and self-harm. It documents the significant -- if still insufficient -- reforms undertaken since 2019, including the establishment of the National Centre for Mental Health, the decentralisation of psychiatric services to regional hospitals, and the introduction of telepsychiatry for remote island populations. Throughout, the analysis foregrounds the gap between policy aspiration and implementation reality, drawing on data from the WHO, UNICEF, the National Drug Agency, and government administrative records.

## 9.1 National Mental Health Policy and Legislative Framework

### Constitutional and Legislative Foundations

The constitutional basis for mental health services in the Maldives rests on Article 23(c) of the 2008 Constitution, which enshrines the right to "good standards of health care, physical and mental" as a directive principle of state policy.[^1] While directive principles are not directly justiciable in Maldivian courts in the same manner as fundamental rights, they establish a normative obligation on the state to progressively realise mental health care as a component of the right to health.

The primary legislative instruments governing health service delivery, under which mental health services operate, include:

- **Health Services Act (No. 29/2015)**: Establishes the regulatory framework for all health service provision, including mental health services, and defines the obligations of health facilities and providers.[^2]
- **Health Care Profession Act (No. 13/2015)**: Regulates health care professionals, including psychiatrists, psychologists, and counsellors, setting registration, licensing, and practice standards.[^3]
- **Drug Act (No. 17/2011)**: Established the National Drug Agency (NDA) and the Drug Court, creating the institutional architecture for substance abuse treatment and rehabilitation -- a domain with significant overlap with mental health services.[^4]

Notably, the Maldives does not possess a standalone Mental Health Act. There is no dedicated legislation governing involuntary commitment, the rights of persons with mental illness in institutional settings, capacity and consent in psychiatric treatment, or the regulation of psychotropic medications distinct from general pharmaceutical regulation. This legislative gap has significant practical consequences: clinicians lack clear legal authority for emergency psychiatric holds; patients' rights during involuntary treatment remain uncodified; and there is no statutory framework for community treatment orders or supervised discharge.

### The National Mental Health Policy 2015-2025

The first comprehensive mental health policy framework for the Maldives was the **National Mental Health Policy 2015-2025**, developed by the Ministry of Health with WHO technical assistance.[^5] This ten-year policy document was the first formal recognition of mental health as a public health priority requiring systematic government intervention.

The policy identified several core objectives:

1. Integration of mental health into primary health care at all levels of the health system
2. Development of human resources for mental health, including training of general practitioners and nurses in basic mental health care
3. Establishment of community-based mental health services to reduce reliance on institutional care
4. Protection of the human rights of persons with mental disorders
5. Development of a mental health information system for surveillance and planning
6. Reduction of stigma through public education and awareness campaigns

The policy was informed by the WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP), which provides evidence-based guidelines for scaling up mental health services in low- and middle-income countries through task-shifting to non-specialist health workers. The mhGAP approach was particularly relevant to the Maldives given the severe shortage of specialist mental health professionals and the need to extend services across a geographically dispersed population.

However, implementation of the 2015-2025 policy was slow and uneven during its first several years. The policy lacked a dedicated implementation budget, no implementation unit was established within the Ministry of Health, and accountability mechanisms for tracking progress against policy targets were weak. A 2021 stakeholder consultation conducted for the UNICEF Situation Analysis found that "counselling and mental health support services for children need to be strengthened" and that "youth and adolescent health services -- in and out of school -- are a key gap," suggesting that five years into the policy period, fundamental service gaps persisted.[^6]

### Subsequent Strategic Frameworks

Recognition of the need for more operational planning instruments led to the development of several successor and complementary strategies:

- **Central and Regional Mental Health Services Development Plan 2022-2025**: This operational plan focused specifically on building the institutional infrastructure for mental health service delivery, including the establishment of Mental Health Centres at regional hospitals and the expansion of the National Centre for Mental Health at IGMH.[^7]
- **Maldives Mental Health Strategic Action Plan 2025-2029**: The most recent strategic framework, this action plan builds on the lessons of the 2015-2025 policy period and sets more specific, time-bound targets for service expansion, workforce development, and integration of mental health into primary care.[^8]

Additionally, mental health features as a cross-cutting concern in several broader health and social policy instruments:

- The **Health Master Plan 2016-2025** includes Goal 6: "Reduce chronic diseases and improve mental and psychological health," with strategic inputs including "provide access to counselling and peer support services for those with mental health problems and addictions" and "develop and implement strategic action plans...including a mental health strategy."[^9]
- The **WHO Country Cooperation Strategy 2013-2017** identified "addressing the challenge of noncommunicable diseases and mental health" as its second strategic priority for WHO collaboration with the Maldives, and committed to providing "policy and technical support to prevent and reduce disease, disability and premature death from chronic NCDs, lifestyle risk factors, mental disorders, injuries and visual impairment."[^10]

### Assessment of the Policy Environment

The Maldives' mental health policy landscape presents a paradox: there is no shortage of policy documents, strategies, and action plans, yet implementation consistently lags behind aspiration. Several structural factors explain this gap:

**Fragmented institutional responsibility.** Mental health governance is split across multiple entities -- the Ministry of Health (policy and regulation), IGMH and regional hospitals (clinical services), the National Drug Agency (substance abuse treatment), the Ministry of Social and Family Development (psychosocial support for vulnerable populations), and the Ministry of Education (school-based mental health). No single coordinating body has authority over the full spectrum of mental health and psychosocial support.

**Absence of dedicated mental health legislation.** Without a Mental Health Act, the entire mental health system operates within general health legislation that was not designed for the specific challenges of psychiatric care, including involuntary treatment, capacity assessment, and anti-discrimination protections.

**Budget constraints.** Mental health has historically received a negligible share of the national health budget. While Husnu'vaa Aasandha (the universal health insurance scheme) exempts user fees for mental health care and substance abuse rehabilitation -- a progressive policy that removes financial barriers to access -- the supply-side funding for expanding services, training staff, and building infrastructure has been insufficient.[^11]

**Political discontinuity.** Changes of government in 2012, 2013, 2018, and 2023 have each brought shifts in health policy priorities, disrupting continuity in mental health programme implementation. The Regional Hospitals Decentralisation and Autonomisation Report (2023) noted a pattern of "constant policy upheaval" that affects long-term health system planning.[^12]

## 9.2 Mental Health Service Delivery Architecture

### The Four-Tier Health System and Mental Health

The Maldivian health system operates on a four-tier model, each tier with different capacity for mental health service delivery:

**Tier 1 -- Island Health Centres (169+ facilities):** Primary health care facilities on inhabited islands, staffed by community health workers, nurses, and in some cases a general practitioner. Until recently, these facilities had virtually no capacity to address mental health concerns. The decentralisation reforms of 2019-2023 began integrating basic mental health screening and video consultation capacity into some Health Centres, but this remains uneven across atolls.[^13]

**Tier 2 -- Atoll Hospitals (14 facilities):** Secondary-level hospitals serving multiple islands within an atoll. These facilities offer a broader range of services than Health Centres but typically lack dedicated mental health professionals. Some have begun receiving outreach visits from regional hospital psychiatrists.[^14]

**Tier 3 -- Regional Hospitals (5 facilities):** The five regional hospitals -- Kulhudhuffushi Regional Hospital, Ungoofaaru Regional Hospital, Gan Regional Hospital, Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital (Thinadhoo), and Addu Equatorial Hospital -- form the backbone of decentralised specialist care. As of 2023, all five regional hospitals have been staffed with specialist psychiatrists, collectively conducting approximately 150-200 psychiatric consultations per week.[^15]

**Tier 4 -- Central Hospitals (IGMH, Hulhumale' Hospital):** The Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) in Male' serves as the apex facility for mental health services in the Maldives. The National Centre for Mental Health was inaugurated at IGMH in 2019 by President Ibrahim Mohamed Solih, and Mental Health Inpatient Wards were opened in 2022.[^16] Hulhumale' Hospital established a Mental Health Centre in October 2022, in collaboration with the Cadabams Group of Health Care from India.[^17]

### The National Centre for Mental Health

The National Centre for Mental Health was established at IGMH in 2019, marking a major step in institutional development for mental health services. Prior to its establishment, psychiatric services at IGMH were limited to a small outpatient department with a handful of expatriate psychiatrists, resulting in waitlists exceeding 3,300 patients and waiting times of over eighteen months -- a period that extended further during the COVID-19 pandemic due to service disruptions and increased demand.[^18]

The National Centre for Mental Health provides:

- **Outpatient psychiatric consultations**, including diagnostic assessment, medication management, and follow-up care
- **Inpatient psychiatric care** through dedicated Mental Health Inpatient Wards opened in 2022, equipped with padded observation rooms for acute psychiatric emergencies[^19]
- **Comprehensive diagnosis and treatment services** including therapy sessions and specialised care pathways
- **Coordination of regional mental health services**, overseeing the network of Regional Mental Health Centres

By August 2023, the impact of the expanded services and decentralisation was dramatic: psychiatry waitlists at IGMH had decreased from over 3,300 to approximately 330 -- a reduction of approximately 90%. The average waiting period for appointments had been shortened from over eighteen months to approximately two months.[^20] A two-month wait remains clinically significant, particularly for patients in acute distress, but the reduction indicates that targeted investment in mental health infrastructure can produce measurable improvements.

### Regional Mental Health Centres

The decentralisation of mental health services through the establishment of Regional Mental Health Centres (MHCs) at strategic locations has been a significant health reform in recent years. MHCs have been established at:

- **IGMH (Male')**: The National Centre for Mental Health, serving as the hub
- **Hulhumale' Hospital**: Established October 2022, in collaboration with the Cadabams Group of Health Care, India. Offers comprehensive diagnosis, treatment, specialised care, and therapy sessions[^21]
- **Addu Equatorial Hospital**: Serving the southern population
- **Ungoofaaru Regional Hospital**: Serving the northern-central atolls, featuring a dedicated "Sidhdha" wing with child-friendly areas for paediatric mental health patients[^22]
- **Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital (Thinadhoo)**: Has opened tender to build a dedicated Mental Health Centre[^23]

These centres offer comprehensive services including psychiatric assessment, pharmacotherapy, psychotherapy, and follow-up care. The Hulhumale' MHC model is notable for its collaboration with a private Indian healthcare provider, representing a public-private partnership approach to mental health service delivery that could be replicated elsewhere.

### Telepsychiatry and Video Consultations

An important development has been the introduction of psychiatry video consultations, enabling residents of remote atolls to access specialist psychiatric care without the cost and disruption of travel to a regional hospital. This programme has been implemented across six atolls:

- All islands within **L. Atoll** and **Th. Atoll** can access video consultations with psychiatrists at Gan Regional Hospital via their local Health Centres
- Islands in **H.Dh. Atoll**, **R. Atoll**, and **G.Dh. Atoll** access video consultations through their respective Regional Health Centres
- The programme provides access to approximately **55,000 people across 56 islands**[^24]

This telepsychiatry model has proved particularly valuable for populations facing the greatest barriers to in-person care: parents of children with mental health concerns, persons with mental disabilities who find travel difficult, and individuals experiencing severe withdrawal symptoms or suicidal ideation who need urgent consultation but cannot safely travel. The foreword of the Regional Hospitals Decentralisation Report noted that "on 56 islands, a video consultation with a psychiatrist is available within two weeks' time."[^25]

### Outreach Services

Beyond fixed-facility services and telemedicine, regional hospitals conduct psychiatric outreach visits to smaller islands. Psychiatrists from regional hospitals visit islands within their atoll catchment on a regular basis, providing consultations to patients who can receive consistent treatment on their home island. This approach is significant because psychiatric care typically requires ongoing follow-up and medication management -- services that are difficult to deliver through a single telehealth consultation.[^26]

Mental Health Centres at Hulhumale' Hospital and Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital carry out **mental health Early Intervention programs in schools**, designed to identify early signs of mental health concerns among students and provide support. This school-based approach represents a shift toward preventive mental health care, reaching young people before their conditions become severe enough to require specialist treatment.[^27]

### Integration with Primary Health Care

The integration of mental health into primary health care remains a work in progress. The most notable initiative is the collaboration between Public Health Units and regional hospital psychiatry teams. At Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital, Public Health Unit workers arrange counselling for patients in need, and during home visits screen for people who might need mental health support or treatment. Critically, health workers "work to convince them directly to consult with a doctor, working on the frontline to destigmatize treatment for those that need it."[^28] This frontline destigmatisation role addresses one of the main barriers to mental health care in the Maldives.

Mental health GAP (mhGAP) trainings have been held for health workers, nurses, and doctors in several atolls, building the capacity of non-specialist health workers to recognise and manage common mental health conditions. These trainings, aligned with the WHO mhGAP framework, equip primary care workers with skills to identify depression, anxiety, psychosis, and substance use disorders, and to initiate basic management or appropriate referral.[^29]

## 9.3 Prevalence and Burden of Mental Illness

### The Data Gap

Any assessment of mental health prevalence in the Maldives must begin with an acknowledgment of the severe limitations of available data. The country has never conducted a dedicated national mental health survey. There is no mental health information system that systematically collects diagnostic data across health facilities. Prevalence estimates must therefore be derived from health surveys that included mental health modules, facility-based administrative data, and extrapolation from regional and global estimates.

The absence of robust epidemiological data has itself been a barrier to policy action: without clear prevalence figures, it has been difficult to make the case for proportionate budget allocations, to plan human resource needs, or to establish baselines against which to measure the impact of interventions.

### Depression and Anxiety

The most comprehensive population-level data on depression prevalence comes from the **WHO STEP Survey 2021**, which found that **18.8% of young people aged 15-29 were classified as depressed**, with a pronounced gender disparity: **33.5% of females** compared to **9.6% of males**.[^30] This gender gap -- with females more than three times as likely to meet criteria for depression -- is consistent with both global patterns and with the specific stressors facing young Maldivian women, including early and sometimes forced marriage, domestic violence, limited economic autonomy, and restrictive gender norms.

The depression data from the STEP Survey, broken down by severity, reveals:

| Classification | Male (%) | Female (%) | Total (%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| No depression | 90.4 | 66.5 | 81.2 |
| Mild depression | 6.9 | 17.3 | 10.8 |
| Moderate depression | 1.8 | 10.3 | 5.0 |
| Moderately severe | 0.6 | 3.5 | 1.7 |
| Severe depression | 0.3 | 2.4 | 1.2 |

*Source: WHO STEP Survey 2021, as cited in UNICEF SitAn 2025, Table 3.5*[^31]

These figures indicate that among young women, approximately one in six (16.2%) experience moderate to severe depression -- a level of distress that significantly impairs daily functioning, educational attainment, and social participation. The concentration of mental health burden among young women intersects with other social vulnerabilities documented elsewhere in this encyclopedia, particularly domestic violence, economic dependence, and the pressures of early motherhood.

### Loneliness, Anxiety, and Worry

The 2009 Global School-Based Student Health Survey (GSHS) provides the most detailed available data on adolescent mental health, though its age means it serves more as a baseline than a current assessment. The survey found:

- **10.1% of students** reported feeling lonely most of the time or always during the past 12 months
- **9.5% of students** reported being so worried about something that they could not sleep at night most of the time or always during the past 12 months
- These rates were higher among female students and among students in atoll schools compared to Male'[^32]

The GSHS data revealed a significant geographical pattern: students in atoll schools reported higher rates of loneliness, worry, and suicidal ideation than their counterparts in Male'. This counterintuitive finding -- given that Male' is one of the most densely populated cities in the world -- likely reflects the limited access to services, recreational activities, and peer support networks available on smaller islands, as well as the disruption caused by the common practice of sending adolescents to Male' or regional centres for secondary education, separating them from family networks.

### Parental Concern

The **Knowledge, Attitudes and Practices (KAP) Survey 2023** found that **80% of parents worried about their children's mental health** and **89% wanted more information on adolescent mental health**.[^33] Regional variation in parental concern was notable: the Central region reported the highest levels of worry (73%), followed by the North (69%), the Greater Male' Area (62%), and the South (61%).[^34]

These figures reveal both a positive development -- widespread parental recognition that mental health is a genuine concern for their children -- and a troubling reality: that the vast majority of parents feel ill-equipped to address these concerns. The 89% demand for more information suggests a population that is ready to engage with mental health but lacks the knowledge and resources to do so, pointing to a significant opportunity for public education interventions.

## 9.4 Youth and Adolescent Mental Health

### Overview

Youth mental health in the Maldives is a central dimension of the broader mental health challenge. Several converging factors -- a young population (with a significant proportion under 25), rapid social and cultural change, the pressures of urbanisation and migration, limited recreational and employment opportunities, exposure to social media, and the stresses of the Maldivian education system -- have created a mental health burden among young people that the existing service infrastructure is not well equipped to address.

The data paints a consistent picture across multiple surveys and time periods:

- **GSHS 2009**: 19.9% of students seriously considered attempting suicide during the past 12 months[^35]
- **WHO STEP Survey 2021**: 18.8% of young people aged 15-29 classified as depressed[^36]
- **KAP Survey 2023**: 80% of parents worried about their children's mental health[^37]
- **MSFD data 2024**: 211 self-harm/suicidal behaviour cases received, 74% female[^38]
- **MPS data 2021-2024**: 37 attempted suicide cases involving child victims reported to police[^39]

### Risk Factors Specific to Maldivian Youth

Several risk factors are particularly salient in the Maldivian context:

**Educational pressure and disruption.** The education system in the Maldives places significant pressure on students, particularly around the O-Level and A-Level examination periods. For students from outer atolls, accessing quality secondary education often requires migration to Male' or regional centres, separating young people from family and community support networks during a critical developmental period. The UNICEF 2021 SitAn stakeholder consultations identified "youth and adolescent health services -- in and out of school -- are a key gap."[^40]

**Limited economic opportunity.** Youth unemployment is significantly higher than adult unemployment, and the Maldivian economy's heavy reliance on tourism creates employment that is often seasonal, geographically concentrated, and characterised by conditions (including isolation on resort islands, separation from family, and exposure to alcohol in a society where it is otherwise prohibited) that can exacerbate mental health vulnerabilities.

**Digital media exposure.** The Maldives has one of the highest rates of internet penetration in South Asia. While connectivity has positive dimensions, exposure to social media has been linked to increased body image dissatisfaction, cyberbullying, and social comparison, particularly among adolescent girls. The KAP Survey 2023 finding that 80% of parents worried about their children's mental health reflects, in part, parental concern about digital influences.[^41]

**Domestic violence exposure.** Children who witness or experience domestic violence -- a pervasive phenomenon in the Maldives, documented extensively in this encyclopedia's Part V -- are at significantly elevated risk for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and behavioural disorders. The vulnerability mapping research identified that children from homes experiencing domestic violence exhibit a range of psychosocial difficulties that often go unaddressed due to lack of counselling services.[^42]

**Early marriage and sexual violence.** Girls who experience early or forced marriage, or who are survivors of sexual violence, face acute mental health risks. The 2021 UNICEF SitAn noted that psychosocial support services for child survivors of violence were inadequate, with counsellors often unavailable or undertrained.[^43]

### Service Gaps for Youth

Despite the scale of the youth mental health burden, dedicated services remain severely limited:

- There is **no specialised child and adolescent psychiatry service** in the Maldives. Children and adolescents requiring psychiatric care are seen by general adult psychiatrists, who may lack specific training in developmental psychopathology, age-appropriate therapeutic approaches, and the legal and ethical considerations of treating minors.
- **School-based mental health services** are nascent. While the early intervention programmes at Hulhumale' Hospital and Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital represent promising pilots, they reach only a small fraction of the student population.[^44]
- The **absence of adolescent-friendly health service models** means that young people often face the same barriers as adults in accessing care -- including long wait times, clinical environments designed for adult patients, and healthcare workers who may lack training in adolescent communication and engagement.
- UNICEF noted that there are **no legal age limits for unmarried adolescents to access mental health services without parental consent** in the Maldives,[^45] which, while removing one potential barrier, raises questions about the adequacy of safeguards for unaccompanied minors in mental health treatment settings.

## 9.5 Psychosocial Support Services

### The MSFD and Family Service Centres

Psychosocial support in the Maldives operates at the intersection of the health system and the social protection system. The Ministry of Social and Family Development (MSFD) provides psychosocial support through several channels:

- **Family and Children's Service Centres (FCSCs)**: Located across atolls, these centres are the primary entry point for psychosocial support related to domestic violence, child abuse, and family breakdown. FCSCs provide counselling, case management, and referral to clinical services. However, their capacity is limited: many FCSCs operate with a single social worker responsible for an entire atoll, and counselling services may be limited to basic supportive intervention rather than evidence-based psychotherapy.[^46]
- **Case referral pathways**: When FCSCs or other social service agencies identify individuals with mental health needs that exceed their capacity, the referral pathway leads to health system services -- regional hospital psychiatrists or the National Centre for Mental Health. In practice, these referral pathways are often slow and fragmented, with limited feedback loops between social services and health services.
- **Emergency psychosocial response**: In cases of acute crisis -- including suicide attempts, severe domestic violence, and child abuse disclosures -- MSFD provides emergency psychosocial support. In 2024, MSFD received 211 cases of self-harm and suicidal behaviour, with 74% involving females, indicating that the psychosocial support system is increasingly being called upon to respond to mental health emergencies.[^47]

### Counselling Services: Availability and Quality

The counselling landscape in the Maldives is characterised by high demand and extremely limited supply. Key challenges include:

**Qualification and regulation gaps.** There is no standardised accreditation system for counsellors in the Maldives. Individuals providing "counselling" may range from holders of postgraduate clinical psychology degrees to persons with short-course certificates in basic counselling skills. The Health Care Profession Act (No. 13/2015) regulates health professionals, but the boundary between regulated clinical practice and unregulated counselling activity remains unclear.

**Geographic concentration.** The majority of qualified counsellors and psychologists are concentrated in Male' and Hulhumale'. Atoll-level counselling services, where they exist, are typically provided by social workers with limited mental health training rather than by trained counsellors or psychologists.

**Cultural competence.** Effective psychosocial support in the Maldivian context requires cultural competence -- an understanding of the role of Islam in shaping attitudes toward mental health, the influence of extended family dynamics, the specific stressors of island life, and the ways in which cultural norms around honour and shame affect disclosure. Many counselling approaches used in the Maldives are adapted from Western models without systematic cultural adaptation.

### Psychosocial Support in Child Protection

The child and family protection system (discussed in detail in Part IV of this encyclopedia) relies heavily on psychosocial support as a core intervention. The vulnerability mapping research found that vulnerable women and children frequently present with complex trauma histories requiring sustained therapeutic engagement, yet the system's capacity to provide this is severely limited. Counselling services within the child protection framework are often crisis-oriented -- responding to immediate distress following an abuse disclosure or family breakdown -- rather than providing the sustained, trauma-focused intervention that evidence indicates is necessary for recovery.[^48]

## 9.6 Suicide Prevention and Self-Harm

### Prevalence Data

Suicide and self-harm represent the most acute manifestation of mental health distress, and the available data indicates high rates, particularly among young people:

**GSHS 2009 findings on suicidal ideation among students:**
- **19.9%** of students seriously considered attempting suicide during the past 12 months
- **16.8%** of students made a plan about how they would attempt suicide
- **12.0%** of students actually attempted suicide one or more times
- Rates were **higher in atoll schools** than in Male', suggesting that geographic isolation, limited recreational opportunities, and reduced access to services are risk factors
- Among those who attempted suicide, **5.1%** made an attempt that resulted in an injury, poisoning, or overdose that had to be treated by a doctor or nurse[^49]

These figures are high by international standards. One in five Maldivian students had seriously considered suicide, and one in eight had made an attempt, rates that exceed global averages.

**MPS data on attempted suicide involving children (January 2021 - September 2024):**

The Maldives Police Service (MPS) recorded 37 attempted suicide cases with child victims over this period, with the following age distribution:

| Age Group | Number of Cases |
|---|---|
| 0-6 years | 0 |
| 7-10 years | 2 |
| 11-14 years | 15 |
| 15-17 years | 20 |

*Source: MPS data as cited in UNICEF SitAn 2025, Table 3.6*[^50]

The concentration of cases in the 11-17 age range -- with 35 of 37 cases involving children aged 11 and older -- corresponds to the adolescent developmental period characterised by identity formation, peer relationship challenges, and, in the Maldivian context, educational pressures and potential exposure to domestic violence.

**MSFD data 2024:** The Ministry of Social and Family Development received 211 self-harm and suicidal behaviour cases in 2024 alone, with 74% involving females.[^51] The pronounced female predominance aligns with the STEP Survey depression data (33.5% of young women depressed, compared to 9.6% of young men) and suggests that gendered social pressures -- early marriage, domestic violence, restricted mobility, and limited economic autonomy -- are significant drivers of self-harm among Maldivian women and girls.

### Risk Factors and Protective Factors

Risk factors for suicide and self-harm in the Maldivian context include:

- **Untreated depression and anxiety**, particularly among adolescent girls
- **Substance abuse**, with the Drugs SitAn 2021 finding that 57.8% of drug users began substance use before age 18, creating a population of young people with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders[^52]
- **Domestic violence and sexual abuse**, which are associated with both acute suicidal ideation and chronic self-harm
- **Geographic isolation**, particularly on smaller islands where there is no access to mental health professionals, limited peer support, and few diversionary or recreational activities
- **Academic pressure**, particularly around examination periods
- **Social media and cyberbullying**

Protective factors include:

- **Family and community cohesion**, which remains strong on many islands
- **Religious faith**, which, while sometimes contributing to stigma around mental illness, also provides meaning, community, and moral frameworks that can be protective
- **Access to mental health services**, which, as documented in Section 9.2, has been expanding but remains inadequate

### Gaps in Suicide Prevention

The Maldives lacks several elements of an effective suicide prevention strategy:

- **No national suicide prevention strategy**: While mental health policies reference suicide prevention, there is no dedicated national strategy with specific targets, protocols, and accountability mechanisms
- **No national crisis helpline**: There is no widely publicised, 24/7 telephone or text-based crisis line for persons in suicidal distress
- **Limited gatekeeper training**: While some mhGAP training has been conducted, systematic gatekeeper training -- equipping teachers, police, community health workers, and other frontline professionals to recognise and respond to suicidal behaviour -- has not been scaled
- **No means restriction policies**: Evidence-based means restriction (such as limiting access to pesticides, medications, or other common methods) has not been systematically implemented
- **Inadequate postvention**: Following a suicide, there is no standardised postvention protocol -- a coordinated response to support bereaved family members, affected community members, and others at risk of contagion effects

## 9.7 Substance Abuse and Mental Health

### Substance Abuse and Mental Health Overlap

Substance abuse and mental health in the Maldives are closely linked, with each contributing to the other in ways that the service system is poorly equipped to address. The **Situational Analysis of Drugs in the Maldives 2021**, conducted by the National Drug Agency (NDA) with WHO support, provides the most comprehensive available data on substance abuse patterns and their intersection with mental health.[^53]

### Substance Abuse Patterns

The 2021 NDA/WHO survey of 403 drug users across all atolls revealed:

**Demographics:**
- 94% male; median age 35; youngest 16, oldest 58
- 49% had education to middle school level (Grades 6-9); 31% to high school level
- 41.4% had family members who also used drugs, indicating intergenerational patterns[^54]

**Substances used:**
- Brown sugar (impure heroin): 34.2%
- Alcohol: 17.4%
- Opioids: 15.1%
- Hashish: 13.4%
- Multiple substance use was common, with many users reporting use of several substances[^55]

**Age of onset:**
- **57.8% began using drugs before age 18**, with onset reported as early as 9 years of age
- Peer influence was the primary reason for initiation (72.2%)
- Addiction (26%) was the primary reason for continued use[^56]

**Injecting drug use:**
- **21.6% reported injecting drug use (IDU)**
- Among IDUs, 43.6% reported sharing needles and 62% reported reusing needles, creating significant HIV/Hepatitis transmission risk
- The NDA reported over 50,000 lab tests between 2016 and 2020, with patients testing positive for opiates doubling over that period[^57]

### Treatment Access and Outcomes

The treatment landscape shows significant gaps:

- **34.2% of surveyed drug users had never sought treatment** -- suggesting that over a third of the drug-using population has had no contact with the treatment system
- **56.1% accessed treatment through the Drug Court** -- indicating that the criminal justice system, not the health system, is the primary gateway to treatment. This raises serious questions about the therapeutic quality of court-mandated treatment and the human rights of persons compelled into treatment
- Only **8.9% sought treatment voluntarily**, suggesting profound barriers to voluntary help-seeking, including stigma, distrust of treatment services, and lack of accessible services
- **41.4% completed treatment**, but **42.7% relapsed**, with peer influence (43%) the most commonly cited reason for relapse
- **40% received no community support** after treatment, and 23% identified job opportunities as their primary need[^58]

The UNICEF 2021 SitAn stakeholder consultations were blunt about the gaps: **"Drug treatment and rehabilitation for adolescents is not available"** and **"primary prevention and early intervention for drug abuse need to be strengthened."**[^59]

### The Mental Health-Substance Abuse Nexus

The intersection of substance abuse and mental health in the Maldives manifests in several ways:

**Self-medication.** Many individuals use substances to cope with untreated depression, anxiety, trauma, and other mental health conditions. The lack of accessible mental health services pushes people toward readily available substances as a means of managing psychological distress.

**Substance-induced mental health conditions.** Chronic substance abuse, particularly of opioids and synthetic drugs, can trigger or exacerbate depression, psychosis, anxiety disorders, and cognitive impairment.

**Co-occurring disorders.** The treatment system is poorly equipped to address co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders. Treatment facilities typically specialise in either mental health or substance abuse, with limited capacity for integrated dual-diagnosis treatment. The seven treatment facilities operating by 2021 were primarily focused on substance abuse, with limited psychiatric support.[^60]

**Family impact.** With 41.4% of surveyed drug users having family members who also use drugs, the cycle of substance abuse and mental health distress operates at a family level, affecting children's development, family stability, and intergenerational wellbeing.

### Treatment Infrastructure

As of 2021, the Maldives had seven drug treatment and rehabilitation facilities, operated primarily under the National Drug Agency. The Drug Act (No. 17/2011) established the institutional framework, including the Drug Court, which serves as the primary referral pathway for compulsory treatment. However, stakeholders have consistently identified critical gaps:

- Absence of adolescent-specific treatment programmes
- Limited post-treatment community reintegration support
- Heavy reliance on residential rehabilitation with insufficient community-based alternatives
- Geographic concentration of treatment facilities, requiring travel away from home communities
- The Drug Court model, which compels treatment through the criminal justice system, raises questions about the therapeutic effectiveness of involuntary treatment and the criminalisation of what is fundamentally a health issue

## 9.8 COVID-19 and Mental Health Impacts

### Disruption to Services

The COVID-19 pandemic, which reached the Maldives in early 2020, had substantial effects on mental health both directly -- through increased psychological distress -- and indirectly -- through disruption of already-limited mental health services. The government response, including lockdowns in Male' and movement restrictions between atolls, suspended non-essential health programmes and severely constrained access to psychiatric care during a period of heightened need.[^61]

Key impacts on service delivery included:

- **Suspension of outpatient psychiatric services** during lockdown periods, leaving patients without medication reviews and follow-up care
- **Exacerbation of IGMH psychiatry waitlists**, which were already exceeding 3,300 patients pre-pandemic and grew further due to service backlogs
- **Disruption of inter-island referral pathways**, with movement restrictions preventing patients from traveling to Male' or regional hospitals for psychiatric appointments
- **Cancellation of school-based mental health programmes** during school closures
- **Reduced access to substance abuse treatment**, with residential facilities operating at reduced capacity or suspending admissions

### Increased Psychological Distress

The pandemic generated multiple stressors that increased mental health burden across the population:

**Economic stress.** The near-total collapse of the tourism sector -- which accounts for approximately 28% of GDP and a far larger share of direct and indirect employment -- caused widespread job losses, income reduction, and economic uncertainty. The economic shock disproportionately affected young workers, resort employees, and informal sector workers.

**Social isolation.** Lockdowns and movement restrictions intensified the social isolation that is already a feature of island life for many Maldivians. For individuals living alone or in crowded conditions (particularly in Male', one of the most densely populated cities globally), the psychological toll of confinement was severe.

**Domestic violence escalation.** As documented globally, lockdown conditions were associated with increased domestic violence. In the Maldivian context, where domestic violence prevalence was already high, confinement with abusers exacerbated both direct violence and the associated mental health consequences for victims and children.

**Health anxiety.** Fear of infection, grief over loss of loved ones, and uncertainty about the future contributed to elevated anxiety and stress, particularly among health workers and vulnerable populations.

**Children and adolescents.** School closures disrupted education, social connections, and the structure and routine that support adolescent mental health. The digital divide meant that children on remote islands without reliable internet access were particularly disadvantaged. The Regional Hospitals Decentralisation Report acknowledged that "the pandemic disrupted our development plans" and that the health system had to pivot rapidly to pandemic response at the expense of ongoing service development.[^62]

### Pandemic as Catalyst

Paradoxically, the pandemic also served as a catalyst for mental health reform. The dramatic increase in visible psychological distress -- and the inability of the existing system to respond -- created political momentum for the service expansions documented in Section 9.2, including the acceleration of regional mental health centre establishment and the scaling of telepsychiatry. The pandemic also normalised discussions about mental health and emotional wellbeing in public discourse, potentially reducing stigma.

## 9.9 Mental Health Workforce and Capacity

### Workforce Shortages

The mental health workforce shortage is a major constraint on mental health service delivery in the Maldives. The health workforce overall is heavily dependent on expatriate professionals -- over 70% of doctors in the Maldives are expatriates, and 27% of all health personnel were foreign nationals as of 2021.[^63] In mental health, this dependence is even more pronounced: nearly all psychiatrists practicing in the Maldives are expatriate professionals, primarily from India and Sri Lanka.

### Psychiatrists

Prior to the decentralisation reforms, specialist psychiatrists were concentrated almost exclusively at IGMH. The Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital at Thinadhoo, for example, "lacked any psychiatrists at the time of decentralization."[^64] As of 2023, all five regional hospitals have been staffed with specialist psychiatrists, collectively conducting approximately 150-200 consultations per week -- a significant improvement but still insufficient for the estimated need across a population of approximately 521,000.

The reliance on expatriate psychiatrists creates several vulnerabilities:

- **Cultural and linguistic barriers**: Expatriate psychiatrists may not speak Dhivehi, requiring consultations to be conducted in English or through interpretation. Psychiatric assessment is highly dependent on nuanced verbal communication, and language barriers can compromise diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic rapport.
- **High turnover**: Expatriate contract terms are typically short (1-3 years), creating constant turnover that undermines continuity of care and institutional memory.
- **Limited training contribution**: Short-term expatriate psychiatrists have limited opportunity or incentive to train local professionals, perpetuating the dependency cycle.

### Psychologists and Counsellors

The psychologist and counsellor workforce is even more limited than the psychiatrist workforce. There is no postgraduate clinical psychology training programme in the Maldives, meaning that all clinical psychologists must be trained abroad. The Maldives National University and private institutions offer some undergraduate and certificate-level programmes in counselling and psychology, but these do not produce graduates with the clinical competencies required for independent practice with complex mental health conditions.

### Community Mental Health Workers

The mhGAP training programme, supported by WHO, has begun building capacity among non-specialist health workers, but coverage remains limited. Mental health GAP trainings have been conducted for "health workers, nurses and doctors" in some atolls, but the programme has not been systematically rolled out across all Health Centres and Health Posts.[^65] The task-shifting model -- where trained community health workers can identify, provide basic management for, and refer common mental health conditions -- is essential given the impossibility of posting specialist mental health professionals on every inhabited island, yet the investment required to scale this approach has not been forthcoming.

### Training Pipeline

The pipeline for locally trained mental health professionals remains inadequate:

- **Psychiatry**: No psychiatry residency programme exists in the Maldives. Maldivian doctors wishing to specialise in psychiatry must train abroad, typically in India, Sri Lanka, or Malaysia, at significant personal cost and with no guarantee of return.
- **Clinical psychology**: No doctoral-level clinical psychology programme exists domestically.
- **Social work**: While the Maldives National University offers some social work training, the programmes do not provide specialised mental health social work competencies.
- **Psychiatric nursing**: No specialised psychiatric nursing programme exists.

## 9.10 Stigma, Cultural Barriers, and Help-Seeking

### The Nature of Stigma

Mental health stigma in the Maldives operates at multiple levels:

**Supernatural attribution.** A widespread cultural belief attributes mental illness to supernatural causes -- *jinni* (spirit) possession, *sihr* (sorcery or black magic), or *hassadh* (evil eye). These beliefs are not confined to uneducated or rural populations; they coexist with biomedical understandings even among educated urban Maldivians. The practical consequence is that families may seek treatment from traditional or religious healers (*fanditha* practitioners) before, instead of, or alongside biomedical mental health services. While some traditional practices are benign, others can be harmful, and the delay in accessing evidence-based treatment can worsen outcomes.

**Moral attribution.** Mental illness is sometimes understood as a consequence of personal weakness, lack of faith, or moral failing. This framing is particularly damaging for conditions such as depression and anxiety, which may be dismissed as insufficient gratitude to God or inadequate prayer. For substance abuse, moral attribution compounds the stigma of drug use itself, making it doubly difficult for individuals to seek help.

**Social stigma.** In the tight-knit communities of Maldivian islands, where privacy is limited and social reputation is paramount, a mental health diagnosis can bring shame not only to the individual but to the entire family. Concern about community gossip and social standing deters many families from seeking professional help, particularly for conditions perceived as shameful -- including substance abuse, attempted suicide, and psychotic disorders.

**Institutional stigma.** The health system itself has historically contributed to stigma by marginalising mental health services -- locating them in underresourced, poorly maintained facilities; failing to integrate mental health screening into routine primary care; and providing inadequate training to general health workers, leading to dismissive or discriminatory attitudes toward patients with mental health conditions.

### Help-Seeking Patterns

The combined effect of these stigma dimensions is to suppress help-seeking. The Drugs SitAn 2021 finding that 34.2% of drug users had never sought treatment, despite the severe impact of substance abuse on their lives, illustrates the depth of this barrier.[^66] Similarly, the GSHS 2009 data showing that nearly one in five students considered suicide, yet the health system records far fewer cases, suggests that most adolescents experiencing suicidal ideation do not disclose their distress to adults who could help.

The work of Public Health Unit workers in integrating frontline mental health screening into home visits -- "working on the frontline to destigmatize treatment for those that need it" -- represents a promising, culturally grounded approach to reducing stigma at the community level.[^67] By embedding mental health engagement within the familiar and trusted framework of community health outreach, this approach avoids the stigma associated with presenting at a mental health facility.

### Awareness Initiatives

Mental health awareness sessions are held for school students, hospital staff, and the general public throughout atolls. Focus groups are held in some locations to provide support and encouragement for those in need.[^68] However, these awareness initiatives remain ad hoc and small-scale. A systematic, national-level mental health literacy campaign -- drawing on the communication channels that proved effective during COVID-19 public health messaging -- has not yet been implemented.

The Viber community groups established by hospitals and Health Centres for health information dissemination offer a potential channel for mental health awareness content, but leveraging these platforms for mental health requires careful content development that addresses cultural sensitivities and avoids inadvertently reinforcing stigma.[^69]

## 9.11 Research Gaps and Recommendations

### Research Needs

The evidence base for mental health policy and programming in the Maldives is thin. Several research priorities are urgent:

1. **National mental health survey.** A dedicated, population-level survey of mental health prevalence across all age groups, geographic regions, and demographic categories is the most pressing research need. The current reliance on modules within broader health surveys, facility-based data, and decade-old school surveys is inadequate for planning purposes.

2. **Updated adolescent mental health data.** The GSHS 2009 data is now over fifteen years old. A repeat survey, or a dedicated adolescent mental health assessment, is essential to understand how the mental health landscape has evolved since the original survey, particularly in the context of social media penetration, COVID-19 impacts, and the expansion of services.

3. **Help-seeking behaviour research.** Understanding the pathways through which Maldivians recognise, interpret, and respond to mental health distress -- including the role of traditional and religious healers, family decision-making, and community attitudes -- is essential for designing culturally appropriate interventions.

4. **Treatment outcomes research.** As mental health services expand, there is a need for systematic data on treatment outcomes, including medication adherence, symptom improvement, functional recovery, and patient satisfaction. This data is essential for quality improvement and for demonstrating the value of investment in mental health services.

5. **Suicide epidemiology.** The absence of reliable data on completed suicides is a significant gap. Establishing a national suicide registry and conducting psychological autopsies would provide critical data for suicide prevention programming.

6. **Substance abuse and mental health comorbidity.** Research on the prevalence and patterns of co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders, and on the effectiveness of integrated treatment approaches in the Maldivian context, is needed to inform treatment programme design.

7. **Economic burden of mental illness.** Quantifying the economic costs of untreated mental illness -- including lost productivity, educational impairment, health care utilisation, and social welfare costs -- would strengthen the economic case for mental health investment.

### Policy Recommendations

Based on the analysis presented in this chapter, the following policy recommendations are advanced:

**Legislative reform:**
1. Develop and enact a comprehensive **Mental Health Act** that codifies patient rights, regulates involuntary treatment, establishes oversight mechanisms for mental health facilities, and provides anti-discrimination protections for persons with mental health conditions.
2. Review the Drug Act (No. 17/2011) with a view to **rebalancing the approach to substance abuse** from criminalisation toward a public health model, with greater investment in voluntary treatment, harm reduction, and community-based rehabilitation.

**Service expansion:**
3. **Complete the rollout of Mental Health Centres** to all five regional hospitals, ensuring that all regions have access to comprehensive outpatient psychiatric services without travel to Male'.
4. **Scale the telepsychiatry programme** beyond the current six atolls to achieve universal coverage, ensuring that every inhabited island has access to specialist psychiatric consultation within a reasonable timeframe.
5. Establish **dedicated child and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS)**, including specialised clinical pathways, age-appropriate therapeutic environments, and practitioners trained in developmental psychopathology.
6. Develop a **national crisis helpline** providing 24/7 telephone and text-based support for persons in suicidal distress, with protocols for warm transfer to emergency services when needed.

**Workforce development:**
7. Establish a **psychiatry residency programme** in the Maldives, potentially through partnership with an international university, to develop a locally trained psychiatrist workforce and reduce dependence on expatriate professionals.
8. **Scale the mhGAP training programme** to all Health Centres, ensuring that every inhabited island has at least one health worker trained in basic mental health recognition and management.
9. Develop a **certified counsellor training and accreditation framework** to ensure quality standards and expand the counselling workforce.

**Prevention and promotion:**
10. Develop and implement a **national suicide prevention strategy** with specific targets, protocols, gatekeeper training, and means restriction measures.
11. **Integrate mental health education into the national curriculum** at primary and secondary levels, building mental health literacy from an early age.
12. Launch a **national mental health awareness campaign** designed with cultural sensitivity, addressing supernatural and moral attributions of mental illness while respecting religious values.

**Data and accountability:**
13. Establish a **national mental health information system** that collects standardised diagnostic and service utilisation data across all health facilities, enabling surveillance, planning, and accountability.
14. Conduct a **national mental health survey** as a baseline for the Mental Health Strategic Action Plan 2025-2029 period.
15. Publish **annual reports on mental health service delivery** including waitlist data, service utilisation, workforce metrics, and outcome indicators.

---

## Conclusion

The period since 2019 has seen measurable progress after decades of limited attention to mental health: the establishment of the National Centre for Mental Health, the decentralisation of psychiatric services to all five regional hospitals, the introduction of telepsychiatry for remote islands, the opening of Mental Health Inpatient Wards, and a 90% reduction in IGMH psychiatry waitlists.

Substantial unmet need remains. Nearly one in five young Maldivians experiences depression. One in five adolescent students has seriously considered suicide. Over half of drug users began substance use before age 18, and a third have never accessed treatment. The mental health workforce remains very limited, with no locally trained psychiatrists and no domestic training pipeline to produce them. Stigma, though beginning to erode, continues to suppress help-seeking across all population groups.

Realising the constitutional promise of Article 23(c) -- "good standards of health care, physical and mental" -- will require sustained investment, legislative reform, workforce development, and greater prioritisation of mental health within the broader social policy architecture.
