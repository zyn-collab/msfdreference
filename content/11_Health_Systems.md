# Part XI: Health Systems and Social Determinants of Health

The Republic of Maldives has achieved health outcomes that are, by virtually every conventional metric, remarkable for a small island developing state (SIDS) with a population dispersed across 187 inhabited islands in 26 natural atolls. Life expectancy at birth rose from approximately 47 years in 1977 to 78.9 years by 2020; infant mortality fell from over 100 per 1,000 live births in the 1970s to approximately 6.2 per 1,000 by 2020; and the country was certified malaria-free in 1984, the first in the South-East Asia Region.[^1] These gains were secured through a combination of sustained public investment, progressive universalisation of health insurance, an expanding network of primary care facilities on inhabited islands, and concerted vaccination and communicable-disease-control campaigns.

Yet the picture is more complicated than the headline indicators suggest. Behind the national averages lie stark geographic disparities in access to specialist care, a health workforce in perpetual crisis, a non-communicable disease (NCD) epidemic that now accounts for 78 per cent of the disease burden, rising fiscal pressures on the universal insurance scheme *Aasandha*, persistent corruption vulnerabilities across procurement and governance, and a nutritional double burden in which childhood stunting coexists with rapidly rising adult obesity. The COVID-19 pandemic further exposed the fragility of a system overwhelmingly concentrated in Male and dependent on expatriate health professionals.

This chapter provides an encyclopedic examination of the Maldivian health system: its strategic planning architecture, service delivery tiers, financing mechanisms, disease burdens, workforce challenges, and the social determinants that shape population health outcomes. It draws on the Health Master Plan 2016--2025, the Maldives Health Statistics 2020 compendium, the Transparency Maldives *Corruption Vulnerabilities in the Maldivian Health Sector* report (2022), the Regional Hospitals Decentralisation and Autonomisation Report (2023), WHO Country Cooperation Strategies, the Demographic and Health Surveys of 2009 and 2016--17, the WHO STEPS Survey 2020--21, and the Global Youth Tobacco Survey 2019, among other primary sources.

## 11.1 Health Master Plan 2016--2025

### Historical Lineage of Health Planning

Development planning in the Maldivian health sector dates to the late 1980s. The first health sector plan was a three-year medium-term plan developed in 1980, following the principles of primary health care adopted at the Alma-Ata Declaration of 1978. The Health Master Plan (HMP) 1996--2005 was the first long-term plan, developed in 1995. The second HMP, covering 2006--2015, was "implemented satisfactorily, particularly in the first half of the plan period," but the latter half was "challenging with a shift in policy direction to short term planning" driven by the volatile political transitions that followed the 2008 constitutional reform and the change of government in 2012.[^2]

The HMP 2016--2025, published by the Ministry of Health in December 2014, is thus the third long-term strategic health plan. It was developed through a five-stage process initiated in June 2014:

1. **Situational analysis**: evaluation of the previous HMP, desk reviews, interviews with key informants and service providers, individual meetings with stakeholders, and consultation workshops.
2. **Identification of priority focus areas and national goals**: analysis of outcomes from the situation analysis, inputs from a social media forum, and further consultations with stakeholders including public and political parties.
3. **Consensus building**: discussion and feedback from technical health professionals, workshops with service providers, public health agencies, collaborating sectors, and separate meetings with senior management, policy makers, and political parties.
4. **Development of the monitoring and evaluation framework**: identification of appropriate indicators to measure goal, outcomes, and outputs, obtaining baseline data, and discussions with partners to set targets.
5. **Endorsement**: by the Government of Maldives, followed by publication in the Government Gazette and on the Ministry of Health website.[^3]

### Vision, Goals, and Core Values

The HMP 2016--2025 articulates a vision of "a population that enjoys high levels of physical, mental and social wellbeing irrespective of their age, gender and socio-economic position and island environment."[^4] Its core values are human rights, equity, inclusion, accountability, sustainability, and professionalism.

The overarching national health goal is to "Enhance health and wellbeing of the population," pursued through three specific outcomes:

1. Build trust in the national health system.
2. Reduce disease and disability among the population.
3. Reduce inequities in access to health care services and medicines.[^5]

Twelve national-level outputs operationalise these goals, ranging from adopting evidence-based public policy making (Output 1) to maintaining an adequate skill-mix of the health workforce (Output 9) to ensuring a responsive, integrated health information system (Output 10).[^6]

### Strategic Focus Areas

The critical areas for action are categorised under three focus domains:

**Governance** (Strategic Directions 1--4): Establishing an efficient health system governed by legislation and oversight mechanisms; ensuring transparent, evidence-based policy making; developing public-private partnerships; and ensuring the financial sustainability of both the health system and the Aasandha social health insurance scheme.

**Public Health Protection** (Strategic Directions 5--11): Providing a healthy start in life through effective reproductive, maternal and child health services; reducing chronic diseases (diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, stroke, cancers) and improving mental health; maintaining communicable disease control; enabling healthy behaviours among adolescents and young adults; improving quality of life of older people and people with disabilities; strengthening health promotion and health education; and providing a clean, safe, and supportive environment.

**Health Care Delivery** (Strategic Directions 12--17): Ensuring public delivery of primary health care on all inhabited islands; establishing a coordinated referral system from primary to secondary and tertiary care; enabling timely surveillance through an integrated health information system; ensuring uninterrupted supply of essential medicines, vaccines, and medical technologies; investing in training and retention of the health workforce; and establishing capacity for health and medical response in national disasters and emergencies.[^7]

### Frameworks for Action

The HMP 2016--2025 adopts two technical frameworks. The first is the **determinants of health** approach, which classifies factors affecting health at three levels: upstream factors (social cohesion, political harmony, trade policies, food availability, water and sanitation, housing, environmental protection), midstream factors (income and employment, access to preventive health care, availability of skilled workforce, school-based education and life skills), and downstream factors (social support networks, rehabilitation services, de-addiction programmes, behaviour change programmes). The HMP notes that previous policy action had focused predominantly on midstream and downstream factors, neglecting upstream determinants.[^8]

The second framework is the **WHO health system building blocks**: service delivery, health workforce, health information systems, medicines and technologies, health financing, and governance. The plan recognises that action on all six building blocks is required "to bring stability and re-build the health system, improve efficiency and quality of services as well as improving access and coverage of population with essential health services."[^9]

### The Planning Model and Political Resilience

A distinctive feature of the HMP 2016--2025 is its design as a strategic management framework rather than an operational plan. It focuses on general strategic directions, outcomes, and broad strategies, but does not prescribe detailed operations or resource requirements. This design was deliberate: as the plan was expected to traverse at least two election cycles, the model provides political contenders "the flexibility to integrate their manifestos' health priorities to contribute to the population health goals."[^10] Business plans for each financial year would translate the high-level strategic directions into specific action plans relevant to the government's manifesto and institutional priorities.

## 11.2 Healthcare Delivery Architecture -- Tertiary, Regional, Atoll, Island

### The Four-Tier System

The Maldivian public health care delivery system is organised into a four-tier structure:

**Tier 1 -- Island-Level Primary Health Care**: Health posts and health centres on inhabited islands, categorised into four levels (with health posts sometimes referred to as level-four health centres). These facilities provide basic primary care services including medical examinations, immunisations, antenatal care, basic drugs, and health education. As of 2014, there were 169 primary healthcare centres (30 health posts and 139 health centres). The distribution of these facilities is island-based rather than population-based -- meaning that even islands with very small populations have a facility, while more populous islands may be relatively under-resourced. For example, Haa Alif Atoll has 13 centres serving over 13,405 people, while in Vaavu, 3 health posts serve a population of fewer than 1,600.[^11]

**Tier 2 -- Atoll Hospitals**: Each atoll (excluding Kaafu Atoll, which is served by Male facilities) has an atoll hospital catering to the population of that atoll. In 2014, there were 14 atoll hospitals. These provide somewhat more advanced services than health centres, including basic inpatient care, minor surgeries, and laboratory services. The atoll hospital acts as the main coordinating body for primary and curative health care in the atoll, with each atoll covering a population of approximately 5,000 to 15,000 people.[^12]

**Tier 3 -- Regional Hospitals**: Five regional hospitals serve groups of 2--4 atolls each, providing specialty care including surgical services, obstetric care, and diagnostic capabilities. The five regional hospitals are:
- **Kulhudhuffushi Regional Hospital** (Haa Dhaalu Atoll) -- serving the northern region
- **Ungoofaaru Regional Hospital** (Raa Atoll) -- serving the north-central region
- **Gamu Regional Hospital** (Laamu Atoll) -- serving the central region
- **Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital** (Gaafu Dhaalu Atoll) -- serving the upper-southern region
- **Addu Equatorial Hospital** (Addu City) -- serving the southern region

By 2023, under the decentralisation programme, all five regional hospitals had been elevated to fully tertiary status with specialists in core departments, biomedical laboratories, mammograms, ECG, and CT scans as standard offerings. Several regional hospitals also offered advanced medical procedures such as chemotherapy, knee replacements, cataract surgery, and dialysis.[^13]

**Tier 4 -- Tertiary Level (Male)**: The Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) serves as the national referral hospital and the primary tertiary care facility. Located in Male, it provides the widest range of specialist services in the country. Hulhumale Hospital, under a corporate management board, also provides tertiary-level services. In addition, the ADK Hospital -- a private tertiary facility in Male with 50 beds -- provides a wide range of medical and surgical services, with outpatient visits approaching levels seen at IGMH.[^14]

### Challenges of the Island-Based Distribution Model

The geographic reality of the Maldives creates unique and persistent challenges for health service delivery. The 1,192 islands are spread across a chain stretching 820 km in length and 120 km in width, covering approximately 90,000 square kilometres of ocean with a total land area of only 298 square kilometres.[^15] This extreme geographic dispersion means:

- **Logistic complexity**: Providing necessary supplies and equipment, assuring quality services, and maintaining regular equipment maintenance are all complicated by the need for sea and air transport between islands.
- **Human resource maldistribution**: Despite relatively high ratios of skilled health personnel to population (the doctor-population ratio was 1:609 in 2012), the geographic situation leads to limited availability of skilled health workforce on smaller islands. Poor human resource management, limited career development, and limited professional development opportunities make retention of trained staff on outer islands extremely difficult.[^16]
- **No public pharmacies**: All pharmacies in the country are in the private sector, including those located in public sector facilities. There are 224 pharmacies across the country, with 80 concentrated in Male. Due to diseconomies of scale, the private sector is not attracted to provide pharmacy services on smaller islands, making access to medicine a major problem for residents of small islands.[^17]
- **Absence of emergency medical services**: Ambulance and paramedic services were not established as of the HMP situational analysis, contributing to preventable deaths. The Maldivian Red Crescent, established in 2009, has been developing community capacity for emergency preparedness and response.[^18]
- **Inefficiency through duplication**: Because the distribution of primary health care centres is island-based rather than population-based, there are inherent inefficiencies in terms of material, human, and financial resources -- with some very small populations supporting underutilised facilities while others are stretched thin.

### The Private and Voluntary Sectors

The private health sector, although small, is vigorous. As of 2014, there were 65 registered private health care facilities, of which 73 per cent were located in Male. The ADK Hospital dominates the private sector with 50 beds and a wide range of services. Some private providers offer traditional Maldivian medicine (*Dhivehi beys*) and alternative medicine services such as acupuncture, Ayurvedic medicine, and Chinese medicine, though regulatory capacity for these services is weak.[^19]

Over 700 NGOs are registered in the Maldives, though NGO capacity in health is limited. Those that have sustained health-related programmes include the Society of Health Education, Diabetes Society of Maldives, Care Society, Maldives Thalassaemia Association, AgedCare Maldives, and Journey.[^20]

Public-private partnerships (PPPs) were experimented with during 2009--2012 in the provision of health care. The outcomes were largely negative due to lack of exposure to the financier model, lack of knowledge about PPP implementation, and retrenchment worries among public sector workers.[^21]

## 11.3 Aasandha -- Universal Health Insurance

### Origins and Evolution

The social health insurance scheme in the Maldives was established in 2009 to protect the public from catastrophic health expenditure. Since its inception, the scheme has undergone several fundamental policy changes:

- Initially a contributory scheme with defined benefits.
- Changed to a non-contributory scheme with an annual individual financial limit of MVR 100,000.
- Since January 2014, the current scheme -- **Husunvaa Aasandha** -- operates without annual individual financial limits, effectively providing unlimited coverage.[^22]

The scheme is managed by a private company (Aasandha Company Ltd.), with the Ministry of Health as the main provider of health care (both curative and preventive) and the National Social Protection Agency (NSPA) as the regulating agency.

### Coverage and Benefits

Aasandha covers all Maldivian citizens. The scheme provides:
- Free public health care at all government facilities
- Free referrals to the nearest hospital, including sea transport in emergencies
- Coverage for treatment abroad for services not available in the country
- No annual individual financial ceiling under Husunvaa Aasandha

The private sector provides curative services to a limited population on a fee-for-service basis, though only a small number of private providers are registered in the scheme. Foreign nationals resident in the country are not covered.[^23]

### Fiscal Sustainability Challenges

The Aasandha scheme faces severe fiscal sustainability challenges that are well documented across multiple sources:

**Over-utilisation**: Beneficiaries tend to over-utilise services due to poor information and the perception of Aasandha being an "unlimited pre-paid scheme." The absence of gate-keepers in the system results in inefficient use of resources.[^24] There is no established primary healthcare referral system; hospitals are patients' first port of call for health services, which inflates costs and congests secondary and tertiary facilities.

**High out-of-pocket expenditure despite universal coverage**: Despite the social health insurance scheme, direct out-of-pocket expenditures remained high at 49 per cent in 2011--12, of which almost 53 per cent was spent at public providers and 47 per cent at private providers. This paradox reflects the gaps between the scheme's coverage and the actual costs borne by patients for medicines, travel, and services at private providers not in the network.[^25]

**Cost of overseas treatment**: Medical travel to India, Sri Lanka, and other countries for procedures unavailable domestically represents a significant cost driver. Providers in other countries account for 23.7 per cent of total health expenditure, mainly for overseas treatments paid for by the people themselves.[^26]

**Health financing as corruption vulnerability**: Transparency Maldives found that institutions responsible for health service financing are "inherently vulnerable to corruption" because there is no robust, automated system to prevent, detect, or sanction abuses. Health facilities engage in upcoding on claims, and professionals overmedicalise their patients. The non-contributory nature of the scheme means beneficiaries have no incentive to support cost-effectiveness, leading to irrational service use.[^27]

### Health Expenditure Profile

According to the HMP 2016--2025, total health expenditure in the Maldives in 2011 was 9 per cent of GDP, corresponding to US$561 per capita. The Government spent US$247 per capita. There are three main sources of health finance: the public (government, 44 per cent), the people (49 per cent), and external sources (donations and grants, less than 3.3 per cent).[^28]

Public funds for health are primarily spent on curative care (66.8 per cent), both inpatient and outpatient. Administration accounts for almost 11 per cent, preventive care for 5.5 per cent, and medicines for 17 per cent. Per capita spending was US$130 on inpatient curative services, a comparable amount on outpatient treatment abroad, and US$95 on medicines. Only US$11 per capita was spent on public health programmes -- a stark illustration of the system's curative bias.[^29]

Public sector providers are the major recipients of total health expenditure: IGMH (10.6 per cent), regional and atoll hospitals (30 per cent), health centres (7.5 per cent), and health posts (1 per cent). Private providers (physicians, clinics, dentists, and private pharmacies) account for 28 per cent.[^30]

## 11.4 Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child, and Adolescent Health (RMNCAH)

### Maternal Health

The Maldives achieved the Millennium Development Goal of reducing maternal mortality. The maternal mortality ratio (MMR) decreased from 69 per 100,000 live births to 49 per 100,000 during the period 2006 to 2012. However, due to the small population, the MMR shows significant fluctuations -- an increase in even a single death can cause a substantial rise in the ratio.[^31]

The regular review of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality for the period 2009--2011 identified causes of maternal deaths including eclampsia, complications of abortion, postpartum haemorrhage, puerperal sepsis, amniotic fluid embolism, and rupture of the uterus. Preventable causes of maternal deaths, such as rupture of the uterus and puerperal sepsis, continue to be found, pointing to quality-of-care concerns.[^32]

Key reproductive health indicators from the DHS 2009 and HMP situational analysis include:

- **Antenatal care (ANC) coverage**: 97 per cent in 2009, with 90 per cent having their first ANC visit in the first trimester.
- **Facility-based births**: 95 per cent of births (2011) occurred in a health facility -- 85 per cent in a public facility, 10 per cent in a private facility.
- **Skilled birth attendance**: 95 per cent, with 71 per cent assisted by a gynaecologist, 9 per cent by a doctor, and 14 per cent by a nurse or midwife.
- **Postnatal care**: 94 per cent coverage, with 67 per cent receiving a postnatal check-up within two days of delivery.
- **Caesarean section rate**: 32 per cent -- high by WHO standards, which may subject some women to unnecessary risks during childbirth and postpartum.[^33]

Unsafe abortion contributes to maternal morbidity and mortality. Abortion is permitted only within 120 days of conception and only on grounds of multiple congenital anomalies, anencephaly, thalassaemia and other haemoglobinopathies, and for victims of rape and incest.[^34]

About 12 per cent of Maldivian women have short stature below 145 cm, and 4.6 per cent have a BMI below 18.5, indicating undernutrition. Anaemia prevalence among women was 15.1 per cent in 2007, compounded by the high prevalence of thalassaemia and other haemoglobinopathies.[^35]

### Child Health

Child health-related MDGs were achieved in the Maldives. During 2006--2012:
- Infant mortality decreased from 16 to 9 per 100,000 live births.
- Under-five mortality decreased from 18 to 11 per 100,000 live births.
- Neonatal mortality decreased from 11.5 to 5.9 per 100,000 live births.[^36]

Despite these gains, concerns remain regarding slow reduction in stillbirth rates, low birth weight in babies, and increasing numbers of premature and large-for-gestational-age babies and congenital abnormalities. EPI vaccination coverage reached 93 per cent for all EPI vaccines in 2012.[^37]

Emerging areas of concern for children include increasing observance of Autism Spectrum Disorders (ASDs), Global Developmental Delay (GDD), and congenital heart diseases (CHDs), which require new investment in diagnostic and therapeutic services.[^38]

### Adolescent and Young People's Health

About 30 per cent of the Maldivian population is in the age group 15--25 years, making adolescent health a strategic priority. Key challenges include:

- **Tobacco use among adolescents**: The Global Youth Tobacco Survey (GYTS) 2019 found 45.7 per cent current tobacco use among 13--15 year olds, with smokeless tobacco use at 46.1 per cent -- among the highest rates globally.[^39]
- **Substance abuse**: The drug use survey 2011/12 showed opioid and cannabinoid use as most common among drug users in Male, with the estimated prevalence of drug use at 6.64 per cent for Male and 2.02 per cent for the atolls.[^40]
- **Reproductive health**: The contraceptive prevalence rate is low (27 per cent for modern methods) despite a low total fertility rate (2.5 as of 2009), suggesting high rates of pregnancy termination, infertility, or traditional method use. The unmet need for family planning was high at 29 per cent.[^41]
- **Sexual and reproductive health information**: School health programmes face challenges in providing need-based sexual and reproductive health information due to changing religious beliefs in society. An adolescent health clinic piloted at IGMH was under-utilised because the service environment was perceived as stigmatising to young people.[^42]

The Adolescent/Youth Friendly Health Services Standards (2014) established nine national standards for youth-friendly service delivery, aiming to create accessible, non-judgemental health services. However, implementation across the atolls remains uneven.[^43]

### RMNCAH Strategy 2020--2025

The RMNCAH Strategy 2020--2025 was the first combined national strategy addressing reproductive, maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health as a continuum of care. It identifies seven strategic areas and emphasises the need to address the full life-course approach, moving beyond the traditional siloed approach to maternal and child health programming. The strategy acknowledges the dual challenges of maintaining gains in maternal and child survival while responding to emerging issues including adolescent mental health, rising NCD risk factors among young people, and the nutritional double burden.[^44]

## 11.5 Non-Communicable Diseases -- Burden and Response

### The Epidemiological Transition

The Maldives has undergone a rapid epidemiological transition from a country dominated by communicable diseases to one where NCDs are the primary cause of morbidity and mortality. Cardiovascular diseases, chronic respiratory diseases, accidents and injuries, and cancers are the leading causes of death. In terms of disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), NCDs (inclusive of injuries) account for 78 per cent of the total disease burden, while only 22 per cent of DALYs come from communicable diseases, maternal and child health, and nutrition issues combined.[^45]

### NCD Risk Factors

The WHO STEPS Survey 2020--21 provided comprehensive data on NCD risk factors in the Maldivian adult population:

- **Tobacco use**: Current tobacco use was significant across the population. The survey documented both smoking and smokeless tobacco use patterns, building on earlier data that showed smoking prevalence among adults in Male at 18 per cent in 2011.[^46]
- **Physical inactivity**: 42 per cent of adults in Male had low levels of physical activity (defined as fewer than 600 MET-minutes per week) as of 2011. The STEPS survey provided updated national-level data confirming widespread physical inactivity as a major risk factor.[^47]
- **Unhealthy diet**: The survey documented dietary patterns including low fruit and vegetable intake, high processed food consumption, and excessive salt and sugar intake -- a pattern driven by the country's near-total dependence on food imports and the limited availability of fresh produce on outer islands.
- **Raised blood pressure**: Hypertension was 16 per cent in Male in 2011 and continues to be highly prevalent across the country.[^48]
- **Diabetes**: The national estimate of type 2 diabetes is 4--7 per cent, with the Diabetes Society of Maldives operating as a key civil society partner in awareness and management programmes.[^49]

### Mental and Neurological Disorders

The national estimate of mental and neurological disorders is as high as 16.5 per cent (WHO, 2011), representing a significant and historically neglected burden. Mental health was identified in the HMP 2016--2025 as an area that "has not been in the limelight till very recently, thus needing a high focus and investment."[^50] The situation is compounded by substance abuse: among drug users, about 15 per cent in Male and 9 per cent in the atolls had been diagnosed with a psychological disorder.[^51]

### Thalassaemia and Other Chronic Conditions

Other chronic diseases of major public health concern include thalassaemia and other haemoglobinopathies, chronic renal diseases, congenital heart diseases, and auto-immune diseases. Disabilities remain a challenge, with a prevalence of functional difficulties in vision as high as 18 per cent (MDHS 2009), followed by mobility restriction (7.4 per cent) and hearing defects (5.7 per cent).[^52]

### Response Architecture

Interventions to address the NCD burden have focused on:
- Improving disease management through standard treatment guidelines.
- Strengthening health services for early detection of NCDs.
- Legislation supporting public health protection (tobacco control law enacted 2010), though enforcement remains weak.
- NGO-led programmes in diabetes management, physical activity promotion, and nutrition education.
- A recognition that addressing the social determinants of chronic diseases is essential for sustainable reduction in disease burden.[^53]

## 11.6 Nutrition and Food Security

### The Double Burden

The Maldives faces a classic nutritional double burden: persistent undernutrition among children coexisting with rapidly rising overweight and obesity among adults and increasingly among children.

**Child undernutrition**: The DHS 2009 showed that 17.3 per cent of children under 5 years were underweight (weight-for-age). Child malnutrition has not shown significant decrease over the preceding decade despite growth monitoring efforts.[^54]

**Child overweight**: Simultaneously, 5.9 per cent of children under 5 years were overweight (weight-for-height above +2SD), signalling an emerging obesity problem among the very young. The World Obesity Report Card has documented childhood obesity prevalence and flagged the country for the double burden of underweight and overweight among children.[^55]

**Micronutrient deficiencies**: The micronutrient survey conducted in 2007 showed:
- Anaemia prevalence among children 6 months to 5 years: 26 per cent
- Iron deficiency among children: 57 per cent
- Vitamin A deficiency among children 6 months to 5 years: more than half (5.1 per cent severely, 50.1 per cent moderately deficient)
- Zinc deficiency: 16 per cent of children
- Iodine deficiency: 19 per cent of children
- Zinc and iodine deficiency among reproductive-aged women: 27 per cent for both minerals.[^56]

### Infant and Young Child Feeding

Only 48 per cent of infants are breastfed exclusively for 6 months, with 25 per cent of babies mixed fed with breast milk substitutes. At the time of weaning, 53 per cent of infants are given commercial baby food as their first food. Grains are the most frequently fed food to children, followed by milk and milk products, while beans, legumes, and nuts are usually not fed to infants. Fruit and vegetable intake is less than twice a day.[^57]

### Food Security Context

The main drivers of the poor nutrition situation include:
- **Import dependency**: The Maldives imports most of its staple foodstuffs, basic necessities, and items for the tourism industry. This external dependence, combined with geo-spatial vulnerabilities, makes sustainable food security a continuous challenge.
- **Availability and cost**: Nutritious food is not always available or affordable, especially on outer islands, leading to over-reliance on processed food, breast milk substitutes, and commercial food services.
- **Limited nutrition education**: There is limited functional knowledge of food and infant feeding among caregivers, compounded by poor nutrition education in health services and schools.
- **Absent coordination**: There are no mechanisms to coordinate between food security interventions, food safety interventions, and nutrition programme interventions. Agricultural and fisheries programmes are primarily implemented as economic activities, with food security as a secondary output.
- **No food quality control system**: There is no proper system in place for food quality control.[^58]

## 11.7 Tobacco Control

### Legislative Framework

The Tobacco Control Act (15/2010) was enacted as part of a broader effort to address the rising NCD burden. The law provides the legislative basis for regulating tobacco products, restricting advertising and promotion, creating smoke-free environments, and mandating health warnings on tobacco packaging.

However, the HMP 2016--2025 noted bluntly that "policy and intersectional support to implement the law and its regulation is weak, thus undermining public health efforts to reduce tobacco use."[^59] The disconnect between legislation and enforcement is a recurring theme across Maldivian social policy.

### Prevalence Data

Tobacco use remains high, particularly among young people:

- **GYTS 2019**: Among students aged 13--15, current tobacco use was 45.7 per cent, and smokeless tobacco use was 46.1 per cent. These figures are among the highest in the South-East Asia Region and represent a dramatic increase from the period 2001--2011, during which tobacco use among the 13--15 age group increased from 10.4 to 11.2 per cent -- suggesting an acceleration in the subsequent years driven partly by the emergence of new tobacco products.[^60]
- **Adult prevalence**: Smoking prevalence among adults in Male was 18 per cent in 2011. The WHO STEPS Survey 2020--21 provided updated data on both current and former tobacco use across the adult population, including patterns of smokeless tobacco, electronic cigarettes, and water-pipe use.
- **Alcohol**: Although alcohol is prohibited in the Maldives, 6.7 per cent of school children aged 13--15 reported consuming alcohol in a self-enumerated survey (Ministry of Education, 2009). Among prison inmates serving sentences for drug-related offences, 47.9 per cent reported having used alcohol.[^61]

### Implementation Gaps

The key gap is not in policy formulation but in enforcement. Availability and promotion of unhealthy food and drinks are on the rise, often conflicting with health messages. School canteen standards have been developed, and the national curriculum has been revised to include health and physical education, but school environments continue to be permeable to tobacco and unhealthy food influences. The school health programme aims to make the school environment health-promoting through tobacco-free standards, but achieving this in practice has proven difficult.[^62]

## 11.8 Physical Activity and Obesity

### Adult Obesity Trends

Adult obesity has risen sharply. The HMP 2016--2025 reported that 15 per cent of adults in Male had obesity (BMI above 30 kg/m2) as of 2011, and the WHO STEPS Survey 2020--21 is expected to confirm further increases. This rise is driven by a combination of physical inactivity, dietary transition towards processed and imported foods, urbanisation (with Male's extreme density limiting opportunities for active recreation), and cultural factors.

### Physical Activity Levels

The 2011 data showed 42 per cent of adults in Male had low levels of physical activity, defined as fewer than 600 MET-minutes per week. The geographic reality of the Maldives -- small islands with limited open spaces, extreme heat and humidity, and cultural norms that may discourage outdoor exercise, particularly for women -- compounds the challenge.[^63]

### Physical Activity Guidelines 2022

The Ministry of Health published Physical Activity Guidelines in 2022, providing age-specific recommendations aligned with WHO global recommendations. These guidelines cover:
- Children and adolescents (5--17 years): at least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily
- Adults (18--64 years): at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic physical activity per week
- Older adults (65 years and above): same as adults, with additional emphasis on balance and strength training to prevent falls

The guidelines represent an important policy instrument, but translation into behavioural change requires addressing structural barriers including the lack of recreational infrastructure on most islands, the absence of safe walking and cycling spaces in Male (one of the most densely populated cities in the world), and limited public awareness of the health risks of physical inactivity.[^64]

## 11.9 WHO Country Cooperation Strategies

### CCS 2007--2011

The first WHO Country Cooperation Strategy (CCS) for the Maldives, covering 2007--2011, was developed in the context of a country transitioning from a least-developed country to middle-income status, with the attendant implications for declining development assistance. The CCS identified strategic priorities including strengthening health systems governance, addressing the emerging NCD epidemic, sustaining communicable disease control achievements, and building human resources for health.[^65]

### CCS 2013--2017

The second CCS, covering 2013--2017, was developed against the backdrop of the 2008 constitutional reform, political instability, and the corporatisation of the public healthcare delivery system in 2010--2011 (which fragmented the previously unified system into six separate entities, causing significant operational disruption). Key strategic priorities included:

- Supporting the government in rebuilding a unified health system after the failed corporatisation experiment.
- Addressing the epidemiological transition with particular attention to NCDs and mental health.
- Strengthening health information systems for evidence-based decision-making.
- Supporting the implementation of the International Health Regulations (IHR).
- Building capacity for emergency preparedness and disaster risk reduction.
- Addressing health workforce challenges including brain drain and reliance on expatriate professionals.[^66]

### Ongoing WHO Technical Cooperation

WHO technical cooperation with the Maldives has been instrumental in several areas:
- Development and review of national health plans and strategies.
- Surveillance and response systems for communicable diseases.
- Technical support for vaccination programmes.
- NCD prevention and control, including the STEPS survey methodology.
- Capacity building for the health workforce.
- Emergency preparedness, including during the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami recovery.

## 11.10 Health Workforce -- Distribution and Challenges

### Workforce Composition

The Maldivian health workforce comprises medical professionals (doctors, specialists), nurses, midwives, allied health professionals (laboratory technicians, radiographers, pharmacists, physiotherapists), and non-medical staff. According to the Maldives Health Statistics 2020 compendium, the health workforce data are organised by category, facility type, and geographic distribution.[^67]

### The Brain Drain and Expatriate Dependency

The most fundamental challenge facing the Maldivian health workforce is the combination of domestic brain drain and heavy reliance on expatriate health workers. The Transparency Maldives report documented that:

- There are many vacant positions in the health workforce, with a huge reliance on foreign doctors.
- Public sector wages are "famously low" compared to the private sector, leading to moonlighting -- working in both the public and private sector simultaneously -- which can lead to absenteeism in public facilities.
- Many students seek education and careers outside the Maldives, where greater opportunities exist. As a result, there is "a huge demand for migrant workers; estimates suggest that there are currently over 230,000 migrant workers in the Maldives, or a third of the total population."[^68]
- Once foreign health workers arrive in the country, oversight is challenging. Many are said to switch to work for the private sector while keeping their publicly subsidised visas and accommodations.

### Recruitment as Corruption Vulnerability

Recruitment procedures for healthcare professionals are "very complex and opaque," creating corruption vulnerabilities including:
- Conflicts of interest in recruiting agencies.
- Bribery in the recruitment process.
- Lack of transparent criteria for appointments, particularly at senior levels.
- Non-meritocratic processes for appointing high-level decision makers, with political appointments undermining institutional stability.
- Absence of a robust conflicts-of-interest management regime.[^69]

### Geographic Maldistribution

Despite having aggregate ratios that appear reasonable (doctor-population ratio of 1:609 in 2012), the geographic distribution is severely skewed. Male and the Greater Male area concentrate the vast majority of specialist doctors, while outer island health centres and atoll hospitals struggle to attract and retain qualified staff. The limited career development opportunities, professional isolation, poor infrastructure, and lack of social amenities on outer islands make recruitment and retention extremely difficult.[^70]

### Workforce Development

Key health professional regulatory bodies include:
- **Maldives Medical and Dental Council (MMDC)**: Registration and regulation of doctors and dentists.
- **Maldives Nursing and Midwifery Council (MNMC)**: Regulation of nursing and midwifery professionals.
- **Maldives Allied Health Council (MAHC)**: Regulation of allied health professionals.
- **Health Protection Agency (HPA)**: Public health surveillance and regulation.

The HMP 2016--2025 identifies the need to "invest in training and retention of professional and ethical standards of the health workforce" as a critical strategic direction, and to "maintain an adequate skill-mix of the health workforce, committed to provide holistic, customer-centred, quality care."[^71]

## 11.11 Regional Hospital Decentralisation

### The Decentralisation and Autonomisation Policy

The Regional Hospitals Decentralisation and Autonomisation Report, published in November 2023 by the Special Programs Unit of the President's Office, documents one of the most ambitious health system reforms undertaken during the presidency of Ibrahim Mohamed Solih (2018--2023). The report was prepared with data from IGMH, Hulhumale Hospital, Addu Equatorial Hospital, Kulhudhuffushi Regional Hospital, Gamu Regional Hospital, Dr Abdul Samad Memorial Hospital, and Ungoofaaru Regional Hospital, with data up to August 2023.[^72]

### Rationale

The foreword by President Ibrahim Mohamed Solih states plainly: "For too long, a centralized development approach led to significant disparities in service access between Male and the rest of the Maldives." The pursuit of equitable national development and access to services for all was "the central principle guiding this Administration." The policy promised the development of five regional hospitals in urban population centres, ensuring comprehensive care for every Maldivian citizen.[^73]

### Key Developments

The decentralisation programme achieved the following by 2023:

**Service expansion**: All five regional hospitals now boast specialists in core departments, with additional support from specialists at IGMH and Hulhumale Hospital who conduct regular outreach visits. Biomedical laboratories, mammograms, ECG, and CT scans are standard offerings at all five regional hospitals. Dialysis, physiotherapy, long-term wound care, and non-invasive surgeries are also available. Several regional hospitals offer advanced procedures including chemotherapy, knee replacements, cataract surgery, and more.[^74]

**Mental health access**: Psychiatric consultation wait times in Male were reduced from 18 months to 2 months. All five regional hospitals can now provide psychiatric appointments within two weeks. Video consultations with psychiatrists are available on 56 islands within two weeks' time.[^75]

**Sample transfer mechanisms**: Diagnostic sample transfer mechanisms from islands to regional hospitals have been implemented, enabling residents of remote islands to access laboratory and diagnostic services without travelling.

**Child development centres**: Regional hospitals offer support for paediatric developmental care, including assessment and intervention services.

**Reproductive health services**: Regional hospitals operate reproductive health centres, addressing a gap previously filled only in Male.

**Telemedicine**: Video consultation services have been rolled out, connecting island health centres with regional hospital specialists.[^76]

### The Autonomisation Process

The autonomisation component involved transferring regional hospitals from direct Ministry of Health management to autonomous governing boards. The process included:

1. Reconstitution of the Male Hospitals Board of Trustees.
2. Autonomising and decentralising the five regional hospitals, each with its own governing board.
3. Defining responsibilities for the governing board, CEO, Director General (Medical Services), and Director General (Nursing Services).
4. Training for board members and senior management.
5. Development of five-year strategic action plans for each hospital.
6. Establishment of annual reporting and documentation requirements.
7. Capacity building for staff across departments.[^77]

### Improvements in Efficiency

The report documents operational improvements including:
- Streamlined procurement processes.
- Improved human resources procedures.
- Finance department reforms.
- Outpatient and patient relations improvements.
- New pay frameworks with better wages.
- Data analysis units offering youth training in high-tech industries.
- Substantial budget increases for hospitals, generating local economic multiplier effects.[^78]

### Remaining Challenges

Despite progress, the report identifies areas needing further development:
- Reducing red tape and bureaucratic delay.
- Improving stock forecasting and inventory management.
- Monitoring outward referrals by doctors (to prevent unnecessary overseas referrals).
- Reducing constant policy upheaval across government transitions.
- Ensuring easily accessible public documentation on policy, bids, and procurement.
- Clarifying governance roles, responsibilities, and standards.
- Improving incentives to attract and retain high-performing staff.[^79]

## 11.12 Corruption Vulnerabilities in the Health Sector

### The Transparency Maldives Assessment

The report *Corruption Vulnerabilities in the Maldivian Health Sector* (October 2022), authored by Dr Hady Fink and Sarah Steingruber and published by Transparency Maldives, is described as "the first to examine the unique corruption vulnerabilities within the Maldivian health sector." The study was based on a comprehensive review of publicly available information, legislative and regulatory documents, and interviews with 80 key informants in 41 interview sessions. The informants included diverse expert stakeholders with local knowledge and extensive relevant experience within health or other government institutions.[^80]

### Systemic Context

The report identifies a set of overarching challenges that create fertile ground for corruption across the Maldivian public sector generally, and the health sector specifically:

- **Small island developing state context**: Small population, limited human resources, import dependency, and geographic dispersion.
- **Availability of human resources**: Severe shortages of qualified Maldivian health professionals, creating dependence on expatriate workers and informal recruitment channels.
- **Public sector wages and turnover**: Low wages compared to the private sector drive moonlighting, unfilled positions, and prevent the establishment of sophisticated public administration.
- **Frequency of governmental reforms**: Constant restructuring through repeated political transitions makes it difficult to correctly apply legislation or governance policies.
- **Government transparency**: Very little transparency across government, state-owned enterprises, and public health facilities, making it difficult to understand how services are regulated, who is responsible, and what consequences exist for non-compliance.
- **Accountability for misconduct**: Lack of effective accountability undermines anti-corruption efforts and incentivises corruption. Few effective governance and anti-corruption entities exist, and civic participation is generally weak.
- **Civic participation and the media**: Little civic accountability for government failures, made worse by the poor situation of press freedom.
- **Informal networks and kinship relations**: Striking imbalances of political, economic, and social activity. Informal networks and kinship relationships "unofficially govern society and political life," creating fertile ground for conflicts of interest, nepotism, and corruption.[^81]

### Six Functional Areas of Vulnerability

The report identifies 35 corruption vulnerabilities across six functional areas:

**1. Health Sector Governance and Regulation**: Vulnerabilities related to the way the system is structured, including the roles and responsibilities of relevant institutions. There is a lack of transparency and accountability across all institutions, a failure to implement recommendations for systemic improvements from anti-corruption oversight bodies, the absence of a conflicts-of-interest management regime, and non-meritocratic appointments of high-level decision makers.[^82]

**2. Service Delivery**: High public perception of corruption in the health sector, though actual experiences of corruption such as bribery are reported to be rare. Vulnerabilities include patients using personal connections to jump waiting lines, lack of transparency in the oversight of patient referrals, and difficulty exercising oversight of the accreditation process for empaneled facilities abroad.[^83]

**3. Hospital Governance and Administration**: No established primary healthcare referral system means hospitals are patients' first port of call. Tertiary hospitals and some regional hospitals have been in a "state of constant administrative and managerial change for over a decade," with a lack of systematic and robust approaches to governance creating opportunities for corruption. The current model for administration and management "is contributing to poor value for money and low or at least inconsistent service quality."[^84]

**4. Health Workforce**: Brain drain, poor public sector wages, many vacant positions, huge reliance on foreign doctors, considerable moonlighting, complex and opaque recruitment procedures creating corruption vulnerabilities such as conflicts of interest and bribery.[^85]

**5. Health Financing**: Institutions responsible for health service financing are inherently vulnerable to corruption, with no robust automated system to prevent, detect, or sanction abuses. Health facilities engage in upcoding on claims, and the non-contributory insurance scheme provides no incentive for cost-effective service use.[^86]

**6. Health Procurement and the Medical Supply Chain**: Pharmaceuticals alone constitute a third of all government health spending. The vast majority of procurement of medical commodities is outsourced to the State Trading Organization (STO). The regulations and policies governing procurement are "very opaque and allow much room to circumvent accountability." Vulnerabilities include conflicts of interest or collusion in procurement tenders and contracts, absence of a robust management regime, and the "revolving door" of public sector employees switching to the STO or private supplier companies without a cooling-off period.[^87]

## 11.13 Thalassaemia -- National Prevention Programme

### Disease Burden

Thalassaemia is one of the most significant genetic diseases in the Maldives. The carrier rate for beta-thalassaemia trait is estimated at approximately 18 per cent of the population -- among the highest in the world. This means that nearly one in five Maldivians carries the genetic trait, and when two carriers have children together, there is a one-in-four chance of producing a child with thalassaemia major, which requires lifelong blood transfusions and iron chelation therapy.[^88]

The high prevalence of thalassaemia and other haemoglobinopathies underlies the situation of anaemia among pregnant women (15.1 per cent prevalence in 2007) and contributes to the overall anaemia burden among children (26 per cent among children 6 months to 5 years).[^89]

### Legal and Institutional Framework

The **Thalassaemia Control Act (4/2012)** provides the legal basis for the national prevention programme. The Maldives Thalassaemia Society, one of the country's most established health NGOs, has played a critical role in awareness, screening, patient support, and advocacy.

### Prevention Programme

The national thalassaemia prevention programme includes:
- **Premarital screening**: Mandatory haemoglobin electrophoresis testing before marriage registration, designed to identify carrier couples and provide genetic counselling.
- **Carrier counselling**: Couples where both partners are carriers are informed of the risks and provided counselling on reproductive options.
- **Prenatal diagnosis**: Available for at-risk pregnancies, allowing parents to make informed decisions. Abortion is legally permitted within 120 days when the foetus is diagnosed with thalassaemia major or related conditions.
- **Newborn screening**: Identification of affected newborns for early treatment initiation.
- **Treatment services**: Blood transfusion services and iron chelation therapy are available at IGMH and regional hospitals, though the geographic dispersion of the population means that patients on outer islands must frequently travel to access transfusion services.

### Ongoing Challenges

- The programme's effectiveness is undermined by incomplete compliance with premarital screening requirements.
- Treatment costs are substantial and place fiscal pressure on the health system.
- Access to regular transfusions and chelation therapy on outer islands remains a significant geographic barrier.
- Quality of care for thalassaemia patients varies across facilities.
- Bone marrow transplantation, the only curative option, is not available in the country and must be sought abroad at very high cost.[^90]

## 11.14 COVID-19 -- Response and Social Impacts

### Initial Response

The Maldives confirmed its first COVID-19 case on 7 March 2020, in a resort worker. The government responded swiftly with border closures, lockdowns, and public health measures. The Decentralisation Report notes that despite the "unprecedented challenges of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, our Administration responded swiftly, implementing effective lockdowns and public health measures. The Maldives emerged with commendable health outcomes, avoiding the tragic scenes witnessed globally."[^91]

The Health Protection Agency (HPA) coordinated the public health response, working with the National Emergency Operations Centre (NEOC), the WHO, and other international partners.

### Health System Mobilisation

The pandemic tested every aspect of the health system:
- **IGMH and Male facilities**: Designated COVID treatment facilities were established, with IGMH serving as the primary treatment centre.
- **Regional hospitals**: Were mobilised for COVID response, with some designated as regional COVID treatment centres.
- **Island health centres**: Provided frontline surveillance, contact tracing, and sample collection.
- **Quarantine and isolation**: Purpose-built and repurposed facilities were established across multiple islands, including resort islands converted to quarantine centres.

### Vaccination Campaign

The Maldives launched its COVID-19 vaccination campaign in early 2021. The Formative Assessment of COVID Vaccine Hesitancy (2023) documented drivers and barriers to vaccine uptake. While overall uptake was relatively high, vaccine hesitancy was identified as a concern in certain population segments, driven by:
- Misinformation and disinformation on social media.
- Religious concerns and conservative interpretations of Islamic teachings.
- Concerns about vaccine safety and side effects.
- Distrust of government institutions.
- Logistic challenges in reaching outer island populations.[^92]

### Social and Economic Impacts

The Rapid Livelihood Assessment conducted in 2020 documented devastating economic impacts:
- **44 per cent** of workers experienced redundancy or income loss.
- Tourism -- the country's primary industry accounting for 25 per cent of direct GDP and up to 90 per cent of government tax revenue -- collapsed as borders closed.
- Informal workers, women, and outer island residents were disproportionately affected.
- The poverty rate, which had been declining, reversed course.[^93]

The COVID-19 Income Support Environmental and Social Management Framework (ESMF) was developed to structure the government's pandemic response and social protection measures, including income support payments, utility subsidies, and small business loans.

### Impacts on Non-COVID Health Services

The pandemic disrupted routine health services:
- Childhood vaccination coverage regressed, with the Transparency Maldives report noting "regressions in the number of children vaccinated" during the pandemic period.
- Routine antenatal care and chronic disease management were disrupted.
- Mental health impacts were significant, compounding pre-existing challenges.
- The pandemic exposed the fragility of a health system heavily dependent on Male-based facilities and expatriate workers, some of whom left the country during lockdowns.

### Pandemic as Corruption Risk

The Transparency Maldives report emphasised that the pandemic created new corruption risks: "It is critical that further research into these vulnerabilities is carried out, particularly to better understand the impact that COVID-19 pandemic has had."[^94] Emergency procurement processes, reduced oversight during lockdowns, and the large-scale injection of public funds into the health system all created opportunities for misuse.

## 11.15 Research Evidence

### Health Research Infrastructure

The Maldives has a nascent but growing health research infrastructure. The Health Information Management and Research Division of the Ministry of Health is responsible for health statistics compilation, research coordination, and data dissemination. The Maldives Health Statistics 2020 publication, compiled by the Health Statistics and Publication Section, represents the official statistical compendium covering health system data, natality, morbidity, mortality, and health workforce statistics.[^95]

### Key Surveys and Data Sources

The following surveys constitute the primary evidence base for health policy in the Maldives:

- **Maldives Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) 2009**: The first nationally representative DHS, providing baseline data on fertility, family planning, maternal and child health, nutrition, mortality, and HIV/AIDS knowledge.
- **Maldives DHS 2016--17**: The second national DHS, providing updated data and trend analysis across the same domains.
- **WHO STEPS Survey 2020--21**: The first comprehensive national NCD risk factor survey, covering tobacco use, alcohol and drug use, diet, physical activity, NCD prevalence (hypertension, diabetes, obesity), and mental health indicators.
- **Global Youth Tobacco Survey (GYTS) 2019**: Survey of tobacco use among 13--15 year olds.
- **Biological and Behavioural Survey (BBS) 2009**: Focused on most-at-risk populations (IDUs, FSWs, MSM) for HIV/STI surveillance.
- **Drug Use Survey 2011/12**: Prevalence of drug use in Male and the atolls.
- **Micronutrient Survey 2007**: Nutritional status of children and reproductive-aged women.
- **National Health Accounts**: Health expenditure tracking.

### Research Bulletin

The Research Bulletin Volume 5 (2018) published by the Ministry of Health contains health research abstracts covering a range of topics including disease surveillance, health system performance, and clinical studies conducted in Maldivian health facilities. The publication represents an effort to build a domestic research culture, though the volume and scope of health research remains limited compared to needs.[^96]

### Evidence Gaps

Significant evidence gaps persist:
- **No regular national health survey**: The gap between DHS 2009 and DHS 2016--17 (eight years) is too long for a country undergoing rapid epidemiological and demographic transitions.
- **Limited health system research**: There is very little published research on health system performance, quality of care, patient satisfaction, or health workforce dynamics.
- **No cancer registry**: Despite cancers being among the leading causes of death, there is no functional national cancer registry.
- **Limited mental health data**: Despite the estimated 16.5 per cent prevalence of mental and neurological disorders, systematic data on mental health service utilisation, treatment gaps, and outcomes are largely absent.
- **No systematic NCD monitoring**: Prior to the STEPS Survey 2020--21, there was no systematic national-level monitoring of NCD risk factors.
- **Absence of health technology assessment**: There is no health technology assessment framework to guide decisions on the introduction of new technologies, medicines, or interventions.
- **Research culture**: The HMP 2016--2025 called for facilitating "a research culture and support regular research such as demographic health surveys, national health accounts and other research to address national health information needs," but progress has been slow.[^97]

### International Partnerships in Research

The WHO, UNICEF, UNFPA, and other development partners have been instrumental in supporting national health surveys and building research capacity. The WHO STEPS methodology, the DHS programme, and UNICEF's nutrition surveillance represent critical external technical partnerships that underpin the evidence base for health policy.[^98]

## Conclusion

The Maldivian health system stands at a critical juncture. The remarkable gains of the past five decades -- in life expectancy, child survival, communicable disease control, and universal health insurance coverage -- are now threatened by a convergence of challenges: the escalating NCD epidemic, fiscal pressures on Aasandha, persistent geographic inequities in access to specialist care, a health workforce in crisis, governance and corruption vulnerabilities that undermine system efficiency, and a nutritional double burden that affects the full life-course.

The Health Master Plan 2016--2025, the regional hospital decentralisation programme, the RMNCAH Strategy, and the Physical Activity Guidelines represent genuine policy responses, but their effectiveness is constrained by weak enforcement capacity, political instability, frequent institutional restructuring, and the fundamental geographic and economic constraints of a small island developing state.

The social determinants of health framework adopted in the HMP -- recognising upstream, midstream, and downstream factors -- provides the right analytical lens, but translating it into intersectoral action has proven difficult. Health policy in the Maldives remains overwhelmingly curative in orientation: 66.8 per cent of public health funds go to curative care compared to 5.5 per cent for prevention and only US$11 per capita on public health programmes. Until this balance shifts -- and until the upstream determinants of food security, urban planning, environmental health, education, and economic equity are systematically addressed -- the health system will continue to treat symptoms while the underlying causes of ill-health persist and grow.
