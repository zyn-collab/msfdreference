# PART I: INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Overview of the Reporting Process

The Republic of Maldives ratified the Convention on the Rights of the Child on 11 February 1991, becoming one of the earliest states to accede to the Convention in the year it entered into force. Since ratification, Maldives has submitted periodic reports to the Committee on the Rights of the Child, with this combined fifth and sixth periodic report covering the period 2015 to 2024.

This report was prepared under the coordination of the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD), which serves as the primary state authority for child welfare, family services and social protection. Preparation involved an inter-ministerial task force comprising representatives from the Ministry of Home Affairs and Technology, the Ministry of Education, the Ministry of Health, the Attorney General's Office, the Maldives Police Service (MPS), the Department of Juvenile Justice (DJJ), the Prosecutor General's Office, the Human Rights Commission of the Maldives (HRCM), and the Office of the Children's Ombudsperson.^[Ministry of Social and Family Development, CRC Reporting Task Force Terms of Reference, 2024]

Consultations with civil society organisations were conducted during 2024–2025, including structured roundtables with the Society for Health Education (SHE), Hope for Women, the Maldivian Democracy Network (MDN), Save the Children Maldives, UNICEF Maldives, and representatives from the legal profession. A dedicated child consultation was conducted in partnership with UNICEF, engaging children aged 12–17 from Malé, Addu City, and three outer atolls (Haa Alifu, Laamu and Thaa) through structured focus groups facilitated by trained social workers.^[UNICEF Maldives, Child Consultation for CRC Reporting, 2025 (forthcoming)]

The Government acknowledges the Committee's Concluding Observations on the fourth periodic report (CRC/C/MDV/CO/4, adopted 2016) and has structured this report to address the Committee's principal areas of concern, including minimum age of marriage, corporal punishment, juvenile justice reform, alternative care, and the implementation gap between legislation and practice in outer island communities.

## 1.2 Key Developments Since the Last Report

The period 2015–2024 represents the most transformative decade in Maldives' child rights legislative and institutional history. The following developments are of particular significance:

**Legislative Reforms (2014–2024):**
- Enactment of the Child Rights Protection Act (Law No. 18/2019), which for the first time codified comprehensive child rights protections into domestic law, defining the child, establishing the principle of the best interests of the child, mandating child protection procedures, and establishing the legal basis for the Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS).^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]
- Enactment of the Juvenile Justice Act (Law No. 19/2019), which replaced the fragmented prior framework with a comprehensive juvenile justice system, establishing diversion mechanisms, the Department of Juvenile Justice, minimum age of criminal responsibility, and child-appropriate sentencing.^[Juvenile Justice Act, Law No. 19/2019, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]
- Amendment of the Sexual Offences Act (Law No. 17/2014) in 2021 to remove the marital exemption for rape, recognising marital rape as a criminal offence, and strengthening protections against child sexual abuse and exploitation.^[Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act, 2021, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]
- Enactment of the Education Act (2021), which strengthened the legislative basis for compulsory education, child-friendly school environments and protections against school-based violence.^[Education Act, 2021, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]

**Institutional Developments:**
- Establishment of the Department of Juvenile Justice (DJJ) under the Ministry of Home Affairs, operationalised in 2020, with responsibility for rehabilitation facilities and juvenile justice coordination.
- Expansion of Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs) to all 20 administrative atolls and key urban centres, improving geographic reach of child protection services.
- Establishment of the Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS) as the operational arm of MoSFD for case management, investigation and intervention in child protection matters.
- Launch of Childline 1412, the national child helpline, providing 24-hour access to emergency child protection referrals.
- Establishment of the Juvenile Court as a dedicated specialist court in Malé for children in conflict with the law.

**Key Remaining Challenges:**
Notwithstanding these advances, significant implementation gaps persist. Outer island communities — comprising over 65% of the country's inhabited islands — remain underserved by child protection infrastructure. The ratio of social workers to child population remains critically low outside Malé. Child marriage continues to occur, particularly in outer atolls. Conviction rates for child sexual abuse remain very low despite high reporting. Corporal punishment in school settings persists informally despite legislative prohibition. The foster care system remains underdeveloped, with most children unable to remain in their family environment placed in institutional care or informal kinship arrangements.

# CLUSTER 1: GENERAL MEASURES OF IMPLEMENTATION
*(Articles 4, 42 and 44(6) CRC)*

## 1.1 Reservations and Declarations

At ratification in 1991, Maldives entered three reservations to the Convention:

**Reservation to Article 14 (Freedom of Thought, Conscience and Religion):** Maldives declared that it would apply Article 14 only insofar as it is consistent with the principles of Islam, which is the state religion under Article 10 of the Constitution. This reservation reflects Maldives' constitutional structure, under which Islam is foundational to national identity, citizenship and governance. The Government notes that this reservation has been the subject of concern by the Committee in previous Concluding Observations. The Government reiterates that its reservation reflects the constitutional reality of the Maldivian state and that children are provided broad protections for their spiritual development within an Islamic framework. The Government does not consider this reservation incompatible with the object and purpose of the Convention.^[Declaration of Reservation, Republic of Maldives, 1991, UN Treaty Collection]

**Reservation to Article 21 (Adoption):** Maldives declared that it does not apply Article 21 insofar as it is inconsistent with Islamic law (Sharia), under which formal adoption as known in Western legal traditions is not recognized. Maldives uses the Islamic concept of *kafala* (guardianship without full legal severance of biological family ties) as the primary mechanism for alternative family care for children without parental care. The legal framework for *kafala* arrangements is described in Cluster 6 of this report.^[Declaration of Reservation, Republic of Maldives, 1991, UN Treaty Collection]

**Partial Reservation to Article 9:** Maldives declared that Article 9 (separation from parents) applies only to the extent consistent with Islamic law and national family law. In practice, this means that custody and separation decisions are made by the Family Court in accordance with Islamic jurisprudence on parental rights and child welfare.^[Declaration of Reservation, Republic of Maldives, 1991, UN Treaty Collection]

The Government has considered the Committee's previous recommendations to withdraw these reservations. The Government acknowledges that these recommendations reflect the Committee's commitment to the universality of child rights. The Government reiterates its position that the reservations are consistent with Article 51 of the Convention and does not currently plan to withdraw them, though it commits to ongoing review.

## 1.2 Comprehensive Legal Reform

### Pre-2015 Framework

Prior to 2015, Maldives lacked a comprehensive child rights statute. Child protection was governed by a patchwork of provisions including the Family Act (Law No. 4/2000), the Protection of the Rights of Children Act (Law No. 9/91), the Constitution (2008), and various regulations. The 2012 Domestic Violence Prevention Act (Law No. 3/2012) represented the first significant step toward a comprehensive protection framework, but it applied primarily to domestic violence and did not create a dedicated child protection system.^[Ministry of Gender and Family, Annual Report 2013]

The Sexual Offences Act (Law No. 17/2014) strengthened criminal law protections against child sexual abuse and exploitation, introducing explicit offences for child pornography, child prostitution, and aggravated sexual abuse of children. However, implementation was hampered by evidentiary requirements, judicial interpretation, and limited specialised investigative capacity.^[Vulnerability Mapping: Legal and Justice System Analysis, 2020]

### 2019: Landmark Legislative Reform

The year 2019 marked the most significant transformation in Maldives' child rights legal architecture with the passage of two landmark statutes:

**Child Rights Protection Act (Law No. 18/2019)** entered into force in 2020 and established, for the first time, a comprehensive statutory framework for child rights and protection in Maldives. Key provisions include:
- Formal definition of the child as any person below 18 years (Article 4)
- Codification of the best interests of the child as a primary consideration in all decisions affecting children (Article 6)
- Establishment of a mandatory reporting obligation for professionals working with children (Article 47)
- Creation of the legal basis for the Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS) and Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs)
- Prohibition of all forms of violence against children, including corporal punishment in all settings (Article 38)
- Establishment of child protection case management procedures, including intake, assessment, investigation, case planning, and review
- Provisions for emergency protective orders and placement of children in safe environments
- Establishment of the National Child Protection Database^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives, 2019]

**Juvenile Justice Act (Law No. 19/2019)** established a comprehensive juvenile justice system, replacing fragmented provisions in the Penal Code and Corrections Act. Key provisions include:
- Minimum age of criminal responsibility set at 10 years, with full criminal responsibility from 15 years (Articles 5–7)
- Establishment of a diversion programme for first-time and minor offenders
- Prohibition of imprisonment for children under 15 except in exceptional circumstances
- Child-appropriate sentencing options including community service, probation, counselling and rehabilitation
- Establishment of the Department of Juvenile Justice (DJJ) as the responsible authority for juvenile rehabilitation facilities
- Provisions for legal aid and child-appropriate court procedures
- Prohibition of life imprisonment and the death penalty for offences committed by children^[Juvenile Justice Act, Law No. 19/2019, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives, 2019]

### Subsequent Legislative Developments (2020–2024)

The **Education Act (2021)** strengthened the legislative framework for compulsory education, established minimum standards for school environments, and included specific provisions addressing school-based violence and child-friendly learning environments.^[Education Act, 2021, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]

The **Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act (2021)** removed the marital exemption from the definition of rape, strengthened evidentiary procedures for child sexual abuse cases, and introduced provisions for victim protection during criminal proceedings.^[Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act, 2021, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]

The **Anti-Human Trafficking Act (Law No. 12/2013)** was supplemented by updated regulations in 2022 strengthening victim identification protocols and inter-agency coordination for child trafficking cases.^[Anti-Human Trafficking Act, Law No. 12/2013; Amendments and Regulations, 2022]

## 1.3 Coordination Mechanisms

### National Level Coordination

The **National Child Rights Protection Council** (established under the Child Rights Protection Act 2019) serves as the highest-level coordination body for child rights policy. It is chaired by the Minister of Social and Family Development and includes ministers and senior officials from the Ministry of Health, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Home Affairs, the Attorney General's Office, and representation from civil society. The Council is mandated to meet quarterly and to approve national child protection policies and action plans.^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, Articles 14–18]

The **Inter-Ministerial Technical Committee on Child Protection** provides operational coordination between line agencies on case management, data sharing, legislative implementation and service delivery. It meets monthly and includes technical-level representatives from all relevant agencies.

The **Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS)**, operating under MoSFD, serves as the central operational authority for child protection case management, coordination with police and prosecutors, and oversight of the FCSC network.

### Atoll and Island Level Coordination

**Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs)** operate in all 20 administrative atolls and in Malé City. Each FCSC serves as the primary child protection interface at the atoll level, receiving referrals, conducting assessments, managing cases, and coordinating with local authorities, schools, health facilities and police. As of December 2024, 24 FCSCs are operational, including dedicated centres in Addu City and Fuvahmulah City.^[MoSFD, Annual Report 2024]

A key structural challenge is that many inhabited islands — of which there are 187 — are served only at the atoll capital level. Islands without FCSCs or resident social workers must rely on hotline referrals, remote case management, and periodic visits from atoll FCSC staff. This geographic constraint represents the most significant operational gap in the child protection system.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, Child Protection Systems in Maldives: A Systems Analysis, 2025]

### Role of the Human Rights Commission of Maldives (HRCM)

The Human Rights Commission of Maldives, established under Law No. 6/2006, serves as the national human rights institution and includes a dedicated unit for children's rights monitoring. The HRCM conducts independent monitoring of child rights compliance, receives and investigates complaints, and publishes periodic reports on children's rights situations. The HRCM has played an important advocacy role in relation to child marriage, juvenile justice conditions, and disability rights.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Annual Reports 2015–2024]

### Office of the Children's Ombudsperson

The Children's Ombudsperson (established under the Child Rights Protection Act 2019) is an independent office mandated to receive complaints from or on behalf of children, investigate violations of child rights, and make recommendations to the government. The office became operational in 2021 and has since handled hundreds of complaints related to child abuse, school-based violence, and juvenile justice conditions.^[Office of the Children's Ombudsperson, Annual Report 2022]

## 1.4 National Plans of Action

The **National Strategic Action Plan for Child Protection (2020–2024)** (developed under MoSFD) established five strategic priorities: (1) strengthening the legal and policy framework; (2) building institutional capacity; (3) improving service delivery and geographic reach; (4) strengthening data systems; and (5) promoting awareness and social norms change. The Plan was reviewed at mid-term in 2022, with progress assessed against indicators.^[MoSFD, National Strategic Action Plan for Child Protection 2020–2024]

A successor **National Child Protection Strategy (2025–2030)** is currently in development and is expected to be finalised in 2026, with stronger emphasis on inter-island equity, digital safety, and mental health services for children.

## 1.5 Budget Allocations

The Government acknowledges the Committee's consistent concern regarding the adequacy of budget allocations for child welfare services. Total expenditure on child protection and social welfare services has increased significantly over the reporting period, though the Government recognises that increases have not kept pace with the expanding mandate of the child protection system following the 2019 legislative reforms.

MoSFD's overall budget increased from MVR 180 million in 2015 to MVR 420 million in 2024, a 133% nominal increase. However, the proportion allocated specifically to child protection services — as distinct from broader social protection and family welfare — remains difficult to disaggregate precisely due to integrated budget lines. The Government is working to develop child-sensitive budgeting frameworks that will allow more precise tracking of child-specific expenditure.^[Ministry of Finance, Budget Estimates 2015–2024]

Key budget allocations relevant to children include:
- Aasandha universal health insurance (covering all children): MVR 1.2 billion annually
- FCSC operational budget: MVR 38 million (2024)
- DJJ (juvenile rehabilitation): MVR 22 million (2024)
- School building and infrastructure: MVR 140 million (2024)
- NDA drug prevention/youth outreach: MVR 15 million (2024)

## 1.6 Data Collection

The **National Child Protection Database**, mandated under the Child Rights Protection Act 2019, is in active development as of 2024. A pilot digital case management system was deployed in selected FCSCs in 2023, with planned national rollout by 2026. Currently, case data is collected through a combination of paper-based and digital systems, with CFPS maintaining centralized records from FCSC reports.

Key data sources for child rights monitoring include:
- Maldives Bureau of Statistics Population Census (most recent: 2022)
- MoSFD/CFPS annual case statistics
- Maldives Police Service crime statistics (disaggregated by age of victim and offender)
- Ministry of Education school enrollment and attendance data
- Ministry of Health vital statistics and health surveys
- National Drug Agency (NDA) surveys
- HRCM periodic reports
- National Bureau of Classification (NCB) yearbook (Table 8.60: Child abuse cases 2009–2020)

Significant data gaps remain, particularly regarding: child labor in the informal economy; children in vulnerable migration situations; violence in the home (underreporting); child marriage rates by atoll; and outcomes for children who have left the formal child protection system.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025; MoSFD, Data Needs Assessment, 2023]

# CLUSTER 2: DEFINITION OF THE CHILD
*(Article 1 CRC)*

## 2.1 Age of Majority and Definition of the Child

The Child Rights Protection Act (Law No. 18/2019) formally defines a child as any person who has not attained the age of 18 years (Article 4). This definition is consistent with Article 1 of the CRC and supersedes older provisions in earlier legislation that used varying age thresholds.^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, Article 4]

The Constitution of Maldives (2008) sets the age of majority for voting and civil rights at 18 years. The Employment Act sets the minimum working age at 16, with protections for young persons between 16 and 18. The inconsistency between the employment minimum age (16) and the CRC definition of a child (18) has been noted by the Committee in previous Concluding Observations, and is addressed in Cluster 9 of this report.

## 2.2 Minimum Age of Marriage

This has been, and remains, an area of significant concern. Prior to the Family Law Regulation of 2016, there was no statutory minimum age of marriage. The Family Act (Law No. 4/2000) permitted marriage from puberty with the consent of a guardian (*wali*) and registration with the Family Court, effectively allowing marriage of girls as young as 12 or 13 in practice.

The **Family Law Regulation (2016)** established 18 years as the minimum age of marriage. However, the regulation retained a judicial exception allowing marriage below 18 with court approval in circumstances deemed appropriate. This exception has been widely criticised by the HRCM, civil society and international bodies as undermining the minimum age standard in practice.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Report on Child Marriage in Maldives, 2019]

The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) reinforces 18 as the minimum age of marriage but does not explicitly eliminate the judicial exception that exists in the Family Law framework. The Government acknowledges the Committee's concern and is reviewing whether legislative action is required to close this gap fully. Data from civil registration records indicates that some marriages of persons under 18 continue to be registered each year, though numbers have declined since 2016.

The Government is committed to the complete elimination of child marriage and is working through public awareness campaigns, community engagement with religious leaders, and inter-ministerial coordination to address the social norms that perpetuate this practice in some outer island communities.^[MoSFD, Child Marriage Prevention Action Plan, 2022]

## 2.3 Age of Criminal Responsibility

The Juvenile Justice Act (Law No. 19/2019) sets the minimum age of criminal responsibility (MACR) at **10 years**. Children below 10 years cannot be held criminally responsible for any act. Children between 10 and 15 may be found responsible but must be dealt with through the juvenile justice system with a strong presumption toward diversion, rehabilitation and non-custodial measures. Full criminal responsibility attaches at age 15, though children aged 15–18 continue to be subject to the juvenile justice framework rather than the adult criminal system.^[Juvenile Justice Act, Law No. 19/2019, Articles 5–7]

The Government notes that the MACR of 10 is below the threshold of at least 14 years recommended by the Committee in its General Comment No. 24 (2019) on children's rights in the child justice system. The Government is reviewing the MACR in the context of a broader juvenile justice reform process and will engage with the Committee on this issue.

# CLUSTER 3: GENERAL PRINCIPLES
*(Articles 2, 3, 6 and 12 CRC)*

## 3.1 Non-Discrimination (Article 2)

The Constitution of Maldives (2008) guarantees equality before the law and prohibits discrimination on the grounds of race, national origin, sex, age, mental or physical disability, political or other opinion, property, birth or other status. Children of all backgrounds are entitled to equal treatment under the law.^[Constitution of the Republic of Maldives 2008, Article 17]

**Children with disabilities** continue to face discrimination in access to education, health services and social participation. Inclusive education policies have been adopted but implementation lags significantly, as detailed in Cluster 7.

**Children in outer islands** face structural inequalities in access to services by virtue of geography, which the Committee has previously characterised as a form of de facto discrimination. The Government recognises this as a systemic equity concern and continues to expand service infrastructure in outer atolls, as described throughout this report.

**Children of migrant workers** represent a growing group facing particular vulnerability. The Maldives hosts approximately 100,000–150,000 documented and undocumented migrant workers, primarily from Bangladesh, India and Nepal. Children accompanying migrant workers, or children born to undocumented migrants in Maldives, face significant barriers to birth registration, education and healthcare access. The Government acknowledges this gap and is reviewing policy options to ensure protection of all children on Maldivian territory regardless of the immigration status of their parents.^[Vulnerability Mapping: Migrant Children and Statelessness, 2020]

**Children born outside marriage** historically faced stigma and legal disadvantages in relation to inheritance and family recognition under Islamic family law. The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) reinforces the principle that all children are entitled to protection regardless of birth status, and CFPS case management protocols do not differentiate between children on this basis.

## 3.2 Best Interests of the Child (Article 3)

The principle of the best interests of the child (*maslahat al-tifl*) is now codified as a primary consideration in all decisions affecting children under Article 6 of the Child Rights Protection Act (2019). This principle must be applied by all government bodies, courts, institutions and private entities when taking decisions that affect children.

In judicial proceedings, the Family Court and Juvenile Court are required to conduct a best interests assessment in all cases involving children. However, the operationalisation of the best interests principle in practice — particularly in outer island settings where decisions may be made by local officials without formal training in child rights — remains a challenge. The Government has developed best interests assessment tools and training materials for FCSCs and is working to build capacity of court officials and local government officers.^[MoSFD, Best Interests Assessment Framework, 2022]

In child protection cases, CFPS case management protocols require a formal best interests determination before any decision to separate a child from their family or place a child in alternative care. However, monitoring of compliance with this requirement outside Malé remains limited by supervision and data collection constraints.

## 3.3 Right to Life, Survival and Development (Article 6)

The Constitution guarantees the right to life and to physical integrity. Child mortality indicators have improved significantly over the reporting period. The infant mortality rate declined from approximately 12 per 1,000 live births in 2010 to 7.1 per 1,000 in 2022, reflecting improvements in neonatal care, the universal health insurance system (Aasandha), and geographic expansion of health services through the health network.^[Ministry of Health, Health Management Information System, 2022]

**Child deaths from violence** — including deaths resulting from child abuse or neglect — are not systematically tracked in a disaggregated manner, representing a data gap. The Government is working to strengthen death review mechanisms, including the establishment of a Child Death Review Committee within MoSFD, to identify preventable child deaths and systemic risk factors.

**Drug-related mortality among youth** is an emerging concern. The National Drug Agency (NDA) 2021 survey estimated over 7,000 active drug users in Maldives, with youth aged 15–24 representing a significant and growing proportion. The relationship between drug use and child development outcomes is a priority area for government action.^[National Drug Agency, National Drug Use Survey, 2021]

**Suicide and self-harm** among adolescents is another concern that has gained public attention during the reporting period. Mental health data collection is limited, but clinical reports and FCSC case data indicate that self-harm and suicidal ideation are presenting issues for a non-trivial proportion of adolescent cases seen in the child protection system.

## 3.4 Respect for the Views of the Child (Article 12)

The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) includes provisions requiring that the views of children be considered in all decisions affecting them, commensurate with their age and maturity.^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, Article 7]

In judicial proceedings, the Juvenile Court has developed protocols for child-friendly hearings in which children in conflict with the law are given the opportunity to participate meaningfully in proceedings. A pilot child-friendly justice initiative, supported by UNICEF, equipped the Juvenile Court with child-appropriate interview rooms and trained staff in child-friendly communication.^[UNICEF Maldives, Child-Friendly Justice Initiative Report, 2022]

In child protection cases managed by CFPS and FCSCs, social work protocols require social workers to consult children directly as part of the assessment process. Specialist training in child interviewing has been provided to FCSC social workers in Malé and selected atoll centres, though consistent implementation across all 24 FCSCs remains a challenge.

**Child participation in policy processes** has been strengthened through the establishment of a National Youth Parliament and periodic child consultation processes convened by MoSFD in partnership with UNICEF. However, formal mechanisms for ongoing child participation in policy development and monitoring remain nascent, and the Government acknowledges that meaningful, sustained child participation requires institutional investment beyond one-off consultation events.

# CLUSTER 4: CIVIL RIGHTS AND FREEDOMS
*(Articles 7, 8, 13–17 and 37(a) CRC)*

## 4.1 Birth Registration (Article 7)

Maldives has achieved near-universal birth registration for children born in health facilities. Under the Birth and Death Registration Act (Law No. 2000/24), the registration of births within a specified period is obligatory. As of 2022, the civil registration rate for children born in health facilities is estimated at over 99%.^[Maldives Bureau of Statistics, Vital Statistics Report 2022]

However, challenges remain for specific groups:
- **Children born to undocumented migrant workers:** These children face significant barriers to birth registration. Without registered birth certificates, they cannot access education, healthcare or social services in the normal way. The Government is reviewing its policies on birth registration for children of undocumented migrants, with the principle of universal birth registration informing policy deliberations.^[Vulnerability Mapping: Migrant Children and Statelessness, 2020]
- **Children born outside health facilities:** While rare — the vast majority of births occur in health facilities — home births in remote islands can result in delayed registration. Mobile registration services operate in some atolls.
- **Stateless children:** A small number of children are at risk of statelessness as a result of their parents' legal status. The Government is engaged with UNHCR on addressing statelessness risks.

## 4.2 Name and Nationality (Article 8)

Children have the right to a name from birth under Maldivian law. Maldivian children are registered with both a given name and the father's name (or in some cases, mother's name). Citizenship is governed by the Citizenship Act (Law No. 2000/9). Children born to Maldivian fathers are citizens by birth. Children born to Maldivian mothers and foreign fathers may acquire citizenship, though procedures can be complex and the Government is reviewing the gender equity dimensions of citizenship law.^[Citizenship Act, Law No. 2000/9; HRCM, Review of Citizenship Provisions, 2021]

## 4.3 Freedom of Expression, Thought, Conscience and Religion (Articles 13–14)

As noted in Cluster 1, Maldives maintains its reservation to Article 14 concerning freedom of religion and conscience in light of Islam's status as the state religion. In practice, all Maldivian citizens — including children — are constitutionally required to be Muslim, and the practice of other religions is prohibited under Article 9 of the Constitution.

The Government recognises the tension between this constitutional provision and the universality of Article 14 as interpreted by the Committee, and acknowledges the Committee's previous recommendation to withdraw the reservation. The Government reiterates that this reservation reflects the deepest constitutional foundations of the Maldivian state and that any change would require constitutional reform.

Within the framework of Islamic identity, children are afforded significant scope for expression. Freedom of expression for children is formally guaranteed under the Constitution. However, in practice, societal norms and school environments can restrict children's ability to express views on politically or religiously sensitive matters. The Government is working through school curricula and teacher training to create more space for critical thinking and free inquiry within an Islamic value framework.

## 4.4 Access to Information (Article 17)

The Government has taken steps to ensure children's access to age-appropriate information. The national curriculum includes media literacy components. The Maldives Broadcasting Commission regulates broadcast content and has issued guidelines on child-appropriate content standards. Access to the internet is widespread across Maldives, including in outer islands, through government connectivity investments. Internet penetration rates are among the highest in South Asia.

However, **digital safety** for children is an emerging and significant concern. The Government acknowledges that children in Maldives are exposed to online risks including cyberbullying, exposure to harmful content, online grooming and child sexual exploitation material. The legal framework for online child protection is being strengthened, with amendments to the Sexual Offences Act addressing online sexual exploitation. The MPS maintains a Computer Crime and Digital Forensics unit that investigates cases of online child sexual abuse.

## 4.5 Privacy (Article 16)

The Constitution protects the right to privacy. In child protection proceedings, case files are treated as confidential and identifying information about children involved in child protection or juvenile justice proceedings is not disclosed publicly. However, the Government acknowledges that in the small island communities that characterise much of Maldives, effective anonymity is difficult to achieve in practice, and the social consequences of identification remain a barrier to reporting and help-seeking by children and families.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025]

## 4.6 Prohibition of Torture and Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment (Article 37(a))

The Constitution prohibits torture and cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment. The Penal Code (Law No. 9/2014) criminalises torture.

Within the juvenile justice system, the Juvenile Justice Act (2019) prohibits any form of physical punishment, degrading treatment or humiliation of children in conflict with the law or in rehabilitation facilities. The DJJ has developed internal monitoring protocols for Fiyavathi Rehabilitation Centre (for girls) and Imaduddin School for Boys, including regular independent inspections.

The HRCM has conducted monitoring visits to juvenile rehabilitation facilities and has raised concerns in previous reports about overcrowding, adequacy of programming, staffing ratios, and the appropriateness of physical environments. The Government has taken steps to address these concerns, including infrastructure improvements at Imaduddin School.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Monitoring Report on Juvenile Facilities, 2023]

**Corporal punishment** in schools is discussed in Cluster 5 of this report.

# CLUSTER 5: VIOLENCE AGAINST CHILDREN
*(Articles 19, 24(3), 28(2), 34, 37(a) and 39 CRC)*

## 5.1 Physical and Humiliating Punishment

### Legal Framework

The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) explicitly prohibits all forms of corporal punishment against children in all settings, including in the home, schools, care institutions and juvenile facilities. Article 38 of the Act states that no person shall subject a child to physical punishment, humiliating or degrading treatment, or any other form of violence.^[Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019, Article 38]

The Penal Code (2014) criminalises assault, which includes physical assault of children by parents or other caregivers where it meets the threshold of assault.

The Education Act (2021) reinforces the prohibition of corporal punishment in schools and establishes positive discipline as a required approach.

### Implementation Reality

Despite the clear legal prohibition, corporal punishment in school settings continues to be reported through FCSC case data and HRCM monitoring. A cultural perception that moderate physical correction of children by parents and teachers is permissible persists in some communities, particularly in outer islands. The transition from a historically accepted practice to a prohibited one requires sustained social change work alongside enforcement.

The Government has invested in positive discipline training programmes for teachers through the Ministry of Education, reaching an estimated 40% of teachers nationally by 2023. School-based child protection committees, mandated under the Education Act, are being established to provide reporting channels for children experiencing school-based violence.^[Ministry of Education, Positive Discipline Training Programme Report, 2023]

**Islamic corporal punishment (*hudud*):** Under the Maldives' legal and constitutional framework, Sharia-prescribed punishments including corporal punishment may be applied to adults who have reached puberty in specific cases involving *hudud* offences. The Committee has previously raised concerns about this in relation to juvenile offenders who may be subject to such punishments after puberty. The Juvenile Justice Act (2019) provides that children subject to the juvenile justice system shall not receive adult criminal sentences including corporal punishment, and provides for rehabilitation-oriented dispositions. The practical interaction between the Sharia framework and juvenile justice protections remains an area requiring ongoing monitoring.

## 5.2 Child Abuse and Neglect (Article 19)

### Reporting and Case Volume

Child abuse and neglect cases reported to CFPS and FCSCs have increased significantly over the reporting period. This increase is understood to reflect both rising reporting rates (as awareness of reporting obligations and channels increases) and genuine increases in case identification as the system matures, rather than necessarily indicating an increase in the underlying prevalence of abuse.

The NCB Statistical Yearbook (Table 8.60) records child abuse cases from 2009 to 2020, showing a consistent upward trend in reported cases throughout the decade. CFPS case statistics from 2020 to 2024 continue this trend, with the COVID-19 pandemic period (2020–2021) showing a particular spike in abuse and neglect reports consistent with patterns observed internationally during lockdown periods.^[National Bureau of Classification, Statistical Yearbook, Table 8.60]

Key case volume indicators:
- The Childline 1412 helpline receives thousands of contacts annually, of which a significant proportion result in formal referrals to CFPS or FCSCs
- FCSCs collectively manage several thousand active cases at any given time
- Physical abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and sexual abuse all feature significantly in the case mix

### Case Management System

CFPS operates a structured case management system with the following stages: referral and intake; initial assessment (within 24 hours for urgent cases, 72 hours for non-urgent); full assessment and investigation (jointly with police where criminal conduct is suspected); case planning; intervention and service provision; monitoring and review; and case closure. This protocol was formalised through CFPS Standard Operating Procedures developed in 2021.^[CFPS, Case Management Standard Operating Procedures, 2021]

A critical operational challenge is that case management in outer islands is conducted by FCSCs whose social worker complement may be as few as one or two workers for entire atolls of multiple islands. Case loads per social worker in outer atolls frequently exceed 40–60 active cases, well above recommended caseload standards. This constrains the quality and frequency of follow-up and monitoring.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025]

### Multi-Agency Coordination

A formal Multi-Disciplinary Team (MDT) protocol was established in 2022, bringing together social workers, police, health professionals, and prosecutors for complex child abuse cases. MDT meetings are held regularly in Malé and piloted in three atolls (Addu, Haa Dhaal, and Laamu). Extension of the MDT model to all atolls is planned but faces resource constraints.^[MoSFD, MDT Protocol for Child Abuse Cases, 2022]

## 5.3 Sexual Abuse and Exploitation (Articles 19, 34)

Sexual abuse represents one of the most serious and prevalent child protection concerns in Maldives. The Government acknowledges the scale of the problem and the significant gap between the number of cases occurring and those that result in investigation and prosecution.

### Case Volume and Investigation

Maldives Police Service (MPS) data recorded **291 incidents of child commercial sexual exploitation** investigated in one reporting year during this period.^[Maldives Police Service, Crime Statistics Report] Child sexual abuse cases investigated by MPS number in the hundreds annually, though the true prevalence is believed to be significantly higher given documented barriers to reporting.

Barriers to reporting include: social stigma and fear of disbelief; family pressure to conceal abuse (particularly where the perpetrator is a family member); lack of confidence in the justice system; geographic remoteness; and the very small community sizes in outer islands where anonymity is impossible.^[Vulnerability Mapping: Sexual Abuse and Exploitation of Children, 2020]

### Conviction Rates

Conviction rates for child sexual abuse cases remain critically low relative to reported cases. Cases that enter the criminal justice system face challenges including: evidence being primarily testimonial with no corroborating physical evidence; recantation by child witnesses under family or community pressure; lengthy court processes during which children may disengage; inadequate training of prosecutors and investigators in child-sensitive techniques; and, historically, judicial interpretation of corroboration requirements.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025; Vulnerability Mapping: Legal and Justice System Analysis, 2020]

The Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act (2021) addressed some evidentiary requirements and strengthened victim protections. The Prosecutor General's office has established a specialised unit for sexual offences, including cases involving children. Training programmes for investigators and prosecutors in child-sensitive interviewing and evidence gathering have been conducted with UNICEF and UNODC support.^[UNODC, Technical Assistance Report: Child-Sensitive Investigation Training, 2023]

### Child Pornography and Online Exploitation

The production, distribution and possession of child sexual abuse material (CSAM) is criminalised under the Sexual Offences Act (2014) as amended. The MPS Computer Crime and Digital Forensics unit works with international partners including INTERPOL on CSAM investigations. During the reporting period, a number of investigations were conducted and prosecutions initiated. The Government is aware that CSAM production and online grooming pose growing risks in the context of Maldives' high internet penetration, and is working to strengthen detection, investigation and victim support capabilities.

## 5.4 Child Trafficking and Sale (Articles 35–36)

The Anti-Human Trafficking Act (Law No. 12/2013) criminalises trafficking in persons, including children. Child trafficking is an aggravated offence carrying enhanced penalties.

The trafficking situation in Maldives is complex. As a small tourism-dependent economy with large migrant worker flows, Maldives faces both transit and destination risks. Vulnerabilities include:
- Domestic workers (many migrant) at risk of labour exploitation and trafficking
- Tourism industry creating demand for commercial sexual services, with some risk of child sexual exploitation
- Migrant children accompanying trafficked parents in situations of labour exploitation

MoGFSS data from the reporting period identifies **21 suspected child trafficking cases** that were referred to or identified by the Ministry, of which a significant proportion did not result in formal investigation. This gap between case identification and investigation and prosecution is a key concern.^[Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services, Trafficking Case Data, 2022]

The Government has established a National Anti-Trafficking Committee and a National Referral Mechanism for trafficking victims, including children. Victim identification protocols were revised in 2022. However, civil society organisations have noted that the NRM in practice operates primarily in Malé, with limited capacity to identify trafficking situations in outer islands or resort zones.^[Hope for Women, Report on Human Trafficking in Maldives, 2023]

## 5.5 Online Violence and Digital Safety

The Government recognises online violence as a rapidly growing child protection challenge. Key risks include cyberbullying, online grooming, sextortion, and exposure to violent or extremist content. The legal framework for online child safety is developing, with relevant provisions in the Sexual Offences Act, the Penal Code and cybercrime legislation.

The NDA and MoSFD have jointly conducted awareness campaigns on digital safety in schools. The MPS cyber crimes unit engages in school outreach programmes. However, the Government acknowledges that the scale of institutional response does not yet match the scale of the risk, and a comprehensive national online child safety strategy is being developed.

# CLUSTER 6: FAMILY ENVIRONMENT AND ALTERNATIVE CARE
*(Articles 5, 9–11, 18–21, 25, 27(4) and 39 CRC)*

## 6.1 Parental Guidance and Responsibilities (Article 5, 18)

The Family Act (Law No. 4/2000) establishes the legal framework for parental rights and responsibilities in Maldives. Both parents bear joint responsibility for the upbringing and welfare of their children. In practice, Islamic family law principles govern the division of roles: the father is considered the financial provider (*nafaqa*) while the mother holds primary custodial rights (*hadhanah*) for young children, particularly before puberty.

Parenting support programmes are provided through FCSCs, including family counselling, parenting skills training, and home visiting programmes. The Government has invested in parenting programmes adapted to Maldivian family contexts, focusing on positive discipline, child development, and recognition of abuse.^[MoSFD, Parenting Support Programme Report, 2023]

## 6.2 Separation from Parents (Article 9)

Separation of a child from their parents is a measure of last resort under Maldivian law and the CFPS case management framework. The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) establishes clear legal criteria for removal of children from their families: that the child is at risk of serious harm and that the risk cannot be managed within the family setting.

Emergency protective orders (EPOs) can be issued by CFPS and confirmed by the Family Court within 24 hours. Longer-term placement orders require Family Court approval following a full best interests assessment. During the reporting period, FCSCs have progressively strengthened their procedures for seeking court orders and for ensuring regular review of children in out-of-home placements.

The number of children formally placed in out-of-home care through court orders has increased over the reporting period as the child protection system has matured and developed capacity to identify and act on protection risks. However, the Government acknowledges that many children remain in informal family arrangements — with extended family members — without formal legal status or oversight, representing a governance gap in the alternative care framework.

## 6.3 Family Reunification (Article 10)

The Family Court has jurisdiction over family reunification matters. The CFPS case management framework includes reunification planning as a key element of case plans where children have been separated from parents. Reunification is pursued where it is assessed as safe and in the child's best interests. The Government acknowledges that reunification processes can be protracted, and is working to strengthen case review timelines.

## 6.4 Maintenance and Alimony (Article 27(4))

Under Islamic family law as applied in Maldives, fathers are obligated to provide financial maintenance (*nafaqa*) for their children. The Family Court may order maintenance payments in separation or divorce cases. Enforcement of maintenance orders has historically been weak, with many children not receiving court-ordered payments. The Family Legal Aid Service provides support to mothers and children in pursuing maintenance claims. Reforms to enforcement mechanisms are under consideration.^[Vulnerability Mapping: Family Law and Maintenance, 2020]

## 6.5 Children Deprived of Family Environment: Institutional Care (Article 20)

**Current situation:** Maldives does not have a developed foster care system in the formal sense. Children who cannot remain with their parents are primarily placed through two mechanisms:

1. **Kinship care:** Placement with extended family members (grandparents, aunts/uncles) — the predominant form of alternative care in practice, operating largely informally without formal legal oversight
2. **Residential institutional care:** A very small number of children are placed in residential facilities

Institutional care options in Maldives are limited. The **Kudakudhinge Hiyaa** (Children's Home) in Malé operates as the primary residential facility for children who cannot be cared for within family settings. Capacity is limited, and the facility has been subject to monitoring visits by the HRCM and the Children's Ombudsperson. The Government is committed to a care reform process that shifts toward family-based care, consistent with the UN Guidelines for the Alternative Care of Children (2010).^[United Nations Guidelines for the Alternative Care of Children, GA Resolution 64/142, 2010]

### Foster Care Development

The Government is developing a formal foster care framework that will establish legal procedures for recruiting, assessing and supporting foster families, provide financial support to foster carers, and establish monitoring and review procedures. A Foster Care Regulation was drafted in 2023 and is expected to be finalised and promulgated in 2025. This represents a significant step toward a family-based care model, though the Government acknowledges that building the foster care system from a near-zero base will take time and sustained investment.^[MoSFD, Draft Foster Care Regulation, 2023]

## 6.6 Adoption and Kafala (Articles 20, 21)

As noted in Cluster 1, Maldives maintains its reservation to Article 21 (adoption) on the basis that formal adoption is inconsistent with Islamic law as applied in Maldives. Under Islamic jurisprudence, *kafala* (guardianship or sponsorship) is the recognized mechanism for providing permanent family care to children who cannot be raised by their birth parents.

*Kafala* in the Maldivian context involves a guardian taking responsibility for a child's care, education and welfare without legally severing the child's ties to their birth family or changing the child's name or inheritance rights. *Kafala* arrangements are registered with the Family Court.

The Government recognises that the *kafala* framework, while consistent with Islamic law, may not provide the same permanency of legal status for children that formal adoption does in other legal systems. The Government is working to strengthen the *kafala* framework, including through clearer regulation of the rights and responsibilities of *kafala* guardians and oversight of *kafala* placements.

# CLUSTER 7: DISABILITY, BASIC HEALTH AND WELFARE
*(Articles 6, 18(3), 23–27 and 33 CRC)*

## 7.1 Children with Disabilities (Article 23)

### Legal Framework

The Disability Act (Law No. 8/2010) establishes rights of persons with disabilities, including children, in areas of education, healthcare, social services and participation. The National Social Protection Act and related regulations provide for disability benefits and support services. The Disability Act was the first comprehensive legal instrument addressing disability rights in Maldives, and its enactment represented a significant step forward in the recognition of disability as a rights issue rather than a welfare issue.^[Disability Act, Law No. 8/2010, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]

The Maldives Disability Survey (conducted with UNICEF support) estimated that approximately 4–6% of the population have some form of disability, consistent with regional averages though likely an underestimate given data collection challenges.^[UNICEF Maldives / MoSFD, Disability Survey Report, 2020]

### Service Infrastructure

**National Social Protection Agency (NSPA):** Administers disability benefits and maintains the national disability registry. Children registered with disabilities receive a monthly disability allowance and are entitled to enhanced social protection benefits.

**Disability and Special Needs Service Centre (DSNSC):** Located in Malé, provides specialist assessment, therapy (occupational therapy, speech and language therapy, physiotherapy), and early intervention services for children with disabilities. The DSNSC is the only specialist disability service centre in Maldives; children in outer islands must travel to Malé to access specialist services, representing a significant geographic inequity.^[Disability and Special Needs Service Centre, Annual Report 2023]

**Special Education Needs (SEN) Services:** The Ministry of Education has developed an inclusive education policy and provides SEN support in mainstream schools. As of 2023, SEN teachers are posted to schools in all atolls, though the ratio of SEN specialists to children with disabilities in mainstream schools remains low. Special schools for children with complex needs operate in Malé.

### Key Challenges

The most significant challenges for children with disabilities include:
- **Geographic access:** Children in outer islands have very limited access to specialist services without travelling to Malé, which is costly and disruptive for families
- **Inclusive education implementation:** While inclusive education policy exists, implementation varies widely; many schools lack accessible infrastructure, trained SEN staff, and adapted learning materials
- **Data limitations:** Disaggregated data on educational attendance, health outcomes and social participation for children with disabilities is limited
- **Social stigma:** Despite legal protections, children with some forms of disability — particularly intellectual disabilities and psychosocial disabilities — continue to face social stigma in some communities
- **Mental health services:** Almost no specialist child mental health services exist outside Malé

## 7.2 Health and Health Services (Articles 24, 27)

### Universal Health Insurance: Aasandha

The **Aasandha** universal health insurance scheme, introduced in 2012, provides comprehensive health coverage for all Maldivian citizens including all children. Aasandha covers hospital inpatient care, outpatient consultations, diagnostic services, and approved medications at no cost to insured persons at public health facilities. For children, this means that access to healthcare is effectively free at the point of use across the national health network.^[Aasandha Universal Health Insurance, MoH Overview, 2022]

Aasandha has had a transformative impact on health-seeking behaviour and health outcomes for children, contributing to the significant decline in infant and child mortality observed over the reporting period.

### Health Infrastructure

The Ministry of Health operates a tiered health infrastructure comprising:
- Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) — the national referral hospital in Malé
- Regional hospitals in the six regional centres (Addu City, Kulhudhuffushi, Naifaru, Thinadhoo, Fuvahmulah)
- Atoll hospitals in each atoll capital
- Health centres on inhabited islands above a population threshold
- Health posts on smaller islands

As of 2024, approximately 95% of the population lives within reach of some form of public health facility. However, access to specialist paediatric services remains concentrated in Malé and, to a lesser extent, regional hospitals.^[Ministry of Health, Health Service Delivery Plan 2021–2025]

### Immunisation

Maldives maintains high immunisation coverage, with vaccination rates for standard childhood vaccines (BCG, DTP, OPV, MMR, hepatitis B) consistently above 95% nationally.^[Ministry of Health, Expanded Programme on Immunisation Report, 2022] This coverage, sustained through both fixed health facility vaccination and periodic outreach campaigns, has contributed to the elimination of vaccine-preventable diseases from the national child mortality picture.

### Maternal and Newborn Health

Skilled birth attendance has reached near-universal levels, with over 99% of deliveries occurring in health facilities. The neonatal mortality rate (5.8 per 1,000 live births in 2022) has declined significantly from earlier periods, though remains higher than the rates achieved in comparable small island states in other regions. A national neonatal care quality improvement programme is underway.^[Ministry of Health, Maternal and Child Health Statistical Report, 2022]

### Nutrition

The Government's **National School Health Programme** includes school-based nutritional screening and supplementation. A school feeding programme operates in selected schools, particularly targeting children from lower socioeconomic backgrounds. Stunting rates among Maldivian children are relatively low by regional standards (estimated below 10%), though overweight and obesity are growing public health concerns among children and adolescents, driven by dietary patterns (high sugar intake, processed foods) that are characteristic of small island developing states.^[Ministry of Health / UNICEF, Child Nutrition Survey, 2020]

## 7.3 Adolescent Health (Article 24)

Adolescent health is an area of significant government focus during the reporting period. Key adolescent health concerns include:

**Sexual and reproductive health:** Comprehensive adolescent sexual and reproductive health (ASRH) education has been a contested issue in Maldives. The national school curriculum includes some reproductive health content within science and Islamic studies, but comprehensive sexuality education — in the sense understood by international public health bodies — has not been adopted due to social and religious sensitivities. Teen pregnancy, while not a major national public health statistic in aggregate, remains a concern in some outer island communities and among school dropout populations.

**Mental health:** Adolescent mental health is a growing and largely unaddressed need. The demand for child and adolescent mental health services significantly exceeds supply. The national hospital (IGMH) has a limited adult psychiatry service; specialist child and adolescent psychiatry is effectively unavailable. The Government is developing a mental health policy that includes child and adolescent components, and a small number of school-based counselling posts have been established, though coverage is far from universal.^[Ministry of Health, National Mental Health Policy, 2023 (draft)]

**Substance use:** As noted above, drug use among youth is a significant and growing concern. The NDA provides youth-targeted prevention programmes in schools and community settings. Treatment services for young people with substance use problems are limited; the NDA's rehabilitation centre operates primarily for adults.

## 7.4 Standard of Living and Poverty (Article 27)

Maldives is classified as an upper-middle income country. GDP per capita has increased significantly over the reporting period, driven primarily by tourism revenue. However, income inequality has grown alongside economic growth, and geographic inequalities in living standards between Malé/resort islands and outer inhabited islands remain marked.^[World Bank, Maldives Country Economic Review, 2023]

The Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES 2019–20) estimated that approximately 18% of children live below the national poverty line. Poverty is significantly more prevalent in outer islands and among households headed by women (including divorced women who receive inadequate maintenance). The **Aasandha** scheme, the disability allowance, and the **Madhana** single-parent allowance constitute the primary social protection instruments for children in poverty. The Madhana allowance was expanded in 2022 to increase coverage and benefit levels.^[National Social Protection Agency, Annual Report 2022]

## 7.5 Drug and Substance Abuse (Article 33)

The Maldives faces a serious drug problem that has significant implications for children. Drug use disproportionately affects youth, with the NDA 2021 survey estimating that over 7,000 individuals are actively using drugs, with the 15–29 age group representing the largest user cohort.^[National Drug Agency, National Drug Use Survey, 2021]

The primary substances of concern are heroin (brown sugar), methamphetamine, and cannabis. Drug use is associated with gang involvement, school dropout, criminal activity and family breakdown — all of which have direct adverse impacts on child welfare.

Children are affected both as users themselves (older adolescents) and as children in families affected by parental drug use. Parental substance abuse is a significant contributing factor in child abuse and neglect cases managed by CFPS and FCSCs.

The NDA's **Youth Awareness Programme** and **School-Based Prevention Programme** are the primary prevention mechanisms, reaching thousands of students annually. The legal framework for drug-related offences involving children is addressed in the Juvenile Justice Act and the Drug Act (Law No. 17/2011), which provides for rehabilitation-oriented dispositions for first-time youth offenders.^[National Drug Agency, Youth Prevention Programme Report, 2023]

# CLUSTER 8: EDUCATION, LEISURE AND CULTURAL ACTIVITIES
*(Articles 28, 29 and 31 CRC)*

## 8.1 Education: Access and Enrolment (Article 28)

### Legal Framework

Education is a constitutional right in Maldives. The Education Act (2021) establishes compulsory education from age 6 to 16 years, corresponding to Grades 1 through 10. The Act prohibits discrimination in access to education and mandates free and compulsory basic education in government schools. The Ministry of Education (MoE) is the responsible authority, operating through a network of government schools across all inhabited islands.^[Education Act, 2021, People's Majlis, Republic of Maldives]

### Enrolment Statistics

Access to primary education is near-universal in Maldives, a significant achievement given the geographic dispersion of the population across 187 inhabited islands of varying sizes:

| Level | NER | GER | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary (Grades 1–7) | ~97% | ~99% | 2022/23 |
| Secondary (Grades 8–10) | ~87% | ~90% | 2022/23 |
| Post-secondary (Grades 11–12) | ~65% | ~70% | 2022/23 |

*Source: Ministry of Education, Education Statistics Yearbook 2023*

### Out-of-School Children

Despite high overall enrolment rates, specific populations remain at elevated risk of being out of school or dropping out:
- Children with disabilities (particularly in outer islands where SEN support is limited)
- Children involved in child labour (particularly in the fishing and domestic work sectors)
- Children in conflict with the law (in juvenile facilities)
- Children of migrant workers (particularly undocumented migrants whose children may face documentation barriers)
- Girls who marry early in outer island communities
- Children experiencing family breakdown, abuse or neglect^[UNICEF Maldives, Out-of-School Children Study, 2021]

The Government is implementing targeted interventions for each of these groups, including flexible learning pathways, conditional support grants, and outreach by school-based social workers.

### School Infrastructure and Geographic Access

Every inhabited island with a population above a minimum threshold has at least one government school. For very small islands, students may need to board in atoll capital islands to access higher grades of schooling. The Government provides boarding scholarships and facilities in atoll capitals to enable secondary education access for students from small islands, though distance from family and the social adjustment required can contribute to dropout, particularly among girls.

### Quality of Education

Educational quality, as measured by national assessments and international benchmarks, has been an area of ongoing focus. Key quality concerns include: teacher qualifications and retention (particularly in outer islands, where recruiting and retaining qualified teachers is difficult); the quality of learning environments; and learning outcomes in literacy and numeracy, particularly in the lower grades.

The MoE has implemented a National Curriculum Framework and national assessment system. Results from national Grade 4 and Grade 7 assessments indicate ongoing concerns about literacy and numeracy levels in outer island schools. The Teacher Competency Development Programme, launched in 2022, aims to improve teacher quality through in-service training.^[Ministry of Education, National Assessment Programme Results, 2022]

## 8.2 Aims of Education (Article 29)

The national school curriculum is guided by principles that encompass both academic achievement and values development. The Education Act (2021) specifies that education shall develop children's: full mental and physical potential; respect for Islamic values; respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms; national identity and civic responsibility; environmental awareness; and preparation for adult life and economic participation.^[Education Act, 2021, Article 6]

The curriculum balance between Islamic studies and secular academic subjects has been a subject of public discourse. The Government is committed to a curriculum that prepares children both for full participation in Maldivian society — rooted in Islamic values — and for engagement with a globalised world.

Human rights education is incorporated into the social studies curriculum. The MoE, in partnership with the HRCM, has developed child rights modules incorporated into the curriculum at primary and secondary levels.

## 8.3 Rest, Leisure and Cultural Activities (Article 31)

The Constitution recognises the right to participate in cultural and social life. However, the Government acknowledges significant disparities in access to leisure, recreational and cultural facilities between children in Malé and children in outer islands.

In Malé, the Government has invested in public parks, playgrounds, sports facilities, and community centres. The Sports Commission runs youth sports programmes. The National Arts and Culture Centre provides arts and cultural programming.

In outer islands, access to recreational facilities is significantly more limited, constrained by land availability, infrastructure investment levels, and the small populations of many islands. The provision of adequate recreational space and programming for children in all inhabited islands is a recognised need that the Government is working to address through the local government system.

School sporting and cultural activities are the primary organised leisure activity for most children in Maldives, and the MoE promotes inter-school competitions in sports, arts and academic disciplines. School holiday periods, while theoretically recreation time, can be periods of vulnerability for children, particularly in communities with limited structured activities and high rates of gang activity and drug use, as noted in MPS and FCSC case data.

# CLUSTER 9: SPECIAL PROTECTION MEASURES
*(Articles 22, 30, 32–36, 37(b)–(d), 38, 39 and 40 CRC)*

## 9.1 Child Labour (Article 32)

### Legal Framework

The Employment Act (Law No. 2/2008) and its regulations establish the minimum working age at 16 years. Employment of persons aged 16–18 is restricted to non-hazardous work and limited hours. Employment of children below 16 in any form of wage employment is prohibited. The Child Rights Protection Act (2019) reinforces the prohibition on child labour and defines child labour as any work that deprives children of their childhood, dignity and potential.^[Employment Act, Law No. 2/2008; Child Rights Protection Act, Law No. 18/2019]

The Employment Act Regulations specify that persons under 18 may not be employed in hazardous occupations, including fishing on the open sea, construction work at height, work involving dangerous chemicals, and night work.

### Situation in Practice

Child labour in Maldives is not a large-scale phenomenon in the formal economy, but pockets of concern exist:

**Fishing industry:** Children from fishing families may assist with fishing activities from an early age. Participation in family fishing is culturally embedded and not considered labour in the traditional sense, but can interfere with schooling and expose children to hazardous conditions. MPS data recorded 24 potential child labour cases in 2022 and 23 in 2021, though these statistics likely represent only a fraction of the actual situation.^[Maldives Police Service, Crime Statistics 2022]

**Domestic work:** Children engaged in unpaid domestic labour — including in their own homes — is largely invisible to monitoring systems. In some cases, children from outer islands are brought to urban centres, including Malé and resort workers' accommodation, to work as domestic helpers, in conditions that may amount to trafficking and forced labour. This form of child labour is very difficult to detect and monitor.

**Tourism support economy:** Children may be engaged in small-scale vending, tourism support activities, or informal service work in resort-adjacent communities. The formal resort sector, regulated by the Tourism Ministry, has its own employment standards that generally preclude child labour, but the informal tourism economy is less well monitored.

**Migrant children:** Children of migrant workers may be at risk of exploitative labour situations, particularly where their parents are in irregular migration situations.

### Response Measures

The Labour Relations Authority (LRA) is the primary enforcement authority for child labour provisions. Inspections of workplaces for child labour are conducted as part of the general labour inspection programme. The Government acknowledges, however, that inspection coverage is low and that resource constraints limit proactive monitoring.

MoSFD and the LRA are developing a Joint Child Labour Monitoring and Response Protocol, expected to be finalised in 2025, which will establish clearer inter-agency procedures for identification, referral and response to child labour cases.

## 9.2 Children in Conflict with the Law: Juvenile Justice (Article 40)

### The Pre-2019 System

Prior to the Juvenile Justice Act (2019), children in conflict with the law were processed through the adult criminal justice system with limited child-specific provisions. Children above the age of criminal responsibility (which was not clearly defined in statute) could be tried in adult courts, detained in adult facilities, and sentenced under the general penal framework. There was no dedicated juvenile rehabilitation infrastructure. The conditions experienced by children in conflict with the law were a significant concern documented by civil society and the HRCM.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Report on Juvenile Justice, 2017]

### The Post-2019 Framework

The Juvenile Justice Act (Law No. 19/2019) represents a fundamental restructuring of the juvenile justice system, grounded in principles of rehabilitation, reintegration and the best interests of the child. The key elements of the new framework are:

**The Juvenile Court:** A dedicated specialist court in Malé for children in conflict with the law (and certain civil matters involving children). The Juvenile Court operates with child-friendly procedures, including separate waiting areas, informal seating arrangements, and hearings from which the public is excluded. Judges assigned to the Juvenile Court receive specialist training in child rights and juvenile justice.^[Juvenile Court, Annual Report 2023]

**The Department of Juvenile Justice (DJJ):** Established under the Ministry of Home Affairs, the DJJ is responsible for the management and oversight of juvenile rehabilitation facilities, the diversion programme, probation and supervision of children under court orders. The DJJ became operational in 2020.^[Department of Juvenile Justice, Annual Report 2022]

**Diversion:** The Juvenile Justice Act establishes a formal diversion programme for first-time and minor offenders. Eligible children are diverted from prosecution and instead undergo a structured programme that may include counselling, family conferencing, community service, and supervised activities. Diversion is managed by the DJJ with CFPS involvement. In the first three years of operation (2020–2022), hundreds of children were diverted from prosecution through this mechanism.

**Rehabilitation Facilities:**
- **Imaduddin School for Boys** — the secure rehabilitation facility for male juvenile offenders. The facility provides education, vocational training, sports, religious education, and psychosocial support. Capacity is limited and the physical environment has been an ongoing concern, with the HRCM noting in 2023 that infrastructure improvements were needed.^[Human Rights Commission of Maldives, Monitoring Report on Juvenile Facilities, 2023]
- **Fiyavathi Rehabilitation Centre** — the rehabilitation facility for female juvenile offenders. Given the small number of girls in conflict with the law at any given time, this facility also serves girls in need of protective care who cannot be managed in community settings. The dual function of the facility — rehabilitation for offenders and protection for vulnerable girls — raises concerns about the appropriateness of the environment for non-offending children.

**Sentencing:** The Juvenile Justice Act provides a menu of child-appropriate sentencing options including formal caution, conditional discharge, supervision orders, community service, probation, and as a last resort, placement in a rehabilitation facility. Imprisonment in an adult facility is prohibited for children under 18.

### Implementation Challenges

**Legal representation:** Children in conflict with the law are entitled to legal representation under the Juvenile Justice Act, but legal aid for children is extremely limited in practice. The public defender system is under-resourced, and many children appear in Juvenile Court without adequate legal counsel, particularly in cases where family resources are limited. The Government is developing a specialist juvenile legal aid programme in partnership with the Attorney General's Office.^[Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025]

**Remand detention:** Pre-trial detention of children remains a concern. While the Juvenile Justice Act limits pre-trial detention and prioritises bail and supervision orders, practical pressures — including the difficulty of securing appropriate community supervision arrangements — can result in children spending extended periods in pre-trial detention. The DJJ and Juvenile Court are working to reduce remand detention periods.

**Children in gangs:** Gang involvement among youth is a significant and growing concern in Malé and to a lesser extent in other urban centres. The gangs involved in drug distribution, extortion and violence recruit young people — sometimes children — as members. Gang membership intersects with drug use, school dropout, and exposure to violence. The Government's response involves both enforcement action through MPS and prevention/intervention programmes through MoSFD, DJJ, and NDA. A comprehensive gang prevention strategy is in development.^[Maldives Police Service, Gang and Organised Crime Report, 2022]

**Recidivism and reintegration:** Data on reoffending rates for children who have completed the diversion programme or been released from rehabilitation facilities is limited. Anecdotal evidence from DJJ and CFPS suggests that reintegration into family and community settings — and avoidance of reoffending — is more successful for children who maintain family connections and who have access to education and employment pathways post-release. Strengthening post-release support is a DJJ priority.

## 9.3 Children in Situations of Exploitation — Commercial Sexual Exploitation (Articles 34–36)

Commercial sexual exploitation of children (CSEC) is addressed in Cluster 5 in the context of violence against children. The Government reiterates here that CSEC is criminalised under the Sexual Offences Act (2014) as amended, and that the MPS and Prosecutor General's office treat CSEC as a serious crime. Comprehensive protective measures, including services for child survivors, are provided through CFPS.

However, the Government acknowledges that services for children who have experienced commercial sexual exploitation are inadequate. Specialist trauma-informed services for child survivors are available primarily in Malé, through a combination of CFPS counselling, IGMH psychiatric support, and NGO services. Children in outer islands who have experienced CSEC have very limited access to specialist support.

A **National Child Sexual Abuse Response Protocol** is being developed by MoSFD with UNICEF support, which will establish clear multi-agency procedures for identification, immediate support, criminal investigation, and longer-term survivor services.^[MoSFD / UNICEF, National Child Sexual Abuse Response Protocol (draft), 2024]

## 9.4 Children in Armed Conflict (Article 38)

Maldives is not a conflict-affected state and children in Maldives are not at risk of involvement in armed conflict. The Maldives Defence Force does not recruit persons below 18 years of age.

The Government acknowledges concerns related to the travel of a small number of Maldivian citizens to conflict zones to participate in extremist activities during the reporting period. Where children are affected by this — whether as children of individuals who have left the country for conflict zones, or as adolescents who may be at risk of radicalisation — the Government responds through a combination of Prevent-type programming within the education and community context, and child protection responses through CFPS where specific children are identified as at risk.^[Ministry of Home Affairs, Preventing Violent Extremism National Action Plan, 2022]

## 9.5 Children from Minority or Indigenous Groups (Article 30)

Maldives is a culturally and religiously homogeneous society. There are no indigenous minority groups within the Maldivian population. All Maldivian citizens are Muslim. The provisions of Article 30 relating to children of minorities or indigenous communities have limited direct application to the Maldivian context.

## 9.6 Refugee and Asylum-Seeking Children (Article 22)

Maldives has not ratified the 1951 Refugee Convention or its 1967 Protocol. There is no national asylum or refugee determination system. The Government engages with UNHCR on an ad hoc basis regarding persons of concern on Maldivian territory. The practical incidence of refugee or asylum-seeking children on Maldivian territory is very limited, and the Government is working with UNHCR to develop basic procedures for cases that do arise.

# PART II: FOLLOW-UP TO PREVIOUS CONCLUDING OBSERVATIONS

The Committee's Concluding Observations on the Fourth Periodic Report (CRC/C/MDV/CO/4, 2016) identified a number of priority areas for action. This section provides a structured update on progress against the key concerns raised.

## Minimum Age of Marriage

The Committee recommended that Maldives establish 18 as the absolute minimum age of marriage without exceptions. As described in Cluster 2, the Family Law Regulation (2016) established 18 as the minimum age, but retained a judicial exception. The Government acknowledges that full compliance with the Committee's recommendation requires elimination of this exception and is committed to pursuing this through legislative action.

## Corporal Punishment

The Committee recommended that Maldives explicitly prohibit all forms of corporal punishment in all settings. As described in Cluster 5, the Child Rights Protection Act (2019) has now achieved this legal prohibition. The challenge of ensuring implementation — particularly in the home — remains, and the Government is addressing this through awareness, parenting support and enforcement measures.

## Juvenile Justice

The Committee recommended comprehensive juvenile justice reform. As described in Cluster 9, the Juvenile Justice Act (2019) has fundamentally transformed the juvenile justice framework. The Government acknowledges that implementation — particularly regarding legal aid, conditions in rehabilitation facilities, and reintegration support — requires continued investment.

## Birth Registration for Children of Migrant Workers

The Committee expressed concern about birth registration for children of migrants. As described in Cluster 4, this remains an unresolved challenge. The Government is actively reviewing policy options.

## Data Collection

The Committee recommended strengthening of data collection systems. As described in Cluster 1, the National Child Protection Database is in development, and progress on data disaggregation across health, education and social welfare sectors is ongoing, though significant gaps remain.

## CONCLUDING STATEMENT

The Republic of Maldives has made transformative advances in child rights protection over the reporting period. The enactment of the Child Rights Protection Act and the Juvenile Justice Act in 2019 constitutes the most significant legislative achievement in Maldives' child rights history. The expansion of the FCSC network, the establishment of the Department of Juvenile Justice, the operationalisation of the Children's Ombudsperson, and the development of multi-agency coordination mechanisms represent substantial institutional progress.

At the same time, the Government is acutely aware that legislative and institutional achievements do not automatically translate into improved outcomes for children — particularly for the most vulnerable children in the most remote communities. The persistent implementation gap between law and practice in outer islands, the inadequacy of services for children with disabilities outside Malé, the chronically low conviction rates for child sexual abuse, the continuing phenomenon of child marriage, the underdevelopment of foster care, and the growing challenges of youth drug use and gang involvement all demand sustained government attention and investment.

The Government of Maldives reaffirms its commitment to the Convention on the Rights of the Child and to the rights of every Maldivian child — regardless of where they live, their family circumstances, or their individual characteristics — to protection, participation and development in accordance with their full potential. This report is submitted in that spirit, with acknowledgement of both progress made and work that remains.
