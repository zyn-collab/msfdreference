# Action Plans Analysis

This chapter provides a systematic, cross-cutting analysis of the action plans and policy documents that operationalise the Maldives' social policy commitments. Rather than describing each plan in isolation – as the preceding thematic chapters have done – this analysis examines the action plan architecture as a whole: its internal coherence, its alignment with evidence and international obligations, and its capacity to translate legislative mandates into measurable change for the populations it is designed to serve.

---

## Section 1: Inventory of Action Plans and Policy Documents (Status Assessment)

The Maldives' social policy architecture rests on a constellation of action plans, national strategies, and policy documents produced by the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD) and partner agencies. These documents vary widely in their maturity, legal grounding, operational specificity, and implementation status. This section provides a systematic inventory of each plan, beginning with currently active or proposed instruments and then cataloguing lapsed predecessors.

### 1.1 Active and Proposed Action Plans

#### 1.1.1 National Action Plan on Disability Inclusion (NAPDI v6), 2024–2028

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full Title** | National Action Plan on Disability Inclusion (NAPDI v6) |
| **Period** | 2024–2028 |
| **Authoring Body** | Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD), with support from UN agencies |
| **Status** | Active (revised clean version dated December 2024) |
| **Legal Basis** | Disability Act 2010 (Act 8/2010); CRPD (ratified 2010) |
| **Number of Policy Goals** | 5 |
| **Number of Policy Statements** | 25 |
| **Number of Strategies** | 40+ |
| **Key Implementing Agencies** | MoSFD, NSPA, Ministry of Health, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Higher Education, Ministry of Housing, Ministry of Economic Development, local councils, Disability Council |
| **Target Population** | 24,401 persons with disabilities (Census 2022); 13,656 registered in NDR (December 2024) |

**Scope Summary:** The NAPDI v6 is structured around five policy goals drawn from SDG targets, each with multiple policy statements and strategies:

1. **Good Health and Wellbeing** (4 policy statements, 6+ strategies): Covers universal access to healthcare, social protection reform, institutional capacity-building for service delivery, and early identification/intervention for children with disabilities. Specific strategies address physical accessibility of health facilities, accessible communication materials, review of welfare policies to recognise disability diversity, inclusivity training for health providers, and early identification tools for children.[^1]

2. **Quality and Inclusive Education** (5 policy statements, 7+ strategies): Addresses early childhood development, completion of primary and secondary education, access to technical/vocational/tertiary education, adult literacy and numeracy, and safe and accessible learning environments. Strategies include capacity assessment for parents and educators, curriculum review for inclusivity, adult learning programmes, and building/upgrading education facilities for disability-friendliness.[^2]

3. **Decent Work** (6 policy statements, 10+ strategies): Covers vocational training, full employment and equal pay, labour rights and workplace accessibility, on-the-job training, entrepreneurship promotion, and financial services access. Strategies include partnerships between training institutions and employers, workplace inclusivity policies, compliance monitoring under the Disability Act, mentoring programmes, entrepreneurship training, and strengthening domestic financial institutions' capacity to serve persons with disabilities.[^3]

4. **Accessible and Sustainable Cities and Communities** (6 policy statements, 10+ strategies): Addresses community awareness, housing and independent living, transport accessibility, public spaces, island council adoption of inclusion policies, and climate change adaptation. Strategies include disability awareness campaigns, building code enforcement, financial/technical support for accessible housing, public transport design, road safety, public space accessibility, and inclusion of persons with disabilities in climate change decision-making.[^4]

5. **Access to Justice** (6 policy statements, 8+ strategies): Covers rule of law and equal access to justice, non-discriminatory laws, ending violence against persons with disabilities, access to information and freedoms, participatory decision-making, and building effective and accountable institutions. Strategies include legal framework review, accessible courtrooms and legal documents, prevention programmes against violence, accessible legal awareness programmes, representation at decision-making levels, and civic participation mechanisms.[^5]

**Key Observation:** The NAPDI v6 is the most structurally comprehensive of the active action plans, with clearly delineated policy goals, statements, and strategies. However, it is notable for the absence of costed budgets, specific numerical targets, or baseline data against which progress can be measured. The plan reads as a comprehensive aspiration framework rather than a costed implementation roadmap. Given that the HRCM CRPD Shadow Report (2023) documented "incongruence between key legislations, and the regulations, policies or guidelines formulated to aid implementation" as "a critical barrier predominant in many areas," the gap between the NAPDI's ambitions and the operational reality of disability inclusion in the Maldives is substantial.[^6]

#### 1.1.2 National Action Plan on Preventing and Responding to Violence Against Children (NAPVAC / NAP-PRVAC), 2024–2028

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full Title** | National Action Plan on Preventing and Responding to Violence Against Children (NAP-PRVAC) |
| **Period** | 2024–2028 |
| **Authoring Body** | MoSFD; prepared by Dr. Mariya Ali, Aneesa Yoosuf, and Mariyam Ishraag Waheed |
| **Status** | Active (Draft II, Clean, dated April 2024) |
| **Legal Basis** | Child Rights Protection Act 2019 (Act 19/2019); CRC (ratified 1991) |
| **Number of Strategic Areas** | 5 |
| **Number of Outcomes** | 12 |
| **Number of Output Indicators** | 20+ |
| **Key Implementing Agencies** | MoSFD, CFPS, FCSCs, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Health, Maldives Police Service, Prosecutor General's Office, Family Protection Authority, UNICEF, civil society |
| **Target Population** | 128,208 children (Census 2022); 2,936 cases received by MoSFD in 2023, of which 1,278 (43.5%) were VAC cases |

**Scope Summary:** The NAPVAC is grounded in a socio-ecological model (Guerrero and Rojas, 2016) and uses the UNDRR disaster risk dimensions framework to analyse risk and protective factors across five strategic areas:

1. **Home: Safer Families** (3 outcomes): Establishing clear pathways for early detection and intervention; empowering parents to create positive environments and seek help; strengthening evidence-based strategies and policies. Outcome indicators track percentages of high-risk families receiving primary-level services, trained social service providers, parents equipped with information on non-violent discipline, and policy-level strategies based on research.[^7]

2. **School: Safer Educational Settings** (2 outcomes): Ensuring children feel protected from bullying, violence, and harassment in schools; building educator capacity through training and policy. Indicators track percentages of children reporting VAC to school-appointed child protection leads and decreases in bullying/harassment cases.[^8]

3. **Community: Safer Communities** (2 outcomes): Creating safe community environments with active participation; fostering child-friendly community cultures with gender-equitable norms promoted by public officials. Indicators include VAC cases identified by IBAMA community groups, population reached by awareness campaigns, VAC cases reported by children, political parties implementing codes of conduct, public officials trained in VAC, and islands following child-friendly urban planning.[^9]

4. **Online: Safer Online Spaces** (2 outcomes): Establishing institutional and legislative frameworks for online child protection by 2027; protecting children through increased capacity and awareness. Indicators track reviewed/implemented cyber-crime regulations, decreases in online violence, trained social service providers, and children trained on safe internet usage.[^10]

5. **CP System and Services** (3 outcomes): Strengthening the integrated child protection system by 2025; establishing a resilient system for disasters and shocks by 2027; increasing access to CP services through civil society involvement by 2026. Indicators track legislative frameworks reviewed and enacted, improvements in justice and healthcare addressing VAC, trained child protection professionals, and civil society organisations providing services.[^11]

**Key Observation:** The NAPVAC is methodologically the most sophisticated of the active plans, incorporating a theory of change, a socio-ecological model, and trauma-informed care principles. It is also the most explicit about the scale of the problem it addresses: 2,936 cases in 2023, with negligence and sexual abuse as the most common types.[^12] However, the plan's own introduction acknowledges that the predecessor NAP-PRVAC 2017 was never effectively implemented due to "lack of a systematic and holistic approach" and "difficulties in establishing collaborative efforts with stakeholders," and that "similar challenges were identified during the consultations conducted for the formulation of renewed NAP 2024-2028."[^13] This candid self-assessment raises the question of whether the structural barriers that defeated the 2017 plan have been sufficiently addressed to enable the 2024 plan to succeed.

The plan explicitly references UK child protection frameworks (the Children Act 1989, Section 47 investigations) as conceptual touchstones, continuing the pattern identified in Part IV of this encyclopedia wherein Maldivian child protection frameworks are substantially modelled on English systems without adequate contextualisation to the Maldivian operational reality – a reality characterised by 61 social workers serving 128,208 children across 189 inhabited islands.[^14]

#### 1.1.3 National Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP), 2022–2026

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full Title** | National Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP) |
| **Period** | 2022–2026 |
| **Authoring Body** | Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services (now MoSFD) |
| **Status** | Active |
| **Legal Basis** | Gender Equality Act 2016 (Act 18/2016), Article 42; National Gender Equality Policy (2019) |
| **Number of Policy Goals** | 5 |
| **Number of Strategies** | 20+ |
| **Key Implementing Agencies** | MoSFD (lead), Ministry of Health, Ministry of Economic Development and Trade, Ministry of Housing, Civil Service Commission, Ministry of Higher Education, Ministry of Sports, Elections Commission, Ministry of Finance, Family Protection Authority, Maldives Police Service, Prosecutor General's Office, Attorney General's Office, and 25+ additional agencies |
| **Target Population** | All citizens, with particular focus on women and girls |

**Scope Summary:** The GEAP derives directly from the Gender Equality Act 2016 and the National Gender Equality Policy (2019), translating them into operational strategies across five policy goals:

1. **Leadership and Governance** (4 strategies): Creating awareness to increase women's political participation; recognising shared parental responsibilities; reviewing electoral laws to address barriers to women's political leadership; adopting affirmative action measures for equal representation.[^15]

2. **Economic Empowerment** (4 strategies): Supporting programmes for economic empowerment of vulnerable women (including persons with disabilities, elderly, single mothers, GBV victims); addressing gender stereotyping; reviewing employment legislation and parental leave schemes; enforcing economic empowerment schemes across all sectors.[^16]

3. **Institutional Gender Mainstreaming** (7 strategies): Updating the MoSFD mandate; establishing inter-agency mechanisms for the Maldivian Gender Equality Model (MGEM); reviewing legislation for CEDAW and Gender Equality Act compliance; incorporating gender equality into the national curriculum; introducing gender-responsive budgeting; integrating gender training into civil service appraisal; and revising national data collection for sex and age disaggregation.[^17]

4. **Elimination of Gender-Based Violence** (3 strategies): Strengthening institutional and legislative frameworks for GBV/DV; creating support mechanisms for universal SRH rights education; strengthening health sector response to GBV aligned with the Domestic Violence Prevention Strategy.[^18]

5. **Access to Justice** (3 strategies): Creating awareness for legal empowerment of women; strengthening mechanisms for protection orders for GBV victims; ensuring equal participation of men and women as employees and decision-makers in the justice sector.[^19]

**Key Observation:** The GEAP is the most institutionally grounded of the action plans, as it is a statutory requirement under Article 42 of the Gender Equality Act. This gives it a legal mandate that other plans lack. However, the SAP light chapter on Women and Gender (2024) noted that the GEAP "is still in effect" but that the Gender Focal Point network and capacity-building infrastructure "remained incomplete" nearly six years after the Act's passage – and that the Terms of Reference for updating the gender training manual in Dhivehi were only issued in October 2022.[^20] The GEAP's dependence on 25+ implementing agencies, each with their own priorities and capacity constraints, makes coordination a critical vulnerability. The plan assigns extensive responsibilities across government but provides limited detail on accountability mechanisms for non-compliance.

#### 1.1.4 National Ageing Policy (Proposed Revision), 2024

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full Title** | Maldives National Ageing Policy (Proposed Draft – Revision of 2018 National Elderly Policy) |
| **Period** | Proposed (draft dated 28 August 2024, revised 9 September 2024) |
| **Authoring Body** | MoSFD (commissioned external consultant) |
| **Status** | Proposed / Under Revision |
| **Legal Basis** | No dedicated elderly law exists; general basis in Constitution Articles 17, 21, 23, 35; Pension Act 2009; Social Protection Act 2014 |
| **Number of Policy Areas** | 4 |
| **Number of Goals** | 14 |
| **Key Implementing Agencies** | MoSFD (coordinating body), Ministry of Health, NSPA, MPAO, local councils, Ministry of Housing, Ministry of Higher Education |
| **Target Population** | 43,000 persons aged 60+ (8.3% of population, 2023); projected to reach 34.1% by 2054 |

**Scope Summary:** The revised policy expands the 2018 National Elderly Policy, aligning with the Fourth Review and Appraisal of MIPAA in Asia-Pacific and the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. It is structured around four policy areas:

1. **Development, Prevention, Protection and Life-long Learning** (6 goals): Mainstreaming ageing in development planning; protection from discrimination, violence, and abuse; protecting rights through legislation; creating opportunities for knowledge transfer and lifelong learning; increasing research and data collection; developing communication strategies for social protection.[^21]

2. **Optimal Health and Well-being** (4 goals): Achieving healthy and active lifespan; enabling ageing-in-place through age-friendly decentralised communities; ensuring unhindered access to care; ensuring adequate emergency/crisis preparation for older persons.[^22]

3. **Enabling and Supportive Environment** (4 goals): Developing age-friendly communities with accessible infrastructure; supporting unpaid family caregivers (especially women); enabling digital empowerment; developing geriatric/gerontological competencies across all sectors.[^23]

4. **Income Security** (2 goals, added in 2024 revision): Ensuring sustainable and equitable income security mechanisms; establishing social protection and social security for caregivers of older persons.[^24]

**Key Observation:** The revised ageing policy is the only instrument in the current portfolio that is still in draft status. The situational analysis accompanying the draft identifies concerning trends: 46% of the elderly population has hypertension, 28% has diabetes, 45% has a disability, and the informal employment rate stands at 89.9% (with 99.1% for women), meaning the vast majority of the ageing population has no contributory pension entitlements beyond the universal basic pension.[^25] The draft calls for a "whole of government and community approach" but does not address the fundamental challenge identified in the government's own MIPAA survey – that "lack of overall political support" for ageing issues is "always" present, with "other pressing issues tak[ing] precedence when designing policies."[^26] The absence of a dedicated elderly law, which the policy itself acknowledges, means the policy has no statutory enforcement mechanism.

#### 1.1.5 Strategic Action Plan (SAP) Light Chapters – Social Sector (Shelved Draft)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full Title** | SAP Light Chapter Writeups – Women and Gender Equality; Persons with Disabilities; Child and Family Protection; Social Protection |
| **Period** | Was intended to align with government's medium-term planning cycle (successor to SAP 2019–2023) |
| **Authoring Body** | Government of Maldives / MoSFD input |
| **Status** | **Shelved** – drafting was initiated but the plan has been abandoned; it will not proceed to implementation. Retained here as a reference because it contains useful situational analysis and data. |
| **Legal Basis** | Government planning framework |
| **Number of Chapters** | 4 sub-chapters within the social sector |
| **Key Implementing Agencies** | MoSFD (lead), NSPA, Ministry of Health, Ministry of Homeland Security and Technology |

**Scope Summary:** The SAP light chapters were drafted as an overarching government planning framework within which the sector-specific action plans would sit. They cover:

- **Women and Gender Equality:** Medium-term direction focused on women's rights protection, economic and political empowerment, equal opportunity, healthcare access (including maternity/infertility through Aasandha), and addressing drug dependency among women.[^27]

- **Persons with Disabilities:** Medium-term outcomes centred on eliminating discrimination, fostering full participation, promoting independent living, and public-private sector collaboration for accessibility and inclusion. References NAPDI as the guiding document.[^28]

- **Child and Family Protection:** Outcomes include strengthening alternative care centres, developing a competent workforce, addressing juvenile offender rehabilitation and reintegration, and addressing children recruited by gangs. Notes the Ministry operates 19 FCSCs across the country.[^29]

- **Social Protection:** Outcome of a more rational social protection system with better-targeted benefits. NSPA statistics: 3,245 parents and 5,563 children benefiting from single parent allowances; 121 parents and 149 children from foster parent allowances; 12,374 people from disability allowance (as of 2023).[^30]

**Key Observation:** The SAP light chapters were shelved before completion – medium-term targets, headline indicators, and Phase 2 content were all marked as "To be developed in Phase 2" but this Phase 2 never materialised before the plan was abandoned. The shelving of the SAP means the Maldives currently lacks an overarching government planning framework within which the sector-specific action plans can be coordinated. This absence is significant: it means that the individual action plans (NAPDI, NAPVAC, GEAP, Ageing Policy) operate without a unifying medium-term strategy that would sequence priorities, allocate resources across sectors, and resolve inter-plan coordination challenges. The situational analysis and data contained in the SAP draft chapters remain valuable as reference material, even though the plan itself will not proceed.

### 1.3 Ministry Core Mandate and Subordinate Organisations

The MoSFD mandate document (January 2024) lists **39 core responsibilities** spanning policy establishment and implementation for women, children, elderly, persons with disabilities, and individuals requiring state care; gender mainstreaming; child protection systems; social protection programmes; psychosocial support; emergency preparedness; family strengthening; licensing of social service providers; research; human resource development; international treaty implementation; and management of the Children's Rights Protection Council and Disability Council.[^37]

The Ministry operates through the following subordinate institutions:[^38]

| Institution | Function |
|---|---|
| **National Social Protection Agency (NSPA)** | Administers disability allowance, single parent allowance, foster parent allowance, and other social protection benefits |
| **Family and Children Service Centres (FCSCs)** | 19 atoll-level centres providing frontline child and family protection services |
| **Child and Family Protection Service (CFPS)** | Centralised entity managing child protection across the atoll network |
| **Fiyavathi** | Residential care facilities for children in state care (post-COVID restructured into 9 smaller units) |
| **Amaanveshi** | Safe shelter facilities for GBV/DV survivors |
| **Special Care Centre** | For persons requiring special assistance |
| **Amaan Hiyaa** | Residential facility for girls aged 9–18 |
| **Vilingili Social Service Centre** | Island-level social service delivery |
| **Social Centres for Elderly** | Community-based elderly care |

The breadth of the mandate – 39 responsibilities spanning five vulnerable population groups, gender mainstreaming, family strengthening, emergency preparedness, licensing, research, and international treaty reporting – assigned to a single ministry with limited staff and fiscal resources, illustrates the breadth of mandate relative to available institutional capacity.

## Section 2: Evidence Cross-Reference – What the Research Says About Each Plan's Priorities

This section maps each active or proposed action plan's goals against the evidence base documented across the encyclopedia, assessing whether each plan addresses the right problems in the right way and identifying evidence gaps that the plans do not account for.

### 2.1 NAPDI v6 (Disability Inclusion) – Evidence Cross-Reference

#### Policy Goal 1: Good Health and Wellbeing

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Universal access to healthcare for PWDs | Census 2022 found 24,401 PWDs (7% of population) but only 13,656 registered in NDR (56%); the UNPRPD 2024 assessment found healthcare providers "infantilise adults with disabilities" and hold "commonly held belief... that medical conditions experienced by persons with disabilities are a direct result of their disability"[^39] | Partially – the goal correctly identifies physical accessibility and communication as barriers, but does not address attitudinal barriers among healthcare providers or the diagnostic bias documented in evidence | No data on health outcomes for PWDs; no measurement of diagnostic error rates; no baseline on accessibility compliance of health facilities |
| Social protection reform recognising disability diversity | Only 25.6% of PWDs receive disability allowance (Hameed et al., 2022); the allowance was rated as insufficient by all FGD participants; lowest coverage among women, older adults, those in Male', and those with sensory impairments[^40] | Yes – the strategy to "review and revise social protection and medical welfare policies to recognize the diversity of disabilities" directly addresses the documented one-size-fits-all problem | No cost modelling for expanded tiered benefits; no data on unmet needs by disability type |
| Early identification and intervention for children | SEN units in 87% of government schools by 2017, but many removed in effort to practise "inclusion" without providing reasonable accommodations; children with disabilities in school face bullying and exclusion from activities[^41] | Yes – early identification is a critical gap; however, the strategy focuses on tools and sensitisation rather than the structural capacity of the health and education systems to act on early identification findings | No prevalence data on specific childhood disabilities; no data on early intervention service capacity outside Male' |

#### Policy Goal 2: Quality and Inclusive Education

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inclusive primary and secondary education | Inclusive Education Policy updated 2021 but implementation poor; deaf students lack sign language interpreters; students with intellectual disabilities assigned to higher grades by age not ability; visual impairment students lack accessible materials; switch from Dhivehi to ASL sign language caused "major disruption"[^42] | Partially – the goal addresses curriculum review and educator capacity but does not confront the specific crises documented (sign language switch, removal of SEN units, lack of IEP implementation) | No data on educational attainment of students with disabilities; no tracking of transition rates from primary to secondary to tertiary |
| Access to tertiary education | Higher education institutions "remain largely inaccessible"; lecturers do not make accommodations; students asked to "find their own helpers during examinations"[^43] | Yes – but the strategy (review and assess opportunities) is diagnostic rather than prescriptive | No data on enrolment, retention, or completion rates for students with disabilities in higher education |

#### Policy Goal 3: Decent Work

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full employment and equal pay | Labour force participation: 41% for men with disabilities, 28% for women with disabilities; jobs created for PWDs are "tokenistic and lack concrete job descriptions"; teachers with disabilities "discouraged or forced to resign"[^44] | Partially – the plan addresses vocational training and workplace policies but does not confront the documented corruption and nepotism in hiring ("people with either political or family connections usually become hired for jobs") or the active discrimination against employees who develop disabilities | No data on employment rates by disability type; no wage comparison data; no data on workplace accommodations provided |

#### Policy Goal 4: Accessible Cities and Communities

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accessible housing and infrastructure | Most public buildings lack ramps, large doorways, or lifts; mosques not wheelchair-accessible; roads have limited wheelchair accessibility; "no supported living housing options" for independent living; social housing does not include disability considerations[^45] | Yes – but the Minimum Standards for Accessibility (2013/R-557) have narrow scope and poor compliance; the strategy to "enforce existing building codes" assumes codes are adequate when they are not | No systematic accessibility audit of public infrastructure; no data on housing adaptations funded or completed |
| Accessible transport | Buses not fully accessible; no accessible taxis (except MAPD vehicles); RTL ferries not fully accessible; wheelchair accessibility on speedboats impossible independently[^46] | Yes – but the strategy focuses on design improvement rather than enforcement or interim solutions for the current inaccessible fleet | No data on transport usage by PWDs; no cost estimates for accessibility retrofitting |

#### Policy Goal 5: Access to Justice

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Equal access to justice | Court proceedings involving children with disabilities conducted "without interpreters"; legal documents and proceedings largely inaccessible; 60% prevalence of sexual violence against children with disabilities (predominantly girls); inaccessible police reporting mechanisms[^47] | Yes – the plan correctly identifies courtroom accessibility and legal framework review as priorities | No data on case outcomes for PWDs in the justice system; no data on protection order applications by or for PWDs |

### 2.2 NAPVAC (Violence Against Children) – Evidence Cross-Reference

#### Strategic Area 1: Home (Safer Families)

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clear pathways for early detection and intervention | No established referral systems or case conferences between schools and CFPS; police checks and child protection training for school staff absent; care plans are "rare" and "regular review meetings are not common practice"[^48] | Yes – this is a primary operational gap in child protection | No data on time between first concern and first intervention; no mapping of existing informal identification pathways |
| Empowering parents for positive environments | Children are "both the primary reason women stay in violent relationships AND the primary reason they eventually leave"; reproductive coercion documented as a tool of control; overcrowded housing identified as a driver of violence and pressure[^49] | Partially – the parent empowerment frame assumes parents have agency they may not possess; the evidence shows many parents (primarily mothers) are themselves trapped in violent circumstances and cannot create safe environments without structural support (housing, income, legal protection) | No prevalence data on positive parenting practices; no baseline on parent help-seeking behaviour |
| Evidence-based policy strengthening | No national prevalence study of child maltreatment has ever been conducted; the primary DV study dates from 2007; no evaluation of the CSG/IBAMA volunteer model has been conducted[^50] | Yes – the goal to ground policy in evidence is essential, but the plan does not itself commission the missing studies | The entire evidence base for VAC prevalence in the Maldives is outdated or absent |

#### Strategic Area 2: School (Safer Educational Settings)

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Protecting children from bullying and violence in schools | Education Act 2020 banned corporal punishment in schools but no prohibition in home, alternative care, or penal settings; no mandatory child protection training for school staff; children with disabilities face bullying with no school action[^51] | Partially – the plan focuses on educator training and reporting but does not address the absence of police checks for persons working with children or the corporal punishment gap outside schools | No national data on school bullying prevalence; no data on teacher-perpetrated violence |

#### Strategic Area 3: Community (Safer Communities)

| Plan Goal | What the Evidence Shows | Does the Goal Address the Right Problem? | Evidence Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|

### Critical Gaps in International Obligation Coverage

The bidirectional mapping reveals several obligations for which **no action plan exists**:

1. **Juvenile justice** (CRC Art. 40, SDG 16): Despite five juveniles sentenced to death between 2013–2015, an 11.2% rehabilitation completion rate, and the non-abolition of judicial flogging for children, no action plan addresses juvenile justice reform.

2. **Child poverty** (CRC Art. 27, SDG 1): No plan addresses multidimensional child deprivation despite documented geographic disparities in access to education, health, and protection.

3. **Mental health** (SDG 3, CRPD Art. 25): No action plan addresses the mental health crisis despite documented gaps in services (emergency mental health care only at IGMH; no Mental Health Act).

4. **FGM/C elimination** (CRC, CEDAW): No plan specifically addresses criminalisation or elimination of FGM/C despite documented 13% prevalence.

5. **Geographic equity** (SDG 10): The fundamental Male'–atoll divide in service delivery is not addressed as a primary objective by any plan.

6. **Family law reform** (CEDAW Art. 16): The CEDAW reservation on Article 16 means the structural source of gender inequality in family law is explicitly excluded from reform commitments.

## Section 4: Coordination and Overlap Analysis

The multiplicity of action plans creates both opportunities for synergy and risks of fragmentation, duplication, and gaps. This section analyses the coordination architecture through four lenses: population coverage, mandate overlap, agency responsibility concentration, and temporal alignment.

### 4.1 Population Coverage Matrix

| **Informal workers** | – | – | Women in informal economy | Informal sector elderly | – | – | – | Social protection | – |

### 4.2 Gaps in Population Coverage

The matrix reveals several populations that fall outside or between the coverage of existing plans:

1. **Youth aged 18–24 not in education, employment, or training (NEET):** No plan provides comprehensive coverage for this group. Youth with disabilities are partially covered by NAPDI; youth at risk of radicalisation by NAP P/CVE; but the broader NEET population – which includes young people involved in gangs, substance use, and informal employment – has no dedicated action plan.

2. **Male victims of violence:** While the NAPVAC covers boys experiencing violence, and the DVPNS technically covers all DV victims regardless of gender, the evidence base and service architecture are overwhelmingly oriented toward female victims. Men experiencing domestic violence (18% of FPA caseload) have no targeted intervention framework.

3. **Migrants and foreign workers:** No plan addresses the social protection or vulnerability of the significant foreign worker population in the Maldives, despite documented concerns about exploitation in construction and domestic work sectors.

4. **Families in crisis (integrated):** Individual plans target specific vulnerabilities within families – child abuse, DV, disability, elderly care – but no plan provides an integrated family support framework that addresses the documented reality of compounding, intersecting vulnerabilities within the same household.

### 4.3 Mandate Overlap and Duplication

Several areas of significant overlap exist between plans:

**Violence against children and domestic violence:** The NAPVAC and the DVPNS 3rd both address violence within the home, with the NAPVAC focusing on children as victims and the DVPNS focusing on intimate partner violence. However, the evidence is unambiguous that these are not separate phenomena: children are "both the primary reason women stay in violent relationships AND the primary reason they eventually leave."[^71] The separation of child protection and DV response into distinct plans with distinct implementing structures risks fragmenting the response to what is functionally a single household crisis.

**Gender equality and disability:** The GEAP mentions women with disabilities as a vulnerable group requiring economic empowerment support. The NAPDI v6 includes gender-specific provisions and strategies addressing women with disabilities. However, the HRCM CRPD Shadow Report (2023) found that "feminist and women's rights discourses in Maldives do not adopt an intersectional lens and thus exclude women with disabilities in their advocacy."[^72] The two plans operate in parallel rather than in an integrated manner.

**Gender equality and child protection:** The GEAP addresses GBV elimination including violence against children; the NAPVAC addresses gender-equitable norms as part of its community strategy. The overlap is logical – gender norms drive both GBV and VAC – but the implementing structures are different, with FPA leading DV response and CFPS leading child protection, and no systematic integration between the two.

**Elderly care and disability:** The revised Ageing Policy notes that 45% of the elderly population has a disability, but the NAPDI and the Ageing Policy do not cross-reference each other or establish shared strategies for older persons with disabilities. The Special Care Centre at Guraidhoo houses both elderly and disabled residents, but this co-location has not been translated into coordinated policy.

### 4.4 Agency Responsibility Concentration

The following agencies carry responsibilities across multiple plans:

| Agency | Plans Assigning Responsibilities | Risk Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| **MoSFD** | All active plans (lead agency for NAPDI, NAPVAC, GEAP, Ageing Policy; implementing for all others). Was also designated lead for the now-shelved SAP light. | **Broad mandate concentration.** A single ministry with 39 mandated responsibilities is the lead implementing agency for every social policy action plan. The ministry's capacity to simultaneously drive disability inclusion, child protection, gender equality, elderly care, and social protection reform is shaped by the scale of this mandate relative to available staff and fiscal resources. |
| **NSPA** | NAPDI (disability allowance), Ageing Policy (pension/allowance interface); was also referenced in shelved SAP light (social protection) | **Moderate overlap.** NSPA administers the disability allowance, single parent allowance, and foster parent allowance, creating interaction points with multiple plans. |
| **FCSCs (19 centres)** | NAPVAC (frontline child protection), DVPNS (DV response), NAPDI (disability services at atoll level) | **High overlap and capacity constraint.** FCSCs are expected to deliver child protection, DV response, and disability services across the atolls with limited staff. The centres serve as the primary access point for multiple vulnerable populations but are resourced for none at the scale required. |
| **Ministry of Education** | NAPDI (inclusive education), NAPVAC (school safety), GEAP (gender in curriculum) | **Moderate overlap.** Education-related strategies in three plans assign responsibility to the Ministry of Education but without a coordinating mechanism to ensure coherent implementation. |
| **Ministry of Health** | NAPDI (healthcare access), RMNCAH, Health Master Plan, Ageing Policy (health and wellbeing) | **Moderate overlap.** Health-related strategies span disability, maternal health, ageing, and general health, but the ministry's planning is primarily driven by the Health Master Plan rather than the social sector action plans. |
| **Maldives Police Service** | NAPVAC (child protection response), DVPNS (DV response), GEAP (access to justice), NAP P/CVE | **Moderate overlap.** Police responsibilities in child protection, DV, gender-based crime, and counter-extremism are distributed across multiple plans without an integrated policing framework for vulnerable populations. |

### 4.5 Timeline Misalignment

The action plans currently in force or under development cover overlapping but non-aligned timeframes:

| Plan | Start | End | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Health Master Plan (3rd) | 2016 | 2025 | 10 years |
| RMNCAH Strategy | 2020 | 2025 | 5 years |
| NAP P/CVE | 2020 | 2024 | 4 years |
| GEAP | 2022 | 2026 | 5 years |
| DVPNS 3rd | 2023 | 2027 | 5 years |
| NAPDI v6 | 2024 | 2028 | 5 years |
| NAPVAC | 2024 | 2028 | 5 years |
| National Ageing Policy | 2024 | TBD (draft) | TBD |
| SAP Light (shelved) | N/A | N/A | N/A – plan abandoned before timeline was set |

The Health Master Plan and RMNCAH Strategy are in their final year (2025), meaning the health policy framework within which disability, gender, and ageing health goals must be pursued will expire before the social sector plans reach their midpoint. The NAP P/CVE has already lapsed (2024). The GEAP will expire in 2026, two years before the NAPDI and NAPVAC, creating a potential gender equality policy vacuum during the final years of those plans.

The shelving of the SAP light chapters means the Maldives has no overarching government social policy planning framework at all – only sector-specific plans operating independently. Combined with the National Ageing Policy's draft status, the coordination architecture that all other plans depend upon is fundamentally absent.

### 4.6 Coordination Mechanisms

The formal coordination mechanisms specified across the plans are:

- **NAPVAC:** Proposes a coordination mechanism for collaborative monitoring and evaluation but does not specify its structure or membership.[^73]
- **GEAP:** Assigns the Gender Focal Point network across ministries as the coordination mechanism, with MoSFD as lead. However, the network remained incomplete as of 2024.[^74]
- **NAPDI v6:** References the Disability Council as a monitoring body, but the Council has only one member with a disability and is widely assessed as "not adequate or effective."[^75]
- **National Ageing Policy:** Proposes the National Committee on Healthy Ageing and Rights of Elderly (established 2019) as the coordinating body, but the committee's monitoring has not occurred since the pandemic.[^76]

There is **no cross-plan coordination mechanism.** No inter-ministerial body is tasked with ensuring that the NAPDI, NAPVAC, GEAP, Ageing Policy, DVPNS, and other plans are implemented in a coordinated manner, resolve conflicts, eliminate duplication, or address the gaps identified in this analysis. Each plan has its own (often non-functional) coordination mechanism, but no supra-plan governance structure exists.

This absence is particularly consequential for populations that sit at the intersection of multiple plans – children with disabilities (NAPDI + NAPVAC), elderly women experiencing DV (Ageing Policy + DVPNS + GEAP), women with disabilities experiencing GBV (NAPDI + GEAP + DVPNS) – whose needs cannot be met by any single plan and require coordinated multi-plan implementation.

### 4.7 Structural Gaps in the Action Plan Architecture

The analysis reveals a fundamental structural problem in the Maldivian social policy architecture: **the action plans are designed as parallel vertical instruments for distinct population groups, but the populations they serve experience intersecting, compounding vulnerabilities that cannot be addressed through vertical delivery.**

A woman with a disability experiencing domestic violence on an outer island needs:
- The NAPDI's healthcare access and justice access provisions
- The DVPNS's protection order mechanisms and safe shelter access
- The GEAP's economic empowerment strategies and legal empowerment
- The social protection system's disability allowance and potentially single parent allowance
- The health system's mental health support (no plan)
- The housing system's accessible housing (no plan)

Each of these needs is addressed (partially) by a different plan, implemented by different agencies, coordinated (poorly) by different mechanisms, and funded (inadequately) from different budget lines. The woman on the outer island, however, encounters these systems – to the extent she encounters them at all – through a single entry point: the local FCSC, which is staffed by a small number of workers responsible for child protection, domestic violence response, disability services, and social protection referrals across an entire atoll.

The action plans describe what should happen. The institutional architecture determines what can happen. The gap between the two is the central challenge of Maldivian social policy implementation.

## Section 5: Indicators, Monitoring Gaps, and Baselines

### 5.1 The Indicator Problem Across Maldivian Action Plans

A recurring weakness across the Maldives' major action plans is the gap between stated objectives and measurable, monitorable indicators. Most plans articulate ambitious goals -- eliminating violence against children, achieving gender equality, ensuring disability rights -- but the indicator frameworks intended to track progress toward those goals are, with few exceptions, underdeveloped, lacking baselines, or disconnected from the data systems that would need to generate them. This section assesses the indicator quality and monitoring readiness of each active plan, drawing baseline data from across the encyclopedia.

### 5.2 National Action Plan on Violence Against Children (NAPVAC)

**Indicator quality.** NAPVAC's targets are defined in terms of reductions in violence prevalence and improvements in service response, but the plan relies on administrative data from CFPS and police reporting systems as its primary data sources. These are not population-level prevalence measures; they capture reported cases, which are a function of both actual prevalence and reporting behaviour. An increase in reported cases could indicate either worsening violence or improved reporting -- without a population-based survey, NAPVAC cannot distinguish between the two.[^1]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total child protection cases reported | 2,936 | CFPS administrative data | 2023 |
| Cases involving sexual abuse | ~30% of total | MCPD data | 2022 |
| Reporting channel: 1412 helpline | Active, statutory basis (CRPA Art. 76) | Rogers, Ali & Naeem (2025) | -- |
| Social worker caseloads | 160+ per worker | UNICEF (2022) | 2022 |
| Child protection workforce | 61 workers for 20 atolls | Part IV data | 2024 |
| Children living with one parent | 22% | Census 2022 | 2022 |

**Monitoring framework.** The Maldives Child Protection Database (MCPD), launched in 2016, is the primary monitoring tool. It links all 20 FCSCs to central CFPS and enables case tracking with unique identifiers. However, the MCPD's utility depends on overloaded social workers entering data promptly, and it does not integrate with police crime databases or NSPA's Social Protection Information System (SPIS).[^2] The result is a monitoring system that tracks individual cases in one silo but cannot generate the cross-sectoral view needed to assess whether NAPVAC's strategic objectives are being met.

**Missing data:**
- No population-based prevalence survey of violence against children since the 2009 DHS, which used indirect proxy measures rather than direct victimisation questions
- No outcome data on what happens to children after cases are closed -- whether re-abuse rates decline, whether educational outcomes improve
- No data on violence against children with disabilities, despite evidence of heightened vulnerability
- No systematic data on children in state care outcomes (educational attainment, employment, mental health) after ageing out

**SMART assessment.** NAPVAC's indicators are **partially specific** (they define categories of violence to track), **partially measurable** (administrative data exists but population data does not), **questionably achievable** (given workforce constraints), **relevant** (aligned to CRC obligations), but **weakly time-bound** (timelines exist but without resource-linked milestones).[^3]

### 5.3 National Action Plan for Disability Inclusion (NAPDI)

**Indicator quality.** NAPDI's indicators are structured around CRPD articles and cover accessibility, education, employment, health, and social protection. However, the plan relies on a disability classification system that uses a medical-model approach inconsistent with the CRPD's social model, creating a fundamental measurement problem: the indicators may not capture the experiences of persons whose disabilities would be recognised under international standards but fall outside the Maldivian registry's categories.[^4]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total PWDs registered (Census) | 24,401 (4.8% of population) | Census 2022 (WG-SS) | 2022 |
| PWDs receiving Disability Allowance | 25.6% of eligible | Hameed et al. (2022) | 2022 |
| Disability Allowance amount | MVR 2,000/month | NSPA data | 2024 |
| PWDs on National Disability Registry | ~6,000 | MSFD data | 2024 |
| Employment among PWDs | No systematic data | -- | -- |
| Accessible public buildings | No baseline audit completed | UNPRPD (2024) | 2024 |
| PWDs in education (mainstream) | Limited tracking | MoE data | -- |

**The registry gap.** The most significant monitoring weakness is the discrepancy between the Census 2022 figure of 24,401 persons with disabilities (using the Washington Group Short Set) and the approximately 6,000 persons on the National Disability Registry who receive the Disability Allowance. This four-fold difference means that roughly 75% of persons with disabilities are invisible to the primary administrative data system that NAPDI depends on for monitoring. Any indicator framework built on registry data alone will dramatically undercount the population it claims to serve.[^5]

**Missing data:**
- No central disability database accessible across government agencies
- No gender-disaggregated disability data across education, employment, and justice sectors
- No data on psychosocial or intellectual disability prevalence (the WG-SS methodology used in Census 2022 undercounts these categories)
- No accessibility audit baseline for public buildings, transport, or digital platforms
- No data on disability-related discrimination in custody proceedings, despite FGD evidence of such discrimination[^6]

**SMART assessment.** NAPDI's indicators are **specific** in their alignment to CRPD articles, but **not measurable** given the absence of a central database and the registry-Census gap. They are **aspirationally achievable** but without the budget allocations or institutional reforms needed to reach targets. Relevance is strong; time-binding is weak.

### 5.4 Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP)

**Indicator quality.** The GEAP operates through a steering committee housed in MoSFD and a Gender Focal Point network across public institutions, as mandated by the Gender Equality Act 2016. Its indicators span GBV prevalence, women's political participation, economic empowerment, and institutional compliance with gender mainstreaming requirements. However, many indicators are process-oriented (e.g., "number of trainings conducted") rather than outcome-oriented (e.g., "reduction in IPV prevalence").[^7]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| IPV prevalence (lifetime) | 1 in 3 women | Part V data; DHS 2016-17 | 2017 |
| Physical violence in marriage | ~20% | DHS 2016-17 | 2017 |
| Protection orders issued | Limited compliance data | Vulnerability mapping | 2024 |
| Women in Parliament | ~5% (2024 Majlis) | Elections data | 2024 |
| Women in island councils | Minimum 33% (quota) | Decentralisation Act amendments | 2021 |
| WDC budgets | Minimal, dependent on council allocation | Transparency Maldives (2015) | 2015 |
| Gender-responsive budgeting | No systematic implementation | ADB GESI (2025) | 2025 |
| GBV case data (FPA) | ~600 cases/year | FPA database | 2022 |

**Monitoring framework.** FPA maintains a GBV case management database that tracks reported cases, and MoSFD coordinates Gender Focal Points across institutions. However, there is no integrated monitoring framework that tracks the GEAP's full set of indicators across agencies. The Monthly Case Management Coordination Committee reconciles data between police, FPA, and CFPS on reported cases and outcomes, but this covers only the GBV reporting dimension, not the broader economic empowerment, education, or political participation pillars of the GEAP.[^8]

**Missing data:**
- No updated IPV prevalence survey since the 2016-17 DHS; a 2026 baseline would require a new survey
- No data on technology-facilitated abuse prevalence
- No comprehensive data on economic abuse (documented qualitatively in vulnerability mapping but not quantified)
- No tracking of protection order breach rates or enforcement timelines
- No gender-disaggregated budget analysis at the ministry level
- No data on legal aid access rates for women in family law matters

**SMART assessment.** The GEAP's indicators are **mixed in specificity** (some clear, others vague), **partially measurable** (GBV case data exists but prevalence data is outdated), **questionably achievable** without significantly increased resources for WDCs and the Gender Focal Point network. Relevance is strong given CEDAW obligations. Time-binding depends on the GEAP cycle, which has been inconsistently implemented.

### 5.5 National Ageing Policy and Plan of Action

**Indicator quality.** The National Elderly Policy (2018) and its associated Plan of Action articulate goals around health services, social participation, economic security, and care infrastructure. The indicators are directional rather than quantified -- the policy calls for "expanding" services and "developing" infrastructure without specifying targets, timelines, or measurable milestones for most actions.[^9]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Population aged 60+ | 8.3% (~42,000) | Census 2022 | 2022 |
| Projected 60+ population (2045) | 12.7% | UN population projections | -- |
| Basic Pension recipients | ~19,400 (65+) | MPAO data | 2023 |
| Basic Pension amount | MVR 5,000/month | Pension Act | 2023 |
| Bedridden home visit patients | 67 (Greater Male' only) | Part VII data | 2024 |
| Residential elderly care facilities | 1 (K. Guraidhoo) + Fiyavathi | Part VII data | 2024 |
| Geriatric specialists in-country | 0 | Part VII data | 2024 |
| Elderly abuse reporting | No systematic data collection | -- | -- |

**Monitoring framework.** No dedicated monitoring framework exists for the Ageing Policy. The MPAO tracks pension coverage and disbursement with precision, but this covers only one dimension of elderly wellbeing. There is no national registry of elderly persons, no standardised assessment of care needs, and no system for tracking health outcomes, social isolation, or abuse experienced by older persons.[^10]

**Missing data:**
- No national registry of persons aged 65+ with health status, living arrangements, and care needs
- No data on elder abuse prevalence
- No geriatric health service utilisation data (because geriatric services barely exist)
- No caregiver burden assessment
- No data on elderly housing needs or accessibility of existing housing stock
- No dementia prevalence estimates

**SMART assessment.** The Ageing Policy's indicators are **low in specificity** (aspirational rather than quantified), **largely unmeasurable** (data systems do not exist to track most goals), **currently not achievable** (zero geriatricians, one residential facility), **relevant** but **not time-bound** in any enforceable sense. This is the weakest indicator framework of the major plans.

### 5.6 National Mental Health Strategic Plan 2025-2029

**Indicator quality.** The Mental Health Strategic Plan includes more operational indicators than some other plans, in part because it can draw on health information systems. However, mental health data in the Maldives is fragmented: there is no national mental health information system collecting standardised diagnostic and service utilisation data across all health facilities.[^11]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Depression prevalence (youth 15-24) | ~19% | GYTS/GSHS data | 2019 |
| Suicidal ideation among adolescents | ~20% seriously considered | GSHS 2014 | 2014 |
| Psychiatrists (Maldivian-trained) | 0 | Part IX data | 2024 |
| IGMH psychiatry waitlist reduction | 90% (from 6+ months to weeks) | NCMH data | 2024 |
| Mental Health Centres at regional hospitals | 5 | Part IX data | 2024 |
| Telepsychiatry coverage | 6 atolls | NCMH data | 2024 |
| Mental Health Act | Not enacted | -- | 2024 |

**Missing data:**
- No national mental health prevalence survey at population level
- No standardised outcome data from Mental Health Centres
- No data on counselling workforce size, qualifications, or distribution
- No suicide surveillance system with standardised case definition

### 5.7 Drug Policy and NDA Action Plans

**Indicator quality.** Drug policy monitoring suffers from the most fragmented data environment of any social policy domain. Each institution -- NDA, police, customs, courts, corrections, health -- collects data in its own format for its own purposes. The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis identified this as a critical barrier to evidence-based policy.[^12]

**Available baselines:**

| Indicator | Baseline value | Source | Year |

**Critical gap:** No updated population-based drug prevalence survey has been conducted since 2011-12. Fourteen years without updated prevalence data means that all drug policy planning is based on guesswork or extrapolation from outdated figures.[^13]

## Section 6: Institutional Readiness Assessment

### 6.1 The Capacity-Mandate Gap

The central finding of this encyclopedia's analysis across all social policy domains is a persistent and widening gap between what laws and action plans require institutions to do and what those institutions can actually deliver. This section systematically assesses the institutional readiness of the key agencies assigned responsibility in the active action plans.

### 6.2 Ministry of Social and Family Development (MoSFD)

**Mandated responsibilities.** MoSFD is the lead or co-lead agency for NAPVAC, the GEAP, disability policy coordination, elderly care policy, and oversight of all FCSCs. It chairs or co-chairs the National Domestic Violence Coordinating Committee, the GEAP Steering Committee, and the Social Sector Coordination Committee. It is the primary coordinating body for the CFW recommendations.[^15]

**Staffing capacity.**
- The child protection workforce consists of approximately 61 social workers across 20 atoll-level FCSCs and central CFPS, giving an average of roughly three workers per atoll serving populations spread across multiple islands.[^16]
- Average caseloads exceed 160 per worker -- at least three times international safe-practice standards of 15-25 cases per qualified worker.[^17]
- The ministry has experienced chronic difficulty recruiting and retaining qualified social workers in atoll postings. The first cohort of trained social workers emerged only in 2006-2007; the full four-year BSW programme at MNU has never produced graduates in sufficient numbers to staff the system.[^18]
- Workers are generalists handling child protection, domestic violence, elderly welfare, disability services, and family disputes simultaneously, with no specialisation possible given staffing levels.[^19]

**Geographic coverage.** MoSFD has a physical presence on only 20 of approximately 199 inhabited islands (the atoll capitals where FCSCs are located). The remaining 179 islands depend on community-based mechanisms (IBAMA groups, CSGs) whose quality varies enormously. An FCSC social worker may be responsible for 10-15 islands, some accessible only by multi-hour boat journeys that become impossible during rough weather.[^20]

**Budget.** No publicly available data exists on MoSFD's total operational budget as a proportion of government expenditure. However, the UNPRPD (2024) Situational Analysis noted that most state institutions lack specific budget allocations for disability inclusion, and budgetary constraints force institutions to "prioritise financing basic functions within their mandate and disregard inclusivity and accessibility measures."[^21] The ministry's budget must cover all four mandate areas -- child protection, gender equality, disability, and elderly care -- with no indication that any recent budget cycle has materially increased allocations.

**Data systems.** MoSFD operates the MCPD for child protection case management, which is its most developed data system. The MCPD enables case tracking across FCSCs and continuity when families move between islands. However, it does not integrate with police crime databases, NSPA's SPIS, or FPA's GBV database, limiting cross-sectoral case management.[^22]

**Coordination mechanisms.** The ministry chairs multiple inter-agency committees, but coordination "relies heavily on informal relationships between individual officers rather than institutionalised protocols, and the effectiveness of inter-agency work varies significantly depending on the personalities in post and the political priorities of any given administration."[^23]

**Readiness verdict: Significant resource investment needed.** MoSFD leads on virtually every social policy action plan. Delivering on NAPVAC, GEAP, NAPDI, the Ageing Policy, and the CFW recommendations simultaneously would require, at minimum, a tripling of the social work workforce, dedicated budget lines for each mandate area, and integrated data systems — investments that would enable the ministry to fulfil the breadth of its mandate effectively.

### 6.3 National Social Protection Agency (NSPA)

**Mandated responsibilities.** NSPA administers Aasandha, the Disability Allowance, the Single Parent Allowance, the Foster Care Allowance, and the Food Assistance Programme. It manages SPIS and is developing a Single Registry of poor and vulnerable households.[^24]

**Coverage performance.**
- Aasandha: Universal nominal coverage, but 49% of health expenditure remains out-of-pocket, indicating significant gaps in effective coverage[^25]
- Disability Allowance: Only 25.6% of eligible PWDs receive it; lowest coverage among women, older adults, those in Male', and those with sensory impairments[^26]
- Single Parent Allowance: Reaches only those who can navigate the application process -- documentation requirements and administrative engagement barriers exclude many vulnerable women[^27]
- Social Registry: A pilot was conducted in 2019-2020 with World Bank support but has not been rolled out nationally[^28]

**Technical capacity.** NSPA has invested in digital systems through the OneGov platform, which allows cross-referencing of Zakat House records with NSPA databases. The SPIS represents one of the more functional data systems in the social sector, with links to the Department of National Registration's ID database for deduplication. However, the Single Registry -- the tool needed for poverty-targeted intervention -- remains at pilot stage after six years.[^29]

**Readiness verdict: Moderate administrative capacity but significant coverage gaps.** NSPA can process applications and disburse benefits, but the system is failing to reach three-quarters of persons with disabilities and an unknown proportion of eligible single parents. Expanding NAPDI's ambitions or the Ageing Policy's goals through NSPA will require addressing the application barriers that currently exclude the most vulnerable.

### 6.4 Family Protection Authority (FPA)

**Mandated responsibilities.** FPA is the semi-autonomous body responsible for GBV prevention, protection, and response. It operates the GBV case management database and coordinates with police and CFPS on case handling.[^30]

**Capacity assessment.** FPA maintains a GBV database and participates in the monthly Case Management Coordination Committee. However, its operational reach beyond Male' is limited. Protection order enforcement is the weakest link in the GBV response chain: vulnerability mapping research documented protection orders being breached with no consequences, perpetrators locating victims through informal networks, and eight-month delays in police evidence collection allowing cases to collapse.[^31]

**Readiness verdict: Functional in Male', limited in atolls.** FPA's monitoring and coordination functions are operational but largely Male'-centric. Delivering on the GEAP's GBV reduction goals and the CFW recommendations on protection order enforcement would require FPA to have operational capacity across all atolls -- capacity it currently lacks.

### 6.5 Maldives Police Service -- Family and Child Protection Department

**Mandated responsibilities.** The FCPW is responsible for investigating domestic violence, child abuse, and sexual offences. Under the MPS Strategic Plan 2024-2028, domestic violence is a strategic priority alongside organised crime, drug trafficking, and terrorism.[^32]

**Capacity assessment.**
- The FCPW maintains a database tracking offenders with prior DV or child abuse allegations
- An MoU with MoSFD provides for information sharing on GBV and child cases
- However, research documents significant inconsistency in practice: police treating DV as a "personal issue," month-long response delays, confidentiality breaches that endangered victims, and failure to share protection order information with Corrections[^33]
- Sensitisation training has been conducted but is not systematically embedded in the Police Academy curriculum or reinforced through regular refreshers

**Readiness verdict: Institutional will exists but frontline practice is inconsistent.** The MPS Strategic Plan identifies the right priorities. However, delivering on the CFW recommendations for evidence-led prosecution, trauma-informed policing, and rapid protection order enforcement would require systematic training rollout, accountability mechanisms for individual officers, and integration of DV response into performance evaluation. These changes require sustained institutional commitment over years, not one-off training workshops.

### 6.6 National Drug Agency (NDA)

**Mandated responsibilities.** NDA oversees demand reduction, treatment, and rehabilitation for substance use disorders. It operates Drug Rehabilitation Centres (DRCs) and coordinates with the Drug Court on mandated treatment.[^34]

**Capacity assessment.**
- Treatment centres operate below capacity while waiting lists grow, due to administrative bottlenecks and eligibility restrictions
- Counsellors are under-qualified and unsupported, with a World Bank evaluation (2010) finding "inadequate professional monitoring and training"
- Aftercare and community reintegration services are non-existent
- Treatment capacity is concentrated in the Male' region, with minimal services in atolls
- No systematic outcome data: it is unknown what proportion of DRC graduates achieve sustained recovery[^35]

**Readiness verdict: Structurally unable to meet current demand, let alone expanded plans.** NDA's treatment system operates with fundamental deficiencies identified 15 years ago that remain unresolved. Drug policy action plans that assume an expanded, evidence-based treatment system must first address the existing system's documented failures.

### 6.7 Judiciary -- Family Court and Magistrate Courts

**Mandated responsibilities.** The Family Court and magistrate courts handle divorce, custody, maintenance, protection orders, and child protection judicial orders (Interim Assessment Orders, Protection Orders, Supervision Orders under CRPA Articles 80, 90, 93).[^36]

**Capacity assessment.**
- Family Court in Male' handles the majority of family law matters, with magistrate courts on outer islands handling local cases
- Magistrate court judges typically lack specialised family law or trauma-informed training
- Case tracking systems are fragmented: key information is stored in scanned forms, disconnected spreadsheets, or separate databases
- Child support payments take 30-45 days to process through the system
- No integrated case management system (ICMS) covering family cases across the judiciary[^37]

**Readiness verdict: Capable of processing cases but lacking specialised capacity and data systems.** The CFW recommendations call for mediation pathways, custody weaponisation protocols, case tracking with status visibility, and child support payment reform. Implementing these would require significant judicial modernisation, training, and IT investment.

### 6.8 Coordination Across Plans

Beyond individual agencies, the most significant institutional readiness gap is coordination. A family facing multiple vulnerabilities -- domestic violence, substance abuse, disability, poverty -- must interact with MoSFD, NSPA, FPA, police, NDA, the judiciary, health services, and potentially Zakat House, with no single case manager coordinating across agencies. Integration relies on informal relationships rather than institutional protocols.[^38]

The OneGov platform represents the most promising coordination mechanism, potentially enabling a citizen to access all benefits through a single portal. But OneGov is a digital access layer, not a case management system. It can simplify applications but cannot replace the inter-agency coordination, joint case conferences, and shared outcome accountability that genuine integrated service delivery requires.

### 6.9 Summary: Institutional Readiness Scorecard

| Institution | Staffing | Geographic reach | Budget | Data systems | Coordination | Overall readiness |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MoSFD/CFPS | Critical shortage | 20 of 199 islands | Inadequate | MCPD (siloed) | Informal | **Low** |
| NSPA | Adequate for admin | Nationwide (digital) | Moderate | SPIS (functional) | OneGov developing | **Moderate** |
| FPA | Limited | Male'-centric | Limited | GBV database | Monthly committee | **Low-Moderate** |
| Police FCPW | Variable | Nationwide but inconsistent | Unknown | Offender database | MoU with MoSFD | **Low-Moderate** |
| NDA | Critical shortage | Male'-centric | Inadequate | Fragmented | Siloed from CFPS | **Low** |
| Judiciary | Functional | Nationwide | Unknown | Fragmented | Case-by-case | **Moderate** |
| LGA/Councils | Highly variable | 199 islands | Highly variable | None | IBAMA (inconsistent) | **Variable** |

The overarching conclusion is that no institution currently has the capacity to fully deliver on the action plans assigned to it. Partial delivery is possible -- and is happening -- but the gap between plan ambitions and institutional capacity is structural, not incidental. Closing it requires sustained multi-year investment in workforce, systems, and institutional reform, not simply writing better plans.[^39]

## Section 7: Historical Policy Lessons -- What to Revive, What to Avoid

### 7.1 The Pattern of Policy Cycles

The Maldives' social policy history, documented in Part I of this encyclopedia, reveals a recurring pattern: policies are introduced with genuine ambition, operate effectively for a period, then lose momentum as administrations change, champion officials rotate out, or donor funding cycles end. This pattern means that the country has repeatedly developed institutional knowledge, materials, procedures, and trained personnel that subsequently lapsed -- creating a reservoir of prior investment that, in some cases, could be revived rather than rebuilt from scratch.

### 7.2 Programs Worth Reviving

**Community Social Groups (CSGs) and the original IBAMA model.** The CSG concept, initiated in 2010 and revisited in 2016 with UNICEF support, represented a genuinely innovative approach to community-based social protection. When functioning well, CSGs brought together school counsellors, health workers, and community representatives to develop multi-sectoral support plans for vulnerable families at the island level. The model was revived under IBAMA, which was given legal standing under the CRPA 2019 and the Decentralisation Act. However, activity levels vary enormously across islands, and many groups exist in name only.[^40]

**What to revive:** The formal structure already has legal recognition. The task is not to redesign IBAMA but to invest in making the existing model work consistently. This means dedicated funding for community coordinators on each island, regular training and support from FCSCs, simple reporting tools, and accountability mechanisms that track whether groups are actually convening and acting. The CFW recommendations on community volunteer training (R47) align directly with reviving this model.

**The 2006 Child Protection Procedure Manual.** Based on England's "Working Together" guidance and developed with UNICEF technical support, this was the Maldives' first standardised child protection procedure manual. Rogers, Ali and Naeem (2025) document that it was developed under a needs-based framework through UNICEF technical assistance, though it created "cultural tensions with local practices." Subsequent revisions have occurred, but the basic procedural architecture remains relevant.[^41]

**What to revive:** The manual's core framework -- structured assessment, referral pathways, inter-agency protocols -- remains sound. What is needed is not replacement but contextualised updating: integrating Maldivian cultural dynamics, addressing the small-island confidentiality challenges, and adding protocols for technology-facilitated abuse, which did not exist in 2006.

**The Narcotics Control Board's early demand-reduction work.** Before the shift toward criminalisation that characterised the 2000s-2010s drug policy, early institutional responses included community-based awareness and youth engagement programmes. The 2008 Drug Control Master Plan developed with UNODC support provided a strategic framework that balanced supply reduction with demand reduction and harm reduction. Elements of this balanced approach were lost as policy shifted toward incarceration -- a shift that has resulted in 47% of prisoners being drug offenders without reducing drug use.[^42]

**What to revive:** The public health orientation of early demand-reduction work, including community-based prevention, voluntary treatment pathways, and aftercare services. The 2021 NDA/WHO Situational Analysis essentially recommends returning to the principles of the 2008 Master Plan with updated evidence.

**The Rights Side of Life baseline survey series.** HRCM, with UNDP and FPA support, conducted baseline human rights surveys in 2005, 2011, and 2019, examining public attitudes, awareness, and perceptions on human rights issues. This longitudinal data source is invaluable but was not systematically linked to action plan monitoring.[^43]

**What to revive:** Commission a fourth wave (due around 2027) with expanded coverage of GBV attitudes, disability rights perceptions, and digital rights awareness, and formally integrate its findings as baseline data for the GEAP and NAPDI monitoring frameworks.

### 7.3 Programs That Addressed Issues Now Re-emerging

**The Presidential Commission on Child Rights Violations (2020).** Established in response to public outrage over child abuse cases, this commission conducted joint audits of cases involving CFPS, police FCPW, and HRCM, identifying coordination lapses that the National Council for the Protection of the Rights of the Child (NCPRC) should have prevented. The commission's findings -- particularly on inter-agency coordination failures -- remain directly relevant to NAPVAC implementation.[^44]

**Early women's crisis response.** Before formal shelters existed, community-based responses to women fleeing violence relied on informal networks -- families, neighbours, and mosques providing temporary accommodation. The CFW recommendations on emergency safe accommodation (R26) and transition support (R27) would benefit from understanding how these informal systems worked, what made them effective, and what their limitations were, rather than designing shelter models entirely from international templates.

### 7.4 Past Failures to Learn From

**The implementation-without-capacity pattern.** The Maldives has repeatedly enacted laws -- the DVPA (2012), the CRPA (2019), the Gender Equality Act (2016), the Juvenile Justice Act (2019) -- that impose duties on agencies without ensuring those agencies have the capacity to fulfil them. The CRPA requires court orders within five days of taking a child into state care; the DVPA requires mandatory response to all DV reports and counselling for all perpetrators. These mandates were "designed for a system with significantly more human resources than currently exist."[^45]

**Lesson:** Future action plans must include a capacity-matching assessment: before assigning new duties to an agency, verify that the agency has (or will be given) the staffing, budget, and training to deliver. The CFW recommendation framework is notably stronger in this regard, specifying which stakeholders should do what and identifying the resources needed.

**The training-without-retention cycle.** Multiple training programmes have been delivered across sectors -- police sensitisation, teacher awareness, health worker screening -- but without systematic reinforcement, refresher training, or integration into institutional incentives. Training delivered to individual officers has limited impact if those officers transfer to different roles, retire, or face no accountability for applying what they learned.[^46]

**Lesson:** Training investment must be embedded in institutional systems: integrated into academy curricula, linked to performance evaluation, reinforced through regular refreshers, and supported by SOPs that make the trained behaviour the default process.

**Social housing concentration.** The Hulhumale' Hiya Flats development concentrated vulnerable populations in one area, creating what the CFW research documented as an environment where "vulnerable youth were often recruited into drugs or crime" and where "dysfunctional families in a fishbowl environment led to high rates of youth suicidal ideation and mental health incidents."[^47]

**Lesson:** The CFW recommendations on mixed-allocation social housing (R29) directly respond to this failure. Future housing policy must distribute social housing allocations across mixed-income developments rather than concentrating vulnerability. This lesson is already documented and its solution clearly articulated; what remains is implementation.

**The project-dependent model.** Many social policy innovations in the Maldives have been funded through time-limited donor projects -- UNICEF child protection programming, World Bank social registry pilot, ADB gender initiatives. When project funding ends, the innovation often atrophies. The pilot social registry, for example, was conducted in 2019-2020 with World Bank support but has not rolled out nationally in the six years since.[^48]

**Lesson:** Sustainability must be designed into programmes from inception. Any action plan item funded by donor projects should include a transition-to-government-budget plan with a specific timeline and committed budget line. The CFW stakeholder commitments model, which envisions distributed delivery across 39 stakeholder groups rather than dependence on a single funding source, represents a more resilient approach.

### 7.5 Proposed but Never Implemented Policies

Several significant policy proposals remain unimplemented:

1. **Mental Health Act:** Proposed since at least the National Mental Health Policy 2015-2025 cycle, a comprehensive Mental Health Act would codify patient rights, regulate involuntary treatment, and provide anti-discrimination protections. It has not been enacted as of 2026.[^49]
2. **Elderly Rights and Protection Act:** In draft since at least 2021, this would establish specific legal protections for the elderly, mandatory reporting of elder abuse, and care standards. Not yet passed.[^50]
3. **FGM/C criminalisation:** The CRC Committee has repeatedly recommended criminalisation of female genital mutilation/cutting. It has not been legislated despite being identified as a priority in both child protection and gender equality frameworks.[^51]
4. **Comprehensive prohibition of corporal punishment:** The CRC Committee has called for prohibition in all settings including the home. Not fully implemented, and judicial flogging of children remains technically possible under the Juvenile Justice Act.[^52]
5. **National Social Registry:** Piloted but not scaled. A functioning social registry would transform the targeting capacity of all social protection programmes.[^53]
6. **Regional women's shelters:** Plans for shelters outside Male' have been discussed for years but none had been established at the time of writing.[^54]

These represent a substantial backlog of ready-to-implement (or nearly ready) reforms. A five-year action plan should prioritise clearing this backlog as an early deliverable.

## Section 8: Condensed Five-Year Next-Step Action Plans (2026-2031)

### 8.1 Synthesis Methodology

This section synthesises recommendations from four sources into a single integrated five-year plan:

1. **CFW Recommendations (49 recommendations across 8 areas):** The Child and Family Wellbeing vulnerability mapping research produced the most comprehensive set of evidence-based recommendations, covering protection before marriage (R1-R4), protection against vulnerability factors (R5-R12), responding to abuse (R13-R22), easing divorce and post-divorce vulnerabilities (R23-R28), protective social housing (R29-R33), legal and procedural reforms (R34-R40), perpetrator accountability (R41-R44), and building a non-revictimising society (R45-R49).[^55]

2. **CFW Stakeholder Commitments (39 stakeholder groups):** A participatory commitments framework identifying specific actions for government agencies, international organisations, NGOs, the private sector, councils, communities, and individuals.[^56]

3. **Unmet goals from current action plans:** NAPVAC, NAPDI, GEAP, and the Ageing Policy all contain goals that remain unrealised and require continued or renewed effort.

4. **Encyclopedia Part recommendations:** Each thematic Part (IV through XIII) concludes with evidence-based recommendations synthesised from the research literature.

The plan is structured in four phases, with each action item specifying the action, lead agency, timeline, resources needed, monitoring indicator, and the source recommendation it responds to.

### 8.6 Cross-Cutting Requirements

Several requirements cut across all phases and should be treated as ongoing rather than time-limited:

**1. Budget transparency and allocation.** Every action item above requires resources. The Maldives' social protection spending was estimated at 3.5% of GDP in 2012 (ADB SPI). Delivering on the five-year plan would require increasing this allocation, but without transparent budget data disaggregated by social policy function, it is impossible to hold government accountable for resource commitments. The first cross-cutting requirement is therefore to publish annual budget data by social policy programme area.[^57]

**2. Geographic equity.** Nearly every action plan fails on geographic coverage. The default mode of social policy delivery in the Maldives is Male'-centric, with atoll and island populations receiving degraded or no services. The five-year plan must include explicit geographic equity targets for every service expansion item -- not just "nationwide" but specifying which atolls and islands will be reached in which year.[^58]

**3. Political continuity.** The Maldives has changed its social ministry's name, structure, and portfolio with every change of administration. Action plans that span election cycles risk losing momentum when political transitions occur. The five-year plan should seek formal multi-party endorsement through parliamentary committee engagement and embed key commitments in legislation or regulation that survives administration changes.[^59]

**4. Civil society sustainability.** NGOs such as Family Legal Clinic and Hope for Women provide services that the state currently cannot deliver. Their survival depends on donor funding that fluctuates year to year. Government should develop a sustainable co-funding model -- annual grants, service contracts, or dedicated budget lines -- for CSOs that deliver frontline services identified in this plan.[^60]

**5. Community engagement.** The CFW stakeholder commitments framework identifies 39 stakeholder groups who can contribute. This distributed model -- where resorts, banks, telecoms, councils, WDCs, religious leaders, and individual professionals each contribute what they can -- is the only realistic way to achieve nationwide coverage in an archipelago of 199 inhabited islands where no single institution has universal reach.[^61]

### 8.8 Accountability Framework

The five-year plan should be monitored through the following mechanisms:

1. **Quarterly progress reports** to the Social Sector Coordination Committee at Deputy Minister level, tracking each action item against its timeline and indicator
2. **Annual Social Policy Scorecard** (Action 3.13), published publicly, tracking key indicators across all domains
3. **Mid-term review** (end of Year 2) by an independent evaluator assessing progress, identifying blockages, and recommending course corrections
4. **End-of-cycle evaluation** (Action 4.13) providing the evidence base for the next planning cycle
5. **Parliamentary engagement**: annual briefings to the relevant Majlis committee on progress, using Parliamentary questions as an accountability mechanism (per CFW stakeholder commitment for the People's Majlis)
6. **Civil society monitoring**: engagement of HRCM, the Children's Ombudsperson, and civic oversight organisations in independent monitoring and social auditing

The central lesson of this chapter is that the Maldives does not lack plans, policies, or recommendations. What it lacks is the institutional capacity, data infrastructure, budget allocation, and sustained political commitment to implement them. This five-year plan is designed to address those gaps directly -- not by adding more policy ambition, but by building the implementation machinery that would make existing ambitions achievable.[^63]
