# Literature Review

This appendix provides a comprehensive annotated bibliography of every source document consulted in the preparation of this encyclopedia. Unlike a conventional bibliography that merely describes what each document contains, each entry here reports **what the document actually found** – the specific statistics, conclusions, policy gaps, and recommendations that make each source valuable to researchers and policymakers. Sources are organised thematically; where a document spans multiple domains, it is placed under its primary category with cross-references noted.

## 1. Child Protection and Child Rights

### Law on the Protection of the Rights of Children (Law No. 9/91)

**Citation:** Government of Maldives. (2019). *Law No: 9/91 – Law on the Protection of the Rights of Children* [English translation]. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Law 9/91 was the Maldives' first legislative acknowledgment of children's rights, enacted in the same year the country ratified the Convention on the Rights of the Child (11 February 1991). However, the law was **largely declaratory in nature**: it set out broad principles covering child health, maternal care, education, treatment of children with disabilities, rehabilitation of juvenile delinquents (with preference for rehabilitation over punishment under Shari'ah), and protection of orphans, but it contained **no definitions of child abuse or maltreatment, no mandatory reporting requirements, and no provisions for state intervention in families** where children were at risk. In practical terms, Law 9/91 served primarily as a symbolic gesture of compliance with CRC obligations rather than a functional piece of child protection legislation. It remained the sole child protection statute for **28 years** until being superseded by the Child Rights Protection Act 2019.

**Methodology:** Legislative text; English translation of the original Dhivehi statute produced in 2019 to make the law accessible to international audiences.

**Context:** Enacted in the same year as CRC ratification, the law was driven by international engagement dynamics rather than domestic advocacy. It predated any substantive institutional response – the Unit for the Rights of Children was not established until 1992.

**Relevance:** Core statutory reference establishing the legal baseline against which the 28-year implementation gap is measured. The contrast between Law 9/91's declaratory provisions and the CRPA 2019's operational framework illustrates the evolution of the Maldivian child protection system from symbolic compliance to substantive legislation.

### Special Provisions Act to Deal with Child Sex Abuse Offenders (Act No. 12/2009)

**Citation:** HRCM / Attorney General's Office. (2009). *Act number 12/2009 – Special Provisions Act to Deal with Child Sex Abuse Offenders: English Translation*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The Act defined specific sexual offences against children with imprisonment terms of **7 to 14 years**, significantly strengthening criminal sanctions beyond the general Penal Code. It **lowered evidentiary standards** for prosecution of child sex abuse cases, mandated **public disclosure of offender identities**, and provided for **victim compensation**. The Act was enacted in a context where population-based survey data found that **15% of adolescents** reported having experienced sexual abuse (with rates among girls approximately double those of boys), and that **nearly one-third of secondary school girls** reported sexual abuse. Hospital data from Indira Gandhi Memorial Hospital (IGMH) for 2005–2009 recorded 43 rape cases, of which **22 involved minors**, and **almost half of the cases involving minors were gang-rapes**. Despite its strengthened provisions, the Act's implementation has been hampered by limited forensic capacity for child sexual abuse investigation outside Male', the absence of a dedicated child sexual abuse investigation unit, and minimal victim support services.

**Methodology:** Legislative text with English translation.

**Context:** Enacted against a backdrop of growing awareness of the scale of child sexual abuse, driven by data from the 2007 Women's Health and Life Experiences study (which found childhood sexual abuse prevalence of 12.2% overall and 16.3% in Male') and escalating hospital presentations.

**Relevance:** Key statute in the child protection legal framework; its implementation effectiveness remains a recurring concern in CRC reviews and NGO shadow reports. The Act's provisions on offender disclosure and reduced evidentiary standards represented significant legal innovations in the Maldivian context.

### Number of Reported Child Abuse Cases (under 18) by Type, 2017–2022

**Citation:** Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services. (2023). *Table 8.60 – Number of Reported Child Abuse Cases (under 18) by type, 2017–2022*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The data reveals that **sexual abuse consistently constituted the largest single category** of reported child abuse cases across the six-year period, followed by physical abuse and neglect. The Ministry reported **349 child abuse cases in the three-month period March–May 2018 alone**, of which **48% involved sexual abuse or physical violence**. This represents an average of approximately 116 reported cases per month – a figure that captures only cases reported to the Ministry and excludes cases reported to police, handled informally within communities, or never disclosed. The introduction of **cyberbullying as a new category** in more recent years reflects the growing recognition of online harms to children in a society with high mobile phone and internet penetration. By 2023, the Ministry was receiving **2,936 cases annually**, of which **1,278 (43.5%) were violence against children cases**, with negligence and sexual abuse as the most common types.

**Methodology:** Administrative data compiled from the Ministry's case management records across six years, disaggregated by type of maltreatment.

**Context:** The data represents cases entering the formal system; actual prevalence is substantially higher given pervasive under-reporting driven by community surveillance norms, fear of retaliation, and the social proximity of small island communities that suppresses disclosure.

**Relevance:** Essential quantitative baseline for tracking child protection system responsiveness and the evolving profile of child maltreatment in the Maldives.

### 2009 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives Country Profile

**Citation:** U.S. Department of Labor. (2010). *2009 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives Country Profile*. Bureau of International Labor Affairs.

**Key Findings:** Found that children in the Maldives were exploited in the worst forms of child labour primarily as **domestic servants and in forced prostitution**. Critically, the assessment documented that **no compulsory education law existed** in 2009, leaving children without legal protection from labour exploitation. The report identified a **lack of government coordination mechanisms** for combating child labour and recommended legal reforms establishing compulsory education, prohibiting child labour, and creating enforcement infrastructure. Children – particularly girls – sent to relatives in Male' for education were found to be vulnerable to coerced domestic work, where the line between age-appropriate household chores and exploitation was culturally contested.

**Methodology:** Annual country assessment using standardised U.S. Department of Labor international criteria, drawing on government data, NGO reports, and international monitoring.

**Context:** External evaluation providing an international benchmark. The absence of compulsory education at the time made the Maldives an outlier in the region.

**Relevance:** Documents the legal vacuum that existed before the Education Act 2020 and Child Rights Protection Act 2019, providing the baseline against which subsequent reforms are measured.

### 2018 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives

**Citation:** U.S. Department of Labor, Bureau of International Labor Affairs. (2018). *2018 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives*. U.S. Department of Labor.

**Key Findings:** Rated the Maldives as making only **"minimal advancement"** in eliminating the worst forms of child labour – the second-lowest rating on a four-tier scale. Found that children continued to be engaged in **forced domestic labour, commercial sexual exploitation, and drug trafficking**. The involvement of children in drug trafficking was linked to gang violence: gang leaders recruited minors for distribution activities **in part because juveniles faced less severe penalties than adults**. The rating reflected significant gaps in both the legal framework and enforcement despite legislative progress since 2009.

**Methodology:** Annual assessment using standardised international criteria; rates country progress on a four-tier scale from "no advancement" to "significant advancement."

**Context:** The "minimal advancement" rating came despite nine years of policy development since the 2009 assessment, highlighting the persistent gap between legislative intent and enforcement.

**Relevance:** The low rating provided international pressure for the legislative reforms that followed in 2019–2020 (CRPA and Education Act).

### 2020 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives Advancement Profile

**Citation:** U.S. Department of Labor. (2020). *2020 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor: Maldives Advancement Profile*. Bureau of International Labor Affairs.

**Key Findings:** Upgraded the Maldives from "minimal" to **"moderate advancement"**, reflecting the enactment of the Child Rights Protection Act 2019 (which criminalised child labour below age 16) and the Education Act 2020 (which established compulsory education until age 16). However, the assessment identified **persistent gaps** including the lack of hazardous work identification for children, continued evidence of children in forced domestic labour and sexual exploitation, and insufficient enforcement capacity. The upgrade demonstrated the measurable impact of legislative reform on international assessments while underscoring the distance remaining.

**Methodology:** Annual assessment using standardised criteria; comparative evaluation against prior year.

**Context:** The timing – immediately after the CRPA 2019 and Education Act 2020 – allowed the assessment to capture the legislative progress while noting implementation shortfalls.

**Relevance:** Documents the direct impact of the 2019 legislative reforms on international assessments and maps the remaining implementation agenda.

### Maldives National Child Protection Policy (Ministry of Education, 2017)

**Citation:** Ministry of Education. (2017). *Maldives National Child Protection Policy*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Established the school-based tier of the national child protection response, defining types of abuse (emotional, physical, sexual, neglect), specifying responsibilities including the **appointment of child protection liaison officers in schools**, and establishing reporting and referral protocols aligned with the UNCRC. However, the policy's operationalisation revealed critical gaps: **no established referral system or case conference mechanism existed between schools and CFPS**; teachers designated as focal points **lacked clear protocols** for referring concerns, receiving feedback on referrals, or participating in multi-agency planning; **police checks and child protection training for school staff were absent**; and no formal vetting system existed for persons working with children in educational settings. The policy was introduced in the context of the 2015 establishment of child protection focal points in schools, but without the inter-agency infrastructure to make them effective.

**Methodology:** Policy document developed by the Ministry of Education for the school system.

**Context:** Schools are critical sites for identification of child maltreatment – children spend substantial portions of their day in school, and teachers are often the first adults outside the family to observe signs of abuse. The policy recognised this but could not resolve the institutional gaps that prevented schools from functioning as an effective tier of the protection system.

**Relevance:** Documents both the aspiration and the implementation failure of school-based child protection – a gap that persists despite the Education Act 2020's requirements.

### Child Abuse in Male' City of Maldives

**Citation:** Adam, A. (2017). Child Abuse in Male' City of Maldives. *International Journal of Economic Research*, Serials Publications Pvt. Ltd.

**Key Findings:** Documented a specific vulnerability pattern: **relatives' children brought to Male' for education were often forced into domestic work** – performing cooking, cleaning, and childcare duties in exchange for accommodation rather than receiving the educational access that was the stated purpose of the arrangement. These children occupied a liminal status – family members rather than employees – making their exploitation difficult to identify and categorise within formal child labour or child abuse frameworks. The study found that **physical and sexual abuse were the most common forms** of child abuse in Male', and that the overcrowded living conditions of the capital – with multiple families sharing small apartments – created heightened risks of sexual abuse through co-habitation of children with unrelated adult males, including stepfathers, maternal partners, and male tenants. The privacy deficit inherent in overcrowded housing meant that children lacked physical spaces of safety within the home.

**Methodology:** Academic journal article using secondary data analysis from multiple government agencies.

**Context:** Male' is one of the most densely populated cities in the world, with its unique dynamics of internal migration for education creating child protection risks not found in other settings.

**Relevance:** Identifies the intersection of housing pressure, internal migration, and child abuse that is distinctive to the Maldivian context – a vulnerability pattern that has no simple policy solution because it is embedded in the kinship structures that are otherwise a source of social cohesion.

### Child Participation in the Maldives: An Assessment of Knowledge

**Citation:** Naseem, A. (2011). *Child participation in the Maldives: An Assessment of Knowledge*. UNICEF / HRCM.

**Key Findings:** Found **wide gaps between legislative instruments and the implementation of children's participation rights** under the UNCRC. Formal mechanisms for child consultation in decision-making were **limited**. Focus groups with children and discussions with parents and teachers revealed that participation was understood primarily in terms of children's involvement in school activities rather than their meaningful engagement in decisions affecting their lives, communities, or policies. The assessment found that participation rights were the **most neglected dimension** of the CRC in the Maldives, overshadowed by the more visible protection and provision domains.

**Methodology:** Mixed-methods assessment combining legal review with qualitative fieldwork (child focus groups and parent/teacher discussions), commissioned by UNICEF and HRCM.

**Context:** The participation dimension of child rights is often overlooked in favour of protection-focused analysis. This assessment is one of the few documents specifically examining Article 12 (right to be heard) implementation in the Maldives.

**Relevance:** Documents the participation deficit in Maldivian child rights implementation, providing evidence for advocacy around the "third pillar" of the CRC beyond protection and provision.

### Child Rights Symposium 2022: Summary of Presentations

**Citation:** Children's Ombudsperson's Office / UNICEF. (2022). *Child Rights Symposium 2022: Summary of Presentations*. UNICEF Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The symposium, co-hosted by the Children's Ombudsperson (established under the CRPA 2019) and UNICEF, focused on **promoting inter-agency coordination for child protection** – reflecting the persistent coordination gaps documented throughout the child protection system. Presentations included child rights audits across atolls and assessments of CRPA implementation. The symposium represented the first major public review of implementation progress since the CRPA's enactment, providing a multi-stakeholder forum for identifying gaps including the shortage of trained social workers, the absence of care plans for children in state care, and the fragmentation of services across islands.

**Methodology:** Conference proceedings capturing multi-stakeholder perspectives on child protection implementation challenges.

**Context:** The symposium marked the beginning of the institutional oversight function of the Children's Ombudsperson – an office created by the CRPA with authority to investigate complaints, monitor the child protection system, and advocate for children's rights.

**Relevance:** Provides insight into how different government and civil society actors perceive progress and gaps three years after the CRPA's enactment.

### Leave No One Behind – Children in Maldives: Using Census 2022

**Citation:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics / UNICEF. (2022). *Leave no one behind – Children in Maldives: Using Census 2022*. UNICEF Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Using 2022 Census microdata with an equity lens, the analysis found that **128,208 children** lived in the Maldives. Children faced compounding disadvantages: households with more than one child per adult were **more than twice as likely to be poor** (12% vs. 4.9%); households headed by individuals without primary education faced a poverty rate of **12.6%** and a deprivation rate of **39.9% in Male'** – nearly eight times the rate when the head had primary education or above. Having a household head with tertiary education was associated with poverty rates approximately **30 times lower** than when the head had not completed primary school. Children in atoll communities faced particular risks from limited secondary education facilities, health service gaps, and water and sanitation deficits.

**Methodology:** Statistical analysis using national Census 2022 microdata, applying an equity lens to identify children being left behind across demographic and geographic categories.

**Context:** Builds on the earlier 2014 Census analysis to provide the most current picture of child welfare disparities.

**Relevance:** Provides the definitive census-based evidence on child welfare disparities, enabling targeted policy responses for the most disadvantaged child populations.

### Children in Maldives: Analysis of Children from Census 2014

**Citation:** National Bureau of Statistics / UNICEF. (2015). *Children in Maldives: Analysis of Children in Maldives from Census 2014*. UNICEF Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The first detailed demographic profile of the child population found that **74% of children aged 0–17 lived with both parents**, while **22% lived with one parent**. This finding carried significant implications for child vulnerability given the Maldives' high divorce rate and the documented correlation between single-parent households and economic stress. The analysis provided disaggregated data by age, sex, atoll, and household composition, establishing the 2014 baseline for tracking longitudinal changes.

**Methodology:** Statistical analysis based on 2014 Population and Housing Census data, disaggregated across key demographic and geographic categories.

**Context:** The one-parent household rate of 22% intersects with the Maldives' documented inadequacy of maintenance enforcement and post-divorce economic vulnerability documented in vulnerability mapping research.

**Relevance:** Essential for tracking changes in child demographic indicators between the 2014 and 2022 census periods.

### Corporal Punishment of Children: UPR Briefing and Country Report (2015)

**Citation:** Owen, S. (2015). *Corporal Punishment of Children in the Maldives: Briefing for the Universal Periodic Review, 22nd Session, 2015*. Global Initiative to End All Corporal Punishment of Children; and Global Initiative to End All Corporal Punishment of Children. (2015). *Corporal Punishment of Children in the Maldives*. Country Report.

**Key Findings:** Documented that corporal punishment of children remained **lawful in all settings** in the Maldives – the home, schools, care settings, and as a criminal sentence – despite government commitments to prohibition. The 2014 Penal Code draft explicitly **authorised parental use of force**. The Juvenile Justice Act 2019 subsequently raised the age of criminal responsibility from 10 to 15 and prohibited the death penalty for juveniles (between 2013 and 2015, **five juveniles had been sentenced to death**), but **did not abolish judicial flogging** for juveniles – leaving open the possibility that children convicted of certain offences, particularly *zina* (sexual intercourse outside marriage), could be subjected to corporal punishment by court order. The Education Act 2020 subsequently banned corporal punishment in schools, but the home setting remains unaddressed.

**Methodology:** Legal mapping by a specialised international organisation examining each setting (home, school, care, penal) separately; UPR briefing using standardised international advocacy format.

**Context:** The persistence of lawful corporal punishment in the home reflects the tension between international child rights norms and cultural/religious practices around family discipline – a tension unresolved by the CRPA 2019.

**Relevance:** Highlights a critical gap in child protection that intersects with cultural norms and religious law, providing the legal context against which subsequent reforms can be evaluated.

### Moving from Aspirations to Impact: NGO Shadow Report on CRC Maldives

**Citation:** Hama Jamiyya / Care Society. (2006). *Moving from Aspirations to Impact: NGO Shadow Report on CRC Maldives*. UNICEF-funded.

**Key Findings:** Documented **significant gaps between the government's CRC reporting and the lived realities of children**, particularly in areas of child abuse, juvenile justice, and children's participation in decision-making. The shadow report covered implementation measures, definitions of the child, non-discrimination, civil rights and freedoms, and family environment – providing an independent counterpoint to the government's combined first and second CRC periodic state report. The title itself – "Moving from Aspirations to Impact" – captured what would become the defining theme of Maldivian child protection over the subsequent two decades: a persistent gap between policy aspiration and operational delivery.

**Methodology:** Civil society shadow report combining desk review with stakeholder consultations, submitted to the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child.

**Context:** Produced at a time when the Unit for the Rights of Children operated with minimal staff, no formal case management procedures, limited geographical reach beyond Male', and no legal mandate to investigate child protection cases.

**Relevance:** Historical baseline documenting civil society perceptions of child rights implementation in the mid-2000s, before the democratic transition and the major legislative reforms of 2019.

### Combined 6th and 7th Periodic Report under CRC

**Citation:** Government of the Republic of Maldives. (2021). *Combined 6th and 7th Periodic Report submitted under the Convention on the Rights of the Child*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The 59-page report provided the first comprehensive government account of CRC implementation since the democratic transition, covering developments across all CRC articles including legislation (particularly the CRPA 2019 and Juvenile Justice Act 2019), non-discrimination, violence against children, disability, education, and juvenile justice. The report documented the establishment of the Children's Ombudsperson, the child helpline 1412, the Maldives Child Protection Database connecting all 19 FCSCs, and the raising of the age of criminal responsibility from 10 to 15. It also acknowledged continuing gaps in inter-agency coordination, geographic reach of services, and the challenge of implementing international child protection frameworks across 189 inhabited islands.

**Methodology:** State party report under the CRC treaty reporting cycle, compiled by government with inputs from multiple ministries and agencies.

**Context:** The report was supplemented by the UNICEF Situation Analysis 2021 and the Child-Equity-Census-2022 analysis, which provided disaggregated data on child wellbeing indicators.

**Relevance:** Primary government document on child rights implementation; essential baseline for comparing government claims with independent assessments from UNICEF, civil society shadow reports, and the Rogers, Ali & Naeem (2025) systems analysis.

### Maldives: The Position of Girls and Young Women – FGM and Prostitution

**Citation:** Adjei-Danso, A., Tomacek, K., Leo, T., & Shareef, S. (2015). *Maldives: The Position of Girls & Young Women in Society: A Look at Issues of FGM and Prostitution of Girls*. Impact Litigation Project, American University Washington College of Law.

**Key Findings:** Examined FGM/C and child prostitution in the Maldives through an intersectional analysis of Islamic law and international human rights standards. Found that FGM/C persisted despite limited systematic prevalence data, and that the legal framework – while criminalising sexual exploitation – contained gaps in enforcement and implementation. The report analysed health impacts and effects on women's lives, noting that the framing of certain practices as religious or cultural complicated identification, reporting, and institutional response.

**Methodology:** Legal academic shadow report prepared for CRC review, combining legal analysis with empirical evidence on health impacts.

**Context:** Addresses two of the most sensitive child protection issues in the Maldivian context within a framework that bridges Islamic law and international human rights law.

**Relevance:** One of the few documents addressing FGM/C in the Maldives with detailed legal analysis, contributing to a persistently under-researched area.

### A Maldivian Perspective on Juvenile Justice

**Citation:** Aboobakuru, Z. N. (2017). *A Maldivian Perspective on Juvenile Justice*. Juvenile Court, Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Provided an insider perspective on the Juvenile Court's jurisdiction (established 1997), documenting the intersection of Islamic Shari'ah sentencing practices with modern juvenile justice standards. Found that **drug offences were the primary driver of juvenile contact with the justice system**, with arrests increasing from 16 in 2001 to 79 in 2006 and **164 (29% of total juvenile arrests) in 2007**. The rehabilitation completion rate was alarmingly low: between 2010 and 2015, **89 juveniles were enrolled in rehabilitation programmes** but only **11.2% successfully completed** them – reflecting inadequate programme design, absence of community-based follow-up, the pull of gang networks, and limited employment opportunities for justice-involved youth.

**Methodology:** Institutional paper by the Juvenile Court providing operational perspective.

**Context:** Written two years before the Juvenile Justice Act 2019 reformed the system. The paper documents the pre-reform reality against which the 2019 Act's innovations can be assessed.

**Relevance:** Essential for understanding the intersection of Islamic legal tradition, gang violence, drug trafficking, and modern juvenile justice in the Maldivian context.

### Situation Analysis of Children and Youth in the Maldives (2021)

**Citation:** UNICEF Maldives Country Office. (2021). *Situation Analysis of Children and Youth in the Maldives*. UNICEF Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Comprehensive assessment finding that counselling and mental health support services for children **needed to be strengthened** and that **youth and adolescent health services – in and out of school – were a key gap**. The analysis covered country context (poverty, inequality, structural vulnerabilities, SIDS challenges), education (including COVID-19's impact on schooling, ICT/online learning gaps), health and wellbeing (early childhood development, communicable and non-communicable diseases, childhood immunisation), and protection. Stakeholder consultations identified the absence of structured support networks in schools and the shortage of qualified counsellors as critical concerns.

**Methodology:** Mixed methods including quantitative data analysis, literature review, and stakeholder consultations.

**Context:** Produced during the COVID-19 pandemic, the analysis captured both pre-existing structural gaps and pandemic-amplified vulnerabilities. Provides the 2021 baseline from which the 2025 update measures progress.

**Relevance:** The comprehensive UNICEF baseline integrating child welfare across education, health, and protection domains.

### Situation Analysis of Children and Adolescents in the Republic of Maldives (2025)

**Citation:** Haarr, R. (2025). *Situation Analysis of Children and Adolescents in the Republic of Maldives*. UNICEF Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The most methodologically sophisticated child-focused assessment available, employing equity, trend, causal, PESTEL, and capacity gap analyses with stakeholder consultations and foresight/scenario planning. Confirmed that child protection operated through **61 social workers serving 128,208 children across 189 inhabited islands**, with average caseloads exceeding **160 cases per worker**. The analysis documented that **19.9% of students had seriously considered attempting suicide** (GSHS 2009 data), that **18.8% of young people aged 15–29 were classified as depressed** (STEP Survey 2021), and that police recorded **37 attempted suicide cases involving child victims** between January 2021 and September 2024, with 35 of 37 cases involving children aged 11–17. Mortality trends, immunisation coverage, nutrition, early childhood development, adolescent health, violence against children, and TVET were all assessed with updated data.

**Methodology:** Advanced analytical frameworks including PESTEL and foresight/scenario planning, drawing on Census 2022, HIES 2019, STEP Survey 2021, GSHS 2009, and extensive stakeholder consultations.

**Context:** Supersedes the 2021 SitAn with updated data and expanded methodology. The "Building Futures" companion report focuses on securing child rights and responding to the adolescent challenge.

**Relevance:** The definitive current reference on the situation of children and adolescents in the Maldives. Sets the evidence base for child-focused policy and programming through the next CPD cycle.

### Child Protection Systems Analysis (Rogers, Ali & Naeem, 2025)

**Citation:** Rogers, Ali & Naeem. (2025). *Child Protection Systems in the Maldives* [OCR text from original]. Unpublished.

**Key Findings:** The most operationally detailed analysis of how child protection actually functions at the frontline level. Documented four primary case management pathways: children witnessing domestic violence; children experiencing direct abuse; children in custody conflicts (where children are routinely **"used as leverage in custody disputes"**); and children of stressed single parents at risk of neglect. Found that **care plans were rare** – children entering state care frequently lacked documented plans; **regular review meetings were not common practice**; complex politics permeated child protection activities – cases involving families of political actors were **"leaked to media before being reported"** through proper channels, while cases involving perpetrators in positions of power (e.g., **"the father is a gang leader or drug trafficker"**) were not reported due to fear of reprisal. **Social workers received death threats** in the course of their work (Human Rights Watch 2023). The Ministry operated in only **20 of 189 inhabited islands**, leaving the vast majority of communities without direct access to child protection services.

**Methodology:** Systems-level analysis combining policy review with operational assessment, providing granular procedural detail.

**Context:** Sets the depth standard for the encyclopedia. Draws on case files, practitioner interviews, and institutional data to document the gap between the CRPA 2019's provisions and frontline reality.

**Relevance:** The single most operationally detailed source on how child protection actually functions in the Maldives. Essential reading for anyone designing interventions or reforms.

## 2. Gender, GBV, and Women's Empowerment

### The Maldives Study on Women's Health and Life Experiences (2007)

**Citation:** Fulu, E. (2007). *The Maldives Study on Women's Health and Life Experiences*. Ministry of Gender and Family, Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Using WHO multi-country study methodology, this study documented significant rates of physical and sexual violence against women in the Maldives. The study found a childhood sexual abuse prevalence of **12.2% overall and 16.3% in Male'** – indicating that the capital, despite its greater concentration of services, had a higher prevalence than the national average, possibly reflecting overcrowding, transient populations, or greater willingness to disclose in anonymous surveys. It established the empirical basis for the Domestic Violence Prevention Act that followed in 2012. The study remains the **only methodologically rigorous national prevalence study** on intimate partner violence in the Maldives – nearly two decades after its publication, no follow-up national prevalence study has been completed, despite the Family Protection Authority commissioning terms of reference for one in 2019.

**Methodology:** Population-based prevalence study using WHO multi-country study methodology – the international gold standard for IPV prevalence measurement.

**Context:** Commissioned by the Ministry of Gender and Family with UNFPA support at a time when no specific domestic violence legislation existed and the issue had received minimal policy attention.

**Relevance:** The foundational IPV prevalence study for the Maldives. Its age is itself a critical finding – the absence of updated prevalence data means nearly two decades of legislative and institutional reform have occurred without systematic re-measurement of whether prevalence has changed. The HRCM has explicitly flagged this as a gap requiring urgent attention.

### In-depth Study on All Forms of Violence against Women: Contributions from the Maldives (2004)

**Citation:** Fulu, E. (2004). *In-depth study on all forms of violence against women: Contributions from the Maldives*. Ministry of Gender, Family Development and Social Security.

**Key Findings:** Documented the prevalence and nature of GBV and domestic violence in the Maldives at a time when **no formal victim services existed**, no specific domestic violence legislation was in force, and the issue had received minimal public or policy attention. The study provided the earliest systematic documentation of GBV at the national level and established the evidence base that informed subsequent legislative development.

**Methodology:** Government contribution to the UN Secretary-General's in-depth study on all forms of violence against women.

**Context:** Historical baseline document showing the state of GBV awareness and response before any dedicated legislation existed.

**Relevance:** Provides the pre-reform baseline – a picture of the Maldives before the DVPA 2012, the Sexual Offences Act 2014, and the Gender Equality Act 2016.

### Gender-Based Violence During COVID-19 Pandemic in the Maldives

**Citation:** Institute of Research and Development Pvt Ltd / UNFPA. (n.d.). *Gender-Based Violence During COVID-19 Pandemic in the Maldives: An Analysis of Reported Cases*. UNFPA Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Documented how pandemic lockdowns **concentrated victims with their abusers** in enclosed living spaces, eliminating the informal safety mechanisms – visiting family, going to work, seeking refuge at a neighbour's house – that women in abusive relationships routinely rely upon. A rapid livelihood assessment found that **44% of respondents reported redundancy or complete loss of income**, with significant impact on lower-paying service categories where women are concentrated. The study found that the GBV Sub-Cluster activated under the Health Emergency Operations Centre – comprising FPA, CFPS, police, and NGOs – successfully coordinated a **24/7 hotline and safe transport for DV victims** when public movement was restricted. Administrative data showed FPA caseloads had risen sharply from **19 cases in 2013** to **642 in 2016** and **539 in 2017**, with women consistently accounting for approximately **82%** of those reporting. The pandemic period represented a "shadow pandemic" of intensified GBV.

**Methodology:** Multi-agency case data analysis combining administrative data from the Family Protection Authority, Maldives Police Service, and Prosecutor General's Office.

**Context:** Commissioned during the pandemic to build an evidence-based, survivor-centred response roadmap.

**Relevance:** Documents both the intensification of GBV during COVID-19 and the systemic weaknesses in the protection response exposed by crisis conditions. The GBV Sub-Cluster coordination model represents a potential template for future emergency responses.

### Human Rights & Gender Review of GBV-Related Laws in the Maldives

**Citation:** McCabe, C. (2023). *Human Rights & Gender Review of Gender-Based Violence Related Laws in the Maldives*. UNFPA Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Comprehensive legal analysis of **seven national laws** relevant to GBV, examining physical violence (domestic violence, corporal punishment, torture), sexual violence (rape, marital rape, sexual assault, trafficking, harassment, child/forced marriage, FGM, reproductive rights violations), psychological violence (harassment, coercion), and economic harm (dowries, polygamy, divorce). Found that the **DVPA 2012 did not recognise or criminalise marital rape** – encoding in statute the classical *fiqh* position that sexual availability is a wife's duty. The Sexual Offences Act 2014 subsequently introduced **limited criminalisation of marital rape** in certain circumstances, but the applicability and practical benefit of its provisions to victims remained uncertain. The review also identified that religiously framed practices, including *ruqya* (exorcism), provided a context in which sexual violence against women and girls occurred – where the framing of abuse as spiritual care complicated identification and reporting.

**Methodology:** Commissioned legal analysis examining seven distinct national laws against international human rights standards.

**Context:** The single most comprehensive legal mapping of GBV-related legislation available for the Maldives.

**Relevance:** Indispensable for understanding the full legislative architecture governing GBV, including the critical marital rape gap and the intersection of religious practice with violence.

### Understanding Gender in Maldives: Toward Inclusive Development

**Citation:** El-Horr, J., & Pande, R. P. (2016). *Understanding Gender in Maldives: Toward Inclusive Development*. World Bank Group.

**Key Findings:** Documented the paradox at the heart of Maldivian gender relations: women's educational gains were **not translating into equivalent labour market outcomes or political voice**. Found that women's labour force participation was less than half that of men (**44% versus 80%**) despite near-perfect educational parity – the Global Gender Gap Report 2013 ranked the Maldives **first globally for educational attainment** but **99th for economic participation** and **101st for political empowerment** (score of 0.089). The analysis identified care responsibilities, geographic isolation, social expectations, and the tourism sector's gendered employment model (predominantly men in resort settings separated from family life) as structural barriers preventing educational achievement from converting into economic and political equality.

**Methodology:** World Bank analytical work using mixed methods including quantitative data analysis and qualitative research.

**Context:** The most authoritative single-volume gender analysis for the Maldives.

**Relevance:** Provides the analytical framework within which sector-specific gender issues are situated. The finding that educational equality coexists with economic and political inequality points to structural barriers that cannot be resolved through education alone.

### Maldives Gender Gap Index Country Profile (2013)

**Citation:** World Economic Forum. (2013). *The Global Gender Gap Report 2013 – Maldives Country Profile*. WEF.

**Key Findings:** Ranked the Maldives **97th out of 136 countries** with a composite score of **0.660**. The disaggregated scores reveal the Maldivian gender paradox in its starkest form: **Rank 1** globally for educational attainment parity (near-perfect score), but **Rank 99** for economic participation and opportunity, and **Rank 101** for political empowerment (score of **0.089** – meaning women held barely 9% of political power by WEF's composite measure).

**Methodology:** Standardised global index using comparable cross-country methodology across four sub-indices.

**Context:** Enables international benchmarking of the Maldives against 135 other countries.

**Relevance:** The most frequently cited evidence for the Maldivian gender paradox – a nation that educates its women equally but excludes them from economic and political life.

### Research Papers on the Situation of Women in Maldives

**Citation:** Abdullah, F., Azfa, A., Abdulghafoor, H., & Shafeega, F. (2018). *Research Papers on the Situation of Women in Maldives*. British High Commission Sri Lanka / UNRRA / UN Women.

**Key Findings:** The compilation's most significant contribution is the paper applying the **Musawah framework** to the Maldivian Muslim family law context. This research found that laws grounded in *qiwamah* and *wilayah* concepts **"reinforce unequal gendered power relations in marriage and society,"** undermine women's full and equal citizenship, and **"perpetuate gender stereotypes"** – consequences demonstrably inconsistent with the egalitarian values the Quran itself emphasises in the family context. The research observed that women's voices **"were silenced by the time the *fiqh* schools emerged, early in the history of Islam."** The compilation also documented **reproductive coercion as a specific form of GBV**: case workers described men deliberately impregnating women who were trying to leave, understanding that pregnancy would deepen dependence and narrow options. Women described in-laws throwing away their food while eating, mocking disabilities, and exercising control over household decisions. The paper on recently divorced women in Greater Male' found that **the majority were unaware of their legal rights** before or during marriage, learning about maintenance entitlements and custody provisions only after divorce – or not at all.

**Methodology:** Multi-paper compilation covering economic, legal, religious, and family law dimensions, using qualitative research methods including in-depth interviews with vulnerable women and expert caseworkers.

**Context:** Uniquely addresses Islamic jurisprudential arguments for gender equality applied specifically to the Maldivian family law context.

**Relevance:** The Musawah paper provides the only detailed analysis of how Islamic equality arguments apply to Maldivian family law – essential for any reform advocacy that must engage with religious discourse.

### Qualitative Assessment: Perceptions about Women's Participation in Public Life

**Citation:** Abdulghafoor, H., Thayyib, I., & Naadhaa, M. (2015). *Qualitative Assessment: Perceptions about Women's Participation in Public Life in the Maldives*. IFES.

**Key Findings:** Based on **131 interviews from 7 locations** across the Maldives, found that women were societally positioned primarily in the domestic sphere, with the **double burden of work, household and childcare** limiting participation in community activities. Despite this, women expressed **strong aspirations for gender equality**. The study documented how perceptions of appropriate gender roles constrained women's political engagement and candidacy – a constraint that operated not primarily through formal legal barriers but through social expectations that were deeply internalised by both men and women.

**Methodology:** Multi-site qualitative study using a large sample (131 interviewees across 7 locations) for qualitative research.

**Context:** Conducted by IFES (International Foundation for Electoral Systems), bringing election and governance expertise to gender analysis.

**Relevance:** Provides the most detailed qualitative evidence on social and cultural barriers to women's political participation – barriers that legislative reform alone cannot address.

### Assessment of Women's Development Committees in the Maldives

**Citation:** Hamid, M. T., Shareef, A., & Shiuna, M. (2015). *Assessment of Women's Development Committees in the Maldives*. Transparency Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Found **significant variation in WDC functioning** across the country. While some committees actively advocated for women's needs, others were largely inactive. Key findings included: WDC members **frequently lacked training** in governance, advocacy, and project management; financial resources were **minimal and dependent on ad hoc allocations** from island councils; relationships between WDCs and island councils varied from constructive collaboration to marginalisation; and successes where they occurred were **personality-driven rather than institutionally sustained**. The 2019 amendments to the Decentralisation Act subsequently formalised WDCs, but the structural weaknesses identified in 2015 – limited budgets, unclear mandates, inadequate training – persist.

**Methodology:** Institutional assessment by Transparency Maldives examining WDC role, effectiveness, and challenges across the country.

**Context:** WDCs represent the primary institutional mechanism for women's participation in local governance; their effectiveness determines whether gender policy reaches island communities.

**Relevance:** Documents the gap between the formal structure of grassroots gender machinery and its actual functioning – a gap that the Decentralisation Act amendments have not fully addressed.

### Gender-sensitive Tax Policy Analysis: the Maldives

**Citation:** Shareef, A. H. (2022). *Gender-sensitive Tax Policy Analysis: the Maldives*. USAID PFM Project / Ministry of Finance.

**Key Findings:** Found that while the Maldivian tax system does not contain **explicit gender discrimination**, **implicit biases** arise from the differential economic positions of men and women. Specifically, the **regressive impact of GST** disproportionately affects lower-income individuals, who are disproportionately women. The analysis examined the legal framework for gender equality alongside tax structure, rates, unit of taxation, and GST incidence across different demographic groups, producing policy recommendations for more gender-equitable taxation.

**Methodology:** Technical fiscal analysis applying a gender lens to taxation policy, commissioned by USAID through the Ministry of Finance.

**Context:** One of the few analyses anywhere in South Asia examining how economic policy instruments affect men and women differently in a small island state context.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that gender inequality operates through ostensibly neutral economic instruments – not only through family law or labour market discrimination.

**Context:** The HRCM's mandate as an A-status national human rights institution gives its assessments particular authority in international treaty body proceedings.

**Relevance:** Provides the authoritative independent assessment of gender equality implementation, distinct from both government reporting and civil society advocacy.

Women in power and decision-making: political representation had **not materially improved** despite policy commitments. ## 3. Disability and Inclusive Development

### Access to the Disability Allowance in the Maldives

**Citation:** Hameed, S., Banks, L. M., Usman, S. K., & Kuper, H. (2022). Access to the Disability Allowance in the Maldives: National coverage and factors affecting uptake. *London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine / Es-Key, Maldives*.

**Key Findings:** Found that only **25.6% of people with disabilities receive the Disability Allowance** – meaning three-quarters of eligible persons were not receiving the benefit. Coverage was **lowest among women, older adults, those in Male', and those with sensory impairments**. Barriers to uptake included: lack of information about the allowance; perceptions of disability that excluded milder conditions; eligibility criteria that were poorly understood; **geographic factors requiring travel to Male' for specialist assessments**; and stigma. Even after the 2023 increase from MVR 2,000 to MVR 3,000 base allowance (with supplements up to MVR 7,000), the study found that the allowance **"still may not cover all needs."** The gender disparity in the National Disability Registry – more men (58%) than women (42%) registered despite the Census finding more women (55%) with disabilities – corroborated the study's finding that women face greater barriers to registration.

**Methodology:** Mixed-methods research combining quantitative coverage analysis (using Census and registry data) with qualitative barrier assessment, published in *Global Social Policy*.

**Context:** The most rigorous disability-focused study in the Maldivian literature, conducted by the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that the gap between policy intent and actual reach is substantial. A 25.6% uptake rate for a universal entitlement represents a fundamental system failure in reaching intended beneficiaries.

### Situational Analysis of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in the Maldives (2024)

**Citation:** Faisal, A. J. (2024). *Situational Analysis of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities in the Maldives*. UNPRPD / UN Maldives / UNDP / MoSFD / MBS.

**Key Findings:** The most comprehensive disability rights assessment at 123 pages, covering every CRPD domain. Key findings included: the NDR Portal was **not in an accessible format** for persons with disabilities, requiring families to navigate it; application processing delays reached **up to 12 months**; some participants claimed the process was **facilitated through political connections**; status updates were communicated via text messages – **inaccessible to deaf and visually impaired persons**. On education: **87% of government schools** had SEN units but many were being removed in the name of "inclusion" while children received **no reasonable accommodations** – deaf students lacked interpreters, students with intellectual disabilities were placed in higher grades based on age rather than ability. On employment: labour force participation stood at **41% for men with disabilities and 28% for women with disabilities**. The prevalence of sexual violence against children with disabilities was found to be **60%**, with the majority of survivors being girls. On infrastructure: most public buildings lacked ramps or lifts; mosques were not wheelchair accessible; **no accessible taxis** existed aside from MAPD-operated vehicles; there were **no supported living housing options**.

**Methodology:** Multi-agency UNPRPD situational analysis using extensive focus group discussions with persons with disabilities across atolls, combined with legal and institutional analysis.

**Context:** Produced by a consortium of UN agencies and government bodies, providing the definitive current reference.

**Relevance:** Documents comprehensively how the Disability Act 2010's provisions remain largely unimplemented 14 years after enactment.

### HRCM's 2023 CRPD Shadow Report

**Citation:** Human Rights Commission of the Maldives. (2023). *Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities: CRPD Shadow Report*. HRCM.

**Key Findings:** The 57-page shadow report documented **"incongruence between key legislations, and the regulations, policies or guidelines formulated to aid implementation"** as **"a critical barrier predominant in many areas."** Found that despite equal marriage rights in law, there were **no regulations or policies ensuring accessibility** of the marriage process for persons with disabilities. The Mental Health Policy (2021–2025) lacked backing from a Mental Health Act – a **critical legislative gap**. The Disability Council included only **one member with a disability**, elected to represent the entire disability community. Disability allowance coverage was **lowest among women, older adults, and atoll residents**. The report documented intersections between gender and disability, including the particular vulnerability of women with disabilities to domestic violence.

**Methodology:** NHRI shadow report to the UN CRPD Committee, drawing on HRCM monitoring, investigation, and stakeholder consultation.

**Context:** The most comprehensive independent assessment of disability rights implementation by the national human rights institution.

**Relevance:** Provides the independent accountability assessment that the government's CRPD report cannot provide, documenting the incongruence between law and implementation.

### Disability in the Maldives: An Analysis from Census 2022

**Citation:** Riyaza, F. (2023). *Disability in the Maldives: An Analysis from Census 2022*. MBS / UNFPA.

**Key Findings:** Using the Washington Group Short Set on Functioning for the first time in a national census, found **24,401 persons with disabilities** (approximately **7% of the population**). Disaggregation showed: women constituted **55%** (13,456) of persons with disabilities; **65%** resided in atolls (far from specialist services in Male'); **3,401 children** (5–17 years) had disabilities. Despite the Census count of 24,401, only **13,656** were registered in the National Disability Registry – meaning **44% (approximately 10,745 persons)** were not receiving any disability-specific state support. The gender ratio reversal between Census (more women) and registry (more men) confirmed that women face greater barriers to registration and benefit access.

**Methodology:** Statistical analysis using national Census 2022 microdata with Washington Group methodology, disaggregated by type of functional difficulty, sex, age, and atoll.

**Context:** First Maldivian census to use the internationally standardised WG-SS instrument, enabling comparison with international data.

**Relevance:** The definitive current disability prevalence data. The 44% gap between Census identification and registry coverage represents the quantification of the system's failure to reach those it was designed to serve.

### Challenges and Approaches for Agenda 2030: Perspectives of Persons with Disabilities

**Citation:** Moosa, S., Musthafa, H. S., & Hassan, A. (2024). Challenges and approaches for Agenda 2030: Perspectives of persons with disabilities in a small island context. *Te Manawa Teki Trauma Research Centre / University Canada West / The Maldives National University*.

**Key Findings:** Qualitative phenomenological study with 15 participants finding that the **environment is physically disabling**, access to quality education and social protection is **limited**, and that a human rights-based approach to social protection is needed. Participants described experiences of being **forced to resign from teaching positions** when they developed disabilities, being **denied Zakat distributions** because they were registered for disability allowance (even when their families lived in poverty), and being told that **"people with either political or family connections usually become hired for jobs."** Teachers with disabilities had classrooms moved to ground floors without other accommodations; schools refused to make workplace adjustments.

**Methodology:** Qualitative phenomenological study centring the lived experiences of 15 persons with disabilities.

**Context:** Shifts the analytical frame from service provision metrics to the experiential reality of disability in a small island developing state.

**Relevance:** Provides the first-person accounts that statistical analyses cannot capture – essential for understanding why policies fail to reach their intended beneficiaries.

## 4. Elderly Care and Population Ageing

### Voluntary National Survey on MIPAA Implementation

**Citation:** UN ESCAP. (2021). *Voluntary National Survey on the Implementation of the Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing (MIPAA) in Asia and the Pacific: Maldives*. UN ESCAP.

**Key Findings:** The government's own candid self-assessment revealed that the following were **"always" present challenges**: **lack of financial resources** ("Limited Budget dedicated for this area"); **lack of data** on older persons ("Data collection is expensive with limited capacity/resources"); **lack of overall political support** ("Other pressing issues take precedence when designing policies"); **lack of cross-ministerial coordination** ("overarching issue of lack of coordination among government sector and private sector partners"); and **lack of public awareness**. The survey reported **270 elderly abuse cases** to MoGFSS between 2018 and 2021, with **males more frequently among the victims**. On substance abuse among the elderly: **"Government has not established successful strategies to combat substance use issue in the country."** Regarding the National Plan of Action on Older Persons (2019–2023): **"since the Action plan was launched in November 2019, no monitoring has been done since the pandemic situation."**

**Methodology:** Standardised MIPAA survey enabling cross-country comparison within Asia and the Pacific, completed using focus group meetings with key stakeholders.

**Context:** The government's own assessment – making the documented failures particularly authoritative.

**Relevance:** The most comprehensive and honest assessment of the Maldives' ageing policy performance, documenting failures in the government's own words.

### Perceptions of Ageing Among Older Adults Living in Male'

**Citation:** Nazra, A. (2018). Perceptions of Ageing Among Older Adults Living in Male', Maldives and Implications for Provision of Support. *Villa College Institute for Research and Innovation*.

**Key Findings:** Qualitative interviews with **8 older adults aged 65–70** in Male' found that participants preferred living with their children but faced challenges from **inadequate housing, limited geriatric care**, lack of dedicated social and health support, and the **absence of age-friendly built environments**. No formal geriatric medical specialty exists in the Maldives – there are **no geriatricians** in the country, and no medical facility has a dedicated geriatric unit. Participants identified these gaps as among their primary challenges, alongside the lack of structured daily activities and social engagement opportunities.

**Methodology:** Small-scale qualitative study providing lived-experience perspectives – one of the very few studies centring the voices of older Maldivians themselves.

**Context:** Male' presents particular challenges for elderly residents: extreme density, limited accessible infrastructure, and high costs of living that erode the purchasing power of the MVR 5,000 basic pension.

**Relevance:** Provides the experiential dimension of ageing policy that is absent from demographic projections and policy documents.

### Satisfaction of Caretakers towards Government's Home Visit Program for Bedridden Patients

**Citation:** Taj, A., & Suzana, M. (2023). Satisfaction of Caretakers towards Government's Home Visit Program for Bedridden Patients in Greater Male', Maldives. *Ministry of Health / The Maldives National University*.

**Key Findings:** Surveyed **123 caretakers** of all registered bedridden patients in Greater Male'. Patient profile: mean age **81.4 years**, **79% female**, **85% fully bedridden**, **84% on more than three medicines**, **28% on Ryle's tube**, **19% had bedsores**. Caretaker profile: **67% were sons or daughters**, only **10% were hired caretakers**, **69% of caretakers were female**, and **78% had received no training**. Overall satisfaction was high (median 10/10), but satisfaction was **dramatically lower** for two dimensions: **procedures** during home visits (median **2 out of 12**) and **caretaker training** (median **5 out of 10**). The programme served just **67 registered patients** in the Greater Male' region and conducted only **93 home visits/monitoring sessions** nationwide between 2019 and 2021 – indicating an extremely small-scale programme relative to need. **67% of patients did not live with their families**, reflecting housing pressures in the Greater Male' region.

**Methodology:** Quantitative descriptive study using a 49-item research instrument, conducted February to July 2022.

**Context:** The first formal evaluation of the government's primary community-based elderly care programme.

**Relevance:** Documents both the value and severe limitations of the home visit programme – high satisfaction with basic services but critical failure in procedures and caregiver training, operating at a scale (67 patients) that is negligible relative to the elderly population.

### High-Level Panel Statement on Population Ageing in Maldives

**Citation:** Didi, A. M. (2022). *Statement at the High Level Panel Discussion of the Fourth Review and Appraisal of Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing*. Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services.

**Key Findings:** Outlined the Maldives' ageing trajectory: **5% currently over 65**, projected to reach **12.7% by 2045**. By 2054, projections suggest **34.1%** of the population could be aged 60 or older – a **fivefold increase from 2014 levels**. The total fertility rate has collapsed to **1.7** (well below replacement of 2.1), while life expectancy has reached **81.1 years**. Only **20.9% of labour force participation** is in formal employment, while **89.9% is informal** – with 99.1% of women's employment in the informal sector. This means the vast majority of the ageing population will have no contributory pension entitlements. The statement confirmed the 2018 National Elderly Policy (six fundamental rights of older persons) and the 2019 National Plan of Action (34 priority actions), while acknowledging the gender dimension: caregiving is **"mostly on women."**

**Methodology:** Ministerial statement at an international forum providing the government's official policy position.

**Context:** Delivered at the Fourth Review and Appraisal of MIPAA, this statement represents the most authoritative government articulation of the ageing policy challenge.

**Relevance:** Concisely captures the demographic data, policy instruments, and fiscal challenges that define the Maldives' ageing policy landscape. The informal employment figure (89.9%) is particularly significant for pension policy design.

### Demographic Prognosis for South Asia: A Future of Rapid Ageing

**Citation:** UNFPA. (2009). *Demographic prognosis for South Asia: A future of rapid ageing*. UNFPA.

**Key Findings:** Projected that the demographic transition in SAARC countries would occur at a **much faster rate than in European countries**, giving policymakers significantly less time to build institutional capacity for ageing populations. The Maldives' trajectory – from among the highest fertility rates in Asia to below replacement within a single generation – exemplifies this compressed transition. **Methodology:** Regional demographic analysis using UN Population Division data and SAARC country-level projections.

**Context:** The regional comparative framework that situates the Maldives' demographic transition within the broader South Asian pattern.

**Relevance:** Highlights the urgency of ageing policy preparation – the Maldives has far less time than developed nations had to build elderly care infrastructure, pension sustainability, and health system readiness.

## 5. Substance Abuse and Drug Policy

### Situational Analysis of Drugs in the Maldives 2021

**Citation:** National Drug Agency / WHO Maldives. (2021). *Situational Analysis of Drugs in the Maldives 2021*. NDA / WHO.

**Key Findings:** Surveyed **403 drug users** across all atolls with **24 key informant interviews** from eight institutions and longitudinal data analysis covering 2011–2020. Key findings from secondary data (2016–2020): more than **50,000 laboratory tests** for drugs were carried out, averaging 10,000 per year; patients testing positive for **opiates increased more than two-fold**; user preference had **shifted from naturally occurring substances to synthetic drugs**; the mean age of substance users arrested increased from **27.7 years (2015) to 31.3 years (2020)** – suggesting an ageing cohort of entrenched users alongside continued new initiation. Source countries shifted from South Asia to Europe; transport mechanisms shifted from air/sea to **mail**; **local production expanded**; and **migrant workers** became increasingly involved as carriers. The survey confirmed that **57.8% of drug users began substance use before age 18**, linking the drug crisis directly to child and youth vulnerability. The analysis found that **low accessibility due to a severe lack of qualified human resources remains the biggest challenge**.

**Methodology:** Comprehensive multi-source situational analysis combining a quantitative survey, qualitative key informant interviews, and longitudinal secondary data from eight stakeholder institutions (NDA, Police, Drug Court, Correctional Services, Juvenile Justice Unit, IGMH, Customs, Journey/Hands Together).

**Context:** Conducted after a gap of nearly ten years since the 2011/12 National Drug Use Survey – the absence of regular monitoring itself being a significant finding.

**Relevance:** The most current comprehensive assessment of the drug situation, integrating data from enforcement, judicial, and health perspectives. The ten-year data gap illustrates the challenge of evidence-based drug policy in the Maldives.

---

### A Process and Systems Evaluation of Drug Treatment and Rehabilitation Services

**Citation:** Mortimore, G., Stimson, G., & Adam, A. (2010). *A process and systems evaluation of the drug treatment and rehabilitation services in the Maldives*. Ministry of Health and Family / World Bank.

**Key Findings:** Identified fundamental structural problems in the treatment system: **treatment periods were too short** (6–8 months); there was **no sustainable aftercare**; people were being **"recycled" through prison and the treatment system** with no clear exit; services were **operating below capacity** and were not cost-efficient; **multiple agencies operated in silos**, undermining treatment continuity; and the **lack of job opportunities** was the single greatest obstacle to reintegration. The evaluation concluded: **"Many of the components of a good treatment and rehabilitation system are in place. There is a lack of coordination which has a negative impact on helping people become abstinent."** It recommended expanding the methadone maintenance programme (finding **"sufficient positive evidence now to roll out"**), exploring mobile detoxification camps, and establishing detoxification in general hospitals. The Drug Rehabilitation Centre on Himafushi had admitted **2,597 people** by 2010, averaging 207 per year, with optimum capacity of 150 and maximum 250. Operating costs were **$20–25 per person per day**. There was **no access to detoxification services for females**.

**Methodology:** World Bank-supported institutional evaluation covering both compulsory (court-ordered) and voluntary treatment pathways, including operational assessment of specific treatment facilities.

**Context:** Conducted at a critical moment – one year before the Drugs Act 2011 reformed the legislative framework. The evaluation informed the reforms but many of its recommendations remain unimplemented.

**Relevance:** The most comprehensive external evaluation of the drug treatment system, identifying systemic weaknesses (recycling through prison, absent aftercare, no female detox) that persist in various forms to the present day.

### UNODC Profile of Drug and Crime Problem in the Maldives

**Citation:** UNODC. (2005). *UNODC Profile of Drug and Crime Problem in the Maldives*. UNODC.

**Key Findings:** Found that opiates (mainly heroin) and cannabinoids (mainly hashish) were the most frequently used drugs, with approximately **8% of users reporting injection** and **half starting substance use before age 17**. Documented trafficking routes from the Golden Crescent (Afghanistan-Pakistan-Iran), with the main route running Trivandrum (India) to Male'. The profile assessed the country's vulnerability as a transit point given its 1,190 islands spread across 90,000 square kilometres of ocean with no chemical or pharmaceutical industry. The profile established the mid-2000s baseline: commonly reported that **one in 100 adults** struggled with substance abuse, with informal estimates of **10,000 to 30,000 young people** using drugs (8–23% of the youth population). It was also commonly reported that **most Maldivian extended families had at least one active drug user**.

**Methodology:** UNODC country profile using standardised assessment methodology, drawing on government data and UNODC monitoring.

**Context:** Provides the mid-2000s baseline for drug situation analysis, before the 2008 BBS and 2011/12 National Drug Use Survey provided more systematic data.

**Relevance:** Establishes the early profile of the Maldives' drug crisis, including the critical finding of early onset (half starting before age 17) that presaged the youth-focused drug policy challenge.

### Drug Rehabilitation and Practice Dilemmas in the Maldives

**Citation:** Ageel, I. (2006). *Drug Rehabilitation and Practice Dilemmas in the Maldives*. Master's thesis, University of Waikato.

**Key Findings:** Examined tensions between the National Narcotics Control Bureau's formal policies and actual clinical practice, finding **a lack of clinical staff involvement in policy formation**, **inadequate professional monitoring and training**, and **ethical issues in service delivery**. The thesis identified that the DRC's Therapeutic Community model (based on the US Daytop programme, modified for Maldivian culture and Islamic principles) was being implemented by young, inadequately trained counselling staff without proper clinical supervision – creating practice dilemmas where frontline workers lacked both the authority and the skills to resolve clinical challenges.

**Methodology:** Qualitative research providing an insider critique of the rehabilitation system from a practitioner perspective.

**Context:** One of the earliest critical examinations of drug rehabilitation practice in the Maldives, predating the major policy reforms of 2011.

**Relevance:** Identifies structural problems in rehabilitation governance – the disconnect between policy formulation and clinical practice, the absence of qualified clinical staff – that persist in various forms today.

### Strengthening Custodial Services in the Maldives

**Citation:** Harding, R., & Morgan, N. (2004). *Strengthening Custodial Services in the Maldives*. Attorney General's Office / Ministry of Home Affairs.

**Key Findings:** Found that drug offenders constituted a **large proportion of the prison population** – the U.S. Department of State's 2013 Human Rights Report would later document this at **47%**. The review documented conditions at Maafushi Prison, the high number of unsentenced prisoners at Dhoonidhoo Detention Centre, limited rehabilitation programmes, and the absence of adequate provisions for women prisoners. The four-year implementation plan recommended fundamental reform of custodial services, but many recommendations remained unimplemented years later. The Maldives still lacked **separate juvenile detention facilities**, exposing juvenile offenders to adult prisoners.

**Methodology:** Expert review by international corrections specialists, providing a pre-reform baseline of the custodial system.

**Context:** Conducted by international experts during a period when the prison system was overwhelmed by drug offenders.

**Relevance:** Documents the dominance of drug offenders in the prison population and the conditions of incarceration – critical context for understanding why the 2011 Drugs Act sought to divert drug users toward treatment rather than imprisonment.

### Prison Radicalisation in the Maldives

**Citation:** Speckard, A. (2022). *Prison Radicalisation in the Maldives*. International Center for the Study of Violent Extremism / Transparency Maldives / Ministry of Home Affairs.

**Key Findings:** Investigated the drivers and magnitude of prison radicalisation, finding a critical connection between incarceration (often for drug offences) and recruitment into violent extremist networks. The research informed segregation, rehabilitation, and reintegration strategies for violent extremist prisoners, documenting how the overlap between the drug policy system and the radicalisation pipeline creates compounding social risks – individuals entering prison for drug offences are exposed to extremist recruitment, creating a pathway from substance abuse to radicalisation.

**Methodology:** Specialised research using qualitative interviews and institutional analysis by an international extremism research centre.

**Context:** The Maldives has among the highest per-capita rates of foreign fighter travel to Syria/Iraq of any country globally, making the prison-radicalisation nexus a national security concern as well as a social policy issue.

**Relevance:** Documents the critical but under-examined connection between drug policy, incarceration, and radicalisation – three social policy domains that intersect within the prison system.

## 6. Mental Health

### Global School-based Student Health Survey: 2009 Maldives Country Report

**Citation:** Shifa, A. (2009). *Global School-based Student Health Survey: 2009 Maldives Country Report*. Ministry of Education / CDC / WHO.

**Key Findings:** Among the most alarming findings in any Maldivian survey: **19.9% of students** seriously considered attempting suicide during the past 12 months; **16.8%** made a plan for how they would attempt suicide; and **12.0%** actually attempted suicide one or more times. Rates were **higher in atoll schools than in Male'**, suggesting that geographic isolation, limited recreational opportunities, and reduced access to services are risk factors. Among those who attempted suicide, **5.1%** made an attempt requiring medical treatment. The survey also found: **10.1%** reported feeling lonely most of the time or always; **9.5%** reported being so worried about something they could not sleep at night most of the time or always. The survey covered dietary behaviour, hygiene, violence and injury, mental health, tobacco use (45.7% among 13–15 year olds), alcohol use (6.7% among 13–15 year olds), drug use, sexual behaviour, physical activity, and protective factors.

**Methodology:** Standardised WHO/CDC school health survey using internationally comparable methodology.

**Context:** The survey is now significantly dated (2009), but it remains one of the only nationally representative surveys of adolescent health and mental health in the Maldives. The finding that one in five students considered suicide – and one in eight actually attempted it – indicates a population-level youth mental health crisis.

**Relevance:** Provides the baseline for adolescent mental health indicators. The absence of a follow-up survey in the 16 years since its conduct represents a critical evidence gap.

### WHO STEP Survey on Risk Factors for NCDs: Maldives 2020–2021

**Citation:** The Maldives National University / WHO / HPA. (2020–2021). *WHO STEP Survey on Risk Factors for Noncommunicable Diseases: Maldives 2020–2021*. MNU / WHO.

**Key Findings:** Found that **18.8% of young people aged 15–29 were classified as depressed**, with a dramatic gender disparity: **33.5% of females** compared to **9.6% of males** – females more than three times as likely to meet criteria for depression. Among young women, approximately **one in six (16.2%)** experienced moderate to severe depression. The concentration of mental health burden among young women intersects with documented social vulnerabilities including domestic violence, economic dependence, and pressures of early motherhood. The survey also documented comprehensive NCD risk factor data including tobacco use, drug use, alcohol consumption, diet, physical activity, NCD prevalence, and injury data.

**Methodology:** Standardised WHO STEPS methodology enabling international comparison, providing population-level NCD risk factor data including depression screening.

**Context:** Provides the first population-level depression prevalence data for the Maldives, filling a critical gap in the mental health evidence base.

**Relevance:** The depression prevalence data – particularly the gender disparity – is essential for designing gender-responsive mental health services and for understanding the gendered social determinants of mental ill-health.

### Maldives Health Research Bulletin, Volume 5

**Citation:** Ministry of Health. (2018). *Maldives Health Research Bulletin, Volume 5*. Ministry of Health.

**Key Findings:** Contains one of the few studies on **maternal depression in the Maldives**, alongside research abstracts on the School Health Survey 2017, oral cancer trends, universal health coverage, breastfeeding protection, menopausal health, beta-thalassemia screening, cancer control, and disability allowance impact evaluation. The health research priorities for 2017–2019 were also outlined. The maternal depression findings, while limited in scope, addressed an area with virtually no other Maldivian evidence, despite the known links between maternal mental health, child development, and family welfare.

**Methodology:** Health research bulletin compiling multiple research abstracts from government-supported research.

**Context:** The bulletin format means findings are presented as abstracts rather than full analyses – indicating the early stage of the Maldivian health research ecosystem.

**Relevance:** Contains one of the only references to maternal depression research in the Maldivian literature, a critical gap given the links between maternal mental health and child outcomes.

## 7. Education and Youth Development

### Education Sector Analysis: Maldives (2019)

**Citation:** Ministry of Education. (2019). *Education Sector Analysis: Maldives*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The most comprehensive assessment of the education system found significant **geographic inequity in learning outcomes**: the National Assessment of Learning Outcomes (NALO 2016) showed Male' students consistently outperforming atoll students in Grade 4 Mathematics, Grade 4 English, and Grade 7 assessments. A **significant proportion of atoll teachers held qualifications below the diploma minimum** later mandated by the Education Act 2020. Approximately **30% of secondary teachers** in some atolls were expatriates (primarily from India and Sri Lanka). The Quality Assurance Department was **significantly under-resourced**, conducting infrequent inspections. ICT integration varied dramatically – Male' schools had smart boards while some remote islands lacked functional computers. Per-student spending was higher on smaller remote islands (due to fixed overheads) but produced **lower quality outcomes**. Education spending was heavily skewed toward recurrent costs (primarily salaries) with limited allocation for quality improvement. Multi-grade teaching was identified as a practical necessity on smaller islands, but **most teachers had no specific training** in multi-grade pedagogies.

**Methodology:** Full sector analysis by the Ministry of Education covering pre-primary through higher education, developed with UNESCO technical assistance.

**Context:** Informed the Education Sector Plan 2019–2023 and the subsequent Education Act 2020.

**Relevance:** The definitive assessment of the education system's structure, performance, and challenges. Its identification of geographic inequity as the defining challenge remains as relevant as when it was published.

---

### Youth Vulnerability in the Maldives

**Citation:** Government of Maldives / UNDP / Government of Japan. (2019). *Youth Vulnerability in the Maldives*. UNDP.

**Key Findings:** Nationwide study capturing the voices of Maldivian youth, with over **40% of the population aged 18–34**. Found that **"Almost all young people on the outer atolls complained about the quality and variety of education"** – many sciences were not taught, schools lacked equipment and laboratories, and there was **no career guidance**. **"Half of those questioned said their education had neither helped them get a job nor prepared them well for adult life."** The study documented youth concerns about unemployment, housing shortages, drug abuse, gang violence, extremism, and domestic violence. Young people reported **"there is no structured network of support in schools"** and **"the few counsellors they have are not qualified."** Education was described as focused on syllabus content and examination preparation, with no meaningful attention to personal development or emotional wellbeing. Youth unemployment was **15.9%** – two and a half times the adult rate – with about **27% of young men and 23% of young women** classified as NEET.

**Methodology:** Nationwide survey with UNDP support and Japanese government funding, combining quantitative data with extensive youth consultations.

**Context:** Commissioned by the National Counter Terrorism Centre, reflecting the recognition that youth vulnerability to drug abuse, gang violence, and radicalisation is rooted in systemic failures of education, employment, and social support.

**Relevance:** Provides direct evidence of youth-identified priorities – essential for ensuring policy addresses actual concerns rather than assumed ones. The finding that half of outer-atoll youth felt their education did not prepare them for work or adult life is a damning indictment of the education system's relevance.

---

### Maldives TVET Assessment 2012

**Citation:** Rothboeck, S. (2012). *Maldives TVET Assessment 2012*. International Labour Organization.

**Key Findings:** Identified a fundamental cultural barrier: **TVET was perceived as a second-class option** by Maldivian youth and families, a pathway for those who had "failed" academically. Found that Employment Sector Councils (intended to bridge training and industry) were **largely inactive**, meeting infrequently and lacking capacity to influence programme design. TVET enrolment was approximately **1,762** across all institutions in 2018 – negligible relative to the youth population. The tourism industry alone required engineers and technicians for every resort, but training was **insufficient to meet demand**. The assessment noted that the economy's demand for technical skills far exceeded the TVET system's capacity, with the majority of technical positions filled by expatriate workers.

**Methodology:** ILO-commissioned assessment using the Interagency Working Group (IWG) indicator framework for TVET evaluation.

**Context:** Provides the baseline against which TVET reforms – including the MEERY Project and HUNARU programme – can be measured.

**Relevance:** Documents the structural barriers to TVET effectiveness that persist despite repeated reform efforts.

---

### Exploring Strategies to Enhance TVET Engagement in Maldives

**Citation:** Sunny, R., & Ismail, A. (2023). Exploring Strategies to Enhance TVET Engagement in Maldives to Improve Youth Career Development. *Journal of Technical Education and Training*, 15(1).

**Key Findings:** Survey of **200 Maldivian respondents** found that while **81.7% had heard of TVET**, less than half (**45.2%**) understood that it could lead to better employment opportunities. Experts identified **seven strategies** to improve TVET engagement: promoting through electronic media, launching a national campaign, strengthening industry partnerships, branding TVET more effectively, holding skills competitions, improving career guidance in schools, and ensuring curricula align with industry demand. The persistence of the "second-class" perception – first identified by the ILO in 2012 – confirmed that a decade of policy attention had not resolved the fundamental stigma attached to vocational education.

**Methodology:** Academic journal article combining survey data with expert consultation.

**Context:** Published over a decade after the ILO Assessment, enabling assessment of whether TVET perceptions have changed.

**Relevance:** Confirms that the stigma barrier to TVET identified in 2012 persists, and provides actionable strategies for engagement.

---

### Statistics Booklet 2019–2023 (Higher Education)

**Citation:** Ministry of Higher Education. (2023). *Statistics Booklet 2019–2023*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Five years of higher education data revealed several critical patterns. The First Degree Scheme enrolled **18,880 students** and created **1,693 scholarship opportunities** between 2019 and 2022, driving a **62% increase in bachelor's-level enrolments** – part of **MVR 2.70 billion** spent on higher education over the period. Female students consistently outnumbered males – by 2022, **64% were female and only 36% male**. Dropout rates were concerning: the overwhelming majority of dropouts were **male (88% in 2019)**. The most popular fields were management and administration (6,441 enrolments in 2022), religion and theology (peaking at 4,780 in 2020), and nursing and midwifery (3,803 in 2022). Fields with notably low enrolments included **environmental sciences** (concerning given climate vulnerability) and **building and civil engineering** (57 in 2019, 99 in 2022 – reflecting reliance on expatriate construction labour). Heavy reliance on **part-time faculty** (reaching 60% in 2021) raised quality concerns.

**Methodology:** Statistical compendium compiled from institutional data across all registered Higher Education Institutions.

**Context:** Captures the impact of the First Degree Scheme – the government's most significant higher education policy intervention.

**Relevance:** Essential quantitative reference for higher education access, financing, and the emerging concern about gender imbalance in enrolment (with male under-representation and dropout) and field-of-study mismatches with economic needs.

---

### Global Youth Development Report 2020

**Citation:** The Commonwealth. (2020). *Global Youth Development Report 2020*. The Commonwealth.

**Key Findings:** Ranked and benchmarked youth development across **181 countries** using composite indicators on education, employment, health, civic participation, and political participation. The Maldives ranked **41st overall** on the Youth Development Index (YDI) – the highest in South Asia – but performance varied significantly across dimensions: strong on education and health metrics, weaker on employment and civic participation. The report enabled cross-country comparison showing that the Maldives' youth development challenge is not access to education (where it performs well) but the **translation of education into productive employment** – a finding consistent with the domestic evidence.

**Methodology:** International comparative index using standardised indicators across 181 countries.

**Context:** The Commonwealth's biennial youth development assessment provides the only comprehensive international benchmarking of Maldivian youth outcomes.

**Relevance:** Provides the global comparative framework confirming that the Maldives' youth development challenge lies in employment and civic participation, not education access.

---

### Youth in the Maldives: Shaping a New Future

**Citation:** World Bank. (2014). *Youth in the Maldives: Shaping a New Future for Young Women and Men Through Engagement & Empowerment*. World Bank.

**Key Findings:** Multi-dimensional assessment covering health, education, employment/unemployment, family structure, gangs and violence, and civic engagement. Found that more than **50% of women marry before age 20** and nearly **80% of women with children have their first child before 25** – creating early care burdens that constrain labour force participation. Documented that **almost two-thirds of young women and over half of young men** preferred to wait for government or SOE jobs rather than accept private sector positions. Gang involvement emerged as a significant driver of youth vulnerability, with gang networks actively recruiting juveniles for drug distribution and criminal activity. Approximately **27% of young men and 23% of young women** were NEET.

**Methodology:** World Bank analytical report using mixed methods including quantitative data analysis and qualitative research.

**Context:** The most comprehensive single-volume assessment of youth issues, predating the Youth Vulnerability study by five years.

**Relevance:** Provides the structural analysis of youth challenges – early marriage, gang recruitment, NEET rates, public sector preference – that informs current youth policy design.

## 8. Health Systems

### Maldives Health Master Plan 2016–2025

**Citation:** Ministry of Health. (2014). *Maldives Health Master Plan 2016–2025*. Ministry of Health.

**Key Findings:** The third long-term health strategic plan documented that NCDs (inclusive of injuries) account for **78% of the total disease burden** while communicable diseases account for only 22%. Total health expenditure was **9% of GDP** (US$561 per capita) with the government spending US$247 per capita. Despite the Aasandha universal health insurance scheme, direct **out-of-pocket expenditure remained high at 49%**. Only **US$11 per capita** was spent on public health programmes versus US$130 on inpatient curative services – illustrating the system's curative bias. Tobacco control law enforcement was **"weak, thus undermining public health efforts."** Mental health **"has not been in the limelight till very recently, thus needing a high focus and investment."** The Plan identified upstream determinants of health (social cohesion, housing, environment) as neglected areas, with previous policy focused predominantly on midstream and downstream factors.

**Methodology:** Ten-year national strategic plan developed through five-stage process including situational analysis, stakeholder workshops, and consensus building with political parties.

**Context:** Designed to traverse multiple election cycles by providing strategic direction rather than operational prescriptions – giving incoming governments flexibility to integrate manifesto priorities.

**Relevance:** The master strategic document for the health sector, whose diagnosis of the curative bias, NCD burden, and out-of-pocket expenditure challenge remains the framework within which all health sector reforms are situated.

---

### Maldives Demographic and Health Survey 2009

**Citation:** Ministry of Health and Family. (2009). *Maldives – Demographic and Health Survey 2009*. MoHF.

**Key Findings:** The first nationally representative DHS found: **antenatal care coverage at 97%**; **facility-based births at 95%**; **skilled birth attendance at 95%**; **caesarean section rate at 32%** (high by WHO standards); **contraceptive prevalence rate of 27%** for modern methods despite low TFR, suggesting high pregnancy termination or traditional method use; **unmet need for family planning at 29%**. Child nutrition: **17.3% of children under 5 were underweight**. Micronutrient survey findings: anaemia prevalence among children 6 months–5 years at **26%**; iron deficiency at **57%**; vitamin A deficiency affecting more than **half of children** (5.1% severely, 50.1% moderately deficient). Functional difficulties in vision were as high as **18%**, followed by mobility restriction (7.4%) and hearing defects (5.7%).

**Methodology:** First nationally representative DHS using ICF DHS Program methodology – the international gold standard for demographic and health survey data.

**Context:** Provided the foundational household health data for the Maldives, establishing baselines across fertility, nutrition, mortality, disability, and health service utilisation.

**Relevance:** The baseline DHS against which the 2016–17 survey and all subsequent health data must be compared.

---

### Maldives Demographic and Health Survey 2016–17

**Citation:** Ministry of Health / The DHS Program, ICF. (2018). *Maldives Demographic and Health Survey 2016–17*. Ministry of Health.

**Key Findings:** The second DHS enabled comparison with the 2009 baseline across all demographic and health indicators. Found that TFR had declined to **2.1** (near replacement). Spousal violence indicators were collected for the first time using a nationally representative framework, providing data comparable across wealth, education, and geography. Child nutrition trends showed limited progress in reducing underweight prevalence. The survey confirmed the pattern of high healthcare access indicators coexisting with significant geographic disparities in quality and specialist availability.

**Methodology:** Second national DHS using ICF DHS Program methodology, enabling trend analysis with 2009 baseline.

**Context:** Provides the most recent comprehensive household health data, bridging the gap between the 2009 DHS and the 2020–21 STEP Survey.

**Relevance:** The current nationally representative baseline for health indicators, essential for measuring progress and planning health system development.

---

### Corruption Vulnerabilities in the Maldivian Health Sector

**Citation:** Transparency Maldives. (2022). *Corruption Vulnerabilities in the Maldivian Health Sector*. Transparency Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Mapped corruption vulnerabilities across six domains: health governance, service delivery, hospital administration, health workforce, health financing, and medical supply chain. Found that institutions responsible for health service financing are **"inherently vulnerable to corruption"** because there is no robust automated system to prevent, detect, or sanction abuses. Health facilities engaged in **upcoding claims** and professionals **overmedicalised patients**. The non-contributory nature of Aasandha meant beneficiaries had **no incentive to support cost-effectiveness**, leading to irrational service use. Identified both sector-non-specific constraints (small island state dynamics, human resource shortages, informal kinship networks that undermine merit-based recruitment) and sector-specific vulnerabilities (pharmaceutical procurement, overseas referral management, hospital governance).

**Methodology:** Sector-specific corruption mapping identifying vulnerabilities across the health system.

**Context:** Uniquely examines how governance failures affect health service delivery – a perspective absent from most health system analyses.

**Relevance:** Identifies systemic integrity vulnerabilities that undermine health service quality, equity, and fiscal sustainability. The finding on upcoding and overmedicalisation directly threatens Aasandha's financial viability.

---

### GYTS – Global Youth Tobacco Survey: Maldives 2019

**Citation:** WHO / CDC. (2019). *GYTS – Global Youth Tobacco Survey Fact Sheet: Maldives 2019*. WHO.

**Key Findings:** Documented alarmingly high tobacco use among students aged 13–15: **45.7% currently used any tobacco product**; **smokeless tobacco use was 46.1%** – far exceeding smoked tobacco (8.3%); nearly half were **exposed to secondhand smoke in public places**; **3 in 5 current smokers tried to quit**, indicating both high addiction rates and motivation for cessation. These figures are **among the highest in the South-East Asia Region**. The dramatic increase from 10.4–11.2% in 2001–2011 to 45.7% by 2019 suggests a tobacco epidemic driven partly by smokeless products.

**Methodology:** Standardised global survey using WHO/CDC methodology for international comparison.

**Context:** Tobacco use is the leading preventable NCD risk factor. The 45.7% prevalence among 13–15 year olds represents a public health emergency.

**Relevance:** Reveals a distinctive Maldivian pattern – smokeless tobacco use vastly exceeding smoked tobacco – requiring specific policy responses beyond standard smoking cessation approaches.

---

### Decentralisation and Autonomisation Policy: Regional Hospitals

**Citation:** Shaafiu, A. Z. (2023). *Decentralisation & Autonomisation Policy: Development of 5 Regional Hospitals & Greater Male' Hospitals Service Enhancement*. The President's Office.

**Key Findings:** Documented the most consequential health reform of recent years: the elevation of all five regional hospitals to fully tertiary status with specialist psychiatric services. By August 2023, psychiatry waitlists at IGMH had decreased from over **3,300 to approximately 330** – a reduction of **approximately 90%**, and the average waiting period had shortened from **over eighteen months to approximately two months**. All five regional hospitals were staffed with specialist psychiatrists, collectively conducting approximately **150–200 psychiatric consultations per week**. Telepsychiatry video consultations were extended across six atolls, providing access to approximately **55,000 people across 56 islands**. Advanced procedures including chemotherapy, knee replacements, cataract surgery, and dialysis were now available at regional level. The report noted a pattern of **"constant policy upheaval"** driven by changes of government that disrupts long-term health planning.

**Methodology:** Government policy document documenting reforms from 2021 to 2023 across IGMH, Hulhumale' Hospital, and five regional hospitals.

**Context:** Represents the government's approach to reducing the Male'-atoll disparity in health service quality, with mental health decentralisation as a flagship achievement.

**Relevance:** Documents arguably the most impactful social sector reform of the 2019–2023 period – the 90% reduction in psychiatric waitlists and the extension of specialist services to regional hospitals.

## 9. Housing, Poverty, and Economic Vulnerability

### Poverty and Inequality in Maldives – 2022

**Citation:** World Bank. (2022). *Poverty and Inequality in Maldives – 2022*. World Bank.

**Key Findings:** Using HIES 2019–2020 and COVID-19 phone surveys, found: national poverty rate of **5.4%** (at 71.4 MVR/day), but with a **tenfold divide** – **9.5% in atolls versus 0.9% in Male'**. Atolls contain **92% of the country's poor population**. The national Gini coefficient of **29.3** conceals that **inequality between Male' and atolls is greater than inequality within either region**. The expenditure distribution in atolls was tightly clustered around the poverty line, making populations extremely shock-vulnerable: a **16.67% reduction in household budgets** would **double** the national poverty rate to 11.2% and push **nearly one in five atoll residents (19.6%)** below the poverty line. Household size was a powerful correlate: households with **10+ members** faced 12% poverty, three times the rate of smaller households. Overcrowded households were **twice as likely to be poor**. Below-primary education of household head was associated with **12.6% poverty nationally and 39.9% deprivation in Male'**. Households with disabled heads had poverty of **9.6%**. At the international UMIC poverty line (US$5.5 PPP/day), national poverty was just **1.7%**, driven entirely by atoll poverty at 3.2% – **zero poor individuals in Male'** by this benchmark.

**Methodology:** World Bank Poverty Assessment using HIES 2019–2020 data supplemented by COVID-19 phone surveys, with shock simulation modelling.

**Context:** The definitive current poverty analysis for the Maldives.

**Relevance:** The primary reference for poverty and inequality data, documenting the geographic fracture that is the defining feature of Maldivian economic vulnerability. The shock simulation (doubling poverty with a 16.67% income reduction) proved prescient when COVID-19 contracted GDP by 33.6%.

---

### A Comprehensive Housing Market Study for the Greater Male Region

**Citation:** Housing Development Corporation / MNU / IUM / United Nations. (2017). *A Comprehensive Housing Market Study for the Greater Male Region*. HDC.

**Key Findings:** Based on a survey of **672 local residents** across Male', Hulhumale', and Villimale'. Found that **57% of residents had migrated from an outer island** (78.6% for education, 46.2% for employment, 17.3% for medical needs). **56.6%** lived in rented apartments; only **1.9%** in social housing. The most common monthly rent was **MVR 10,000–15,000** (42.3% of renters). **53.3% of individual respondents** reported personal income below MVR 5,000 – meaning rent consumed most or all of their income. The study concluded that **"housing is not affordable for an average person"** and that rent should constitute only 15–20% of household income – a benchmark the vast majority failed to meet. An HRCM rapid assessment had earlier found that **85% of household income in Male' was spent on housing**. Housing prices exceeded **12 times average annual household income**. When asked about preferred place to live, only **17.1%** preferred returning to their home island – suggesting centralisation is driven partly by genuine preference for Male' services, not only atoll push factors.

**Methodology:** Multi-institutional survey combining residential survey data with housing market analysis and migration profiling.

**Context:** The most detailed empirical picture of housing conditions, affordability, and migration in the capital region.

**Relevance:** Documents the housing affordability crisis that drives overcrowding, social stress, and vulnerability – with direct implications for child protection (overcrowding as abuse risk), elderly care (housing insecurity on fixed incomes), and gender-based violence (inability to leave due to housing dependence).

---

### Rapid Livelihood Assessment: Impact of the Covid-19 Crisis

**Citation:** Ibrahim, S., Saeed, N., & Jinan, H. (2020). *Rapid Livelihood Assessment: Impact of the Covid-19 Crisis in the Maldives*. Ministry of Economic Development / UNDP.

**Key Findings:** Found that **44% of respondents reported redundancy or complete loss of income**, **24% were on no-pay leave**, and **32% had partial pay**. Self-employed individuals were **three times more likely** than wage earners to report income losses. Two of three self-employed individuals resided in atolls, driving higher shock incidence in atoll communities: approximately **70% of self-employed atoll residents** experienced a shock versus 54% of self-employed in Male'. Women were **twice as likely to stop working entirely** and nearly **three times as likely** to experience prolonged work absence compared to men. Manufacturing experienced the most severe disruption, with approximately **70% of all workers** reporting shocks. Tourist arrivals fell from **1.7 million in 2019 to 555,494 in 2020** (a 67.4% decline). GDP contracted by **33.6%** – the sharpest decline in South Asia. The government's Economic Recovery Plan deployed **US$162 million** including MVR 5,000/month income support, utility relief, price controls, and debt moratoria.

**Methodology:** Rapid assessment combining macroeconomic analysis, employment data from JobCenter.mv, and enterprise survey data.

**Context:** Commissioned at the peak of the pandemic economic crisis, providing the evidence base for the government's relief response.

**Relevance:** Documents the scale of economic devastation and its differential impacts – by sector, location, gender, and employment type – providing the empirical basis for understanding why the Maldives' tourism-dependent economy is structurally vulnerable.

---

### A Socio-Economic Assessment of the Tuna Fisheries in the Maldives

**Citation:** Hohne-Sparborth, T. (2013). *A Socio-Economic Assessment of the Tuna Fisheries in the Maldives*. IPNLF / Sainsbury's.

**Key Findings:** Framed the pole-and-line tuna fishery not merely as an industry but as **"a social and economic policy tool"** that shapes vulnerability, adaptability, and livelihood assets of island populations. Found that fisheries constitute the **most important primary economic activity on most inhabited islands** and a key income source for men in atolls with lower formal education levels. Returns from fisheries remained low due to **lack of storage and processing facilities** and limited capacity to convert raw catch into value-added products. Fisher incomes were calculated as shares of catch value rather than fixed wages, exposing them fully to price volatility. The sector's social safety net function – providing employment of last resort – meant that its decline would have welfare consequences far exceeding its 4.6% GDP contribution.

**Methodology:** Socio-economic assessment commissioned by the International Pole & Line Foundation with the London School of Economics, examining how government policies shape the fishery's impact on livelihoods and vulnerability.

**Context:** The Maldivian tuna fishery is internationally recognised for sustainable pole-and-line methods; the assessment uniquely examines its social policy function.

**Relevance:** Documents how the fisheries sector functions as a de facto social safety net in atoll communities – essential for understanding why any disruption to fisheries has immediate welfare consequences for the most economically vulnerable populations.

### Maldives National Housing Policy 2008

**Citation:** Ministry of Housing and Urban Development. (2008). *Maldives National Housing Policy 2008*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Identified six focus areas for addressing housing needs: Male' urban region, other urban regions and centres, outer islands, housing finance, housing facilitation unit formation, and capacity building. The policy acknowledged that housing prices in Male' exceeded **12 times average annual household income** – placing home ownership beyond reach of most residents. The 2008 Constitution recognised housing as a basic human right, but the policy itself acknowledged that supply was grossly insufficient and that the market had failed to deliver affordable housing without state intervention.

**Methodology:** National policy document establishing the framework for housing delivery across the archipelago.

**Context:** The Seventh National Development Plan (2007) had already identified the housing-income ratio as a crisis indicator. The policy followed land reclamation at Hulhumale' (Phase I completed 2003) and the establishment of HDFC in 2004.

**Relevance:** Establishes the policy architecture that shapes housing availability and affordability. Its six focus areas remain the structural framework within which housing challenges are addressed, though implementation has consistently lagged behind ambition.

### Maldives Common Country Analysis (2020)

**Citation:** UNRCO. (2020). *Maldives Common Country Analysis*. United Nations.

**Key Findings:** Comprehensive UN analysis examining political, human rights, economic, social, and environmental context. Identified vulnerable groups being left behind: women, children, youth, migrants, persons with disabilities, and atoll populations. Assessed progress toward the 2030 Agenda and SDGs, finding significant institutional weaknesses, financing gaps, and geographic disparities across all development dimensions. The CCA's identification of structural vulnerabilities informed the UNSDCF 2022–2026 programming, which targets outcomes ensuring "people in the Maldives, especially the most vulnerable and marginalized, benefit from increased access to and use of quality, equitable, inclusive and resilient social and protection services."

**Methodology:** UN system-wide country analysis integrating political, economic, social, and environmental dimensions.

**Context:** Provides the analytical foundation for the UNSDCF 2022–2026 and all UN agency programming in the Maldives.

**Relevance:** The integrated UN system perspective on the Maldives' development challenges, documenting how environmental vulnerability, institutional weakness, and social inequality intersect.

### Climate Risk Country Profile: Maldives

**Citation:** World Bank Group / Asian Development Bank. (2021). *Climate Risk Country Profile: Maldives*. WBG / ADB.

**Key Findings:** Joint assessment synthesising data on climate hazards, projected temperature and precipitation changes, sea level rise, and impacts on key sectors. Found that the Maldives – with 80% of its land area less than one metre above sea level – faces existential risk from sea level rise, with projected impacts on freshwater availability, coastal erosion, and agricultural productivity. Climate risk compounds social vulnerability: atoll communities with higher poverty rates are also those most exposed to climate hazards, and the populations least able to adapt (elderly, persons with disabilities, female-headed households) are disproportionately located in the most climate-vulnerable areas.

**Methodology:** Joint multilateral development bank climate risk assessment using climate modelling and projection data.

**Context:** The climate science basis for understanding the environmental threats that compound every other dimension of social vulnerability examined in this encyclopedia.

**Relevance:** Establishes that the Maldives' social policy challenges cannot be understood in isolation from climate risk – housing, poverty, health, food security, and population distribution are all shaped by environmental vulnerability.

## 10. Social Protection

### Republic of the Maldives: Updating and Improving the Social Protection Index

**Citation:** Ibrahim, A. (2012). *Republic of the Maldives: Updating and Improving the Social Protection Index*. ADB Technical Assistance Consultant's Report. Manila: Asian Development Bank.

**Key Findings:** The study identified **20 distinct social protection programmes** in the Maldives, classified into three categories: social assistance (13 programmes), social insurance (6 programmes), and labour market programmes (1 programme). Total social protection expenditure for the **2009 reference year was MVR 782 million, amounting to 3.5% of GDP**. Social assistance constituted **52%** of total spending, social insurance **47%**, and labour market programmes barely **1%**. The study inventoried the civil service pension scheme (covering approximately 36,000 government employees with an annual expenditure of MVR 99 million and 4,326 beneficiaries), the government provident fund, the old-age basic pension (MVR 310.9 million for 13,421 beneficiaries in 2009), and the disability financial assistance programme (then covering only 250 beneficiaries at MVR 4.5 million). NSPA's role as the lead coordinating agency for social assistance was documented as nascent – the agency having been established only in 2008. The near-complete absence of active labour market programmes (1% of expenditure) was flagged as a critical gap for a country with high youth unemployment.

**Methodology:** ADB Social Protection Index (SPI) standardised methodology, using government administrative data on programme expenditure and beneficiary numbers for the 2009 reference year. Enables cross-country comparison across the Asia-Pacific region.

**Context:** Commissioned as part of ADB's regional SPI updating exercise. Provides the most systematic early inventory of the Maldives' social protection architecture at a time when the system was undergoing rapid expansion following the 2008 democratic transition and the introduction of the basic pension (2009) and Aasandha universal health insurance (2012).

**Relevance:** Establishes the quantitative baseline against which the subsequent expansion of social protection – particularly the dramatic growth of Aasandha from MVR 1.6 million (2008) to MVR 1.9 billion (2024 budget) and the basic pension from MVR 310.9 million (2009) to MVR 1.03 billion (2020) – can be measured. The finding that labour market programmes received only 1% of expenditure remains relevant, as this imbalance persists.

**Link:** ADB Technical Assistance report repository.

### Social Service Workers – National Pay Commission 2023

**Citation:** Ministry of Finance / National Pay Commission. (2023). *Social Service Workers*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The job classification document establishes the pay structure for social service workers in the Maldivian civil service, ranging from Chief Social Services Officer to lead-level positions. Salaries are denominated in MVR with structured allowance steps. The document reveals a critical structural problem: social workers carry **average caseloads exceeding 160 cases per worker** (documented by UNICEF, 2022), against a recommended maximum of 25–30 cases. In the Male area, approximately **30 domestic violence cases are reported monthly but only 3 case workers are available**, producing a ratio of 10 cases per worker per month on top of existing caseloads of 70–80. The first cohort of trained social workers emerged only in 2006–2007, and the supply of BSW graduates from the Maldives National University has never matched demand across 20 atolls and approximately 200 inhabited islands. **Context:** Issued as part of the National Pay Commission's reclassification exercise. Reveals the structural workforce architecture underpinning all social service delivery.

**Relevance:** The staffing architecture documented here is the binding constraint on every social programme examined in this encyclopedia. With only **61 social workers serving 128,208 children** across 189 inhabited islands, the gap between policy ambition and workforce capacity is a central systemic challenge in Maldivian social policy.

### ESMF: Maldives COVID-19 Emergency Income Support Project (P174014)

**Citation:** Ministry of Economic Development. (2020, revised 2021). *Environment and Social Management Framework: Maldives COVID-19 Emergency Income Support Project (P174014)*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The ESMF documents the policy, legal, and regulatory framework for emergency income support delivery during the pandemic. Key findings embedded in the baseline assessment include: COVID-19 contracted Maldives' real GDP by **33.6% in 2020**; approximately **44% of households experienced income loss** during the pandemic; the Income Support Allowance (ISA) provided up to **MVR 5,000 per month** to approximately **23,000 workers** who lost jobs or income, with payments delivered for up to nine months; the project highlighted the **absence of a social registry or unified beneficiary database** as a critical bottleneck in rapidly identifying and reaching affected households; and the ISA reached categories of workers – particularly informal and self-employed workers – who had never previously been part of the social protection system. **Relevance:** Documents the critical weaknesses in the social protection infrastructure exposed by the pandemic – absence of a social registry, informal sector blind spot, limited shock-responsiveness, digital infrastructure gaps, and gender-blind initial design – that directly informed the National Social Protection Framework 2022–2026 and the SAILS project.

### Climate Risk Country Profile: Maldives

**Citation:** World Bank Group / Asian Development Bank. (2021). *Climate Risk Country Profile: Maldives*. Washington, DC: WBG / ADB.

### Vulnerability to Disaster in the Maldives: Maamigili and Fenfushi

**Citation:** Borg Axisa, G., Borg, R. P., Ibrahim, M. H., & Nistharan, F. (2024). Vulnerability to Disaster in the Maldives: The Maamigili and Fenfushi Island Communities. *Island Studies Journal*, 19(1). https://doi.org/10.24043/isj.408

**Key Findings:** The study's central finding has profound implications for social policy: **even geographically proximate islands with similar geophysical hazards develop different vulnerability profiles** due to their distinct socio-environmental dynamics. Maamigili (1.7 km², population 2,359, accessible via ferry and airport, 107.6 km from Male) has relatively diversified economic connections and physical accessibility. Fenfushi (0.22 km², population 837, accessible only by ferry) is isolated with tourism limited to day excursions. The study concluded that **"general assumptions would not address the gaps and needs of communities of specific islands"** and that separate DRR strategies are required even for neighbouring islands. Community members reported that international actors following the 2004 tsunami set generalised objectives that did not adequately address island-specific gaps. The study also documented that the **2004 tsunami killed 82 people, displaced approximately 21,000, caused damage estimated at US$470 million (approximately 62% of GDP)**, and 14 islands were evacuated entirely.

**Methodology:** Mixed methods within the CABARET (Capacity Building in Asia for Resilience EducaTion) research framework: semi-structured interviews, questionnaires, and engagement with both national and local entities, analysed through perceived vulnerability assessment.

**Context:** Published in *Island Studies Journal* in 2024, this is the most methodologically rigorous recent study of island-level disaster vulnerability in the Maldives. The CABARET framework enables cross-national comparison while maintaining island-specific granularity.

**Relevance:** Directly challenges the efficiency logic of centralised social policy design. If DRR strategies must be island-specific even for neighbouring communities, the same principle applies to social service delivery, child protection, and health programming across 189 inhabited islands.

**Link:** https://doi.org/10.24043/isj.408

Key findings: despite upper-middle-income classification, significant populations cluster **just above the poverty threshold** and are vulnerable to falling into poverty from even modest shocks; geographic inequality between Male and atolls is the defining axis of inequality; the **compressed development trajectory** – from one of Asia's poorest nations to upper-middle-income status within a generation – has produced specific tensions including rapid urbanisation, erosion of traditional social structures, and new forms of vulnerability alongside new opportunities; and **the HDI does not capture the distributional dimensions** of development that determine whether growth translates into improved lives for all citizens.

**Methodology:** UNDP Human Development Report methodology applied at the national level, combining quantitative human development indicators with qualitative analysis of vulnerability and inequality dynamics.

**Context:** The most comprehensive single-volume assessment of human development challenges in the Maldives, situating the country's development experience within the UNDP analytical framework.

**Relevance:** Frames the paradox at the heart of Maldivian social policy: a country classified as upper-middle-income but containing populations whose vulnerability – to economic shocks, climate events, and institutional failures – is characteristic of much poorer nations.

### Maldives: Overcoming the Challenges of a Small Island State

**Citation:** Asian Development Bank. (2015). *Maldives: Overcoming the Challenges of a Small Island State – Country Diagnostic Study*. ADB.

**Key Findings:** Examines the structural constraints facing the Maldives as a SIDS: **geographic dispersion** across 1,192 islands creates extreme diseconomies of scale in service delivery; **economic concentration in tourism** (approximately 28% of GDP directly) creates vulnerability to external shocks; **climate vulnerability** is an existential threat; and the **cost of delivering public services** across dispersed island populations is inherently higher per capita than in continental settings. The study quantifies the "SIDS premium" – the additional cost of governance, infrastructure, and service delivery attributable to small size, remoteness, and dispersion. It also identifies the tension between centralised governance (efficient for coordination) and decentralised delivery (necessary for access).

**Methodology:** ADB country diagnostic using structural economic analysis, public expenditure assessment, and comparative SIDS analysis.

**Context:** Provides the structural analysis of how SIDS characteristics shape all aspects of social policy delivery – the foundational context for understanding why standard policy models often fail in the Maldivian setting.

**Relevance:** Essential background for every chapter of this encyclopedia. The "SIDS premium" concept explains why adequate social service delivery in the Maldives requires proportionally higher investment than in continental states with comparable populations.

**Link:** ADB publications.

### Vulnerable Groups and Intersecting Categories of Identity in Laamu Atoll

**Citation:** McNamara, K. E., & Clissold, R. (2019). Vulnerable Groups and Preliminary Insights into Intersecting Categories of Identity in Laamu Atoll, Maldives. *Island Studies Journal*.

**Key Findings:** Applies an **intersectionality framework** to explore how overlapping identity categories shape vulnerability in Laamu Atoll. The study documents that youth, children, women, the elderly, persons with disabilities, displaced persons, migrants, and farmers each face distinct vulnerability profiles, but that **compound vulnerability** – where multiple identity categories intersect (e.g., elderly women with disabilities on remote islands) – produces the most severe disadvantage. Climate exposure, economic marginalisation, geographic isolation, and limited health and education infrastructure compound one another in specific island contexts. The study demonstrates that vulnerability categories used by policy-makers – "women," "elderly," "disabled" – are insufficient because they treat each category in isolation rather than recognising the compounding effects of multiple intersecting disadvantages.

**Methodology:** Qualitative research using intersectionality theory, with fieldwork conducted in Laamu Atoll communities through interviews and focus groups.

**Context:** One of the few studies to apply an intersectionality framework in the Maldivian context, published in the *Island Studies Journal* – providing methodological innovation for vulnerability analysis in SIDS.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that the categorical approach to social policy – separate programmes for women, elderly, disabled, children – fails to address the compounded vulnerabilities that produce the most severe disadvantage. Integrated, person-centred approaches are needed.

**Link:** *Island Studies Journal*.

## 15. Vulnerability Mapping Research Collection

The following sources were produced as part of the Vulnerability Mapping Research project, a government-commissioned exercise examining vulnerabilities among women and families in the Maldives, particularly during marriage and after divorce. This collection constitutes the most operationally detailed body of evidence on women's and family vulnerability in the Maldivian social policy literature.

### Vulnerability Mapping Research Findings (Revised)

**Citation:** Vulnerability Mapping Research Team. (2026). *Vulnerability Mapping Research Findings (Revised)*. Social Ministry, Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The compiled findings document a comprehensive picture of women's vulnerability across legal, economic, social, and institutional dimensions. Key findings include: the **Family Act creates structural gender asymmetries** – wives lose custody priority upon remarrying a non-*mahram* man; the minimum divorce fine for husbands is only MVR 100 (approximately USD 6); approximately **MVR 2 million in maintenance payments accumulated unclaimed** at Family Court; by the time of the CFW research (2026), only approximately **eight prenuptial agreements** had ever been made in the entire country; **women's unpaid care work carries no legal recognition** under the Family Act; and a wife exiting divorce after decades of household management has **no claim to marital property** absent a prenuptial agreement. The research documented that **DV is not specifically criminalised** as a standalone offence – the DVPA provides civil protective remedies only, with criminal prosecution requiring charges under the general Penal Code. This was identified as a factual correction to claims in some government documents.

**Methodology:** Mixed-methods research combining literature review, legal analysis, qualitative interviews with women and service providers, focus group discussions, and audit of existing systems and frameworks.

**Context:** Government-commissioned research for internal policy development, representing the most granular examination of women's vulnerability in the Maldivian context. The research team's access to frontline service providers and affected women produced operational-level detail unavailable in published academic literature.

**Relevance:** Provides the evidence base for the Comprehensive Framework for Women (CFW) policy reform process. The findings on structural gender asymmetries in family law, the non-criminalisation of DV, and the failure of maintenance enforcement mechanisms directly inform every recommendation in the CFW.

**Link:** Internal Social Ministry document.

### Current Systems and Frameworks (Vulnerability Mapping Section 2)

**Citation:** Vulnerability Mapping Research Team. (2026). *Current Systems and Frameworks* [edited full version]. Social Ministry.

**Key Findings:** Maps the existing institutional architecture for vulnerability response, documenting: **only 3% of DV/GBV cases** reported to police are submitted for prosecution; **only 14%** of violence against women cases at the police level are sent forward for prosecution; the conviction rate for those reaching court is only **16.7%** (2016–2019 data); only **3.7% of women who experienced violence received any form of legal assistance**; protection orders are described as **"set up for failure"** with court processing delays of 3 months or more for urgent orders; all documented protection order violations were by men; **4 established DV shelters were "neither functional nor equipped"** (confirmed by HRCM 2021 and CSO reports 2022); the only currently operational longer-term shelter is in Gaafu Dhaalu Atoll; and police response to DV reports involves **delays of weeks or months**, with documented instances of victim-blaming and pressure to reconcile.

**Methodology:** Systems analysis combining institutional mapping, administrative data analysis, stakeholder interviews, and service user experience documentation.

**Context:** The analytical backbone of the vulnerability mapping research, providing the detailed systems-level evidence that the findings document and the recommendations address.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that the gap between legislative ambition and operational reality in domestic violence response is not marginal but systemic – a 3% prosecution rate and non-functional shelters represent near-total system failure despite the existence of the DVPA 2012.

**Link:** Internal Social Ministry document.

### Legal Framework Analysis (Vulnerability Mapping Section 4)

**Citation:** Vulnerability Mapping Research Team. (2026). *Legal Only – Section 4* [edited version]. Social Ministry.

**Key Findings:** Focused analysis finding that: the **Family Act still allows marriage below 18** if the child has "reached puberty" (Section 4(b)), contradicting the CRPA 2019's age-18 minimum – the ADB (2025) documents a **2.2% child marriage rate** before age 18; approximately **165 marriages to a second wife** were registered in 2015 and 157 in 2016 (only registered polygamous marriages; unregistered may be more common); the **"chain-breaking" practice** (*tahlil* marriage) occurs where a man agrees, sometimes for payment, to briefly marry a divorced woman solely to enable her to return to her former husband; and analysis of **24 High Court custody cases** found that in every instance where the mother was awarded custody, she had remained unmarried – fathers awarded custody frequently delegated actual care entirely to relatives while denying mothers contact. DNA and paternity evidence is generally inadmissible in Family Court proceedings.

**Methodology:** Doctrinal legal analysis of relevant statutes, court decisions, and subsidiary regulations, cross-referenced with empirical evidence from administrative data and qualitative research.

**Context:** The most targeted legal analysis of family-related legislation from a vulnerability perspective, examining how specific legal provisions create, maintain, or exacerbate gender-based vulnerability.

**Relevance:** Documents the specific legal mechanisms through which women's vulnerability is produced and maintained – not through the absence of law but through the design and implementation of existing law.

**Link:** Internal Social Ministry document.

### National Action Plan on Disability Inclusion (NAPDI v6), 2024–2028

**Citation:** Ministry of Social and Family Development. (2024). *National Action Plan on Disability Inclusion (NAPDI v6), 2024–2028*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Structured around **5 policy goals, 25 policy statements, and 40+ strategies** addressing health and wellbeing, quality and inclusive education, decent work, accessible communities, and access to justice. The plan targets **24,401 persons with disabilities** (Census 2022), of whom only **13,656 (56%) are registered** in the National Disability Register (December 2024). The NAPDI v6 is notable for the **absence of costed budgets, specific numerical targets, or baseline data** against which progress can be measured – it reads as an aspiration framework rather than a costed implementation roadmap. The HRCM CRPD Shadow Report (2023) documented "incongruence between key legislations, and the regulations, policies or guidelines formulated to aid implementation" as "a critical barrier predominant in many areas."

**Methodology:** Action plan development through multi-stakeholder consultation, SDG target alignment, and UN agency technical support.

**Context:** The most structurally comprehensive of the active action plans, revised to v6 (clean version dated December 2024) following multiple rounds of consultation.

**Relevance:** The gap between the NAPDI's comprehensive design and the operational reality of disability inclusion – only 25.6% of persons with disabilities receive the disability allowance; healthcare providers "infantilise adults with disabilities"; higher education institutions "remain largely inaccessible" – defines the implementation challenge.

**Link:** Ministry of Social and Family Development.

### National Action Plan on Preventing and Responding to Violence Against Children (NAPVAC), 2024–2028

**Citation:** Ali, M., Yoosuf, A., & Waheed, M. I. (2024). *National Action Plan on Preventing and Responding to Violence Against Children (NAP-PRVAC), 2024–2028* [Draft II, Clean, April 2024]. Ministry of Social and Family Development.

**Key Findings:** Grounded in a socio-ecological model with **5 strategic areas, 12 outcomes, and 20+ output indicators**. Documents that MoSFD received **2,936 cases in 2023**, of which **1,278 (43.5%) were VAC cases**, with negligence and sexual abuse as the most common types. The plan explicitly references UK child protection frameworks (Children Act 1989, Section 47 investigations) as conceptual touchstones – continuing the pattern of transposing English systems without adequate contextualisation to a reality of **61 social workers serving 128,208 children across 189 inhabited islands**. The plan's own introduction acknowledges that the predecessor NAP-PRVAC 2017 was **never effectively implemented** due to "lack of a systematic and holistic approach" and "difficulties in establishing collaborative efforts with stakeholders," and that **"similar challenges were identified"** during consultations for the 2024–2028 renewal.

**Methodology:** Policy development using socio-ecological model (Guerrero and Rojas, 2016), UNDRR disaster risk dimensions framework, theory of change, and multi-stakeholder consultation.

**Context:** The most methodologically sophisticated of the active plans, incorporating trauma-informed care principles and international best practice. Its candid acknowledgment of predecessor plan failure provides important institutional learning.

**Relevance:** The question is whether the structural barriers that defeated the 2017 plan – coordination failure, workforce shortages, geographic dispersion – have been sufficiently addressed to enable the 2024 plan to succeed.

**Link:** Ministry of Social and Family Development (draft).

### National Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP), 2022–2026

**Citation:** Ministry of Gender, Family and Social Services (now MoSFD). (2022). *National Gender Equality Action Plan (GEAP), 2022–2026*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** Structured around **5 policy goals and 20+ strategies** across leadership and governance, economic empowerment, institutional gender mainstreaming, elimination of GBV, and access to justice. The GEAP is the most institutionally grounded plan, being a **statutory requirement under Article 42 of the Gender Equality Act 2016**. However, nearly six years after the Act's passage, the Gender Focal Point network and capacity-building infrastructure **remained incomplete** – the Terms of Reference for updating the gender training manual in Dhivehi were only issued in October 2022. The plan assigns responsibilities across **25+ implementing agencies**, each with their own priorities and capacity constraints, but provides limited detail on accountability mechanisms for non-compliance.

**Methodology:** Policy development under statutory mandate (Gender Equality Act 2016, Article 42), deriving operational strategies from the National Gender Equality Policy (2019) and CEDAW obligations.

**Context:** The only action plan with explicit statutory authority, giving it a legal mandate that other plans lack. The legal grounding should, in principle, strengthen accountability and continuity across government changes.

**Relevance:** The GEAP's strength (statutory mandate) is also its weakness – assigning extensive responsibilities across 25+ agencies without enforcement mechanisms produces the coordination failure that is the defining challenge of Maldivian inter-agency work.

**Link:** Ministry of Social and Family Development.

### National Ageing Policy (Proposed Revision), 2024

**Citation:** Ministry of Social and Family Development. (2024). *Maldives National Ageing Policy* [Proposed Draft – Revision of 2018 National Elderly Policy]. Drafted 28 August 2024, revised 9 September 2024.

**Key Findings:** The draft identifies alarming trends: **46% of the elderly population has hypertension, 28% has diabetes, 45% has a disability**; the **informal employment rate stands at 89.9%** (with **99.1% for women**), meaning the vast majority of the ageing population has no contributory pension entitlements beyond the universal basic pension; the elderly population is projected to rise from **8.3% in 2023 to 34.1% by 2054**; and the government's own MIPAA survey acknowledged that **"lack of overall political support" for ageing issues is "always" present**, with "other pressing issues taking precedence." The draft is structured around **4 policy areas and 14 goals** covering development/prevention/protection, health and wellbeing, enabling environments, and income security. The absence of a **dedicated elderly law** means the policy has no statutory enforcement mechanism. The predecessor National Plan of Action on Older Persons (2019–2023) was **"severely disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic"** with **"no monitoring done since the pandemic situation."**

**Methodology:** Policy revision through external consultant engagement, alignment with the Fourth Review and Appraisal of MIPAA in Asia-Pacific, and 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development targets.

**Context:** The only instrument in the current portfolio still in draft status, reflecting the political marginalisation of ageing issues acknowledged in the government's own assessment.

**Relevance:** The demographic projections make this the most time-sensitive of all the action plans – a population reaching 34.1% aged 60+ by 2054 requires preparation now, but the policy lacks statutory basis, political support, and implementation capacity.

**Link:** Ministry of Social and Family Development (draft).

Key data used throughout this encyclopedia: **128,208 children** under 18; **24,401 persons with disabilities** (7% of population, using Washington Group methodology); elderly population of approximately **43,000 (8.3%)**; approximately **199 inhabited islands** across 20 administrative atolls; the proportion of **divorced women exceeds divorced men by 42.86%**; and detailed demographic data disaggregated by atoll, sex, age, disability status, education level, and employment status. The census provides the denominators for all coverage calculations (e.g., disability allowance reaching only 25.6% of eligible persons).

**Methodology:** National census using standardised enumeration methodology with the Washington Group Short Set on Functioning for disability measurement.

**Context:** The most recent national census, providing the definitive demographic baseline for all social policy analysis. Supersedes the 2014 Census as the current reference.

**Relevance:** Every quantitative finding in this encyclopedia ultimately derives from or references Census 2022 data. The transition to Washington Group methodology for disability measurement represents a significant improvement in disability data quality and international comparability.

**Link:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics.

### Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES) 2019

**Citation:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics. (2019). *Household Income and Expenditure Survey 2019*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** The HIES 2019 provides the most recent household-level economic data. Key findings used in the encyclopedia: the **official poverty headcount ratio of 8.2%** (2016 data, the most recent available), but with significant spatial inequality – **0.9% in Greater Male vs. 9.5% in atolls**; the average expenditure of the **bottom 40% is 2.5 times lower** than the top 60%; **over 90% of poor Maldivians live in atolls**; housing consumes up to **37% of household expenditure** in Greater Male; and many Maldivians cluster just above the poverty threshold and are vulnerable to falling into poverty from even modest economic shocks.

**Methodology:** National household survey using standardised income and expenditure measurement methodology with stratified sampling across atolls and Male.

**Context:** Conducted before the COVID-19 pandemic, the HIES 2019 captures the pre-pandemic economic baseline. The pandemic's 33.6% GDP contraction in 2020 would have dramatically altered the poverty and expenditure patterns documented here.

**Relevance:** Provides the economic vulnerability baseline that contextualises all social protection analysis. The Male-atoll poverty divide (0.9% vs. 9.5%) is the defining axis of geographic inequality in the Maldives.

**Link:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics.

### Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) 2016/17

**Citation:** Ministry of Health / ICF. (2018). *Maldives Demographic and Health Survey 2016–17*. Government of Maldives / ICF.

**Key Findings:** The DHS provides health, nutrition, and demographic indicators disaggregated by region, wealth, and other sociodemographic characteristics. Key data used across the encyclopedia: maternal and child health indicators including immunisation coverage, nutritional status, and reproductive health service utilisation; **approximately 49% of total health expenditure is out-of-pocket** despite universal health insurance; and the survey provides the baseline health indicators against which the RMNCAH Strategy 2020–2025 measures progress. Disability prevalence using the Washington Group methodology was first measured systematically in this survey, establishing the methodological approach subsequently adopted in Census 2022.

**Methodology:** Nationally representative household survey using ICF's standardised DHS methodology with face-to-face interviews, anthropometric measurements, and biomarker collection.

**Context:** The most recent DHS for the Maldives, providing the baseline health data that informed the Health Master Plan 2016–2025 and the RMNCAH Strategy.

**Relevance:** The DHS is the primary source for health indicator tracking in the Maldives, providing the data against which health system performance and social determinants of health are assessed.

**Link:** DHS Program website.

### Clean Island Service Datasets

**Citation:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics / Ministry of Social and Family Development. (Various years). *Clean Island Service Datasets*. Government of Maldives.

**Key Findings:** These datasets – cleaned and standardised from raw administrative data – provide island-level information on service availability, infrastructure, and staffing across all inhabited islands. Key data points include: which islands have health facilities and at what level (health centre, health post, hospital); which islands have FCSC presence; school availability by level; transport connections and distances to Male; and utility infrastructure (water, electricity, sewage). The datasets enable the construction of service access indices that reveal the extreme geographic inequality in social service availability – islands accessible only by multi-hour boat journeys with no health facility, no social worker, and intermittent transport connections.

**Methodology:** Administrative data cleaning and standardisation from multiple government agency databases, with island-level georeferencing.

**Context:** Used primarily for the Service Provider Directory and for quantitative analysis of geographic access disparities across the encyclopedia.

**Relevance:** These datasets provide the empirical foundation for understanding the "last mile" challenge of social service delivery in the Maldives – the gap between national policy commitments and the reality of what services are actually available on any given island.

**Link:** Maldives Bureau of Statistics; Social Ministry data files.

### A Maqasid Approach to Protecting Victims of Child Sexual Abuse in the Maldives (2013)

**Citation:** Ali, Mariya. (2013). *A Maqasid approach to protecting victims of child sexual abuse in the Maldives compliant with the U.N. Convention on the rights of the child.* PhD thesis, Institute of Commonwealth Studies, University of London. Published article: Ali, M. (2014). "Child sexual abuse: can the doctrines of al-maqasid al-shariah and maslahah assist in challenging the honour ideology?" *The International Journal of Human Rights*, 18(4-5).

**Key Findings:** The thesis highlights the potential of a humanistic Islamic framework – the doctrines of al-maqasid al-shariah and maslahah – to challenge the honour ideology surrounding child sexual abuse (CSA) in the Maldives. It illustrates the congruency between the two paradigms: that CSA violates the fundamental rights of the victim as considered under maslahah, which mirrors the stipulated rights under international human rights documents, including the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child. The thesis argues that Islamic jurisprudential doctrines and international child rights law are not inherently contradictory but can be aligned to strengthen victim protection.

**Methodology:** Legal analysis drawing on Islamic jurisprudence (maqasid al-shariah and maslahah doctrines) and international human rights law.

**Context:** Written by a Maldivian scholar now at Oxford Brookes University, the thesis addresses the critical tension between Islamic legal traditions and international child rights obligations – a tension that shapes the practical response to child sexual abuse in the Maldives.

**Relevance:** Foundational academic work demonstrating the compatibility of Islamic law with international child rights standards in the specific context of CSA in the Maldives.

**URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/13642987.2014.911478

### Local Implications of International Human Rights Treaties: The Impact of a CEDAW Right to Public Life in the Republic of Maldives (2016)

**Citation:** Jabyn, Marium. (2016). *Local Implications of International Human Rights Treaties: The Impact of a CEDAW Right to Public Life in the Republic of Maldives.* PhD thesis, University of Waikato, New Zealand.

**Key Findings:** While CEDAW provides a framework for substantive equality intended to empower women to pursue the right to public life, the Maldivian State has yet to ensure this right as envisaged by the Convention. CEDAW has had mainly formal-level impact on domestic law and policy but limited impact on achieving actual equality for women. Challenges identified include the international human rights platform's reliance on State consent, limited intercultural dialogue, ad hoc procedures, lack of committed personnel to draft treaty reports, low civil society engagement, and "local struggles" rooted in culture and religion that constrain implementation.

**Methodology:** Analysis of domestic legal instruments to measure CEDAW incorporation; semi-structured interviews with State and non-State actors.

**Context:** Examines the gap between "rights-in-principle" and "rights-in-practice" in a small Muslim-majority state that has ratified CEDAW but with significant reservations.

**Relevance:** Essential for understanding why CEDAW implementation remains limited in the Maldives despite formal legal incorporation – directly relevant to the encyclopedia's gender chapter analysis.

**URL:** https://researchcommons.waikato.ac.nz/handle/10289/10558

### Sexual Health Policies and Youth: A Case Study of the Maldives (2012)

**Citation:** Hameed, Shaffa. (2012). *Sexual health policies and youth: a case study of the Maldives.* PhD thesis, London School of Economics and Political Science (LSE), University of London.

**Key Findings:** Two key characteristics were identified: (1) youth sexual health experiences appear to be disconnected from sexual health policies, services, and data; and (2) there is a mutually reinforcing relationship between official sexual health data and policies, where restrictive policies dictate the type and extent of data that may be collected, which then reinforce justifications for the current restrictive policies and limited services. In an Islamic state where premarital sexual activity is a punishable offence, the disconnect between policy assumptions and youth lived experience is particularly acute.

**Methodology:** Mixed methods – qualitative in-depth interviews (n=61) with youth aged 18-24 from three sites in the Maldives; key informant interviews (n=17) with policy actors and service providers; a web-based quantitative survey of Maldivian youth (n=480); and secondary analysis of the Maldives DHS 2009.

**Context:** One of the most methodologically rigorous studies of youth sexual health in the Maldives, notable for its large sample sizes and mixed-methods design.

**Relevance:** Directly relevant to the encyclopedia's analysis of sexual and reproductive health rights, the criminalisation of premarital sex (zina), and the evidence-policy disconnect in Maldivian health governance.

**URL:** http://etheses.lse.ac.uk/731/

### A Cohort Study of Postnatal Depression, Infant Feeding Practices and Infant Growth in Male' (2014)

**Citation:** Abdul Raheem, Raheema. (2014). *A cohort study of postnatal depression, infant feeding practices and infant growth in Male', the Republic of Maldives.* PhD thesis, Curtin University, Australia.

**Key Findings:** Prevalence of depressive symptoms (EPDS score of 13 or above) was 24% at 36 weeks of pregnancy, 27% at 1 month postpartum, and 12% at 3 months postpartum. Having experienced stressful life events was an established risk factor for maternal depression across these time points. The study also examined associations between maternal depression and breastfeeding practices, and between breastfeeding and infant growth.

**Methodology:** Prospective cohort study of 458 mothers recruited from antenatal clinics at two major hospitals in Male', followed from 36 weeks of pregnancy to 6 months postpartum. Maternal depression measured using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS), with follow-up at 4 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months post-birth.

**Context:** One of the few longitudinal clinical studies conducted in the Maldives, providing rare cohort-level data on maternal mental health prevalence.

**Relevance:** Provides the only Maldivian-specific prevalence data for postnatal depression – directly relevant to the encyclopedia's mental health and women's health analyses.

**URL:** https://australiaawardsmaldives.org/stories/dr-raheema-abdul-raheem/

### The Wellbeing and Social Connectedness of Older People in the SIDS of Maldives (2016)

**Citation:** Moosa, Sheena. (2016). *The wellbeing and social connectedness of older people in the Small Island Developing State (SIDS) of Maldives.* PhD thesis, University of Waikato, New Zealand. Supervisor: Emeritus Professor Dame Peggy Koopman-Boyden. Published chapter: Moosa, S. (2019). "Social Connectedness and Wellbeing of Ageing Populations in Small Islands," IntechOpen.

**Key Findings:** Using a capabilities approach to understanding wellbeing, the study examined older people's experiences across five life domains: health, social connectedness, economic status, access to goods and services, and ability to conform with social norms and values. The most important determinants of wellbeing were health and social connectedness, each having a large impact (overall social connectedness had a 29% shared contribution with wellbeing). Economic status, ability to conform with social values and norms, and access to goods and services had small but significant correlations. The centrality of family in social connectedness pointed to the importance of collectivist social arrangements despite geospatial challenges. Structured community-based social activities for older people were found to be irregular and occasional, and formal voluntary associations working with older people were non-existent outside Male'.

**Methodology:** Custom survey instrument developed in consultation with health and social service sector professionals and older people themselves; 393 older people (65+ years) interviewed across 31 islands.

**Context:** The only doctoral-level study dedicated to elderly wellbeing in the Maldives. Dr. Moosa (MBBS Delhi, MPH Leeds, PhD Waikato) subsequently worked at Health New Zealand. Her supervisor, Dame Peggy Koopman-Boyden, was a leading social gerontologist.

**Relevance:** Provides the empirical foundation for the encyclopedia's elderly chapter analysis of social connectedness, family support erosion, and the geographic dimensions of ageing in the Maldives.

**URL:** https://researchcommons.waikato.ac.nz/handle/10289/9967

### Drawing the Line: A Consensual Approach to Setting Poverty Standards in the Maldives (2011)

**Citation:** Ahmed, Asim. (2011). *Drawing the line: a consensual approach to setting poverty standards in the Maldives and examining the implications for poverty.* PhD in Social Policy, Victoria University of Wellington, New Zealand.

**Key Findings:** *Full abstract not available online.* The thesis applied the consensual method of poverty measurement – where the public is asked to define what constitutes necessities – to the Maldives context, establishing poverty standards and examining their implications. This represents one of the few applications of the consensual deprivation approach in a SIDS context. The author (Asim Ahmed) subsequently had a prominent career in Maldivian public finance.

**Methodology:** Consensual approach to poverty measurement (further details not available online).

### Civil Service in an Emerging Democracy: The Case of the Maldives (2013)

**Citation:** Faizal, Mohamed. (2013). *Civil service in an emerging democracy: the case of the Maldives.* PhD thesis, Victoria University of Wellington, New Zealand.

**Key Findings:** The development of the civil service model and negotiation of the Civil Service Act were part of the democratisation process but were in the hands of political elites with no wider public participation. The transition to democracy in the Maldives is not complete, remaining relatively unstable and fragile, with ongoing contestation between the political and administrative arms of the executive.

**Methodology:** Case study approach with qualitative data.

**Context:** Examines the establishment of a statutory civil service as an element of democratisation in the Maldives.

**Relevance:** Directly relevant to the encyclopedia's analysis of institutional architecture – the civil service provides the bureaucratic infrastructure through which social policy is delivered, and its politicisation undermines service delivery.

### Private Housing Development: Refining Rational Choice (2008)

### A Study of the Cluster Schools Policy in the Maldives (2006)

**Citation:** Ali, Aamaal. (2006). *A study of the cluster schools policy in the Maldives.* Doctor of Education (EdD) thesis, Institute of Education, University of London.

**Key Findings:** Despite universal access to primary education in the Maldives, there are wide disparities in the quality of education provided by schools in the capital and schools on the rural islands. The Ministry of Education introduced a policy in July 1999 forming clusters of schools, consisting of a 'lead' government school intended to serve as a resource and support to a number of nearby community schools. Each cluster is usually made up of 6 to 11 schools.

**Methodology:** Qualitative, based on perceptions of key stakeholders.

**Relevance:** Addresses the urban-rural education quality gap – a structural inequality that shapes access to opportunity across the archipelago and is directly relevant to the geographic dimensions of social policy delivery.

### Assessing Community Participation in Health Promotion Programs: A Case Study of Maldives (2015)

### The Crisis of Iron in Transfusion Medicine: Improved Iron Chelation Therapy and Its Implications for Clinical Practice in the Maldives (2011)

**Citation:** Mustafa, Ibrahim. (2011). *The crisis of iron in transfusion medicine: improved iron chelation therapy and its implications for clinical practice in the Maldives.* PhD in Pathology and Laboratory Medicine, University of British Columbia, Vancouver, Canada.

**Key Findings:** The Maldives has one of the highest incidences of beta-thalassaemia in the world. Treatment involves two phases: treatment of anemia and secondary treatment of transfusional iron overload. Current iron chelation therapy using Desferal (DFO) is challenging due to its short vascular half-life, frequency of injections, toxicity, and expense. The research tested novel, low toxicity, high molecular weight iron chelators; combination therapy (L1+S-DFO) resulted in a 79% decrease in FAC-driven ferritin levels after 48 hours.

**Methodology:** Laboratory-based experimental research testing novel iron chelation compounds.

**Relevance:** Addresses the Maldives' thalassaemia burden (18% carrier rate) from a clinical research perspective – relevant to the encyclopedia's health systems analysis of the NCD and genetic disease burden.

**URL:** https://open.library.ubc.ca/soa/cIRcle/collections/ubctheses/24/items/1.0072029
